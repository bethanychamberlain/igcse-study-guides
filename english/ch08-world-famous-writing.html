Bio vs Auto

About 5 minutes

Read each short passage. Decide: is it biography (B) — written about someone else in third person — or autobiography (A) — written about yourself in first person? Click the correct button.

  • 1 "Serena Williams was born in Saginaw, Michigan, in 1981. She began playing tennis at the age of three and went on to win 23 Grand Slam singles titles, more than any other player in the Open Era." ___
  • 2 "I remember the first time I stood on a proper stage. My hands were shaking so badly I nearly dropped the microphone. But once the music started, something clicked — I forgot the crowd and just sang." ___
  • 3 "The young scientist spent two years working in near-total isolation. According to her colleagues, she rarely left the laboratory before midnight. Her dedication resulted in a breakthrough that changed modern medicine." ___
  • 4 "Looking back, moving to Denmark was the hardest thing I've ever done. Everything was unfamiliar — the language, the food, even the way people said hello. But it was also the best decision of my life." ___
  • 5 "Marcus Rashford grew up in Wythenshawe, Manchester, in a family that often struggled to afford meals. He used his platform as a professional footballer to campaign against child food poverty, forcing the UK government to change its policy." ___
  • 6 "People always ask me why I started the business so young. The truth is, I didn't have a grand plan. I just noticed that nobody at school could get decent coffee, so I bought a second-hand espresso machine and set up in the common room." ___
Key difference
Biography = third person (he, she, they), factual tone, written by someone else. Autobiography = first person (I, me, my), personal tone, written by the person themselves. Look for pronouns — that's the fastest way to tell.

Tone Slider

About 5 minutes

Below are 3 facts about a person. For each one, write the same fact twice: once in a formal tone (for a textbook biography) and once in an informal tone (as if telling a friend).

Fact 1 "She moved to a new country at age 14."
Formal:
Think textbook — no slang, no contractions, measured and objective.
Informal:
Think texting a friend — casual, might use "like" or "basically", shows personality.
Fact 2 "He failed his first exam but later became top of the class."
Formal:
Neutral, factual. Let the facts speak for themselves.
Informal:
Show some emotion. How would you actually tell this story?
Fact 3 "They started their own business at 16."
Formal:
Imagine this sentence in an encyclopedia entry.
Informal:
Imagine telling your best friend this over lunch.
Formal vs Informal
Formal: no contractions (use "did not" not "didn't"), no slang, complete sentences, objective tone.
Informal: contractions are fine, conversational, opinions and reactions welcome, sentence fragments okay.
In Exercise 4, you'll write a biography — that means formal tone. This exercise helps you feel the difference.

Key Detail Selector

  • She usually has toast with Nutella for breakfast.
  • Won a national young scientists competition at age 15 for her research on water purification.
  • Her cat is called Mango.
  • Gave a TEDx talk at age 17 about making clean water accessible in developing countries.

  • Founded a non-profit organisation that has provided water filters to over 5,000 families.
  • Plans to study biomedical engineering at university and wants to develop low-cost medical technology.
0 of 5 selected
How to choose key details
Ask yourself: does this fact tell the reader who this person is, what they achieved, or why they matter? If the answer is no — if it's just a random personal fact — leave it out. A biography is about significance, not trivia.

Your biography should
• Be in third person throughout — this is biography, not autobiography
• Use a formal tone — no slang, no "like", no text-speak
• Select only the key details (like you practised in Exercise 3)
• Follow a logical order — usually chronological (early life → achievements → impact)
• End by showing why this person matters

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your biography from Exercise 4 out loud. Yes, actually out loud — you'll hear awkward phrasing that you won't catch by reading silently. Then check each box honestly.

  • 3rd person throughout — No "I" or "me" anywhere. It's all "he", "she", or "they".
  • Key details selected — Not everything, just what matters. No favourite colours or breakfast habits.
  • Chronological or logical order — The facts flow in a sequence that makes sense to the reader.
  • Formal tone — No slang, no "like", no contractions. Reads like a textbook, not a text message.
  • Shows WHY this person matters — Not just what they did, but why it's significant.
  • Clear and varied sentences — Not every sentence starts the same way. Mix short and long.
