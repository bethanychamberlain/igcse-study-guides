Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each. Define key writing techniques and terms from this chapter.
1
Name and briefly explain the four past tenses in English. For each, write one example sentence about a family holiday.
4 marks
Answer
1. Simple past: completed action — "We visited the beach last summer." [1]
2. Past continuous: ongoing action in the past — "The children were playing in the waves." [1]
3. Past perfect: action completed before another past action — "We had packed the car before sunrise." [1]
4. Past perfect continuous: ongoing action before another past action — "They had been driving for three hours when the storm hit." [1]
Knowing your tenses matters for narrative writing. The past continuous is especially useful for setting scenes ("The sun was setting..."), and the past perfect creates flashbacks ("She had never seen the ocean before").

2
Explain what "show, don't tell" means in creative writing. Give one "telling" sentence and rewrite it as a "showing" sentence.
2 marks
Answer
"Show, don't tell" means using descriptive details, actions, and sensory language to let the reader experience emotions or events, rather than simply stating them [1].
Telling: "She was nervous."
Showing: "Her fingers twisted the hem of her shirt as she glanced at the clock for the third time." [1]
The "showing" sentence must include at least one physical action or sensory detail that implies the emotion without naming it. This is the single most important technique for upgrading your narrative writing from a D to a B.

3
State three rules for punctuating dialogue correctly in a story.
3 marks
Answer
Any three of:
1. Speech marks go around the spoken words only [1].
2. Start a new line (new paragraph) for each new speaker [1].
3. Punctuation goes inside the speech marks [1].
4. Use a comma before the closing speech mark if a speech tag follows (e.g. "Hello," she said).
5. Begin the spoken words with a capital letter.
Dialogue mistakes are one of the easiest ways to lose marks in narrative writing. If you can't remember all the rules, at least remember: new speaker = new line, and punctuation goes INSIDE the speech marks.

What does chronological order mean? Name one alternative structure a writer might use instead, and explain why they might choose it.
2 marks
Answer
Chronological order means telling events in the order they happened, from first to last [1].
Alternative: Starting in medias res (in the middle of the action) or using a flashback structure. A writer might choose this to create immediate suspense or to hook the reader before explaining the backstory [1].
In the exam, if you're writing a story with a time limit, starting in the middle of the action is usually more effective than "I woke up that morning..." — examiners reward confident structural choices.

Intermediate — Can You Explain and Apply It?

Worth 3-5 marks each. Apply writing techniques and analyse how language creates effects.
5
Read this sentence: "The old house stood at the end of the road like a forgotten promise." Write a PEE paragraph explaining the effect of the simile on the reader.
4 marks
Answer
P: The simile creates a sense of sadness and neglect, suggesting the house was once important to someone [1].
E: The writer compares the house to "a forgotten promise" [1].
E: A promise implies something valued and personal, so describing it as "forgotten" suggests abandonment and broken expectations. This evokes sympathy in the reader and hints at a backstory of loss. The word "forgotten" also implies that the house, like a promise, once held meaning that has faded over time, creating a melancholy atmosphere [2].
When analysing a simile, always explain BOTH sides of the comparison: what is being compared (the house) and what it is compared to (a forgotten promise), then explain what this pairing MAKES THE READER FEEL. That emotional effect is where the top marks are.

6
Rewrite this "telling" paragraph using "show, don't tell" techniques:
"It was a hot day. The family was tired after the long journey. The children were excited to see the sea."
4 marks
Answer
Example rewrite: "Heat shimmered off the tarmac as the car doors finally swung open [1]. Dad stretched his back with a groan, and Mum pressed a cold water bottle against her forehead [1]. But the moment the salt air hit them, the twins were already sprinting down the dune path, their flip-flops kicked off and forgotten [1]."

Mark scheme: 1 mark for showing heat through sensory detail (not just saying "hot"), 1 mark for showing tiredness through actions (not the word "tired"), 1 mark for showing excitement through actions (not the word "excited"), 1 mark for overall quality of descriptive writing [1].
The key test: could a reader INFER the emotion without being told it? "Sprinting" and "flip-flops kicked off" show excitement. "Groan" and "pressed a cold bottle against her forehead" show tiredness and heat. If you have to use the adjective (hot, tired, excited), you're telling, not showing.

7
Explain the difference between tone and register. For each, give an example of how a writer might change it depending on whether they are writing a personal diary entry vs. a formal letter about a family event.
3 marks
Answer
Register is the level of formality of the language (formal vs informal) [1].
Tone is the attitude or feeling conveyed by the writing (e.g. angry, humorous, nostalgic, sad) [1].
Example: A diary entry about a family reunion might use informal register ("Gran's roast was INSANE, best ever") with a warm, excited tone. A formal letter about the same event might use formal register ("I am writing to express my gratitude for hosting the family gathering") with a polite, respectful tone [1].
Students often confuse tone and register. Think of it this way: register = how dressed up your language is (casual vs formal). Tone = what emotion your voice carries (happy, sad, angry). You can have formal + angry, or informal + sad. They're independent.

Advanced — Can You Analyse and Write?

Worth 5-8 marks each. These need extended writing with clear structure, PEE paragraphs, and sophisticated techniques.
8
Write the opening paragraph (5-7 sentences) of a short story titled "The Last Family Dinner." You must use: (a) an in medias res opening, (b) at least one line of correctly punctuated dialogue, and (c) at least one "show, don't tell" moment.
6 marks
Answer
Example opening:
"The gravy boat slipped from her fingers and shattered against the tiles. Nobody moved. Dad's fork hovered halfway to his mouth, and across the table, Amir's chair scraped back an inch, as if he was already calculating the distance to the door.
 ‘Leave it,’ Mum said, her voice tight as a wire. ‘Just — leave it.’
But it wasn't the gravy she was talking about."

Mark scheme:
[2] In medias res: starts in the middle of action/tension (not "That morning, I woke up...")
[2] Dialogue: correctly punctuated with speech marks, comma, speech tag, new line for new speaker
[2] Show don't tell: emotions shown through actions (fork hovering, chair scraping, voice "tight as a wire") rather than stated
Notice how the example ends with a hook: "it wasn't the gravy she was talking about." This creates mystery and makes the reader want to continue. For full marks, your opening should make the examiner want to read the next paragraph.

9
A non-fiction text describes a family tradition using a nostalgic tone. Write two PEE paragraphs explaining how a writer could create a nostalgic tone. For each paragraph, name a specific language technique and explain its effect.
6 marks
Answer
PEE 1:
P: A writer can create nostalgia through sensory imagery that triggers memories [1].
E: For example, describing "the smell of cinnamon and woodsmoke that always meant Christmas at Grandma's house" [1].
E: Smell is the sense most strongly linked to memory. By using specific sensory details rather than abstract statements, the writer transports the reader into the scene and creates an emotional connection to the past, making the reader share the warmth of the memory [1].

PEE 2:
P: A writer can also create nostalgia through contrast between past and present [1].
E: For example, writing "Back then, the kitchen table was never empty. Now it collects dust" [1].
E: The contrast between the busy past ("never empty") and the lonely present ("collects dust") highlights loss and the passage of time. The short, blunt sentence "Now it collects dust" creates a stark, emotional impact because of its simplicity, making the reader feel the absence sharply [1].
Each PEE paragraph must name a SPECIFIC technique (sensory imagery, contrast, past tense, listing, etc.) — not just "nice language." The Explanation must link the technique to the emotional EFFECT on the reader.

10
You are asked to write a directed writing response: a letter to a friend describing a memorable family event. Plan your response by listing: (a) the register and tone you would use, (b) three specific "show, don't tell" moments you would include, and (c) how you would structure the opening and closing.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Register and tone: Informal register (contractions, first person, casual vocabulary). Warm, humorous, and slightly exaggerated tone — as if telling a funny story to a friend [1].

(b) Three "show, don't tell" moments: [1 each]
1. Instead of "the food was delicious" → describe Uncle Marco gesturing wildly while sauce dripped down his chin
2. Instead of "my little cousin was annoying" → describe her tugging your sleeve every thirty seconds asking "but WHY?"
3. Instead of "it was chaotic" → describe the dog knocking over the birthday cake while Gran tried to take a photo

(c) Structure: Opening: Start with a hook sentence that summarises the feeling: "You won't believe what happened at my cousin's birthday — honestly, I'm still finding cake in my hair" [1]. Closing: End with a reflective or humorous line that circles back: "Next year, we're doing a restaurant. With no dog" [1].
Planning is not wasted time in the exam — it's where you earn the structure marks. Spending 5 minutes on a plan like this before writing will get you a better grade than starting immediately and rambling for 40 minutes.
