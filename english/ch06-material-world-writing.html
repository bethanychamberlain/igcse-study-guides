2

Weak to Powerful

About 5 minutes

These sentences are weak and forgettable. A persuasive writer would never leave them like this. Rewrite each one using at least one rhetorical device to make it hit harder.

Weak "Schools should have longer breaks."
Try: Rule of 3 or a rhetorical question. Why do students need longer breaks? What happens without them?
Weak "Social media isn't great for your mental health."
Try: Emotive language or statistics. Make the reader feel the impact, not just hear about it.
Weak "The food at school could be better."
Try: Direct address or repetition. Make the reader picture (or taste) the problem.
Remember from Chapter 6
A rhetorical device is only powerful if it fits the point you're making. Don't force a device in — pick the one that strengthens your argument naturally. One well-placed device beats three clumsy ones.

Why this matters
The strongest persuasive writers don't just state their opinion — they acknowledge the other side and then dismantle it. This shows the reader you've thought it through, not just ranted. It's the difference between a C and an A in IGCSE.

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your paragraph from Exercise 4 out loud. Yes, actually out loud — you'll hear weak spots and awkward phrasing that you won't catch by reading silently. Then check each box honestly.

  • Clear position stated — Could a reader tell what you're arguing for within the first two sentences?
  • At least 2 rhetorical devices — Can you underline them? If you can't find them, they're not there.
  • Counter-argument addressed — Did you mention what the other side thinks AND explain why they're wrong?
  • Audience-appropriate tone — Does it sound like you're persuading, not whining? Would a teacher or principal take it seriously?
  • Logical structure — Does your argument build, or does it just repeat the same point? Position, evidence, counter, conclusion.
  • Varied sentence lengths — Do you have both short punchy sentences and longer developed ones? Read it aloud — does it flow?
