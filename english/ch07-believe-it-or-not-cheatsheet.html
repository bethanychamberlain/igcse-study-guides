Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Bias When a text favours one side — it's not neutral. Can be deliberate or unconscious. Opinion (bias is hidden or slanted; opinion is stated openly)
Significant absence What a text deliberately leaves OUT. What's missing can be as important as what's included. —
Colloquial language Informal, everyday speech — slang, contractions. Makes writing feel personal. Cliche (colloquial = informal register; cliche = overused expression)
Cliche An overused expression that has lost its impact. "At the end of the day." Shows lazy writing. Idiom (idioms can be fresh and effective; cliches are worn out)
Emotive language Words chosen to provoke an emotional response. Bypasses logic, appeals to feelings. Descriptive language (emotive triggers feelings; descriptive creates images)
Direct speech Exact words in quotation marks: "I was terrified," she said. Feels immediate and real. Indirect speech (that's reported: She said she had been terrified.)
Discursive writing Explores BOTH sides of an argument, then reaches a balanced conclusion. Persuasive writing (that's one-sided — trying to convince)
Juxtaposition Placing two contrasting ideas side by side to highlight their differences. Oxymoron (that's two contradictory words in ONE phrase, like "deafening silence")
Omniscient narrator An all-knowing narrator who can see into every character's thoughts. First-person narrator (that one only knows their own thoughts)

News Report vs Article vs Feature

News Report Article Feature
Purpose Inform (what happened) Inform + engage Entertain + explore
Person Third person, past tense First or third Often first person
Structure Inverted pyramid (big news first) Subheadings, paragraphs Hook opening, anecdotes
Language Factual, concise Descriptive, some opinion Colloquial, descriptive, human interest
Memory Trick — News Report Opening
Always answer WHO-WHAT-WHERE-WHEN in the first paragraph. If it doesn't answer all four, rewrite it. This is your exam shortcut for news reports.

Discursive vs Persuasive — Know the Difference

Discursive Persuasive
Trigger word "Discuss," "explore," "consider" "Argue," "persuade," "convince"
Sides BOTH sides, then your conclusion ONE side only — yours
Structure Intro, for, against, conclusion Intro, argument, argument, argument, conclusion
Connectives "However," "On the other hand," "Conversely" "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Clearly"
Exam Trap
If the question says "discuss," you MUST show both sides. Writing only one side means you've answered the wrong question — instant structure penalty.

Say It in One Sentence

Fake news check Consider the source, read beyond the headline, check the author, verify evidence, check the date, check your own biases.
Significant absence What a writer leaves OUT shapes how the reader perceives what's left IN.
Colloquial language Informal register that builds rapport — effective in articles, wrong in reports.
Emotive language Swaps neutral words for emotional ones to make the reader FEEL instead of just KNOW.
Show, don't tell Don't say "she was scared" — describe her trembling hands and short gasping breaths.
Juxtaposition Place wealth next to poverty, peace next to war — the contrast makes both more vivid.
Content vs Style Comparing texts = HOW the writer says it (techniques, tone, language), never WHAT they say (content).

Memory Tricks

PEE Paragraph Template
P = make your POINT (name the technique). E = give EVIDENCE (short quote). E = EXPLAIN the effect (this is where ALL the marks are — make it the longest part). Your explanation should be at least twice as long as point + evidence combined.
Emotive Language — The Swap Test
Mentally replace the emotive word with a neutral one. "Slaughtered" becomes "killed." The difference between the two IS the effect. That's exactly what you write about.
Show Don't Tell — Name Zero Emotions
To practise: pick any emotion (anger, joy, fear). Write 2-3 sentences conveying it through actions, body language, and sensory detail WITHOUT ever naming the emotion. If you can do this, Paper 2 narrative marks go up.
Comparison = Weave, Not List
Never: "Text A says... Text B says..." Always: "Text A uses [X], whereas Text B uses [Y]. This creates [different effects]." Both texts in one paragraph, every time.
Discursive Connectives — 3 Groups
FOR: Firstly, One argument is that, Supporters claim...
AGAINST: However, On the other hand, Conversely, Critics argue...
CONCLUSION: On balance, Having considered both sides, Ultimately...
Sentence Starters for Analysis
"The writer uses [technique] to [effect]..." / "The word '[X]' suggests/implies/connotes..." / "This creates a sense of [feeling] for the reader because..." / "This positions the reader to feel..."

PEE Paragraph Templates — Use These in Every Answer

Your #1 Weakness (Easter Test Result)
You scored 100% on factual/recognition questions but only 46% on analytical writing. The gap is HERE — in PEE paragraphs. These templates close the gap.
Template 1 — Analysing Emotive Language
P: The writer uses emotive language to create [sympathy / outrage / urgency].
E: For example, the phrase "[short quote]"
E: The word "[zoom word]" suggests [meaning], which makes the reader feel [emotion]. By choosing "[emotive word]" instead of a neutral alternative like "[neutral word]," the writer intensifies the reader's response, positioning them to [agree / oppose / sympathise].
Template 2 — Comparing Two Texts
P: Both writers address [topic], but they adopt very different [tones / registers].
E: Text A uses [technique + quote], while Text B [technique + quote].
E: Text A's [language choice] creates [effect], making the reader feel [feeling]. In contrast, Text B's [approach] encourages [different response]. This difference reflects their different purposes: Text A aims to [purpose] while Text B aims to [purpose].
Template 3 — Narrative Voice
P: The writer uses [omniscient narration / "show don't tell" / juxtaposition] to create [tension / sympathy / irony].
E: We are told that "[short quote]"
E: This reveals [information the character doesn't know / a contrast the reader notices]. The effect is [tension / dramatic irony / sympathy], because the reader [knows something the character doesn't / sees the contrast / feels the emotion through action rather than statement].

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How does the writer try to influence the reader?" Find bias, emotive language, significant absences. Name the technique + explain its effect on the reader using PEE.
"Write a news report about..." Headline (present tense, punchy), byline, open with WHO-WHAT-WHERE-WHEN, quotes from witnesses, formal and factual.
"Discuss whether..." or "Explore both sides of..." Discursive structure: Intro, 2-3 FOR paragraphs, 2-3 AGAINST paragraphs, balanced conclusion with your own opinion.
"How does the writer use language to create an atmosphere?" Find personification, juxtaposition, sensory detail, "show don't tell". Quote short, explain long. Zoom in on one powerful word.
"Compare how the two writers present their ideas" Compare by FEATURE (tone, language, purpose, viewpoint), NOT by text. Weave both texts in every paragraph. Use "whereas," "in contrast."
"What is the effect of direct speech here?" Direct speech feels immediate and authentic. The reader hears the person's own words, creating drama and trust. Contrast with indirect speech, which feels more distant.
"What is the writer's attitude?" Identify the tone (admiring, critical, sarcastic, etc.). Find the specific word choices that reveal that attitude. Never just say "positive" or "negative" — be precise.
"Identify a pun and explain its effect" A pun = wordplay with double meaning. Explain BOTH meanings. Say it makes the headline memorable and draws the reader in with humour.
