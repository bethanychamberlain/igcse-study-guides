Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define skimming and scanning. How are they different?
2 marks
Answer
Skimming is reading a text quickly to get the general idea of what it is about [1]. Scanning is reading to find specific information, such as a particular word, name, or detail [1].
Key difference: skimming = big picture, scanning = specific detail. Don't mix them up.

2
What does keywording mean, and why should you do it before you read the passage?
2 marks
Answer
Keywording means finding and underlining the important words in the question [1]. You should do it before reading the passage so you know exactly what to look for when you skim and scan the text, saving time [1].
The point is: you read the QUESTION first and keyword it, THEN go to the passage. Not the other way around.

3
What is the difference between a fact and an opinion in a piece of writing? Give one example of each.
2 marks
Answer
A fact is something that can be verified or proven to be true, e.g. "Los Roques consists of 42 small islands" [1]. An opinion is a personal view or judgment, e.g. "It is a charming place" [1].
Any valid examples work. The key: facts can be checked; opinions cannot.

4
What does SQuEE stand for? List all four steps.
2 marks
Answer
S = State the facts or what you believe [½]. Qu = Quote a relevant word or phrase [½]. E = Explain the choice of words [½]. E = Effect — describe the effect created [½].
SQuEE is almost the same as PEE but splits the explanation into two parts (explain + effect). Either framework is fine in the exam.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain HOW and WHY, not just name things.
5
A travel writer describes a beach as having "soft white sand" and "warm turquoise waters." Name two senses being appealed to and explain the effect this sensory language has on the reader.
4 marks
Answer
The two senses are touch ("soft," "warm") and sight ("white," "turquoise") [1 + 1]. The effect is that the reader can imagine being physically present at the beach — feeling the texture of the sand and seeing the colour of the water [1]. This creates a sense of relaxation and appeal, making the reader want to visit the place themselves [1].
Two marks for naming the senses correctly, two marks for explaining the effect. "It sounds nice" is NOT enough — you need to say WHY it sounds nice and how the reader feels.

6
List three key rules for writing a good summary, and explain why you should use your own words rather than copying from the text.
4 marks
Answer
Three rules (any three from): read the text twice / keyword the question / underline relevant information / number your points / write in your own words / edit and proofread [1 + 1 + 1]. You should use your own words because it shows the examiner that you understand the text, not just that you can copy it. Lifting phrases directly from the text suggests you may not actually comprehend what they mean [1].
The "why" part is crucial — copying shows recognition, paraphrasing shows understanding. That's what the examiner is testing.

7
A holiday advertisement says: "Experience the adventure of a lifetime. Navigate between weather-sculpted ice-bergs and groaning glaciers to discover sights rarely seen by humans." Identify two persuasive techniques used and explain their effect on the reader.
4 marks
Answer
Technique 1: Imperative verb — "Experience" and "Navigate" are commands that directly address the reader [1]. Effect: this makes the reader feel personally invited and creates a sense of urgency, as if the adventure is waiting for them right now [1].

Technique 2: Personification — "groaning glaciers" gives the glaciers a human quality [1]. Effect: this creates an atmosphere of awe and power, suggesting the glaciers are alive and making sounds, which adds drama and excitement to the description [1].
Other valid answers: hyperbole ("adventure of a lifetime"), exclusivity appeal ("rarely seen by humans"), compound adjective ("weather-sculpted"). For full marks you must name the technique AND explain its effect.

Advanced — Can You Analyze It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full PEE paragraphs with detailed explanation. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Read the following sentence from a travel article: "A hundred fountains were playing, filling the sky with rainbows and creating an extraordinary dreamlike clamour." Write a full PEE paragraph explaining how the writer makes this description vivid and effective.
5 marks
Answer
P: The writer uses personification and multi-sensory imagery to create a vivid, magical scene [1]. E: The fountains are described as "playing" and creating "an extraordinary dreamlike clamour" [1]. E: The personification of the fountains "playing" suggests they are alive and joyful, as if performing for the writer, which creates a sense of wonder [1]. The oxymoron "dreamlike clamour" combines the quiet, surreal quality of a dream with the loud noise of the fountains, suggesting that the scene is so overwhelming that it feels unreal [1]. The visual image of "filling the sky with rainbows" adds colour and light, creating a magical atmosphere that makes the reader feel as though they are witnessing something extraordinary and almost fantastical [1].
A top-band answer identifies multiple techniques (personification, oxymoron, visual imagery) and explains each one's effect on the reader. The explanation is longer than the Point and Evidence combined.

9
A travel brochure uses facts to inform and persuasive language to entice. Explain, with examples you invent, how a writer might combine both in a single paragraph about a holiday destination. Why is this combination effective?
5 marks
Answer
A writer might combine facts with persuasion like this: "Located just 200 km from the capital, this stunning coastal resort offers pristine beaches and world-class diving." [1 for a valid invented example].

The factual element ("200 km from the capital") gives the reader practical, trustworthy information, making the text feel reliable [1]. The persuasive language ("stunning," "pristine," "world-class") appeals to the reader's emotions and desires, making the destination sound attractive [1].

The combination is effective because: facts alone would be dry and boring — the reader would not feel excited [1]. Persuasive language alone would feel exaggerated and untrustworthy. Together, the facts give credibility while the persuasive language creates desire, making the reader both informed and motivated to visit [1].
This question tests whether you understand the PURPOSE of blending fact and opinion in travel writing. The key insight: facts = trust, persuasion = desire. You need both.

10
Imagine you have been asked to write a 100-word summary of a travel article about a remote island. The article describes the island's wildlife, its history, and the threats from tourism. Write down the steps you would follow to produce the summary, and explain what you would include and what you would leave out.
6 marks
Answer
Steps:
1. Read the article twice to understand the content [1].
2. Keyword the question — note that three topics are required: wildlife, history, and threats from tourism [1].
3. Underline key points in the article, using a different colour for each topic [1].
4. Number the points in order and make brief notes.
5. Write the summary in my own words, keeping to 100 words [1].

Include: The main facts about each of the three topics (wildlife, history, threats). Each topic should get roughly equal coverage [1].

Leave out: Personal opinions, literary analysis, detailed descriptions, minor examples, and any information not directly related to the three topics. Do not repeat the same point in different words [1].
This tests your understanding of the summary PROCESS, not your ability to summarise a specific text. The examiner wants to see that you have a systematic approach, not just "read it and write about it."
