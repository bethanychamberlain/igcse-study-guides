Section 2.1 Personification — Giving Nature a Voice
Chapter 2 uses nature-themed texts to teach a critical technique: personification. This technique is used constantly in writing about nature, and the exam loves it.
W
Write This Down — Personification

Personification = a type of metaphor in which something non-human (an object, animal, or idea) is described as if it were a person.

Examples: "the angry sea," "the wind whispered," "the branches talk in agony."

W
Write This Down — Effects of Personification
What Is Personified Example Effect on Reader
Wind as a tyrant "announced the tyrant's coming" Creates fear; the wind becomes a threatening ruler, giving nature a sense of deliberate cruelty
River as a living force "the golden waterway is a taker as well as a giver of life" Suggests the river has power and intention; creates a sense of respect and danger
Trees feeling pain "the branches talk in agony" Makes the reader feel empathy for the trees, as if nature itself is suffering
?
Why This Matters

Personification is one of the most common techniques in IGCSE English. If you can spot it AND explain its effect, you will score well. The exam will give you a passage about nature, weather, or a landscape and ask how the writer makes the scene vivid. Personification is almost always part of the answer.

P
PEE In Action — Analyzing Personification

P: The writer uses personification to portray the wind as a violent, tyrannical force. E: The wind is described as "the tyrant" whose coming is "announced" by "a gasping messenger." E: By calling the wind a "tyrant," the writer transforms a natural event into something deliberate and menacing. The word "tyrant" implies oppression and cruelty, suggesting the storm has power over everything in its path. The "gasping messenger" adds urgency and panic, making the reader feel the terror of those caught in the storm. The reader no longer sees wind as a weather event but as a hostile, unstoppable ruler.

  • I can define personification and give at least 2 examples
  • I can explain WHY a writer personifies nature (not just identify it)
  • I can write a PEE paragraph about personification
Section 2.2 Writer's Purpose and Audience
Chapter 2 covers several different text types: myths, newspaper reports, feature articles, information texts, and poetry. Each one has a different purpose and audience, which changes how the writer uses language.
W
Write This Down — The Four Main Purposes of Writing

1. To inform — give the reader facts and information (news reports, textbooks)
2. To argue and persuade — convince the reader to agree with a viewpoint (opinion articles, speeches)
3. To describe — create a vivid picture for the reader (travel writing, descriptive essays)
4. To narrate — tell a story (myths, folk tales, novels)

W
Write This Down — How Purpose Affects Language
Purpose Language Features Example from Ch2
Inform Third person, factual, precise vocabulary, statistics, passive voice News reports about the Three Gorges Dam: "2,308 meters long and 185 meters high"
Argue/Persuade Emotive language, rhetorical questions, counter-arguments, linking phrases Articles arguing for or against changing the environment
Describe Sensory language, simile, metaphor, personification, varied sentence length Feature articles about rafting in Alaska
Narrate Past tense, dialogue, character development, chronological or non-chronological structure The folk tale of "How Coyote Gave Fire to the People"
!
Don't Confuse — Purpose vs Text Type

A text type is the format (article, letter, speech, story). A purpose is what the writer is trying to achieve. One text can have multiple purposes. A feature article about Alaska might describe the scenery (describe), give facts about the river (inform), AND persuade the reader that wild places are worth protecting (persuade).

T
Study Tip — Always Identify Purpose First

Before analyzing any passage, ask: "Why was this written?" The answer (to inform, persuade, describe, or narrate) tells you what techniques to look for. If it's persuasive, look for rhetorical questions and emotive language. If it's descriptive, look for imagery and sensory details.

  • I can name the four main purposes of writing
  • I can match language features to each purpose
  • I understand that a text can have multiple purposes

PEE FOR NATURE WRITING

Chapter 2 is packed with descriptive writing about nature. Use PEE to analyze HOW writers bring nature to life.

Nature writing uses imagery, personification, and carefully chosen vocabulary to create atmosphere. The exam will ask you to analyze how a writer does this. Your biggest challenge: you tend to identify the technique but skip the explanation. The explanation is worth 3-4 times more than the identification.
P
Point — What does the writer do? Name the technique and say what effect it creates in general terms.
"The writer creates a sense of danger and power through personification of the river."
E
Evidence — Quote the text Pick the most powerful words. Keep it short.
"The river 'plunges through tight gorges, transforming into angry rapids.'"
E
Explanation — WHY does this work? Explain the effect of the specific words. What does each word suggest? How does the reader feel?
"The verb 'plunges' suggests violent, uncontrolled downward movement, creating a sense of the river's unstoppable force. 'Angry' personifies the rapids, implying they are hostile and threatening to anyone who encounters them. The reader feels the danger of the river as though it has a will of its own, making the natural world seem powerful and unpredictable."
Before and After — See the Difference
Weak Response (1-2 marks)

"The writer uses personification to describe the river. The river 'plunges' and is called 'angry.' This makes it seem alive."

Problem: "Makes it seem alive" is too vague. What KIND of alive? Friendly? Dangerous? The reader feels WHAT exactly?

Strong PEE Response (4-5 marks)

P: The writer uses personification and dynamic verbs to present the river as a violent, unpredictable force. E: The river "plunges through tight gorges, transforming into angry rapids and then dangerous shallows." E: "Plunges" suggests violent, headlong movement, as if the river is throwing itself forward with no control. The word "angry" gives the rapids human emotion, implying the river is hostile and aggressive rather than simply flowing. The contrast between "tight gorges" and "dangerous shallows" shows that the river is unpredictable — it changes character constantly. This makes the reader feel uneasy, as though the river could become threatening at any moment.

Why this works: It analyzes specific word choices ("plunges," "angry"), explains what each word suggests, and describes the reader's emotional response.

P
Key Vocabulary for Nature Analysis

When writing about how an author describes nature, use these precise words instead of vague ones:

Instead of "nice" → serene, tranquil, idyllic, picturesque
Instead of "scary" → ominous, foreboding, menacing, hostile
Instead of "big" → immense, vast, imposing, formidable
Instead of "beautiful" → breathtaking, majestic, awe-inspiring, ethereal
Instead of "sad" → melancholic, desolate, sombre, bleak

  • I can write a PEE paragraph about descriptive nature writing
  • I use precise vocabulary (not "nice" or "scary") when describing mood
  • My explanations are at least twice as long as my Point and Evidence
Section 2.3 Writing to Argue and Persuade
Chapter 2 teaches you how to write argumentative and persuasive texts — a key skill for Paper 2. This is about presenting a case and convincing your reader.
W
Write This Down — Structure for Argumentative Writing

Planning:
• Use a spider-gram, mind map, or bullet list
• Number your points in the order you want to make them
• Include both sides of the argument
• Consider opposing viewpoints and prepare counter-arguments

Writing:
• Begin with a clear opening statement (do not indent the first paragraph)
• Start a new paragraph for each main point
• Each paragraph must have a clear topic sentence
• Link paragraphs logically
• Lead your reader to your conclusion

W
Write This Down — Persuasive Language Techniques

• Emotive language — words that trigger an emotional response ("devastating," "heartbreaking")
• Rhetorical questions — questions that don't need an answer ("How can we stand by and do nothing?")
• Counter-arguments — acknowledge the other side, then argue against it: "While many see this as... this is not convincing because..."
• Linking phrases — "Nonetheless...", "Furthermore...", "In spite of this..."
• Statistics and evidence — use data to support your points

!
Don't Confuse — What NOT to Do in Persuasive Writing

• Do NOT use too many rhetorical questions — one or two is enough
• Do NOT be rude — "which is rubbish" and "it's nonsense" lose you marks
• Do NOT make sweeping statements — "Everyone thinks..." or "It's a well-known fact that..." are weak
• Do NOT use words like "normal" or "ordinary" — what seems normal to you may not be to someone else

T
Study Tip — The Lawyer Test

Imagine you are a lawyer presenting a case in court. What evidence do you need to convince the judge? This mindset helps you write logically: state your argument, present evidence, address the other side, then conclude. If your argument wouldn't convince a judge, it won't convince the examiner.

  • I can plan an argumentative essay that includes both sides
  • I can use at least 3 persuasive techniques from the list
  • I know what NOT to do in persuasive writing
Section 2.4 Writing to Inform and Discuss
Informative writing is factual, impersonal, and organized. You use this style in school projects, reports, and information leaflets. It is also the basis for Directed Writing on Paper 2.
W
Write This Down — 12 Features of Informative Writing

• Clear and factual
• Impersonal and objective (not "I think")
• May include data, diagrams, tables, or maps
• Does not have to be chronological
• Opens with a general statement and introduction
• May use subheadings or divide information into categories
• May include references and citations
• Sentences are short and clear
• Vocabulary is precise
• Written in the third person
• Mixes active and passive verbs
• Mixes present and past tenses

!
Don't Confuse — Informative vs Persuasive

Informative writing presents facts objectively. Persuasive writing uses facts to support an argument. If the question asks you to "inform and discuss," stay balanced and objective. If it asks you to "argue and persuade," take a clear position. Check the question keywords carefully.

W
Write This Down — Style and Tone Checklist

Before writing any text, ask yourself:

• Who is going to read this? (audience)
• What register should I use? (formal, neutral, or informal)
• What vocabulary is appropriate?
• What tone of voice is appropriate?
• Is the information factual or can I use opinion adjectives?
• Are there other ways to say "then, and then, next, after that..."?

  • I can list at least 6 features of informative writing
  • I know the difference between informative and persuasive writing
  • I check my audience, register, and tone before writing

Section 2.6 Preparing for Exams — Directed Writing (Paper 2)
Directed Writing is Section A of Paper 2. You read one or two texts (650-750 words total) and then write a discursive, argumentative, or persuasive piece of 250-350 words based on the information.
W
Write This Down — What Directed Writing Tests

Your Directed Writing answer must show that you can:

• Understand the explicit content of the text
• Understand implicit meanings and attitudes
• Analyze, evaluate, and develop facts, ideas, and opinions
• Support your views by selecting and using information from the text
• Communicate clearly and express what you think, feel, and imagine
• Organize ideas and structure your writing for deliberate effect
• Use a range of vocabulary and sentence structures appropriate to the context
• Use appropriate register (formal/informal)
• Use accurate spelling, punctuation, and grammar

W
Write This Down — Mark Split

Directed Writing is marked in two parts:
• Up to 15 marks for the content of your answer (what you say)
• Up to 25 marks for the quality of your writing (how you say it)

This means how you write matters more than what you write. Good grammar, varied vocabulary, clear structure, and appropriate register are worth more than just including all the facts.

T
Study Tip — Use Your Own Words

In Directed Writing, you MUST use your own words. "Be careful to use your own words" appears in almost every question. This means: read the text, understand the ideas, then express them in your own language. Do not copy phrases from the source text.

  • I understand the format of Directed Writing (Paper 2, Section A)
  • I know the mark split: 15 for content, 25 for quality of writing
  • I understand that HOW I write matters more than WHAT I write
  • I will use my own words, not copy from the source text
Key Takeaway Where the Marks Are
P
The #1 Thing to Remember from Chapter 2

Always explain the EFFECT of the writer's choices.

Whether analyzing personification, imagery, or word choice, the pattern is always the same: name the technique, quote the words, then explain what the words suggest and how the reader feels. The explanation should be longer than everything else combined.

T
Study Tip — Know Your Purposes

For every text you read, whether in the exam or in daily life, ask three questions:

1. WHO is the audience?
2. WHAT is the purpose? (inform, persuade, describe, narrate)
3. HOW does the language serve that purpose?
If you can answer these three questions about any text, you can analyze it.

T
Study Tip — Build Your Word Power

Every time you encounter an unfamiliar word in a passage, look it up and write it down with its definition. Try to use it in a sentence within 24 hours. Building vocabulary is a slow process, but it pays off massively in both reading comprehension and writing quality.

  • I always explain EFFECT, not just identify techniques
  • I ask WHO, WHAT, HOW for every text I read
  • I have added at least 3 new words to my vocabulary today
