Section 5.1 Tone and Register in Non-Fiction
This chapter presents news articles, blog posts, and historical accounts about family and relationships. The key skill is recognising how tone and register differ between texts on the same topic.
W
Write This Down — Tone vs Register (Revision)

Tone = the writer's attitude toward their subject. Is it celebratory? Critical? Nostalgic? Humorous?

Register = the level of formality. Created by diction (word choice), syntax (sentence structure), and form (layout).

Two articles about the same event can have completely different tones. One might be celebratory and personal; the other factual and analytical.

W
Write This Down — Comparing Tone in Two Articles

When comparing articles, look for:

• Word choice — does one use emotional language while the other is neutral?
• Sentence structure — short, punchy sentences = urgency; long flowing sentences = descriptive mood
• Use of quotations — does the writer quote people directly or paraphrase?
• Writer's presence — does the writer include their own opinion or stay invisible?

?
Why This Matters

Paper 2 of the IGCSE often asks you to compare two non-fiction texts. Being able to identify and explain differences in tone and register is worth significant marks. Don't just say "one is formal and one is informal" — explain what specific words or structures create that difference.

  • I can define tone and register and explain the difference
  • I can identify specific features that create a text's tone
  • I can compare the tone of two articles on the same topic
Section 5.2 Chronological Order and Tense Choice
Writers organise events carefully. Understanding chronological order and tense shifts helps you analyse how writers control time in their writing.
W
Write This Down — Past Tenses and Their Effects
TenseExampleEffect / Use
Past simple"Cleopatra fled to Syria"States a fact about a completed action. The main storytelling tense.
Past perfect"Ptolemy had driven her out"Shows that one event happened BEFORE another. Creates a timeline of events.
Present tense"The image has been dramatized"Used to show something is still relevant now. Bridges the past to the present.
Past continuous"She was seeking an audience"Shows an ongoing action in the past. Creates a sense of duration or atmosphere.
!
Don't Confuse — Past Perfect and Past Simple

"Cleopatra fled" (past simple) = she fled. "Ptolemy had driven her out" (past perfect) = he drove her out BEFORE she fled. The past perfect always shows the earlier event. If the exam asks "Why does the writer use the past perfect?" the answer is: to show the order of events — this happened first.

T
Study Tip — Modern Idioms in Historical Writing

Writers covering historical topics often use modern phrases (e.g. "Cleopatra set about the business of ruling Egypt"). This is a deliberate choice — it makes the past feel relevant and accessible to modern readers. If you spot this on the exam, comment on it. It shows you understand the writer's craft.

  • I can identify and name four past tenses and explain their effects
  • I understand why writers use modern idioms when writing about the past
Section 5.3 Writing to Entertain — Short Stories
The exam may ask you to write a short story or continue a narrative. This section covers the skills you need to write engaging fiction.
W
Write This Down — 6-Step Story Writing Process

1. Plan before you write. Decide: Who is your audience? What is the subject? Where and when is it set? Who are the characters? First or third person?

2. Make notes on your plot. Plan the beginning, middle, and end. Keep the number of characters small. Give your main character a flaw that makes them interesting.

3. Don't lose the plot. You can focus on one event and leave it open-ended. A cliffhanger ending is fine — but you must know how it would end even if you don't write it.

4. Think about your style. Get to the point quickly. Use sensory details (the five senses) so the reader can see and feel the scene.

5. Finish the story. Don't let it trail off — give it a satisfying or surprising ending.

6. Proofread. Check spelling, grammar, and word choice. Swap weak words for stronger ones.

W
Write This Down — Dialogue Rules

• Start a new paragraph for each new speaker
• Use speech marks around what is said
• End dialogue with a comma, full stop, question mark, or exclamation mark before closing the speech marks
• Use speech verbs that show HOW the character speaks: whispered, snapped, muttered, exclaimed — not just "said" every time

!
Don't Confuse — First Person Narrator Limits

If you write in first person ("I"), you can only describe what YOUR character sees, hears, thinks, and feels. You CANNOT write "Meanwhile, across town, Sarah was worried" — your character doesn't know that. If you want to show multiple perspectives, use third person omniscient.

T
Study Tip — Show Don't Tell

Instead of "He was angry," write "His jaw tightened and his fingers curled into fists." Instead of "She was nervous," write "She kept glancing at the door, twisting the hem of her sleeve." Show the emotion through actions and details — this is what separates a Grade C story from a Grade A story.

  • I know the 6-step process for writing a short story
  • I know the rules for writing dialogue with correct punctuation
  • I understand the difference between "show" and "tell" in writing
  • I know the limits of first-person narration
Section 5.4 Writing to Inform and Entertain
This type of writing combines factual information with an engaging style. Think of magazine articles, web pages, or blog posts about real events.
W
Write This Down — Features of Informative-Entertaining Writing

• Clear and factual about the subject, with topical references
• Objective but given a personal twist — the writer's voice comes through
• Opens with an attention-grabbing statement and an introduction to the topic
• May include quotations, historical references, and citations
• Language is clear and neutral to informal; may include modern figures of speech
• Vocabulary is precise where necessary
• Mixes active and passive verbs
• Historically accurate but does NOT have to be chronological

P
PEE In Action — Analysing Informative Writing

P: The writer uses a mix of factual detail and modern idioms to make a historical topic engaging. E: While the article is about ancient events, phrases like "set about the business of ruling" use modern business language. E: This makes the distant past feel relevant and relatable to modern readers who are familiar with this kind of language from current news coverage. The effect is to bridge the gap between the ancient world and the reader's own experience, making the article both informative and enjoyable rather than dry and academic.

  • I can list the key features of writing to inform and entertain
  • I understand how writers mix factual content with engaging style
Section 5.5 Vocabulary in Context
The exam often asks you to explain what words or phrases mean in the context of the passage. This is NOT about dictionary definitions — it's about what the word means here, in this sentence.
W
Write This Down — How to Answer "Explain in Your Own Words"

1. Read the sentence around the word, not just the word itself
2. Think about what the word means in this specific context
3. Replace it with a simpler word or phrase that fits
4. Write your answer as a full sentence that makes sense in the original context
5. Do NOT just copy the phrase from the text — you must use your own words

!
Don't Confuse

"In your own words" means you CANNOT just copy the text. If the text says "an integral role" and you write "an integral role means an integral role," you get zero marks. You need to say "an essential or very important part."

T
Study Tip — Build Your Vocabulary

When you encounter unfamiliar words in reading, write them down with: (1) the sentence you found them in, (2) what you think they mean from context, (3) the dictionary definition. This builds the vocabulary you need to paraphrase effectively on the exam.

  • I know the 5-step process for answering "explain in your own words" questions
  • I understand that "your own words" means I CANNOT copy from the text
Section 5.6 Writing to Express Thoughts, Ideas, and Feelings
The exam tests whether you can express personal opinions, describe experiences, and explore ideas in writing. Whether you're writing from your own or a character's perspective, you need to justify your opinions.
W
Write This Down — Types of Expressive Writing Questions

The exam may ask you to:

• Paper 1 Reading: Write a journal entry from a character's point of view — use the text but write in your own words
• Paper 2 Section A: Discuss a real-life situation (directed writing)
• Paper 2 Section B: Descriptive writing (describe a scene) or narrative writing (write a story)
• All types: Justify your opinions. If something is good, say why. If it's bad, say why.

P
The Golden Rule for Expressive Writing

Every opinion needs a reason. Every feeling needs a cause.

Don't write "I was sad." Write "I was sad because I realised the bond between us had quietly broken, like a thread that had been stretching for years until it finally snapped." The exam rewards depth of thought, not just stating how you feel. Push past the first sentence of any feeling or opinion.

T
Study Tip — The Feeling + Image Formula

For every emotion you write about, pair it with a concrete image:

• Loneliness + image: "The house felt enormous and empty, every footstep echoing like a question nobody answered."
• Joy + image: "The sunlight caught the spray from the hose and turned it into a rainbow above the garden."
This technique makes your writing vivid and earns marks for descriptive skill.

  • I know the different types of expressive writing the exam may ask for
  • I understand that every opinion needs a reason
  • I can pair emotions with concrete images to create vivid writing
Key Takeaway Relationships as a Lens for Analysis
P
The #1 Thing to Remember

Every text in this chapter is about relationships — use that as your analytical lens.

Whether it's a historical article about Cleopatra, a poem about a father and son, or a novel extract about neighbours, the exam will ask you HOW the writer presents the relationship. Look for: what the characters say (and don't say), how they act toward each other, what the writer's word choices suggest about the power balance, and how the reader is positioned to feel about each person.

T
Study Tip — Practice Summarising

Summary writing is a key skill tested in Paper 2. Take any article you read online and try to summarise it in exactly 120 words, using your own words. This forces you to identify the key points and practise paraphrasing — both essential exam skills.

  • I have practised summarising an article in my own words
