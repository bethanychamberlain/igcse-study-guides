Section 4.1 Understanding Drama and Theatre
Shakespeare wrote "All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players." This chapter explores how drama works — from ancient traditions to modern scripts — and the skills you need to read and write about dramatic texts.
W
Write This Down — Theatre Vocabulary
TermMeaning
SoliloquyA speech where a character speaks their thoughts aloud, alone on stage. The audience hears their inner feelings.
MonologueA long speech by one character, addressed to other characters on stage (not private like a soliloquy).
Stage directionsInstructions in the script (usually in brackets or italics) telling actors what to do, how to move, or how to speak.
DénouementThe final resolution of the plot — where everything is explained or wrapped up.
DialogueThe words characters say to each other on stage. In scripts, there are no speech marks — just the character name and their words.
?
Why This Matters

Knowing these terms lets you write precisely about dramatic texts. Instead of "the character talks to himself," you can say "the playwright uses a soliloquy to reveal the character's inner conflict." Precise vocabulary = higher marks.

W
Write This Down — Aristotle's Six Elements of Drama

The Greek philosopher Aristotle identified six key elements that make good drama. These are still used to analyse plays, films, and TV shows today:

1. Plot — the sequence of events. Aristotle said this is the most important element.
2. Character — the people in the drama and their personalities, flaws, strengths.
3. Diction — the language and word choices characters use (also called dialogue).
4. Thought — the themes and ideas the drama explores.
5. Spectacle — everything visual: costumes, scenery, special effects, movement.
6. Song — music, sound, rhythm in the performance.

T
Study Tip — Use Aristotle Everywhere

Think about a film or TV show you love. Can you identify all six of Aristotle's elements in it? Try it with an anime or a Marvel film — the framework works for any story, not just ancient plays.

  • I can define soliloquy, monologue, stage directions, dénouement, and dialogue
  • I can name Aristotle's six elements of drama
  • I understand that "plot" is the most important element according to Aristotle
Section 4.2 Tragedy, Comedy, and Flawed Characters
Understanding the difference between tragedy and comedy — and the concept of a "fatal flaw" — is essential for analysing drama on the exam.

W
Write This Down — The Fatal Flaw

A fatal flaw (also called hamartia) is a weakness in a hero's character that leads to their downfall. The audience can either sympathise (feel sorry for them when they knowingly make mistakes) or empathise (feel their pain when they unknowingly make mistakes).

Examples: Macbeth's fatal flaw is ambition. Gatsby's is his obsessive desire to recapture the past. In modern TV, a character who lies to protect someone but destroys everything — that's a fatal flaw too.

!
Don't Confuse — Sympathy vs Empathy

Sympathy = you feel sorry FOR someone. "I feel bad for them."
Empathy = you feel WITH someone. "I understand how they feel because I can imagine being in their situation."
In exam answers, use the right word. Empathy is deeper than sympathy.

  • I can explain the difference between tragedy and comedy
  • I know what a "fatal flaw" is and can give an example
  • I understand the difference between sympathy and empathy
Section 4.3 Reading Scripts and Stage Directions
Scripts look different from novels. There are no speech marks, no "he said" — just character names and their lines. Learning to read this format is a skill you need for the exam.
W
Write This Down — Features of a Script

• Character names appear in the left margin before their lines
• No speech marks — lines are written as direct speech without quotation marks
• Stage directions are in brackets or italics (e.g. (angrily) or (crossing to window))
• Scenes begin with setting information for the director and stage manager
• Dialogue is written to sound like real conversation — short, interrupted, emotional

W
Write This Down — What Stage Directions Tell You

Stage directions are NOT just instructions for actors. They reveal character emotions and power dynamics. Modern playwrights use adverbs in stage directions to show HOW a character speaks:

• (uneasily) — the character is nervous, perhaps hiding something
• (cutting in, massively) — the character is dominant, controlling the conversation
• (dryly) — sarcasm or detachment
• (distressed) — genuine emotional pain

P
PEE In Action — Analysing Stage Directions

P: The playwright uses stage directions to reveal the shifting power balance between the Inspector and Gerald. E: Gerald speaks "(rather uneasily)" and "(with an effort)" while the Inspector "(cutting in, massively)" takes control. E: The contrast in these adverbs shows that Gerald is losing confidence and trying to maintain composure, while the Inspector is completely in command. The word "massively" suggests the Inspector's authority is overwhelming, making the reader feel the weight of his moral judgement crushing Gerald's pretence of innocence.

?
Why This Matters

On the exam, if you're given a script extract, commenting on stage directions (not just the dialogue) shows the examiner you can read at a deeper level. Most students ignore stage directions entirely — don't be one of them.

  • I can identify the key features of a script layout
  • I understand that stage directions reveal character emotions and power dynamics
  • I can write a PEE paragraph about stage directions
Section 4.4 Register, Audience, and Purpose
Every piece of writing has an audience (who it's for) and a purpose (what it's trying to do). The register — the level of formality — must match both.
W
Write This Down — Register

Register = the style of language used to suit a particular situation. It is created by a combination of diction (word choice), tone (formal, neutral, or informal), and syntax (sentence structure).

• Formal register: academic essays, letters of complaint, reports. No slang, no contractions.
• Neutral register: news articles, informative writing. Clear and factual.
• Informal register: blogs, personal letters, dialogue between friends. May include slang, contractions, colloquial language.

W
Write This Down — Audience Factors

When a writer targets an audience, they consider: age, social background, education level, cultural background, and prior knowledge. Shakespeare's genius was writing for both educated nobles and uneducated groundlings at the same time — mixing high poetry with crude humour.

!
Don't Confuse — Audience vs Purpose

Audience = WHO is reading or watching. Purpose = WHY the writer wrote it (to inform, to persuade, to entertain, to describe). The exam often asks about both. Make sure you address the right one. If the question says "Who is the intended audience?" do NOT write about what the text is trying to do — that's purpose.

T
Study Tip — The Register Test

When you read any text, ask: "Could I swap this text into a different context?" Would this advertisement work as a school essay? Would this formal letter work as a blog post? If not, you've identified the register. Practice spotting register shifts — it's a quick exam win.

  • I can define register and name three levels (formal, neutral, informal)
  • I can identify the audience and purpose of a text
  • I know the difference between audience and purpose
Section 4.5 Directed Writing — Articles, Letters, and Reports
In the exam, you may be asked to write an article, a letter, or another text type using information from a passage. This is directed writing — you must match the correct format AND include the content they ask for.
W
Write This Down — Article Writing Checklist

When writing an article (e.g. for a blog, magazine, or newspaper):

• Headline — attention-grabbing, often a question or strong statement
• Opening paragraph — hook the reader, introduce the topic
• Body paragraphs — each paragraph covers one point, with evidence or examples
• Appropriate register — match the publication (blog = informal, broadsheet = formal)
• Conclusion — sum up or leave the reader with something to think about
• Word count — stick to it. The exam typically asks for 250-350 words.

W
Write This Down — Formal Letter Format

• Start with Dear Mr/Mrs/Ms... (if you know the name) or Dear Sir/Madam (if you don't)
• End with Yours sincerely (if you used their name) or Yours faithfully (if you used Sir/Madam)
• State your purpose in the first sentence
• Keep paragraphs focused — one point per paragraph
• Be polite but firm — never rude, even in a complaint letter

T
Study Tip — The "Bullet Points" Trick

Directed writing questions always give you bullet points of what to include. Turn each bullet point into a paragraph. If there are 3 bullet points, write 3 body paragraphs (plus an intro and conclusion). This guarantees you cover everything and don't miss marks.

  • I know the structure of an article (headline, hook, body, conclusion)
  • I know when to use "Yours sincerely" vs "Yours faithfully"
  • I understand the "bullet point to paragraph" strategy
Section 4.6 Comparing Texts with Different Purposes
Chapter 4 presents multiple texts about the same topic (e.g. drama schools) but with different purposes. The exam tests whether you can compare how writers achieve different effects.

P
PEE In Action — Comparing Purposes

P: While both texts discuss drama training, their purposes are fundamentally different. E: The advertisement uses persuasive language such as "award-winning" and "Don't miss your chance!" whereas the article quotes a director saying drama schools are "a waste of time and money." E: The advertisement's enthusiastic, one-sided language is designed to attract applicants by making them feel excited about a potential career. In contrast, the article presents both sides of the debate, allowing the reader to form their own opinion. This difference in purpose changes the register, word choice, and tone of each text entirely.

  • I can identify the purpose of different text types (persuade, inform, entertain)
  • I can compare how two texts on the same topic use language differently
  • I can write a comparative PEE paragraph weaving both texts together
Key Takeaway Reading Drama Like an Analyst
P
The #1 Thing to Remember

In drama, EVERYTHING is a deliberate choice by the writer.

The dialogue, the stage directions, the register, even the silences — the playwright chose each one for a reason. Your job is to explain WHY they made that choice and WHAT EFFECT it has on the audience. Don't just describe what happens ("Sheila cries"). Explain the effect ("Sheila's distress reveals her genuine guilt, which contrasts with Gerald's self-serving attempts to minimise his involvement, making the audience sympathise with her more").

T
Study Tip — Watch and Analyse

Pick any scene from a show you watch. Pause it and ask: What is each character's purpose in this scene? How does the way they speak reveal who they are? What would an audience feel at this moment? This is the same skill the exam tests — you're just practising it with content you enjoy.

  • I understand that every element of drama is a deliberate choice
  • I can explain the EFFECT of a writer's choices, not just describe them
