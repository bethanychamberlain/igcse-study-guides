Section 6.1 Persuasive Writing and Rhetorical Devices
Persuasive writing aims to convince the reader to agree with a viewpoint or take action. This is a core exam skill — you need to both recognise persuasive techniques when analysing texts AND use them in your own writing.
W
Write This Down — Aristotle's Three Strands of Persuasion

1. Logos (Logical) — explain the situation with facts, data, and reasoning. Show how the recommended course of action will bring a positive outcome.

2. Pathos (Emotional) — affect the audience's emotions. Make them feel they ought to help or act, because if they don't, things will get worse.

3. Ethos (Social/Ethical) — make it clear that society will benefit. Make the audience think: "What could happen if everyone does this?" or "What will happen if we stop?"

Why This Matters

Persuasive writing appears in both papers. In Paper 1, you may need to analyse how a writer persuades. In Paper 2, you may need to write a persuasive speech, letter, or article. Knowing these devices means you can both spot them AND use them.

  • I understand the difference between analysing persuasion and writing persuasively
Section 6.2 Writing a Persuasive Speech
A speech is different from an essay. You're writing for someone to speak aloud to a live audience. The language must be direct, punchy, and structured for the ear, not the eye.
W
Write This Down — Speech Writing Checklist

• Open with a memorable image that sticks in the audience's mind
• Use short paragraphs, each addressing one specific point
• Each paragraph should lead to a logical outcome — your desired conclusion
• Use linking phrases to show you're building an argument
• Use tripling to reinforce your message (say it three different ways)
• Use rhetorical questions and the pronoun "you" to address the audience directly
• Use emotive language and imagery to affect emotions
• Use dynamic verbs and active voice — avoid passive sentences
• Keep sentences short — easier to remember and more impactful
• Use "we" not "they" — unite yourself with the audience
• Do NOT end with a question that could prompt a negative response
• If persuading to DO something, end with a pleasant, aspirational image
• If persuading to STOP something, end with a "worst case scenario" image

!
Don't Confuse — Speech vs Essay

A speech uses "you," "we," rhetorical questions, and short punchy sentences. It sounds like someone talking. An essay is more formal, uses "one" or "it can be argued," and has longer, more complex sentences. If the exam says "write a speech," do NOT write an essay. Make it sound spoken.

T
Study Tip — Read It Aloud

After writing a speech, read it out loud. If you stumble on a sentence, it's too long or too complex. Shorten it. If it sounds like a textbook, add more "you" and "we." If it feels flat, add a rhetorical question or a vivid image.

  • I know at least 8 features of a good persuasive speech
  • I understand the difference between speech writing and essay writing
  • I know how to end a speech (aspirational image vs worst-case image)
Section 6.3 Writing a Letter of Complaint
A letter of complaint is a formal text with specific conventions. Getting the format right is easy marks — many students lose them by being too informal or forgetting the structure.
W
Write This Down — Letter of Complaint Structure

DO:
• Start with Dear Mr/Mrs... or Dear Sir/Madam
• State your purpose in the first sentence
• Provide relevant details concisely
• Give all information in a clear, logical sequence
• State what action you want the recipient to take
• End with "Yours sincerely" (if you know their name) or "Yours faithfully" (Sir/Madam)

DON'T:
• Be rude or use abusive language
• Get sidetracked from your main complaint
• Criticise the company as a whole
• Send without checking grammar and spelling

P
PEE In Action — Why Format Matters

P: The format of a complaint letter is just as important as its content. E: Using "Dear Sir/Madam" with "Yours faithfully" follows the standard convention. E: This matters because examiners are testing whether you know the conventions of formal writing. Getting "sincerely" vs "faithfully" right is a free mark. Forgetting it shows you don't understand the form. It takes two seconds to learn but loses marks every year for students who don't bother.

  • I know the DO and DON'T rules for complaint letters
  • I know when to use "Yours sincerely" vs "Yours faithfully"
  • I understand that format = easy marks
Section 6.4 Analysing Descriptive Language
Chapter 6 includes richly descriptive passages. The exam tests whether you can explain HOW a writer uses language to create effects — not just what the passage is about.
W
Write This Down — Analysing Imagery

When the exam asks "How does the writer use language to describe...?" follow this method:

1. Find a technique (metaphor, simile, personification, sensory detail, etc.)
2. Quote the specific words — keep quotes SHORT (3-6 words)
3. Explain the effect — what does it make the reader see, feel, or understand?
4. Zoom in on individual words — pick ONE powerful word from the quote and explain what it suggests
5. Connect to mood/atmosphere — how does this contribute to the overall feeling of the passage?

P
PEE In Action — Word-Level Analysis

P: The writer uses violent imagery to convey the destructive impact of gold mining on the rainforest. E: The land is described as being "gouged" and "ravaged." E: The word "gouged" implies a deep, aggressive wound, as if the earth is a living body being attacked. "Ravaged" suggests not just damage but complete devastation, the kind that cannot be reversed. Together, these words create a sense of brutality, making the reader feel anger and sympathy for the natural environment. The writer positions the miners as aggressors and the forest as a victim, which is a persuasive choice designed to make the reader oppose the mining.

T
Study Tip — The "Zoom In" Technique

After quoting a phrase, pick the ONE most powerful word and write a sentence about it. "The word 'X' suggests/implies/connotes..." This is the move that turns a C-grade answer into an A-grade answer. It shows the examiner you can analyse at word level, not just sentence level.

  • I know the 5-step method for analysing descriptive language
  • I can "zoom in" on a single word and explain what it suggests
  • I can connect word choices to the overall mood/atmosphere of a passage
Section 6.5 Summary Writing
Summary writing is a Paper 2 skill. You need to extract key points from a text, put them in your own words, and stay within the word limit (usually 120 words).
W
Write This Down — Summary Writing Rules

• Read the passage and underline key points
• Use your own words — do NOT copy phrases from the text
• Write in continuous prose (full sentences, not bullet points)
• Include ONLY what the question asks for — don't add extra information
• Do NOT add your own opinion or analysis
• Each sentence should contain a different point — if two sentences say the same thing, cut one
• Stay within the word limit

!
Don't Confuse — Summary vs Analysis

Summary = WHAT the text says. "The writer explains three reasons why mining damages the environment."
Analysis = HOW the writer says it. "The writer uses violent imagery to persuade the reader that mining is destructive."
If the exam says "summarise," do NOT start analysing techniques. Just state the key facts in your own words.

  • I know the rules for summary writing (own words, continuous prose, no opinion)
  • I can tell the difference between summary (what) and analysis (how)
Section 6.6 Writing a Report
Reports are formal documents that convey information for a specific purpose. They have a different structure from essays, letters, and articles.
W
Write This Down — Report Writing Structure

1. Preparation: Gather information, check relevance, create subheadings
2. Planning: Number your points, arrange them in a logical sequence
3. Style:
• Use formal, neutral language — no colloquialisms, no contractions
• Use plain English and passive verbs where appropriate
• Organise with subheadings and bullet points
• Ensure the conclusion clearly states your findings and/or purpose
4. Edit: Proofread for clarity, impact, and accuracy

!
Don't Confuse — Report vs Letter vs Article

Report: Subheadings, formal language, passive voice, bullet points, factual. "It was found that..."
Letter: Dear X, paragraphs, personal but formal, Yours sincerely/faithfully.
Article: Headline, informal-to-neutral register, engaging opening, writer's voice comes through.
If the exam asks for a report, don't write it like a letter. Use subheadings and formal, impersonal language.

  • I know the 4-step process for writing a report
  • I can distinguish between the format of a report, letter, and article
  • I know that reports use formal, impersonal language with subheadings

Study Tip — Comparison Framework

When comparing two texts, organise your answer by FEATURE, not by text. Don't write "everything about Text A" then "everything about Text B." Instead:

Paragraph 1: Compare REGISTER (Text A is... whereas Text B is...)
Paragraph 2: Compare TONE (Text A creates a feeling of... while Text B...)
Paragraph 3: Compare PURPOSE (Text A aims to... whereas Text B...)
This weaves the texts together and scores much higher.

Key Takeaway Persuasion Is Everywhere
P
The #1 Thing to Remember

Every text is trying to position you. Your job is to notice HOW.

Even texts that seem "neutral" make choices about what to include and what to leave out, which words to use and which to avoid, whose perspective to centre and whose to marginalise. When you analyse a text, ask: "What does the writer WANT me to think or feel?" Then find the specific language choices that create that effect. That's where the marks are.

T
Study Tip — Be a Critical Reader

Next time you see an advertisement, a news headline, or even a YouTube thumbnail, ask: What rhetorical devices are being used here? Who is the audience? What is the purpose? This habit turns you into the kind of critical thinker the exam rewards.

  • I understand that all texts position the reader
  • I can ask "What does the writer want me to think or feel?" as my starting point
