Section 8.1 Biography vs Autobiography
This chapter deals with writing about real people. Understanding the difference between biography and autobiography — and the effects of each viewpoint — is an exam skill.
W
Write This Down
Type Who Writes It Point of View Key Effect
Biography Someone else writes about the person Third person (he/she/they) Can be more objective. Can include perspectives the subject is unaware of. Can praise OR criticise.
Autobiography The person writes about themselves First person (I/we) Intimate and personal. Reader shares the subject's inner thoughts. But may be biased — the subject controls the narrative.
!
Don't Confuse

Biography is NOT always more truthful than autobiography. A biographer can have their own agenda (they might admire or dislike the subject). An autobiographer can be brutally honest. The exam tests whether you can evaluate the reliability and bias of either form.

?
Why This Matters

Paper 1 may give you a biographical or autobiographical passage and ask you to explain how the writer presents the subject. You need to notice whether the writing is first or third person and discuss how that affects the reader's trust and emotional connection.

  • I can define biography and autobiography
  • I can explain the effect of each viewpoint on the reader
  • I understand that neither form is automatically more reliable
Section 8.2 Writing a Biography: Content and Structure
Paper 2 (Directed Writing) may ask you to write a biographical piece based on information from a passage. Knowing what to include and how to structure it is worth several marks.
W
Write This Down — What a Biography Should Include

• Early life — background, childhood, formative experiences
• Key achievements — what makes this person notable
• Challenges and setbacks — obstacles they overcame (this makes it compelling)
• Character traits — personality, values, motivations
• Impact on others — how they influenced their field or community
• Quotations — from the person or about them (adds authenticity)

W
Write This Down — Biographical Structure

1. Opening hook — a defining moment or striking detail that captures who this person is
2. Background — early life and context
3. Rise — how they achieved success or fame
4. Complications — difficulties, controversies, turning points
5. Legacy / conclusion — their lasting impact

T
Study Tip — Don't Just List Facts

A biography is not a CV. The examiner wants you to shape a narrative. Use descriptive language, show the person's character through anecdotes, and comment on their significance. Think of it as storytelling about a real person.

  • I know the 6 content areas a biography should cover
  • I can structure a biography with a hook, background, rise, complications, and legacy
  • I understand that biography = storytelling, not just listing facts
Section 8.3 Descriptive Writing: Fiction vs Non-Fiction
Both fiction and non-fiction use descriptive writing, but for different purposes. The exam may ask you to write descriptively OR to analyse how a writer creates vivid descriptions.
W
Write This Down — Fiction vs Non-Fiction Description
Aspect Fiction Non-Fiction
Purpose Create atmosphere, build character, immerse the reader Help the reader picture a real place, person, or event accurately
Techniques Metaphor, simile, personification, sensory imagery, symbolism Sensory detail, precise vocabulary, comparison, factual imagery
Freedom Can invent, exaggerate, use fantasy Must stay truthful; description serves accuracy
W
Write This Down — The 5 Senses Checklist

Strong descriptive writing uses at least 3 of the 5 senses:

• Sight — colours, shapes, movement, light and shadow
• Sound — loud/quiet, rhythm, specific sounds (crunching, hissing)
• Smell — often overlooked but very powerful for evoking memory
• Touch — texture, temperature, pressure
• Taste — less common but distinctive when used

P
PEE In Action — Analysing Description

P: The writer uses sensory imagery to immerse the reader in the scene. E: "The market was a riot of colour and noise — the sharp tang of spices catching in the throat, silk scarves rippling in the breeze like bright flags." E: The appeal to smell ("sharp tang of spices catching in the throat") makes the reader physically react, as though they can taste the air. The simile "like bright flags" suggests celebration and vitality, while "riot" implies something overwhelming and chaotic. The overall effect is to make the reader feel present in the scene, experiencing its energy first-hand.

T
Study Tip — Practise Descriptive Paragraphs

Pick any place you know well (your room, a street, a market). Write one paragraph describing it using at least 3 senses. Then check: did you use any similes or metaphors? Did you create a mood? This is exactly what Paper 2 (Composition) rewards.

  • I know how fiction and non-fiction description differ
  • I can name all 5 senses and use at least 3 in a descriptive paragraph
  • I can analyse descriptive writing using PEE
Section 8.4 Comparing Tones Across Texts
Paper 1 may give you two passages about the same topic and ask you to compare how the writers convey their attitudes. The skill is recognising tone and explaining how language choices create it.
W
Write This Down — Tone Vocabulary

Expand your vocabulary for describing tone:

Positive: admiring, reverential, celebratory, enthusiastic, affectionate, nostalgic
Negative: critical, dismissive, sarcastic, contemptuous, cynical, mocking
Neutral/measured: objective, balanced, informative, detached, matter-of-fact
Complex: bittersweet, ambivalent, ironic, wistful

!
Don't Confuse — Tone vs Mood (Again)

Tone = the writer's attitude toward their subject. Mood = the feeling created in the reader. They overlap but are not the same. A writer's sarcastic tone might create an uncomfortable mood in the reader.

W
Write This Down — How to Identify Tone

Ask these questions:

• What words does the writer choose — positive, negative, or neutral?
• Are they using humour, exaggeration, or understatement?
• Do they seem distant and objective, or personally involved?
• How would this sound if someone read it aloud? What voice would they use?

  • I have a wide vocabulary for describing tone (not just "positive/negative")
  • I can tell the difference between tone and mood
  • I can identify tone by asking "what attitude does the writer show?"
Section 8.5 Synthesising Information from Multiple Texts
Synthesising means combining information from two or more sources into one coherent response. This is different from comparing (which highlights differences).
W
Write This Down — Synthesis vs Summary vs Comparison
Task What You Do
Summary Shorten ONE text to its key points. Your own words. No analysis.
Comparison Show how TWO texts are similar and different. Focus on techniques and effects.
Synthesis Combine information from TWO+ texts into one seamless response, as if writing from scratch.
W
Write This Down — How to Synthesise

1. Read both texts and identify the key points from each
2. Group related points together (even if they come from different texts)
3. Write your response by topic, not by text — don't do "Text A says... Text B says..."
4. Use your own words to blend the information smoothly
5. Where texts agree, combine; where they add different details, include both

!
Don't Confuse

Synthesis is NOT "summarise Text A, then summarise Text B." The examiner wants you to weave information together so it reads as one unified answer. Organise by topic, not by source.

  • I know the difference between summary, comparison, and synthesis
  • I can synthesise by organising information by TOPIC, not by text
  • I can blend information from two sources into one smooth response
Section 8.6 Directed Writing: Writing an Article
Paper 2, Question 1 (Directed Writing) may ask you to write an article. This is one of the most common text types tested. You need to know the format AND demonstrate strong writing skills.
W
Write This Down — Article Structure

1. Headline — catchy and relevant
2. Opening hook — a question, a surprising fact, or a bold statement to grab attention
3. Subheadings (optional) — useful for organising longer articles
4. Body paragraphs — develop your points with evidence and examples
5. Formal to semi-formal register — more polished than a blog post, less stiff than a report
6. Closing paragraph — a call to action, a thought-provoking question, or a memorable final line

W
Write This Down — Formal English and Semi-Colons

Formal register means: no slang, no contractions ("do not" instead of "don't"), sophisticated vocabulary, complex sentence structures.

The semi-colon (;) joins two closely related sentences without a conjunction:

"Fame brings opportunity; it also brings scrutiny."

Using semi-colons correctly shows the examiner you have strong technical accuracy — which contributes to the Writing mark on Paper 2.

?
Why This Matters

On Paper 2, you are marked on both Reading (how well you use the passage content) and Writing (language, structure, accuracy). A well-formatted article with a headline, clear paragraphs, and a strong opening immediately signals to the examiner that you know what you are doing.

Before and After — Article Openings
Weak Opening (Low marks)

"In this article I am going to write about fame and whether it is good or bad. There are many different opinions on this topic."

Problem: Boring and tells the reader what you're going to do instead of doing it. No hook. No personality.

Strong Opening (High marks)

"What would you sacrifice for a million followers? Your privacy? Your friendships? Your sleep? In an age where fame is measured in likes, the cost of celebrity has never been higher — or more invisible."

Why this works: Rhetorical questions engage the reader immediately. The dash creates a dramatic pause. The contrast between "never been higher" and "more invisible" is thought-provoking.

  • I can structure an article with headline, hook, body, and close
  • I can write in formal/semi-formal register
  • I know how to use a semi-colon correctly
  • I can write an engaging opening that avoids "In this article I will..."

PEE FOR BIOGRAPHICAL AND DESCRIPTIVE ANALYSIS

Whether you are analysing a biography, a description, or an article, PEE always applies. The explanation is where the marks live.

P
Point State what the writer does. Name the choice or technique.
"The biographer presents their subject as heroic and inspiring."
E
Evidence Quote the text. Only the relevant words.
"She rose from nothing, defying every expectation placed upon her."
E
Explanation Explain the EFFECT. How does this make the reader feel? What does it suggest?
"The phrase 'rose from nothing' uses a metaphor of upward movement, suggesting triumph over adversity. 'Defying every expectation' implies strength and independence, which creates admiration in the reader. The biographer's word choices are entirely positive, which reveals their reverential attitude toward the subject."
  • I can apply PEE to biographical and descriptive passages
  • My explanations comment on specific word choices, not just the general idea
