Section 3.1 Narrative Point of View
Every story is told from a point of view. Who is telling the story — and what they can see — changes everything about how you experience it.
W
Write This Down

First person — the narrator is a character in the story. Uses "I" and "we." You only know what they know, see, and feel. Example: "I walked through the snow, and the cold bit into my skin."

W
Write This Down

Third person limited — the narrator is outside the story but follows one character's thoughts. Uses "he/she/they." Example: "She could feel the music pulling her forward, though she did not know why."

W
Write This Down

Third person omniscient — the narrator is all-knowing. Can see into every character's thoughts and feelings, and can describe events happening in different places at the same time. Example: "While she danced, unaware, her mother sat at home worrying."

?
Why This Matters

The exam will not just ask you to name the point of view. It will ask you to explain what effect the point of view has. For example: "First person creates a sense of intimacy — the reader feels close to the narrator and shares their emotions directly."

W
Write This Down — Effects of Each POV
Point of View Key Effect on Reader
First person Creates intimacy and empathy. Reader shares narrator's emotions. But the narrator may be unreliable — we only get their side.
Third person limited Creates connection with one character while keeping some distance. Reader can see things the character might not notice about themselves.
Third person omniscient Creates a wider perspective. Reader can see the full picture. Builds dramatic irony when the reader knows things characters don't.
T
Study Tip — Quick Test

To identify POV: look at the first few lines. Do you see "I"? First person. Do you see "he/she" but only one character's thoughts? Third limited. Can you hear multiple characters' inner thoughts? Omniscient.

  • I can define first person, third person limited, and third person omniscient
  • I can explain the EFFECT of each point of view on the reader
  • I can identify the POV of a passage in under 30 seconds

THE PEE FRAMEWORK

This is the single most important skill in your English exam. The marks are in the EXPLANATION, not the identification.

PEE = Point, Evidence, Explanation. Every analytical paragraph you write on the exam should follow this structure. Right now, your biggest weakness is stopping after the Point — you say what a technique is, but you don't explain how it affects the reader or why the writer used it. That's where the marks are.
P
Point State what the writer does. Name the technique or the choice they made.
"The writer uses a metaphor to describe the storm."
E
Evidence Quote directly from the text. Keep it short — just the relevant words.
"The storm was a wild beast clawing at the windows."
E
Explanation Explain the EFFECT on the reader. How does it make them feel? What does it suggest? Why did the writer choose this?
"This suggests the storm is violent and uncontrollable, creating a sense of danger. The word 'clawing' implies something predatory, which makes the reader feel threatened and uneasy."
!
Don't Confuse — This Is Your Biggest Weakness

Identifying a technique is NOT the same as explaining its effect.

Saying "The writer uses a simile" gets you maybe 1 mark. Explaining what the simile makes the reader feel and why gets you 3-4 marks. The explanation is worth more than the identification. Always.

Before and After — See the Difference
Weak Response (1-2 marks)

"The writer uses personification when they say 'the wind whispered through the trees.' This is when you give human qualities to something that isn't human. The wind is being described as whispering."

Problem: This just identifies the technique and defines it. It doesn't explain the EFFECT.

Strong PEE Response (4-5 marks)

P: The writer uses personification to create a gentle, secretive atmosphere. E: The phrase "the wind whispered through the trees" E: suggests something intimate and quiet. The word "whispered" implies the wind is sharing a secret, which creates a sense of mystery for the reader. This makes the setting feel alive and almost magical, as though nature itself has a voice and a purpose.

Why this works: It names the technique, quotes the text, then spends MOST of the answer explaining the effect on the reader.

P
The Golden Rule

Your explanation should be at least twice as long as your Point and Evidence combined. If your explanation is only one sentence, it's not enough. Push yourself: after writing one explanation sentence, ask "So what? What does that make the reader feel?" and write another sentence.

T
Study Tip — Starter Phrases for Explanation

Memorize these and use them as launching pads:

• "This suggests that..."
• "The effect on the reader is..."
• "The word [X] implies..."
• "This creates a sense of..."
• "The reader feels... because..."
• "The writer may have chosen this to..."

  • I can name the three parts of PEE: Point, Evidence, Explanation
  • I understand that the EXPLANATION is worth the most marks
  • I can write a PEE paragraph about a technique without just defining it
  • I know at least 3 starter phrases for explanations
Section 3.2 Literary Techniques
Writers don't just choose what to say — they choose how to say it. These techniques are tools writers use to create effects on the reader. Learn to spot them AND explain what they do.
W
Write This Down — Simile

Simile = a comparison using "like" or "as."

Example: "The fog crept in like a grey blanket over the city."

Effect: Creates a vivid picture in the reader's mind. Here, comparing fog to a blanket suggests something heavy and smothering, giving the reader a sense of the city being trapped or enclosed.

W
Write This Down — Metaphor

Metaphor = saying something is something else (no "like" or "as").

Example: "The classroom was a prison."

Effect: Stronger than a simile because it states the comparison as fact. Here, calling the classroom a "prison" suggests the character feels trapped and powerless, creating empathy in the reader for their frustration.

!
Don't Confuse

Simile uses "like" or "as" — "The car was like a rocket."
Metaphor says it IS the thing — "The car was a rocket."
If you see "like" or "as," it's a simile. If not, it's a metaphor. Simple as that.

W
Write This Down — Personification

Personification = giving human qualities to something non-human.

Example: "The trees groaned in the wind."

Effect: Makes the scene feel alive and emotional. "Groaned" suggests pain or struggle, which can create an ominous or unsettling mood. The reader begins to feel the environment as a living presence rather than a backdrop.

W
Write This Down — Imagery

Imagery = language that appeals to the five senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste).

Example: "The air was thick with the scent of damp earth and rotting leaves."

Effect: Makes the reader feel as though they are physically present in the scene. Engages the senses (smell, touch) to create an immersive, almost uncomfortable atmosphere. The words "damp" and "rotting" suggest decay, which can create a mood of unease.

?
Why This Matters

On the exam, you will be given a passage and asked something like: "How does the writer create a sense of tension?" You need to find the technique (simile, metaphor, etc.), quote the evidence, then explain the effect. That's PEE in action. Knowing the technique names is step 1 — but the marks are in step 3.

  • I can define simile, metaphor, personification, and imagery
  • I can give an example of each
  • I can explain the EFFECT of each technique, not just define it
Section 3.3 Analyzing Mood and Tone
Mood is the feeling the writing creates in the reader. Tone is the writer's attitude toward their subject. They're related but different.
W
Write This Down

Mood = the atmosphere or feeling the reader experiences. "The passage creates a mood of tension and unease."

Tone = the writer's attitude. "The writer's tone is nostalgic and reflective."

W
Write This Down — How Writers Create Mood

Writers create mood through:

• Word choice (diction) — dark, heavy words = ominous mood; light, airy words = cheerful mood
• Sentence length — short, sharp sentences = tension or urgency; long flowing sentences = calm or dreamlike mood
• Imagery — what the reader can "see" or "feel" through the writing
• Punctuation — ellipsis (...) = suspense or trailing off; exclamation marks = excitement or shock; dashes = interruption or afterthought

P
PEE In Action — Mood Analysis

Consider this sentence: "The corridor stretched ahead, silent. Nothing moved. Then — a door, somewhere, creaking open."

P: The writer creates a mood of suspense and tension through short sentences and sound. E: "Nothing moved" and the dash before "a door" E: The short sentence "Nothing moved" creates a pause that makes the reader hold their breath, as the emptiness feels unnatural and threatening. The dash interrupts the flow, mimicking a sudden, unexpected event, which makes the reader feel startled. The word "creaking" adds an eerie sound that makes the silence feel even more unsettling.

T
Study Tip — Mood Word Bank

Learn these words so you can describe mood precisely instead of just saying "scary" or "happy":

Dark/tense: ominous, foreboding, eerie, oppressive, menacing, sinister
Sad/reflective: melancholic, wistful, nostalgic, sombre, poignant
Happy/light: jubilant, whimsical, serene, tranquil, exuberant
Mysterious: enigmatic, unsettling, ambiguous, ethereal

  • I know the difference between mood and tone
  • I can name 4 tools writers use to create mood
  • I can use precise vocabulary to describe mood (not just "scary" or "sad")
Section 3.4 Punctuation and Sentence Structure for Effect
Writers don't just choose their words carefully — they also choose their sentence shapes and punctuation deliberately. The exam rewards you for noticing this.
W
Write This Down
Technique What It Looks Like Effect on Reader
Short sentences "He stopped. Silence." Creates tension, urgency, or dramatic impact. Forces the reader to pause.
Long sentences Multiple clauses joined by commas Creates a flowing, dreamy, or overwhelming feeling. Can mimic breathlessness.
Ellipsis (...) "She turned the corner and..." Creates suspense. Suggests something is left unsaid or trailing off.
Dash (—) "The room was empty — or so he thought." Interruption or surprise. Adds an unexpected afterthought that shifts meaning.
Exclamation mark "Run!" Shows urgency, excitement, or strong emotion.
Rhetorical question "Was there no escape?" Engages the reader directly. Creates a sense of desperation or reflection.
Repetition "Dark. Everything was dark." Emphasises a word or idea. Can create an obsessive or overwhelming mood.
?
Why This Matters

Many students only comment on word choices (metaphors, similes). If you can also comment on sentence structure and punctuation, you stand out. Examiners notice this and it pushes you into the higher mark bands.

  • I can identify at least 4 structural/punctuation techniques
  • I can explain the effect of each, not just name it
Section 3.5 Comparing Texts
Chapter 3 covers both prose (stories) and poetry. The exam may ask you to compare how two writers tackle a similar topic using different techniques.
W
Write This Down — Comparison Structure

When comparing texts, use this structure:

1. State the similarity or difference — "Both writers describe a city, but they create very different moods."
2. Analyze Text A using PEE — Point, Evidence, Explanation for the first text.
3. Link to Text B using a comparison connective — "In contrast," / "Similarly," / "Whereas..."
4. Analyze Text B using PEE — Point, Evidence, Explanation for the second text.
5. Overall comment — What is the overall difference in effect? "The first text makes the reader feel..., while the second creates a sense of..."

W
Write This Down — Comparison Connectives

Use these to link your analysis of the two texts:

Similarities: Similarly, Likewise, Both texts, In the same way, This is echoed in...
Differences: In contrast, However, Whereas, On the other hand, Unlike Text A, While Text A... Text B...

!
Don't Confuse

A comparison question does NOT mean "write about Text A, then write about Text B separately." You must weave them together, constantly linking one to the other. If your answer has two separate paragraphs with no connection between them, you'll lose marks.

T
Study Tip — Prose vs Poetry

When comparing prose and poetry, look for these differences:

• Poetry uses line breaks and stanza structure for emphasis; prose uses paragraph flow
• Poetry often has concentrated imagery in fewer words; prose can build mood over longer passages
• Poetry may use rhyme and rhythm to create musicality; prose uses sentence variation
• Both can use metaphor, simile, personification — compare HOW they use them differently

  • I know the 5-step comparison structure
  • I can use at least 3 comparison connectives from memory
  • I understand that comparison means weaving texts together, not treating them separately
Section 3.6 Character Description and Summary Writing
Describing characters and summarising what a passage is about are practical skills you'll need in both Paper 1 and Paper 2.
W
Write This Down — Analyzing a Character

When analyzing how an author presents a character, look for:

• What they say (dialogue) — polite? aggressive? hesitant?
• What they do (actions) — kind? cruel? nervous?
• What they think/feel (inner voice) — only available if POV allows it
• How others react to them — respected? feared? ignored?
• How the writer describes them — word choices, comparisons, imagery

W
Write This Down — Summary Writing Tips

Summary = key ideas only. No analysis, no PEE, no personal opinion.

• Read the passage and underline the main points
• Use your own words — do not copy long phrases from the text
• Be concise — if a point takes you 2 lines, it's too long
• Don't add anything the text doesn't say
• Check: does each sentence contain a different point? If two sentences say the same thing in different words, cut one.

!
Don't Confuse

Summary and analysis are DIFFERENT tasks. Summary = what the text says. Analysis = how the writer says it and why. If the question says "summarise," do NOT start analyzing techniques. If it says "explain how the writer creates," do NOT just summarise the content.

  • I know the 5 things to look for when analyzing a character
  • I understand the difference between summary and analysis
  • I can write a summary using my own words without adding opinion
Key Takeaway Where the Marks Are
P
The #1 Thing to Remember

The marks are in the EXPLANATION, not the identification.

Naming a technique = 1 mark at best. Explaining what it does to the reader = 3-4 marks. Every time you write about a technique, force yourself to write at least 2 sentences of explanation. Use the starter phrases. Push past "this is a metaphor" into "this makes the reader feel..."

T
Study Tip — Practice PEE Every Day

Pick any sentence from any book you're reading. Write a PEE paragraph about it. It doesn't have to be from a textbook — manga, novels, even song lyrics work. The skill is the same: Point, Evidence, Explanation. Do one per day and this will click within a week.

T
Study Tip — The "So What?" Test

After writing any analytical sentence, ask yourself: "So what?" If you can answer that question, write the answer down — that's your explanation. If you can't, you haven't said enough yet. Keep pushing until you can explain why the reader should care.
