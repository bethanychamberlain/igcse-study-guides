Section 9.1 Implicit vs Explicit Meaning
One of the most important reading skills is understanding what a text implies (suggests without directly stating) versus what it states (says outright). The exam heavily rewards this skill.
W
Write This Down

Explicit = stated directly. The information is right there on the page.

Implicit = suggested or implied. The reader must infer the meaning from clues in the text.

Example: "He slammed the door and did not look back." Explicit: He slammed the door. Implicit: He is angry, he has made a final decision, he does not want to return.

?
Why This Matters

Paper 1 questions often ask "What does this suggest about...?" or "What impression do you get of...?" These are asking for implicit meaning. If you only state what the text says literally, you miss the marks. You need to read between the lines.

!
Don't Confuse

"Suggests" questions need inference. If the question says "what does the writer suggest," do NOT just quote the text. You need to explain what the quote implies or hints at. The text is the evidence; the inference is your analysis.

T
Study Tip — The "What Does This Really Mean?" Game

Pick any sentence from a novel or story. Ask: What is the character REALLY feeling? What is the writer REALLY saying? If the text says "She smiled and said everything was fine" — is everything actually fine? Probably not. That's implicit meaning.

  • I can give an example of implicit meaning from a sentence
  • I understand that "suggests" questions require inference, not just quoting
Section 9.2 Narrative Structure: Freytag's Pyramid
Every well-structured story follows a narrative arc. Freytag's Pyramid is the classic model. Understanding it helps you both analyse fiction AND write your own narrative on Paper 2.
W
Write This Down — The 5 Stages of Freytag's Pyramid
Stage What Happens Example (Generic)
1. Exposition Introduction of characters, setting, and situation. The "normal world." We meet the hero in their village. Everything is peaceful.
2. Rising Action Complications develop. Tension builds. Obstacles appear. The hero receives a quest. Enemies appear. Stakes are raised.
3. Climax The turning point. The moment of greatest tension or conflict. The hero faces the villain. Everything is on the line.
4. Falling Action Consequences of the climax unfold. Loose ends begin to tie up. The villain is defeated but the hero is wounded.
5. Denouement / Resolution The "new normal." The story concludes. (In tragedy, this is the catastrophe.) The hero returns home, changed. Peace is restored.
!
Don't Confuse — Tragedy vs Comedy

In a tragedy: The climax is the hero's downfall. The falling action shows their suffering. The ending is the catastrophe (death, destruction, loss).

In a comedy: The climax is the resolution of complications. The ending is happy — marriages, reunions, harmony restored.

Both follow the SAME five-stage structure — the difference is in the direction of the outcome.

?
Why This Matters

For Paper 1: You may be asked where a passage falls in a narrative arc. Saying "this is the rising action because tension is building" shows structural awareness, which pushes you into higher mark bands. For Paper 2: If you write a narrative, your story should have a clear arc. Stories that ramble without a climax score low on structure.

T
Study Tip — Map Your Favourite Story

Take any anime, film, or book you know well. Map the 5 stages. Where is the exposition? What's the climax? This makes the structure click in a way that just reading about it never will.

  • I can name and describe all 5 stages of Freytag's Pyramid
  • I know the difference between tragedy and comedy endings
  • I can identify where a passage falls in the narrative arc
Section 9.3 Figurative Language: Full Review
Chapter 9 reviews all the figurative language techniques you need for the exam. You must know these by name AND be able to explain their effects.
W
Write This Down — Sound Devices
Technique Definition Example Effect
Alliteration Repetition of consonant sounds at the start of words "Peter Piper picked a peck" Creates rhythm; draws attention; can be playful, punchy, or menacing depending on the sound
Assonance Repetition of vowel sounds within words "The rain in Spain falls mainly on the plain" Creates musicality and flow; can create a dreamy or echoing quality
Consonance Repetition of consonant sounds within or at the end of words "pitter-patter," "odds and ends" Creates a satisfying, rhythmic quality; can feel punchy or abrupt
Sibilance Repetition of "s" or "sh" sounds "She sells seashells by the seashore" Creates a soft, whispering, or sinister quality depending on context
!
Don't Confuse These Four

Alliteration = start of words, consonants only. Assonance = vowel sounds, anywhere in the word. Consonance = consonant sounds, middle or end. Sibilance = specifically the "s/sh" sound (it's a type of consonance). If the question says "identify a sound device," any of these work.

W
Write This Down — Key Literary Terms (Complete List)
Term Definition
Simile Comparison using "like" or "as"
Metaphor Saying something IS something else (no "like" or "as")
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things
Imagery Language appealing to the five senses
Oxymoron Two contradictory words placed together ("deafening silence," "bittersweet")
Hyperbole Deliberate exaggeration for effect ("I've told you a million times")
Irony When the meaning is the opposite of what is said or expected
Symbolism Using an object to represent a bigger idea (a dove = peace)
Pathetic fallacy Using weather/nature to reflect mood ("storm = anger," "sunshine = happiness")
Foreshadowing Hints about what will happen later in the story
Juxtaposition Placing two contrasting ideas side by side for effect
P
Golden Rule Reminder

Naming a technique = 1 mark. Explaining its effect on the reader = 3-4 marks. Every time you spot a technique, immediately ask: "So what? What does this make the reader feel or think?"

Section 9.4 Personification and Atmosphere in Prose
Chapter 9 explores science fiction and prose that uses personification to bring settings alive. Understanding how writers create atmosphere through personification is a key analysis skill.
W
Write This Down — Personification for Atmosphere

When a writer personifies a setting (a house, a city, a landscape), they make it feel like a character in the story. This transforms the setting from a backdrop into an active force.

Example: "The city never slept. It hummed and groaned, its lights blinking like restless eyes."

Effect: The city feels alive and watchful. "Never slept" suggests something relentless, "groaned" suggests discomfort or age, and "restless eyes" creates an unsettling sense of being observed. The reader feels the city is not neutral — it is a presence with its own mood.

P
PEE In Action — Personification of Setting

P: The writer uses personification to create a threatening atmosphere. E: "The house watched them approach, its windows dark and empty, like hollow eyes." E: By giving the house the ability to "watch," the writer suggests it is aware and possibly hostile, which creates a sense of danger. The comparison of windows to "hollow eyes" makes the house feel dead yet sentient, which is deeply unsettling for the reader. The emptiness of the eyes suggests something was once there and has been lost, adding a layer of sadness beneath the fear.

  • I can write a PEE paragraph about personification with detailed word analysis
Section 9.5 Exam Strategy: Paper 1 and Paper 2
Chapter 9 prepares you for the actual exam. Here is what each paper looks like and how to approach it.
W
Write This Down — Paper 1: Reading (1 hour 45 mins)

Question 1 (15 marks): Based on Passage A. Typically "explain how the writer..." or "what impression do you get of..." Use PEE. Comment on language AND structure. Quote short and explain long.
Question 2 (5 marks): Based on Passage A. Often a summary question. Use your own words. Keep it concise. No analysis.
Question 3 (15 marks): Based on Passage B. Same as Q1 but on a different passage. PEE again.
Question 4 (5 marks): Based on BOTH passages. Compare how the writers present a particular topic or idea. Weave the two together.

W
Write This Down — Paper 2: Directed Writing & Composition (2 hours)

Section A — Directed Writing (25 marks): Read a passage, then write in a specific format (letter, article, report, speech) using information from the passage. Marked on Reading (how well you use the content) AND Writing (quality of your language).
Section B — Composition (25 marks): Choose ONE from a selection. Either:
• Descriptive writing (describe a scene, person, or place) OR
• Narrative writing (write a story with a clear arc)

T
Study Tip — Time Management

Paper 1: Q1 = 25 mins, Q2 = 15 mins, Q3 = 25 mins, Q4 = 15 mins. Leave 10 mins to check.
Paper 2: Section A = 45 mins, Section B = 45 mins. Leave 15 mins to plan Section B and 15 mins to check both.

W
Write This Down — Summary Writing Strategy (Paper 1, Q2)

1. Read the question carefully — it tells you exactly what to summarise
2. Underline the key points in the passage (aim for 6-8 distinct points)
3. Write each point in your own words — do NOT copy phrases from the text
4. Keep it concise — one sentence per point
5. Do NOT analyse techniques. Summary = WHAT, not HOW.

W
Write This Down — Narrative Writing Strategy (Paper 2, Section B)

1. Plan first (5 minutes). Map your story onto Freytag's Pyramid: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution.
2. Start in the middle of the action — don't waste time on backstory. "The door burst open" is better than "One day, a long time ago, there was a boy called..."
3. Show, don't tell — convey emotions through actions and sensory detail
4. Limit your characters — 2-3 maximum for a short story
5. End with impact — a twist, a revelation, a powerful image, or a moment of change

W
Write This Down — Descriptive Writing Strategy (Paper 2, Section B)

1. Plan your senses — list at least 3 senses you will use
2. Focus on ONE scene — don't try to describe a whole journey. Depth beats breadth.
3. Use figurative language — at least 2 similes/metaphors, plus personification
4. Vary your sentence structure — short sentences for impact, long sentences for flow
5. Create a mood — every description should have an atmosphere. Ask: what should the reader FEEL?

  • I know the structure of Paper 1 (4 questions, 2 passages)
  • I know the structure of Paper 2 (Directed Writing + Composition)
  • I have a time plan for both papers
  • I know the summary strategy: own words, concise, no analysis
  • I know the narrative strategy: plan with Freytag, start in action, show don't tell
  • I know the descriptive strategy: one scene, 3+ senses, create mood

Final Reminder — Where the Marks Are
What Most Students Do (Low marks)

"The writer uses a metaphor. A metaphor is when you say something is something else. The text says 'the city was a jungle.' This is a metaphor."

This identifies + defines + quotes but never EXPLAINS. It shows you can spot the technique but not what it DOES.

What Top Students Do (High marks)

P: The writer uses a metaphor to convey the chaos and danger of urban life. E: "The city was a jungle." E: By comparing the city to a "jungle," the writer suggests it is wild, unpredictable, and governed by survival of the fittest. This creates a sense of threat for the reader, implying that the character must be constantly alert. The word "jungle" also evokes heat, density, and being lost, which reinforces the overwhelming nature of the city. The reader feels sympathy for the character navigating this hostile environment.

The explanation is three times longer than the point and evidence. That's where ALL the marks are.
