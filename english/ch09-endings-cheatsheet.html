Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Explicit Stated directly. The information is right there on the page. Implicit (that's suggested, not stated — you have to infer it)
Implicit Suggested or implied. The reader must infer the meaning from clues. Explicit (that's stated directly — no inference needed)
Exposition The opening of a story: characters, setting, and "normal world" are introduced. Climax (that's the turning point, not the beginning)
Climax The turning point — the moment of greatest tension or conflict. Denouement (that's the resolution AFTER the climax)
Denouement The resolution — how everything settles after the climax. The "new normal." Falling action (falling action = consequences unfolding; denouement = final resolution)
Pathetic fallacy Using weather or nature to reflect mood. Storm = anger. Sunshine = happiness. Personification (pathetic fallacy is specifically nature reflecting MOOD)
Foreshadowing Hints about what will happen later in the story. Flashback (that goes BACK in time; foreshadowing hints FORWARD)
Oxymoron Two contradictory words in one phrase: "deafening silence," "bittersweet." Juxtaposition (that's contrasting IDEAS side by side, not contradictory WORDS)
Irony When the meaning is the opposite of what is said or expected. Sarcasm (sarcasm is spoken irony meant to mock — irony is broader)
Symbolism Using an object to represent a bigger idea. A dove = peace. A storm = conflict. Metaphor (a metaphor says X IS Y; a symbol is an object that REPRESENTS an idea throughout)

Sound Devices — Side by Side

Alliteration Assonance Consonance Sibilance
What repeats Consonant at START of words Vowel sounds WITHIN words Consonant WITHIN or END "S" or "sh" sounds
Example Peter Piper picked rain in Spain, plain pitter-patter She sells seashells
Effect Rhythm, emphasis, attention Musicality, dreamy flow Rhythmic, punchy Whispering or sinister
Memory Trick — Telling Them Apart
Alliteration = At the start. Assonance = vowels (A, E, I, O, U). Consonance = Consonants inside or at the end. Sibilance = S and Sh sounds.

Freytag's Pyramid — The 5-Stage Story Arc

1 → Exposition = Characters, setting, "normal world" introduced
2 → Rising Action = Tension builds, obstacles appear, stakes rise
3 → Climax = Turning point, greatest tension, everything on the line
4 → Falling Action = Consequences unfold, loose ends tie up
5 → Denouement = Resolution / "new normal" (or catastrophe in tragedy)
Exam Trap — Tragedy vs Comedy
SAME five stages, different direction. Tragedy: climax = hero's downfall, ending = catastrophe. Comedy: climax = resolution of complications, ending = happiness. Don't assume all stories end happily.

All Literary Terms You Need — Quick Reference

Term Definition Effect to Write About
Simile Comparison using "like" or "as" Creates a vivid image; helps reader visualise by linking to something familiar
Metaphor Saying something IS something else Stronger than simile; transforms the subject; creates deeper associations
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things Makes settings/objects feel alive; can create threat, beauty, or sympathy
Imagery Language appealing to the five senses Immerses the reader; makes the scene feel real and present
Oxymoron "Deafening silence," "bittersweet" Shows complexity or contradiction; reveals mixed feelings
Hyperbole Deliberate exaggeration Creates emphasis, humour, or urgency
Irony Opposite of what's said or expected Creates humour, tension, or criticism; makes reader think deeper
Symbolism Object represents a bigger idea Adds layers of meaning; rewards careful reading
Pathetic fallacy Weather/nature reflects mood Reinforces atmosphere; environment mirrors character's emotions
Foreshadowing Hints about what happens later Creates tension and suspense; makes reader anxious or curious
Juxtaposition Contrasting ideas side by side Highlights differences; creates irony, shock, or moral outrage
The Golden Rule
Naming a technique = 1 mark. Explaining its effect on the reader = 3-4 marks. Every time you spot a technique, immediately ask: "So what? What does this make the reader feel or think?"

Say It in One Sentence

Implicit meaning What the text SUGGESTS but doesn't state — "He slammed the door" implies anger without saying "angry."
Freytag's Pyramid Every story: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, denouement.
Paper 1 4 questions, 2 passages. Q1+Q3 = PEE analysis. Q2 = summary. Q4 = compare both texts.
Paper 2 Section A = directed writing (letter, article, report, speech). Section B = narrative or descriptive composition.
Summary rule WHAT (key points, own words, no opinion) not HOW (no techniques, no analysis).
Narrative writing Plan with Freytag, start in the action, show don't tell, 2-3 characters max, end with impact.
Descriptive writing One scene, 3+ senses, figurative language, varied sentences, clear mood throughout.

Memory Tricks

PEE Paragraph Template
P = make your POINT (name the technique). E = give EVIDENCE (short quote). E = EXPLAIN the effect (this is where ALL the marks are — make it the longest part). Your explanation should be at least twice as long as point + evidence combined.
Implicit Meaning — The "Really" Test
After reading any sentence, ask: "What is REALLY going on here?" If "She smiled and said everything was fine" — is everything actually fine? Probably not. That gap between what's said and what's meant = implicit meaning.
Freytag = Anime Arc
Map it to any anime: Exposition (hero in village), Rising Action (quest + enemies), Climax (final boss fight), Falling Action (aftermath), Denouement (hero returns, changed). Works for every show.
Paper 1 Time Plan
Q1 = 25 mins. Q2 = 15 mins. Q3 = 25 mins. Q4 = 15 mins. Check = 10 mins. Stick to this. Don't spend 40 mins on Q1 and run out of time for Q4.
Paper 2 Time Plan
Section A (directed writing) = 45 mins. Section B (composition) = 45 mins. Plan B = 15 mins. Check both = 15 mins.
Narrative Opening Hack
Start IN the action. "The door burst open" is always better than "One day, a long time ago, there was a boy called..." The exam rewards you for grabbing the reader immediately.
Sentence Starters for Analysis
"The writer uses [technique] to convey [idea]..." / "The word '[X]' suggests/implies/connotes..." / "This creates a sense of [feeling] in the reader because..." / "This is effective because it makes the reader feel..." / "This implies that [deeper meaning]..."

PEE Paragraph Templates — Use These in Every Answer

Your #1 Weakness (Easter Test Result)
You scored 100% on factual/recognition questions but only 46% on analytical writing. The gap is HERE — in PEE paragraphs. These templates close the gap.
Template 1 — Analysing Figurative Language
P: The writer uses [metaphor / simile / personification] to convey [idea / mood].
E: For example, "[short quote]"
E: By comparing [X] to "[Y]," the writer suggests [meaning]. The word "[zoom word]" connotes [association], which creates a sense of [feeling] for the reader. This is effective because it makes the reader [feel / think / understand] that [deeper meaning]. The overall atmosphere created is one of [mood].
Template 2 — Analysing Implicit Meaning
P: The writer implies [idea] without stating it directly.
E: For example, "[quote showing action/detail, not stated emotion]"
E: Although the writer does not explicitly say [emotion/idea], the detail of "[specific word/action]" suggests [inference]. This creates [tension / sympathy / unease] for the reader, who must read between the lines. The implicit meaning is more powerful than a direct statement because it forces the reader to engage actively with the text.
Template 3 — Analysing Narrative Structure
P: This passage is positioned at the [rising action / climax / denouement] of the narrative.
E: This is evident because "[quote showing tension building / conflict peaking / resolution]"
E: The [building tension / peak conflict / resolution] at this point creates [anticipation / shock / relief] in the reader. The writer's structural choice to [build slowly / hit the climax suddenly / end ambiguously] is effective because [reason]. This shows an awareness of narrative structure that [effect on reader].

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What does this suggest about the character?" This is asking for implicit meaning. Don't just quote — explain what the quote IMPLIES about the character's feelings, motives, or situation.
"How does the writer create tension / atmosphere?" Find pathetic fallacy, foreshadowing, personification, short sentences, sensory detail. Name, quote, explain effect on the reader. Zoom in on one word.
"Identify the technique and explain its effect" Name the technique (1 mark). Explain what it makes the reader feel or think (3-4 marks). The explanation is worth more than the identification.
"Summarise the key points" (Paper 1, Q2) Own words. 6-8 distinct points. One per sentence. No analysis. No opinion. Stay in word limit. This is WHAT, not HOW.
"Write a narrative" (Paper 2, Section B) Plan with Freytag (5 mins). Start in the action. Show don't tell. 2-3 characters max. End with impact (twist, revelation, powerful image).
"Write a description of..." (Paper 2, Section B) ONE scene only. 3+ senses. 2+ similes/metaphors + personification. Vary sentence length. Every detail should create a mood.
"Compare how the two passages present..." Compare by FEATURE (tone, language, structure, viewpoint). Weave both texts in every paragraph. Use "whereas," "in contrast," "while."
"Write a [letter / article / report / speech]" (Paper 2, Section A) Use the correct format for that text type (see Ch6 and Ch7 cheatsheets). Use content from the passage. Marked on BOTH reading and writing quality.
