Key Vocabulary

Term What It Means Don't Confuse With
Skimming Reading quickly to get the general idea — titles, first/last sentences, subheadings. Scanning (skimming = big picture; scanning = specific detail)
Scanning Reading to find a specific piece of information — a name, date, or keyword. Skimming (scanning = hunting for one thing; skimming = getting the gist)
Keywording Underlining the important words in a question BEFORE reading the passage. —
Explicit meaning What is directly stated in the text. You can point to the exact words. Implicit meaning (explicit = on the surface; implicit = between the lines)
Implicit meaning What is suggested or hinted at. You infer it from clues in the text. Explicit meaning (implicit = you have to figure it out; explicit = it's right there)
Paradox A statement that seems to contradict itself but reveals a truth. "More haste, less speed." Oxymoron (paradox = full statement; oxymoron = two contradictory words together)
Oxymoron Two contradictory words placed together: "bittersweet," "living dead," "organised chaos." Paradox (oxymoron = word pair; paradox = whole sentence or idea)
Sensory language Words that appeal to the five senses: sight, sound, smell, touch, taste. Imagery (imagery is broader — sensory language is one type of imagery)

The Three-Step Reading Process

Step What You Do When You Do It
1. Keyword Underline important words in the question BEFORE reading the passage
2. Skim Read titles, first/last sentences, subheadings First read-through (30 seconds)
3. Scan Hunt for specific words/ideas that match your keywords When answering each question
Memory Trick — KSS
Keyword, Skim, Scan — think of it as "KiSS the text." Keyword the question, Skim for the big picture, Scan for the answer. Do it in that order, every time.

Persuasive vs Descriptive Writing

Persuasive Writing Descriptive Writing
Purpose Make the reader DO or AGREE Make the reader EXPERIENCE
Techniques Imperatives, rhetorical questions, direct address ("you"), positive adjectives Sensory language, simile, metaphor, varied sentence length
Tone Enthusiastic, commanding Vivid, atmospheric
Example "Discover the paradise of Los Roques!" "The turquoise water lapped gently against sun-bleached sand."
Exam Trap
If the question says "describe," do NOT start persuading. If it says "persuade," do NOT just describe. Read the question keyword. They overlap in techniques but differ in PURPOSE.

The Five Senses — Quick Reference

Sense What It Covers Example Effect on Reader
Sight Colour, shape, light, movement "warm turquoise waters" Creates a vivid mental picture
Sound Volume, rhythm, tone "extraordinary dreamlike clamour" Immerses the reader in the atmosphere
Smell Fragrance, stench, freshness "sweet-smelling bread" Triggers memory and emotion
Touch Temperature, texture "refreshingly cold water" Makes the reader feel physically present
Taste Flavour, richness "sharp, white goat's cheese" Creates intimacy and comfort
Memory Trick — Counting Senses
When you read any descriptive passage, count how many senses the writer uses. If 3 or more, comment on the range. If only 1, comment on why that sense dominates and what mood it creates.

Say It in One Sentence

Keywording Underline the important words in the question BEFORE you read the passage — it tells you exactly what to look for.
Skim then scan Skim for the big picture first, then scan for specific answers — never re-read the whole passage for every question.
Summary writing Read twice, keyword the question, underline relevant info, number your points, write in your OWN words, then proofread.
Fact vs opinion A fact can be verified ("150 km off the coast"); an opinion is the writer's feeling ("it's a charming place").
Implicit meaning "Crumbling walls and empty doorways" explicitly means old buildings — implicitly suggests abandonment and decline.
Good travel writing Brevity, personality, anecdotes, vivid senses, good grammar, and useful information.

PEE Paragraph Template — For Travel Writing

Use this template every time you analyse a descriptive passage. The marks are in the Explanation.

P (Point): The writer uses technique name to create a sense of mood/atmosphere.

E (Evidence): This is shown in the phrase "quote from the text".

E (Explanation): The word "specific word" suggests what it implies, which makes the reader feel emotion. This creates a sense of atmosphere because why it works.

The Golden Rule
Your Explanation should be at least TWICE as long as your Point and Evidence combined. If your explanation is only one sentence, you haven't said enough. After writing one explanation sentence, ask "So what? What does that make the reader feel?" and write another.
Common Mistake
Do NOT write: "The writer uses sensory language. Sensory language is language that appeals to the five senses." That's a definition, not an analysis. The examiner already knows what sensory language is. Tell them what THIS example does to THIS reader in THIS passage.

Summary vs Analysis — Know the Difference

Summary Analysis
What you do Say WHAT the text is about Say HOW the writer achieves an effect
Key words in question "Summarise," "What are the main points?" "How does the writer...?", "What is the effect of...?"
Use own words? Yes — paraphrase, don't copy Yes for explanation, but quote the text as evidence
Give opinion? No — just the facts Yes — explain the effect on the reader
Exam Trap
If the question says "summarise," do NOT analyze techniques or give opinions. If it says "how does the writer create an effect," do NOT just summarise. Read the question keyword!

Memory Tricks

SQuEE vs PEE — Same Idea, Different Labels
SQuEE = State, Quote, Explain, Effect. PEE = Point, Evidence, Explanation. SQuEE splits the explanation into two steps; PEE keeps it as one. Either works on the exam — the key is that you always explain the effect on the reader.
The Summary 7-Step Process
R-R-F-N-B-W-E: Read twice, Read the question, Find and underline, Number points, Brief notes, Write in own words, Edit and proofread.
PEE Starter Phrases
Memorize these and use them as launching pads for your explanations:
"This suggests that..." / "The effect on the reader is..." / "The word [X] implies..." / "This creates a sense of..." / "The reader feels... because..." / "The writer may have chosen this to..."
Persuasive Writing Techniques — IRISH
Imperatives ("Visit!"), Rhetorical questions ("Why not?"), I → direct address ("you"), Short paragraphs, Hyperbolic/positive adjectives ("stunning," "unforgettable").

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... Your Answer Should...
"Summarise the main points..." Key points only. Own words. No analysis, no opinion. Be concise. Stay within the word count.
"How does the writer make the description effective?" Use PEE. Name techniques (sensory language, simile, etc.), quote evidence, explain the EFFECT on the reader. At least 2 techniques.
"What does the writer imply?" This is an implicit meaning question. Read between the lines. Explain what is SUGGESTED, not what is directly stated.
"Is this a fact or an opinion?" Can it be proven? Fact. Is it the writer's personal feeling? Opinion. Watch for disguised opinions: "this charming village" is opinion, not fact.
"What persuasive techniques are used?" Look for imperatives, rhetorical questions, direct address, positive adjectives, short paragraphs. Name, quote, explain effect.
"Explain in your own words..." Do NOT copy from the text. Replace key phrases with synonyms that mean the same thing in context. Write as a full sentence.
"Write a description of..." Use all five senses. Be specific ("turquoise" not "nice"). Use literary techniques. Vary sentence length. Avoid clichés.
