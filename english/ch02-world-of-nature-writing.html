World of Nature — Your Turn

Nature Around You

About 5 minutes

Pick ONE natural thing you've noticed since living in Denmark — could be weather, a landscape, an animal, a season changing, the sea, forests, the sky at a certain time.

It could be any of these, or something else entirely:

The wind at the coast A forest in winter The sea on a grey day Snow melting into spring A flock of birds The sky at sunset Rain on the school windows A storm

My natural thing:

Now work through each step below. The last one is the key one — it's your personification bridge.

What is it?
What does it look like?
What does it do?
If it were a person, it would be…
Tip
The last column is the most important one. Personification means giving human qualities to something non-human. If the wind were a person, what kind of person? Angry? Playful? Tired? That's how personification starts.
2

Giving Nature a Voice

About 5 minutes

These sentences are flat and boring. Rewrite each one using personification — give the natural thing a human action, emotion, or personality. Stuck for words? Open the Vocabulary Bank ↗

Flat "The wind was strong today."
Hint: What would the wind DO if it were a person? Whisper? Shout? Push you?
Flat "The trees moved in the wind."
Hint: How do people move? Dancing? Reaching? Bowing?
Flat "The sea was rough."
Hint: Was it angry? Restless? Fighting something?
Remember
Personification isn't just about adjectives — it's about verbs. "The wind screamed" is stronger than "the angry wind." Give nature human actions, not just human labels.
3

Purpose Check

About 5 minutes

Label each statement with its purpose: I = Inform, P = Persuade, A = Argue, E = Entertain.

  • 1 "Denmark has over 7,000 km of coastline." ___
  • 2 "We must protect our forests before it's too late." ___
  • 3 "The old oak tree groaned like a tired grandfather settling into his armchair." ___
  • 4 "You should visit the Danish coast — it will take your breath away." ___
Now connect it
Look back at your personification sentences from Exercise 2. What purpose do they serve? Most descriptive writing aims to entertain — it makes the reader experience something. But persuasive writing about nature often uses personification too (e.g. "the rainforest is crying out for our help"). The technique is the same; the purpose changes.
4

Your Nature Paragraph

About 15 minutes

Now bring it all together. You're going to write a descriptive paragraph about a moment in nature you've experienced in Denmark, using personification.

Step 1 — Fill in the template (this is your planning step, not your final answer):

The natural thing personification verb — e.g. whispered, marched, danced.

It seemed to human action as if comparison.

I noticed a sight detail and a sound or touch detail.

The thing made me feel an emotion because why.

Step 2 — Write it for real. Use the template ideas but write a flowing paragraph in your own voice. Aim for 80–120 words. Vocabulary Bank ↗

Your paragraph should
• Use at least 2 examples of personification
• Include at least 2 senses (not just sight — add sound, touch, smell)
• Have specific details — not "it was cold" but what KIND of cold
• Sound like you, not a textbook — what did this moment actually feel like?

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your paragraph from Exercise 4 out loud. Yes, actually out loud — you'll hear awkward phrasing that you won't catch by reading silently. Then check each box honestly.

  • Personification — Did I give nature human qualities at least twice?
  • Purpose — Would a reader feel like they experienced this moment?
  • Sensory detail — At least 2 senses beyond just sight?
  • Specific details — No "nice," "good," or "beautiful"?
  • Varied sentences — Mix of short and long?
  • My personality — Does it sound like me, not a textbook?
