Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-3 marks each. Define key persuasive and analytical writing terms from this chapter.
1
Name Aristotle's three strands of persuasion and explain what each one means.
3 marks
Answer
1. Ethos — appeal to credibility/authority. The speaker establishes trust by showing expertise or moral character [1].
2. Pathos — appeal to emotion. The speaker makes the audience feel something (anger, sympathy, fear, hope) [1].
3. Logos — appeal to logic. The speaker uses facts, statistics, and reasoned arguments [1].
A strong persuasive text uses all three. If you're analysing a speech, identify which strand each paragraph uses. If you're writing one, make sure you include all three — examiners look for this range.

2
Name four rhetorical devices commonly used in persuasive speeches. For each, write a one-sentence example on the topic of school uniform.
4 marks
Answer
Any four of: [1 each for device + valid example]
1. Rhetorical question: "Do we really want our students judged by what they wear rather than what they think?"
2. Rule of three (tricolon): "Uniforms promote equality, discipline, and pride."
3. Emotive language: "Forcing children into identical outfits crushes their individuality."
4. Repetition/anaphora: "We deserve the right to choose. We deserve the right to express ourselves. We deserve the right to be heard."
5. Direct address: "You know what it feels like to wear something that doesn't represent who you are."
6. Hyperbole: "Without uniforms, our schools would descend into chaos overnight."
7. Contrast/antithesis: "Some call it tradition; I call it control."
You need the DEVICE NAME + a CORRECT EXAMPLE. Just naming the device without an example, or writing an example without naming it, gets zero. The examiner wants both.

3
What are the key structural features of a formal letter of complaint? List at least five.
3 marks
Answer
Any five of: [½ mark each, up to 3]
1. Your address (top right)
2. Date
3. Recipient's name and address (left)
4. Formal greeting: "Dear Mr/Ms [Name]" or "Dear Sir/Madam"
5. Clear subject line or opening stating the purpose
6. Paragraphed body (problem → evidence → impact → desired action)
7. Formal closing: "Yours sincerely" (if you used their name) or "Yours faithfully" (if "Dear Sir/Madam")
8. Your full name
The sincerely/faithfully rule catches students every year. Remember: if you know their name (Dear Mr Smith) = Yours sincerely. If you don't (Dear Sir/Madam) = Yours faithfully. "S" for "S" — Sincerely goes with the Specific name.

4
State three rules for writing a good summary. What should you not include in a summary?
2 marks
Answer
Rules: [1]
1. Use your own words — do not copy phrases from the original.
2. Include only the main points, not minor details or examples.
3. Keep it concise — shorter than the original text.

Do NOT include: [1]
Your own opinions, emotional language, direct quotes, repetition of the same point in different words, or minor examples/anecdotes.
The biggest summary mistake is "lifting" — copying exact phrases from the original. The second biggest is adding your own opinion ("I think this is interesting because..."). A summary is a neutral restatement of someone else's ideas in your own words. Nothing more.

Intermediate — Can You Explain and Apply It?

Worth 3-5 marks each. Apply persuasive and analytical techniques in your own writing.
5
Read this sentence from a persuasive speech: "Every single day, 8 million tonnes of plastic pour into our oceans — that's one garbage truck every minute. Your garbage truck. My garbage truck." Identify three persuasive techniques used and write a PEE paragraph about the most effective one.
5 marks
Answer
Three techniques:
1. Statistic (logos): "8 million tonnes" [1]
2. Concrete image: "one garbage truck every minute" makes the abstract number visual [1]
3. Repetition + direct address: "Your garbage truck. My garbage truck." [1]

PEE paragraph (example for repetition/direct address):
P: The repetition of "garbage truck" combined with direct address creates personal responsibility [1].
E: The speaker shifts from the global statistic to "Your garbage truck. My garbage truck."
E: By using "your" and "my," the speaker makes the audience feel individually accountable, preventing them from dismissing the problem as someone else's responsibility. The short, blunt sentences create a punching rhythm that emphasises guilt and urgency [1].
When the exam gives you a persuasive extract and asks you to analyse it, always try to identify techniques from all three of Aristotle's strands (logos = statistic, pathos = emotive image, ethos = direct address). Then pick the most effective one for your PEE paragraph.

6
Explain three differences you would expect between a blog post and a news report covering the same topic (e.g., a new shopping centre opening). Use the framework: purpose, language, and structure.
3 marks
Answer
1. Purpose: A blog aims to share opinions and entertain, while a news report aims to inform objectively [1].
2. Language: A blog uses informal, personal language (first person, contractions, humour, exaggeration), while a news report uses formal, neutral language (third person, factual, past tense) [1].
3. Structure: A blog has a casual layout (short paragraphs, subheadings, images, links), while a news report follows the inverted pyramid (most important information first, details later) with a headline, strapline, and byline [1].
Comparing text types is a guaranteed exam topic. For each difference, state what BOTH texts do — comparison means two sides. "A blog is informal" alone is description, not comparison. "A blog is informal WHILE a report is formal" is comparison.

7
Read this descriptive sentence: "The market was a riot of colour — pyramids of saffron-yellow spices, bolts of silk the colour of sunset, and everywhere the flash of gold jewellery catching the light." Using the zoom-in technique, write a PEE paragraph analysing the language.
4 marks
Answer
P: The writer creates a sense of overwhelming richness and sensory abundance [1].
E: The description lists "pyramids of saffron-yellow spices, bolts of silk the colour of sunset, and everywhere the flash of gold jewellery" [1].
E (zoom in): The word "pyramids" is particularly effective — it suggests the spices are piled high in neat, impressive shapes, implying both abundance and careful craftsmanship. The colour palette moves from yellow to sunset to gold, creating a warm, rich visual that makes the reader feel immersed in the scene. The metaphor "riot of colour" suggests the colours are so intense they feel almost chaotic and uncontrollable, conveying excitement and energy [2].
The "zoom-in technique" means: start with the overall effect of the whole sentence, then pick ONE or TWO specific words and explain why they are so effective. The examiner wants to see you analyse individual word choices, not just comment on the sentence as a whole.

Advanced — Can You Analyse and Write?

Worth 5-8 marks each. These need extended persuasive or analytical writing with clear structure and multiple techniques.
8
Write the opening and closing paragraphs of a persuasive speech to your school arguing that "fast fashion is destroying the planet." Your opening must use at least two rhetorical devices. Your closing must include a call to action.
6 marks
Answer
Example opening:
"Look down at what you're wearing right now. That t-shirt? It probably cost less than your lunch. But someone, somewhere, paid the real price — and so did the planet. Every year, 92 million tonnes of textile waste is dumped into landfill. That's enough to fill Sydney Harbour. Every. Single. Year." [3]

Techniques used: direct address ("Look down"), rhetorical question ("That t-shirt?"), statistic (logos — "92 million tonnes"), concrete image ("enough to fill Sydney Harbour"), short sentences for emphasis ("Every. Single. Year.")

Example closing:
"So the next time you scroll past a 'Buy 3 get 1 free' deal at 2am, stop. Ask yourself: who made this? And what will happen to it in six months? We don't need another haul video. We need a generation that says: enough. Buy less. Choose better. Make it last." [3]

Techniques used: imperative ("stop," "ask yourself"), rule of three ("Buy less. Choose better. Make it last."), direct address, call to action.
[3] for opening: must include at least two named devices, used correctly. [3] for closing: must include a clear call to action (telling the audience specifically what to DO). The biggest mistake is ending with "So in conclusion, fast fashion is bad" — that's a summary, not a call to action. Tell them what to DO differently.

9
Write a formal letter of complaint (opening paragraph + one body paragraph + closing paragraph) to a company about a faulty product you purchased. You must demonstrate: correct letter format, formal register, a clear statement of the problem with evidence, and a specific request for resolution.
6 marks
Answer
Example:

Dear Mr Harrison,

I am writing to express my dissatisfaction with a pair of wireless headphones (Model: SoundMax Pro, Order #4782) purchased from your website on 15 February 2026. [1 — formal opening, states purpose, includes specific details]

Within two weeks of purchase, the left earpiece stopped producing sound entirely, despite the product being handled with care and used only as intended. I contacted your customer service team on 2 March and was advised to "try restarting them," which I had already done. The product is clearly faulty and does not meet the standard I would expect from a company of your reputation. [2 — clear problem + evidence + measured but firm tone]

I would appreciate a full refund or a replacement unit within 14 days. Should I not receive a satisfactory response, I will be forced to escalate this matter to the relevant consumer protection authority. I look forward to hearing from you at your earliest convenience. [2 — specific request + deadline + escalation + polite closing]

Yours sincerely,
[Your Name] [1 — correct closing: "sincerely" because a specific name was used]
Six marks: [1] format + opening, [2] body with problem + evidence, [2] resolution request + escalation, [1] correct closing. Note "Yours sincerely" not "Yours faithfully" because the letter begins with "Dear Mr Harrison" (a specific name). Getting this wrong is an instant lost mark.

10
A report and a blog post both cover the topic of Fair Trade products. Write two PEE paragraphs explaining how the language and purpose of each text type would differ. You must name specific language features for both text types.
6 marks
Answer
PEE 1 — The Report:
P: A report on Fair Trade would use formal, objective language because its purpose is to inform and present facts impartially [1].
E: It would include features such as third-person perspective, passive voice ("It was found that..."), data and statistics, and formal connectives ("Furthermore," "In addition") [1].
E: These features create a professional, authoritative tone. The passive voice removes the writer's personal presence, making the information feel unbiased and research-based, which increases the reader's trust in the findings [1].

PEE 2 — The Blog Post:
P: A blog post on Fair Trade would use informal, personal language because its purpose is to share opinions and engage readers emotionally [1].
E: It might include first person ("I was shocked to learn..."), rhetorical questions ("But is your morning coffee worth someone's suffering?"), contractions, and direct address [1].
E: The first-person voice creates a personal connection, making the reader feel they are hearing from a real person rather than an institution. The rhetorical question forces the reader to reflect on their own behaviour, which is more persuasive than simply presenting statistics because it creates guilt and personal accountability [1].
This is a "compare text types" question disguised as a PEE exercise. Each paragraph must: (1) state the text type and its purpose, (2) name SPECIFIC language features, (3) explain WHY those features suit the purpose. The highest marks go to answers that explain the EFFECT of the language choices, not just list them.
