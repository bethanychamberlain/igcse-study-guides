Eavesdropping

About 5 minutes

Think of a recent conversation you had or overheard at school. It could be funny, awkward, confusing, or completely ordinary.

It might have been about any of these, or something else entirely:

What to eat at lunch A confusing homework task Weekend plans Something that happened in PE A misunderstanding in Danish A late-night conversation

Who was talking?

Where were they?

What were they talking about?

Now write down 4–6 lines of the conversation as best you remember it — just the words people said, like a script.

Tip
Don't worry about getting the exact words right. The point is to capture how people actually talk — the interruptions, the slang, the half-finished sentences. Real speech is messy, and that's what makes good dialogue.
2

Script It

About 5 minutes

Take your conversation from Exercise 1 and rewrite it as a proper script. Follow this format:

STUDENT A: (leaning back in chair, grinning) You're not serious.

STUDENT B: (dead serious) I'm completely serious.

STUDENT A: (looking around for support) Someone back me up here.

Your script needs:

• Character names in capitals before each line
• Stage directions in brackets describing how they speak or what they do
• At least 4 lines of dialogue

Remember
Stage directions show what you CAN'T hear — facial expressions, movement, tone of voice. They're the difference between flat dialogue and a scene the reader can picture. Think about what the characters are doing while they talk.
3

Register Switch

About 5 minutes

Here is one piece of information:

The information "The school cafeteria will be closed next Tuesday for cleaning."

Rewrite this same information in 3 different registers. Same facts, totally different style.

1. A text message to a friend
2. An announcement from the headteacher
3. A formal letter to parents
Key learning
Same information, different register depending on audience and purpose. In IGCSE English, you'll be asked to write for specific audiences — the examiner wants to see that you can shift your tone and vocabulary to match.
4

Your Article

About 15 minutes

Write a short article (80–120 words) for a school newsletter or website about one aspect of life at your school in Denmark. Vocabulary Bank ↗

Pick one topic:

The food A school tradition A sport or activity An interesting class A rule that's different from American schools Dorm life

Step 1 — Plan with this scaffold (fill in the blanks, then write for real):

Headline: short, catchy title

Opening sentence: who / what / where / when — the key facts in one sentence

Detail 1: a specific thing about this topic

Detail 2: another detail or comparison

Quote: "something someone actually said about it" said who.

Closing: your opinion or a forward-looking statement

Step 2 — Write it for real. Use your plan but write a flowing article that reads like a real newsletter piece. Aim for 80–120 words.

Your article should
• Have a catchy headline — not "My Article" or "Denmark"
• Open with who/what/where/when in the first sentence
• Include 2–3 sentences of specific detail
• Include at least one direct quote from someone
• End with your opinion or a forward-looking statement
• Use a newsletter register — not a text message, not a formal essay

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your article from Exercise 4 out loud. Does it sound like something you'd actually read in a school newsletter? Then check each box honestly.

  • Register — Does my article sound like a real school newsletter, not a text message?
  • Audience — Could a parent or teacher read this and understand it?
  • Opening — Does my first sentence tell the reader what the article is about?
  • Quotation — Did I include at least one direct quote?
  • Fact + opinion — Did I mix factual information with my own perspective?
  • Headline — Is it short, clear, and interesting?
