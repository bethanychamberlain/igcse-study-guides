Key Vocabulary

Term What It Means Don't Confuse With
Tone The writer's attitude toward their subject. Is it celebratory? Critical? Nostalgic? Humorous? Register (tone = attitude; register = formality level)
Register The level of formality: formal, neutral, or informal. Created by diction, syntax, and form. Tone (register = how formal; tone = how the writer feels about the topic)
Past simple "Cleopatra fled." States a completed action. The main storytelling tense. Past perfect (past simple = it happened; past perfect = it happened BEFORE something else)
Past perfect "Ptolemy had driven her out." Shows that one event happened BEFORE another. Past simple (past perfect = the earlier event; past simple = the later event)
Show don't tell Instead of stating an emotion ("He was angry"), describe actions that reveal it ("His jaw tightened"). —
Connotation The associations a word carries beyond its dictionary definition. "Home" connotes warmth, safety, belonging. Denotation (denotation = dictionary meaning; connotation = feelings and associations)
Paraphrase Restating something in your own words while keeping the meaning. Essential for "explain in your own words" questions. Summary (paraphrase = same length, different words; summary = shorter version of key points)

Past Tenses and Their Effects

Tense Example Effect / When Used
Past simple "Cleopatra fled to Syria" States a fact about a completed action. The main storytelling tense.
Past perfect "Ptolemy had driven her out" Shows one event happened BEFORE another. Creates a timeline.
Present tense "The image has been dramatized" Bridges past to present. Shows something is still relevant now.
Past continuous "She was seeking an audience" Shows an ongoing action. Creates atmosphere and duration.
Memory Trick — Past Perfect = The Earlier One
"Cleopatra fled because Ptolemy had driven her out." Which happened first? The driving out. The past perfect (had + past participle) always marks the earlier event.

Comparing Tone in Two Texts

When the exam gives you two texts on the same topic, compare these four features:

Feature to Compare What to Look For Example Observation
Word choice Does one use emotional language while the other is neutral? "Text A calls it 'devastating' while Text B says it 'had an impact'"
Sentence structure Short, punchy = urgency. Long, flowing = descriptive mood. "Text A uses short declarative sentences to create drama"
Use of quotations Does the writer quote people directly or paraphrase? "Text B includes direct quotes from witnesses, adding authenticity"
Writer's presence Does the writer include their own opinion or stay invisible? "Text A is personal and uses 'I,' while Text B is impersonal and objective"
Common Mistake
Don't just say "one is formal and one is informal." Explain WHAT specific words or structures create that difference. Quote examples from both texts.

Short Story Writing — Quick Checklist

Step Action Key Details
1 Plan Audience, subject, setting, characters, first or third person? Plan BEFORE writing.
2 Plot Beginning, middle, end. Keep characters few. Give your main character a flaw.
3 Focus One event is enough. Cliffhanger ending is fine — but you must know how it would end.
4 Style Get to the point quickly. Use sensory details. Show don't tell.
5 Finish Don't trail off — give it a satisfying or surprising ending.
6 Proofread Check spelling, grammar, word choice. Swap weak words for stronger ones.

"Show Don't Tell" — Upgrade Your Writing

Telling (weak) Showing (strong)
"He was angry." "His jaw tightened and his fingers curled into fists."
"She was nervous." "She kept glancing at the door, twisting the hem of her sleeve."
"The house was lonely." "Every footstep echoed like a question nobody answered."
"He was happy." "A grin spread across his face and he bounced on his toes."
"It was cold." "Her breath came out in white clouds and the puddles had turned to glass."
Why This Matters
"Show don't tell" is the difference between a Grade C story and a Grade A story. Show the emotion through actions, body language, and sensory details instead of just naming the feeling.

PEE Paragraph Template — For Relationships

Chapter 5 is about family and relationships. Use this lens for every text you analyse.

P (Point): The writer presents the relationship between Character A and Character B as type of relationship: tense / loving / distant.

E (Evidence): This is shown when what happens / what is said: "quote".

E (Explanation): The word "specific word" suggests what it implies about the relationship. This makes the reader feel emotion because why. The writer positions the reader to sympathise with / feel uneasy about / understand the character.

The Golden Rule for Relationships
Every opinion needs a reason. Every feeling needs a cause. Don't write "I was sad." Write "I was sad because I realised the bond between us had quietly broken, like a thread that had been stretching for years until it finally snapped."
Common Mistake
Do NOT just describe what happens between characters. Explain what the writer's word choices suggest about the power balance, the emotional dynamics, and how the reader is positioned to feel about each person.

Dialogue Rules — Quick Reference

Rule Example
New speaker = new paragraph "I'm leaving," she said.
"Wait," he called after her.
Speech marks around spoken words "Where are you going?" not: Where are you going?
Punctuation INSIDE the speech marks "I don't know," she whispered. (comma before closing marks)
Vary your speech verbs whispered, snapped, muttered, exclaimed — not just "said" every time
First Person Trap
If you write in first person ("I"), you can ONLY describe what YOUR character sees, hears, thinks, and feels. You CANNOT write "Meanwhile, across town, Sarah was worried" — your character doesn't know that.

"Explain in Your Own Words" — 5-Step Process

Step What to Do
1 Read the sentence AROUND the word, not just the word itself
2 Think about what the word means in this specific context
3 Replace it with a simpler word or phrase that fits
4 Write your answer as a full sentence that makes sense in the original context
5 Check you have NOT copied any phrases from the text
Zero-Marks Trap
If the text says "an integral role" and you write "an integral role means an integral role" — you get zero. You must say "an essential or very important part." Use DIFFERENT words.

Say It in One Sentence

Tone vs register Tone = the writer's attitude (nostalgic, critical, warm). Register = the formality level (formal, neutral, informal).
Past perfect Use "had + verb" to show which event happened first: "She fled because he had betrayed her."
Show don't tell Don't name the emotion — describe actions and details that reveal it. This is what separates good writers from average ones.
Own words You CANNOT copy from the text. Replace key phrases with synonyms that mean the same thing in context.
Feeling + image Pair every emotion with a concrete image: loneliness = "every footstep echoed like a question nobody answered."
Relationships lens Look at what characters say (and don't say), how they act toward each other, and what the word choices suggest about power balance.

Memory Tricks

The Feeling + Image Formula
For every emotion you write about, pair it with a concrete image. Joy + image: "The sunlight caught the spray from the hose and turned it into a rainbow." This makes your writing vivid and earns marks for descriptive skill.
PEE Starters for Relationship Analysis
"The writer presents the relationship as..." / "The contrast between [A] and [B] suggests..." / "The reader is positioned to sympathise with..." / "The power dynamic shifts when..." / "This reveals the underlying tension in..."
Modern Idioms in Historical Writing
When a writer uses modern phrases about the past ("set about the business of ruling"), this is a deliberate choice to make history feel relevant. If you spot this in the exam, comment on it — it shows you understand the writer's craft.
Speech Verb Upgrades
Replace "said" with verbs that show HOW the character speaks: whispered (secret/fear), snapped (anger/impatience), muttered (reluctance/resentment), exclaimed (surprise/excitement), pleaded (desperation).

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... Your Answer Should...
"How does the writer present the relationship?" Look at what characters say, do, and don't say. Quote specific words. Explain what they suggest about the power balance and emotional dynamics. Use PEE.
"Compare the tone of the two texts..." Identify specific features: word choice, sentence structure, writer's presence. Quote from BOTH texts in the same paragraph. Use comparison connectives.
"Write a short story about..." Plan first. Small cast, one main event, show don't tell, sensory details, satisfying ending. Proofread for weak words.
"Explain in your own words..." Do NOT copy. Read the context. Replace with simpler words. Write a full sentence. Check you haven't copied ANY phrases.
"Why does the writer use the past perfect tense?" To show the order of events — the past perfect marks the EARLIER action. It creates a timeline for the reader.
"Write a journal entry from [character]'s view..." Use first person. Express thoughts and feelings. Include references to events from the text. Justify every opinion with a reason.
"Describe a scene..." All five senses. Show don't tell. Specific details, not vague ones. Vary sentence length. Pair emotions with concrete images.
