Key Vocabulary

Term What It Means Don't Confuse With
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things. "The angry sea," "the wind whispered." Metaphor (personification IS a type of metaphor, but specifically gives HUMAN traits)
Emotive language Words chosen to trigger an emotional response: "devastating," "heartbreaking," "innocent." Descriptive language (all emotive language is descriptive, but descriptive isn't always emotive)
Counter-argument Acknowledging the opposing view, then arguing against it. Shows you've considered both sides. Concession (similar, but a concession admits the other side has a point; a counter-argument dismantles it)
Topic sentence The first sentence of a paragraph that states the main point. Everything else in the paragraph supports it. Thesis statement (a thesis is for the whole essay; a topic sentence is for one paragraph)
Register The level of formality in writing: formal, neutral, or informal. Created by word choice, tone, and sentence structure. Tone (register = formality level; tone = writer's attitude — e.g. sarcastic, celebratory)
Directed Writing Paper 2, Section A: read a text, then write a new piece (article, letter, speech) using the information. Composition writing (directed writing uses a source text; composition is from your own ideas)

The Four Purposes of Writing

Purpose Language Features Example Text Type
To inform Third person, factual, precise, statistics, passive voice News reports, textbooks
To argue / persuade Emotive language, rhetorical questions, counter-arguments Opinion articles, speeches
To describe Sensory language, simile, metaphor, personification Travel writing, descriptive essays
To narrate Past tense, dialogue, character development Stories, myths, folk tales
Memory Trick — IAPDN
Inform, Argue/Persuade, Describe, Narrate. A single text can have more than one purpose. A feature article might describe scenery (describe), give facts about a river (inform), AND persuade the reader that wild places deserve protection.

Personification — Effects on the Reader

What Is Personified Example Effect on Reader
Wind as tyrant "announced the tyrant's coming" Creates fear; the wind becomes a threatening ruler with deliberate cruelty
River as living force "a taker as well as a giver of life" Suggests the river has power and intention; creates respect and danger
Trees in pain "the branches talk in agony" Makes the reader feel empathy for nature, as if it is suffering
Rapids as hostile "angry rapids" Implies the river is aggressive and threatening; creates unease
Why Personification Matters
Personification is one of the most common techniques in IGCSE English. If you can spot it AND explain its effect, you will score well. Nature passages almost always use it.

PEE Paragraph Template — For Nature Writing

Fill this in for every analytical paragraph. The marks are in the Explanation.

P (Point): The writer uses technique to create a sense of mood in the description of what is being described.

E (Evidence): This is shown in the phrase "quote".

E (Explanation): The word "specific word" suggests what it implies, which makes the reader feel emotion. The writer may have chosen this to purpose, creating an atmosphere that is precise mood word rather than simply vague word you're replacing.

Remember
Your Explanation should be at least TWICE as long as your Point and Evidence combined. After writing one explanation sentence, ask: "So what? What does that make the reader feel?" Then write another sentence.
Common Mistake
Do NOT write: "The writer uses personification. The river is described as 'angry.' This makes it seem alive." "Seem alive" is too vague. Alive how? Friendly? Dangerous? Hostile? The examiner needs precision: "This makes the reader feel uneasy, as if the river could become threatening at any moment."

Say It in One Sentence

Personification Giving human qualities to nature makes the reader feel emotions toward non-human things — fear, sympathy, awe.
Writer's purpose Always ask "Why was this written?" before analysing — the purpose tells you what techniques to look for.
Persuasive writing Present both sides, use emotive language and rhetorical questions, but never be rude or make sweeping statements.
Informative writing Factual, impersonal, third person, precise vocabulary, clear sentences, subheadings — no personal opinion.
Directed Writing 15 marks for content (what you say) + 25 marks for quality (how you say it) — HOW you write matters more.
Own words rule In Directed Writing, you MUST use your own words — do not copy phrases from the source text.

Memory Tricks

WHO-WHAT-HOW
For every text you read, ask three questions: WHO is the audience? WHAT is the purpose? HOW does the language serve that purpose? If you can answer all three, you can analyse any text.
Persuasive Writing Don'ts
Remember the 3 Nos: No rudeness ("which is rubbish"), No sweeping statements ("Everyone thinks..."), No more than 1-2 rhetorical questions.
PEE Starter Phrases for Nature
"The writer creates a sense of..." / "The verb [X] suggests violent movement, implying..." / "The personification makes the reader feel... because..." / "This creates an atmosphere of... which contrasts with..."
The Lawyer Test
When writing persuasively, imagine you're a lawyer in court. What evidence would convince a judge? If your argument wouldn't hold up in court, it won't hold up on the exam.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... Your Answer Should...
"How does the writer bring the scene to life?" Look for personification, sensory language, dynamic verbs. Use PEE. Explain the EFFECT of specific word choices.
"What is the purpose of this text?" Name it (inform, persuade, describe, narrate) AND explain what language features support that purpose. Quote evidence.
"Write an article arguing for or against..." Include both sides. Use topic sentences, counter-arguments, linking phrases, emotive language. Plan before you write.
"Write an informative piece about..." Be factual and impersonal. Use third person, clear sentences, subheadings. NO personal opinion. Mix active and passive verbs.
"How does the writer use personification?" Do NOT just identify it. Quote the specific words. Explain what HUMAN quality is given and WHY. What does the reader feel?
"What is the effect of the word [X]?" Explore connotations. What does the word make you think of? What feeling does it carry? Use precise vocabulary, not "nice" or "scary."
