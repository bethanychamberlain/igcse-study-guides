Key Vocabulary — Drama Terms

Term What It Means Don't Confuse With
Soliloquy Character speaks thoughts aloud, alone on stage. Audience hears their inner feelings. Monologue (soliloquy = alone, private thoughts; monologue = long speech TO other characters)
Monologue A long speech by one character, addressed to other characters on stage. Soliloquy (monologue = spoken to others; soliloquy = spoken to self/audience)
Stage directions Instructions in brackets or italics telling actors what to do, how to move, or how to speak. Dialogue (stage directions are NOT spoken; dialogue is what characters say)
Dénouement The final resolution of the plot — where everything is explained or wrapped up. Climax (climax = the peak of tension; dénouement = the resolution AFTER the climax)
Fatal flaw A weakness in a hero's character that leads to their downfall (also called hamartia). —
Sympathy Feeling sorry FOR someone. "I feel bad for them." Empathy (sympathy = pity from outside; empathy = understanding from inside their experience)
Empathy Feeling WITH someone. "I understand how they feel because I can imagine being them." Sympathy (empathy is deeper — you share the feeling, not just observe it)
Register The style of language suited to a situation. Created by diction + tone + syntax. Tone (register = formality level; tone = writer's attitude)

Tragedy vs Comedy — At a Glance

Tragedy Comedy
Plot direction Good to bad — reversal of fortune Bad to good — complications to happy ending
Ending Suffering, death, or despair Resolution, marriage, "happy ever after"
Audience feels Fear and pity Laughter and relief
Hero's flaw Has a fatal flaw that destroys them Has flaws but overcomes them
Example Macbeth (ambition destroys him) A Midsummer Night's Dream (mix-ups resolved)

Aristotle's Six Elements of Drama

Element What It Means Modern Example
1. Plot The sequence of events. Aristotle said this is the MOST important element. The storyline of a film or show
2. Character The people and their personalities, flaws, strengths. The protagonist, antagonist, supporting cast
3. Diction The language and word choices characters use (dialogue). How a character talks — slang, formal, poetic
4. Thought The themes and ideas the drama explores. Justice, power, identity, love
5. Spectacle Everything visual: costumes, scenery, effects, movement. CGI, set design, fight choreography
6. Song Music, sound, rhythm in the performance. Soundtrack, sound effects
Memory Trick — PC-DTS-S
Plot, Character, Diction, Thought, Spectacle, Song. Or just remember: "A Perfect Cast Delivers The Show's Soul."

Advertisement vs Article — Comparing Purposes

Advertisement Newspaper Article
Purpose To persuade (buy this, join this) To inform (here are the facts)
Language Positive, enthusiastic ("Don't miss your chance!") Balanced, quotes from multiple viewpoints
Tone Exciting, aspirational Neutral or analytical
Bias One-sided — only positive May present opposing views

Register — At a Glance

Formal Neutral Informal
Used for Essays, complaint letters, reports News articles, informative texts Blogs, personal letters, dialogue
Features No slang, no contractions, complex sentences Clear, factual, standard English Slang, contractions, colloquial language
Example "I wish to express my concern..." "The event took place on Monday." "So basically what happened was..."

PEE Paragraph Template — For Drama

In drama, EVERYTHING is a deliberate choice. Your job is to explain WHY the playwright made that choice.

P (Point): The playwright uses technique/element to reveal what it shows about character/theme.

E (Evidence): This is shown when character name says/does "quote or action" stage direction if relevant.

E (Explanation): The word "specific word" suggests what it implies about the character. This makes the audience feel emotion because why. The playwright may have intended this to purpose.

Drama-Specific Tip
Don't forget to analyse stage directions, not just dialogue. Most students ignore them — but stage directions reveal character emotions and power dynamics. Commenting on them shows deeper reading.
Common Mistake
Do NOT just describe what happens: "Sheila cries." Instead, explain the EFFECT: "Sheila's distress reveals her genuine guilt, which contrasts with Gerald's attempts to minimise his involvement, making the audience sympathise with her more." Description = low marks. Analysis = high marks.

Directed Writing — Format Cheat Sheet

Text Type Key Features Quick Tips
Article Headline, opening hook, body paragraphs, conclusion Match register to publication (blog = informal; broadsheet = formal). Stick to 250-350 words.
Formal letter Dear Mr/Mrs... or Dear Sir/Madam; state purpose first; one point per paragraph Name known = "Yours sincerely." Name unknown = "Yours faithfully." Never rude, even in complaints.
Speech Direct address ("You all know..."), rhetorical questions, groups of three Write as if you're speaking out loud. Read it back to yourself — does it sound natural?
The Bullet Point Trick
Directed writing questions always give you bullet points of what to include. Turn each bullet point into a paragraph. 3 bullet points = 3 body paragraphs + intro + conclusion. This guarantees you cover everything.

Say It in One Sentence

Soliloquy A character speaks their private thoughts aloud while alone on stage — the audience becomes their confidant.
Fatal flaw A weakness (ambition, jealousy, pride) that the hero cannot control, which ultimately destroys them.
Stage directions Not just instructions for actors — they reveal character emotions, power dynamics, and hidden tensions.
Register The level of formality must match the audience and situation — a blog wouldn't work as a school essay.
Comparing texts Weave both texts together — if you write a paragraph about only one text, you've lost marks.

Memory Tricks

Soliloquy vs Monologue
Soliloquy = Solo (alone on stage). Monologue = Monopolises the conversation (talks AT other characters).
Yours Sincerely vs Yours Faithfully
Sincerely goes with a Specific name (Dear Mr Smith). Faithfully goes with a Formal greeting (Dear Sir/Madam). S = S, F = F.
PEE for Drama — The Three Layers
Layer 1: What happens (the action/dialogue). Layer 2: What it reveals (about character/theme). Layer 3: What the audience feels (the emotional effect). Your exam answer needs all three layers. Most students stop at Layer 1.
Stage Direction Adverbs = Gold
When a stage direction says (uneasily), (dryly), or (distressed), this is the playwright TELLING you the character's emotions. Quote these in your PEE paragraph — they are easy, free evidence.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... Your Answer Should...
"How does the playwright present [character]?" Look at dialogue, stage directions, actions, and what others say about them. Quote specific words, explain what they SUGGEST about the character.
"What is the effect of the stage directions?" Quote the adverbs (e.g. "uneasily," "massively"). Explain what they reveal about the character's emotions and the power balance in the scene.
"What is [character]'s fatal flaw?" Name the flaw (ambition, jealousy, etc.) AND explain how it leads to their downfall with specific evidence from the text.
"Write an article about..." Headline, hook, body paragraphs, conclusion. Match the register. Turn each bullet point into a paragraph.
"Compare how the two texts..." Weave texts together. For each point: PEE on Text A, link with a connective, PEE on Text B. Never treat them in separate halves.
"Who is the intended audience?" Consider age, education, prior knowledge. Explain how the language/register/tone targets that audience. This is about WHO, not WHY.
