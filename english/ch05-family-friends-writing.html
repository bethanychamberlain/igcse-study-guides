English Ch5 — Writing Task

Family & Friends — Your Turn

Write vivid character descriptions using showing over telling, dialogue, and specific detail — drawing on the people around you at your efterskole.

About 35 minutes — 5 exercises

What You're Practising

Good writing about people doesn't list facts — it shows who someone is through what they do, say, and how they make you feel. This task builds step by step: you'll learn to show emotions, build a character, fix dull dialogue, and write a paragraph that brings a real person to life.

Show Don't Tell

About 5 minutes

These sentences tell you how someone feels. Your job is to show it instead — through actions, body language, or dialogue. Don't use the emotion word at all.

Telling "He was nervous."
Hint: What would you SEE or HEAR if this person were in front of you? Fidgeting? Voice cracking? Avoiding eye contact?
Telling "She was angry."
Hint: What does anger LOOK like? Clenched jaw? Slammed door? A voice that goes quiet and cold?
Telling "The teacher was kind."
Hint: What does a kind teacher actually DO? Think of a specific moment, not a label.
Telling "My friend was excited."
Hint: What does excitement look like in real life? Talking fast? Bouncing? Grabbing your arm?
Key rule
If you wrote the emotion word (nervous, angry, kind, excited), cross it out and try again. Show don't tell means the reader figures out the emotion from what the person does — you never name it directly. This is the single biggest upgrade you can make to your writing.

Character Snapshot

About 5 minutes

Pick someone from your efterskole life — a friend, roommate, teacher, or anyone you see regularly. You don't need to use their real name; a nickname or description is fine (e.g. "the guy who always wears the same hoodie").

A roommate A teacher Someone from your sports team The person who sits next to you Someone who makes you laugh Someone totally different from you

Fill in the character grid. One or two sentences per box is plenty — you're collecting details, not writing an essay yet.

Aspect Prompt Your answer
Appearance One physical detail that stands out
Habit Something they always do
Speech How they talk — fast, slow, catchphrases, accent?
Others How do people react to them?
Why this matters
You're building a bank of specific details. In IGCSE narrative writing, the difference between a C and an A is whether your characters feel like real people or cardboard cutouts. Real people have habits, quirks, and a way of talking that's uniquely theirs. You'll use these details in Exercise 4.
3

Dialogue Fix

About 5 minutes

These dialogue lines are flat and lifeless. Rewrite each one so it includes all three things:

• A speech verb other than "said" (muttered, snapped, whispered, laughed, groaned…)
• An action beat — what the person DOES while talking
• A hint at emotion without naming it directly (use your Exercise 1 skills!)

Flat "I'm fine," he said.
Hint: Is he actually fine? What would his body language give away?
Flat "Let's go," she said.
Hint: Is she impatient? Excited? Trying to escape? The verb and action should show it.
Flat "I didn't do it," he said.
Hint: Guilty? Defensive? Genuinely confused? Pick one and SHOW it.
Example upgrade
Flat: "I'm fine," he said.
Better: "I'm fine," he muttered, pulling his hood up and staring at his shoes.

See the difference? The reader KNOWS he's not fine — but you never said so. That's good writing.
4

Your Character Paragraph

About 15 minutes

Now bring the person from Exercise 2 to life. Write a paragraph (80–120 words) describing them in a specific moment — not a list of facts about them, but a scene where the reader can see them. Vocabulary Bank ↗

Step 1 — Plan with this scaffold (fill in the blanks, then write for real):

Person (nickname or description) was doing what in where.

A physical detail that stands out.

Something they said or how they spoke.

Something you noticed about them — a habit, a gesture.

How being around them made you feel.

Step 2 — Write it for real. Use the scaffold ideas but write a flowing paragraph that reads like a scene from a story, not a fill-in-the-blank. Aim for 80–120 words.

Your paragraph should
• Place the person in a specific moment (not "he is nice" but "he was sitting on his bed, untangling earbuds")
• Include at least one line of dialogue (use your Exercise 3 skills — not just "said")
• Show their personality through actions and details, not labels
• Use at least one detail from your Exercise 2 grid
• Sound like you — your voice, your way of seeing this person

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your paragraph from Exercise 4 out loud. Does the person come alive in your head when you hear it? Then check each box honestly.

  • Show don't tell — Did I use actions and body language instead of emotion labels?
  • Dialogue — Did I include at least one line of dialogue with a speech verb other than "said"?
  • Physical detail — Did I include a specific detail about how they look or move?
  • Real character — Does this person feel real, not a stereotype or a list of traits?
  • Varied sentences — Do I have a mix of short and long sentences?
  • My personality — Does my own voice and perspective come through?
