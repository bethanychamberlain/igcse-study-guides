Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
What is the difference between explicit and implicit meaning?
[2]
Answer
Explicit meaning is information that is stated directly in the text [1]. Implicit meaning is information that is suggested or implied — the reader must infer it from clues [1].
Explicit = on the page. Implicit = between the lines. When the exam says "what does this suggest," it wants implicit meaning.

2
Name the 5 stages of Freytag's Pyramid in order.
[2]
Answer
1. Exposition, 2. Rising Action, 3. Climax, 4. Falling Action, 5. Denouement / Resolution (or Catastrophe in tragedy) [1 for 3+ correct in order, 2 for all 5].
Think of it as a mountain: you climb up (rising action), reach the peak (climax), then come down (falling action). The start and end are the flat ground.

3
Define sibilance and alliteration. How are they different?
[2]
Answer
Sibilance is the repetition of "s" or "sh" sounds [1]. Alliteration is the repetition of consonant sounds at the start of words [1]. Sibilance is specifically about the "s/sh" sound and can occur anywhere in a word. Alliteration is about any consonant but only at the beginning.
Sibilance creates a soft, hissing, or whispering quality. Alliteration creates rhythm and emphasis. If the repeated sound happens to be "s" at the start of words, it's BOTH.

4
What is pathetic fallacy? Give one example.
[2]
Answer
Pathetic fallacy is when weather or nature is used to reflect the mood or emotions in a scene [1]. Example: a thunderstorm during an argument, or sunshine during a happy reunion [1].
Pathetic fallacy is a specific type of personification. It's always about WEATHER or NATURE matching MOOD. "The sky wept" = pathetic fallacy. "The chair groaned" = just personification.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need PEE structure and clear explanation of effects.
5
Read this sentence: "She said she was fine, but her hands would not stop shaking." What is the explicit meaning and what is the implicit meaning? Explain how the writer creates this gap between the two.
[4]
Answer
Explicit: She says she is fine. Her hands are shaking [1]. Implicit: She is NOT fine. She is frightened, anxious, or hiding her true feelings [1]. How the writer creates this: The conjunction "but" signals a contradiction between what she says and what her body reveals [1]. The detail "would not stop" suggests the shaking is involuntary and uncontrollable, which implies the emotion is too strong to hide. The reader understands her words are a lie, creating tension between the surface and the truth [1].
The gap between explicit and implicit is often created through contradiction. The body betrays what words try to hide. When you spot this pattern in the exam, you are reading at the highest level.

6
Explain what happens during the rising action and climax of a story. Why is the climax the most important moment?
[3]
Answer
During the rising action, complications develop, tension builds, and obstacles appear for the characters [1]. The climax is the turning point — the moment of greatest tension or conflict where everything comes to a head [1]. The climax is the most important moment because it determines the outcome of the story — it is where the central conflict is confronted, and after it, the story begins to resolve [1].
On Paper 2, if you write a narrative, your story MUST have a clear climax. Stories that just "things happen, then more things happen" without a turning point score low on structure.

7
A writer describes a storm: "The sky hissed and spat, the rain slashing at the earth like a thousand tiny knives." Using PEE, analyse how the writer uses figurative language. Name at least two techniques.
[4]
Answer
Technique 1 — Personification + Sibilance:
P: The writer uses personification and sibilance to make the storm feel aggressive [1]. E: "The sky hissed and spat" [1]. E: "Hissed" and "spat" give the sky human (or animal) aggression, as though it is deliberately attacking. The "s" sounds in "sky hissed and spat" create sibilance, producing a venomous, snake-like quality that reinforces the threat [1].

Technique 2 — Simile:
P: The writer uses a simile to make the rain feel violent. E: "slashing at the earth like a thousand tiny knives." E: Comparing rain to "knives" turns a natural event into something painful and weapon-like. "Slashing" implies deliberate, repeated cuts, and "a thousand" exaggerates the scale, making the rain feel inescapable and dangerous [1].
Bonus: "pathetic fallacy" also applies here — the storm reflects a mood of violence or turmoil. Naming a third technique shows examiner-level skill.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full PEE paragraphs, detailed analysis, and sometimes your own writing.
8
Read this passage: "The house had been waiting. That was what it felt like when she pushed open the gate — not that it was empty, but that it had been holding its breath. The garden had grown wild, reaching over the path like arms trying to pull her in. And somewhere inside, a light flickered, though no one had lived there for years."

Using PEE, analyse how the writer creates a sense of unease. Comment on at least two techniques and explain their effects on the reader.
[6]
Answer
Technique 1 — Personification:
P: The writer personifies the house to create a sense of it being alive and sentient [1]. E: "The house had been waiting" and "holding its breath" [1]. E: "Waiting" implies the house has intention — it expected her, which is deeply unsettling because houses cannot think. "Holding its breath" suggests anticipation, as though the house is about to act. This creates tension because the reader feels the house is not a safe, passive space but an active, watchful presence with its own agenda [1].

Technique 2 — Simile + Personification:
P: The writer uses a simile combined with personification to make the garden feel threatening [1]. E: "reaching over the path like arms trying to pull her in" [1]. E: The comparison of the plants to "arms" gives the garden a predatory, grabbing quality. "Trying to pull her in" suggests the garden is not merely overgrown but actively trying to trap her, which creates a sense of danger and claustrophobia. The reader feels that nature itself is conspiring against the character, making the house impossible to resist entering [1].

Bonus point: the "light flickered, though no one had lived there for years" creates mystery and fear because something unexplained is happening. This is foreshadowing — it hints at a presence inside.
6 marks for two fully developed PEE paragraphs. Each needs: name the technique, quote specifically, explain effect on the reader. The best answers explore individual word choices ("waiting," "holding its breath," "arms," "pull") rather than making general comments.

9
Write the opening paragraph of a narrative that begins at the climax (in the middle of the action). Your opening should use at least one simile or metaphor, one example of "showing" (not telling) emotion, and create a clear mood. The topic is: "The last day."
[6]
Answer
Example response:

"The siren split the morning in two. One moment, the corridor was full of laughter and the squeak of trainers on linoleum; the next, silence — the kind of silence that presses against your eardrums like water. My bag slid from my shoulder and hit the floor, but I did not pick it up. Around me, faces I had known for five years froze into masks I did not recognise, their eyes wide, searching for someone to tell them what to do. Nobody moved. Nobody breathed. And in that stillness, I understood: this was it. The last day had begun, and it was nothing like we had imagined."

Marking: [1] starts at the climax (immediate action/tension), [1] simile or metaphor ("silence... like water" / "faces... froze into masks"), [1] showing emotion through physical detail ("bag slid from my shoulder," "eyes wide, searching"), [1] clear mood (tension, disorientation, fear), [1] varied sentence structure (short + long), [1] strong vocabulary and sensory detail.
You don't need to match this example. The examiner wants: immediate action (not backstory), figurative language, shown emotion (not "I was scared"), clear atmosphere, and varied sentence structure. Starting with a sound or action is always stronger than starting with a description of the setting.

Exam-style question (Paper 1 format): Explain how the following passage creates a sense of sadness and loss. You should comment on the writer's use of language and structure.

"The playground was still there, smaller than he remembered. The swings hung motionless, their chains rusted to a dull orange. He sat on the bench where his mother used to wait, and for a moment he could almost hear her voice, calling him in for tea. Almost. The word hung in the air like smoke, beautiful and impossible to hold."
[6]
Answer
Language point 1:
P: The writer uses sensory and symbolic detail to suggest decay and the passage of time [1]. E: "The swings hung motionless, their chains rusted to a dull orange" [1]. E: "Motionless" suggests the playground is frozen in time — life has stopped here. "Rusted" implies neglect and age, and the colour "dull orange" replaces what was once bright and shiny, symbolising how joy has faded. The reader feels the weight of abandoned childhood [1].

Language point 2:
P: The writer uses a simile to convey the fragility of memory [1]. E: "The word hung in the air like smoke, beautiful and impossible to hold" [1]. E: Comparing the word "almost" to smoke suggests his memories are both vivid and intangible — they exist for a moment then dissolve. "Beautiful and impossible to hold" captures the bittersweet nature of nostalgia: the memory brings comfort but also pain because it cannot be recaptured [1].

Structure point (bonus): The one-word sentence "Almost." is placed alone for emphasis, creating a pause that mirrors the character's heartbreak. The reader shares his moment of reaching for something and losing it.
This is a Paper 1 style response. The examiner wants: at least 2 techniques identified, both quoted, both explained in terms of EFFECT ON THE READER. Commenting on structure (short sentence, paragraph breaks) as well as language pushes you into the highest marks.
