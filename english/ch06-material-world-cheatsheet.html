Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Logos Persuasion through logic, facts, and reasoning. Pathos (that's emotion, not logic)
Pathos Persuasion through emotion — making the audience feel something. Ethos (that's credibility/ethics, not feelings)
Ethos Persuasion through social/ethical appeal — "society benefits if we act." Logos (ethos = what's right, logos = what's logical)
Rhetorical question A question that doesn't expect an answer — makes the audience think. —
Tripling Saying something three ways for emphasis. "We will fight, we will endure, we will win." Repetition (tripling is specifically THREE; repetition can be any number)
Hyperbole Deliberate exaggeration for emphasis or urgency. Lying (hyperbole is a known exaggeration used for effect)
Emotive language Words chosen to trigger an emotional response (anger, sympathy, guilt). Descriptive language (emotive is about feelings, descriptive can be neutral)
Register The level of formality in writing (formal, semi-formal, informal). Tone (register = formality level; tone = attitude of the writer)
Summary WHAT the text says — key points in your own words, no analysis. Analysis (that's HOW the writer says it — techniques and effects)

Memory Trick — Sincerely vs Faithfully
Sincerely = you know their Surname (Dear Mr Smith). Faithfully = you're Fishing (Dear Sir/Madam — you don't know who they are).

Blog Article vs News Report

Blog Article News Report
Register Informal to neutral Formal and impersonal
Writer's voice Strong ("I think...") Invisible (facts speak)

Say It in One Sentence

Persuasive writing Uses logos, pathos, and ethos to make the reader agree with a viewpoint or take action.
Speech writing Written for the ear, not the eye — short sentences, "you" and "we," rhetorical questions, vivid images.
Complaint letter State your purpose in sentence one, stay polite, give facts in order, say what action you want.
Summary writing WHAT the text says in YOUR words — no analysis, no opinion, no copying phrases.
Report writing Formal, impersonal, with subheadings — findings first, opinions nowhere.
Comparing texts Organise by FEATURE (tone, register, purpose), not by text — weave both together in every paragraph.
"Zoom In" Pick ONE powerful word from a quote and explain what it suggests — this is the move that turns a C into an A.

Memory Tricks

PEE Paragraph Template
P = make your POINT (name the technique). E = give EVIDENCE (short quote). E = EXPLAIN the effect (this is where ALL the marks are — make it the longest part). Rule: your explanation should be at least twice as long as your point and evidence combined.
Logos-Pathos-Ethos = Head-Heart-Society
Logos = appeals to the Head (facts, logic). Pathos = appeals to the Heart (emotions). Ethos = appeals to Society (what's right for everyone).
The Comparison Weave
Never write "everything about Text A, then everything about Text B." Instead, weave: "Text A uses X, whereas Text B uses Y. This creates [different effect]." One paragraph, both texts, always.
Sentence Starters for Analysis
"The writer uses [technique] to [effect]..." / "The word '[X]' suggests/implies/connotes..." / "This creates a sense of [feeling] for the reader because..." / "This is effective because it makes the reader feel..."
Summary vs Analysis — One-Word Test
Ask: am I explaining WHAT or HOW? If WHAT = summary. If HOW = analysis. The question tells you which one it wants.

PEE Paragraph Templates — Use These in Every Answer

E: For example, the phrase "[short quote]"

Template 2 — Comparing Texts
P: Both writers address [topic], but they adopt very different [tones/approaches].
E: Text A uses [technique + quote], while Text B [technique + quote].
E: Text A's [language choice] creates [effect], making the reader feel [feeling]. In contrast, Text B's [language choice] encourages [different response]. This difference reflects their different purposes: Text A aims to [purpose] while Text B aims to [purpose].

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How does the writer persuade the reader?" Find rhetorical devices (rhetorical Q, tripling, emotive language, direct address). Name them + explain their effect using PEE.
"Write a speech arguing that..." Use "you" and "we," short punchy sentences, rhetorical questions, tripling, and end with a vivid image (aspirational or worst-case).
"Write a letter of complaint" Dear Mr/Mrs X, state purpose in first sentence, give facts in order, say what action you want, sign off Yours sincerely.
"Summarise the reasons why..." List key points in your own words. No analysis. No opinion. Stay in the word limit. One point per sentence.
"Write a report on..." Subheadings, formal language, passive voice ("It was found that..."), bullet points. Do NOT write it like a letter or essay.
"Compare how the writers present..." Organise by feature (tone, register, purpose), not by text. Weave both texts in every paragraph. Use "whereas," "in contrast," "while."
"How does the writer use language to describe..." Find technique, quote short (3-6 words), explain effect, zoom in on ONE word, connect to mood/atmosphere.
"What does the writer want the reader to think or feel?" Identify the purpose (persuade, shock, sympathise). Then find the specific language choices that create that response. Always answer: "The writer wants the reader to feel [X] because..."
