Think of a moment at your school that involved you AND someone else — it doesn't have to be dramatic, just something you both experienced.

It could be any of these, or something else:

Eating in the dining hall A class activity Arriving for the first time A sports game A misunderstanding Trying to communicate A group project Free time in the evening

My moment:

Now fill in both columns — YOUR perspective and how the other person might have seen the same moment.

How I saw it How they might have seen it
What I/they noticed first
What I/they felt
What I/they didn't understand
Tip
You don't need to know what the other person actually thought. The point is to imagine it — that's what writers do. The same moment always looks different depending on who's experiencing it. That difference is what makes perspective powerful in writing.
2

First vs Third Person

About 5 minutes

Here's a short paragraph written in first person. Read it, then rewrite it in third person below.

First person — original "I walked through the gate and immediately felt out of place. Everyone seemed to know each other. I shoved my hands in my pockets and tried to look like I belonged."
Task: Rewrite this in third person ("He walked..."). Change "I" to "he" and adjust the verbs.

Now answer these questions (just a sentence or two each):

Which version feels more personal and immediate?

Which gives a wider view — like a camera zoomed out?

Why might a writer choose one over the other?

Key difference
First person ("I") puts the reader inside the character's head — you feel what they feel. Third person ("he/she") steps back and can show things the character might not notice. Neither is "better" — they do different jobs.
3

Mood Shift

About 5 minutes

Here's a basic scene: "A student sits alone in the dining hall at a new school."

The facts don't change. But your word choices completely change how it feels. Write two versions of 2–3 sentences each.

Version A — Lonely and uncomfortable
Make the reader feel the isolation. What words create discomfort? Think about sounds, emptiness, self-consciousness.
Version B — Peaceful and calm
Same scene, but make the solitude feel chosen and pleasant. What words create peace? Think about warmth, quietness, contentment.
The lesson here
The FACTS didn't change — only the WORD CHOICES changed the mood. "Sat alone" can feel sad or content depending on every other word around it. This is one of the most powerful tools a writer has. In your IGCSE exam, you'll be asked to create a specific mood — and this is how you do it.
4

Your Perspective Paragraph

About 15 minutes

Write a short passage (80–120 words each) about a moment from your life in Denmark. Write it TWICE:

Version A: First person, present tense (you're there right now)
Version B: Third person, past tense (looking back on it)

Each version should create a clear mood through word choice. They can have different moods or the same mood seen from two angles — your choice.

First day at school A conversation you struggled with A moment you felt at home Something that surprised you A weekend trip Missing home
Before you write
• Version A (1st person, present): "I walk into the room. The noise hits me..." — the reader is IN the moment
• Version B (3rd person, past): "He walked into the room. The noise hit him..." — the reader is watching from outside

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read both versions from Exercise 4 out loud. Do they sound different? They should. Check each box honestly.

  • Perspective — Is Version A clearly first person and B clearly third person?
  • Tense — Is A in present tense and B in past tense throughout?
  • Mood — Could a reader name the mood of each version?
  • Word choice — Did I use specific words to CREATE the mood, not just name it? ("He felt sad" doesn't count.)
  • Show don't tell — Did I show feelings through actions and details, not just say "I felt sad" or "He was nervous"?
  • Different effect — Do the two versions feel genuinely different to read?
