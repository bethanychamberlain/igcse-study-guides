Same Moment, Two Eyes

About 5 minutes

Pick ONE everyday moment from efterskole life. It doesn't need to be dramatic — ordinary moments work best for this exercise.

It could be any of these, or something else entirely:

Lunch in the dining hall A morning assembly PE class Walking between buildings Lights-out time A bus ride to town

Step 1 — First person ("I"): Write 2–3 sentences about this moment from YOUR point of view.

Step 2 — Third person ("he" or "they"): Rewrite the SAME moment, but as if someone else is watching you. Same details, different perspective.

Tip
Notice what changes when you switch. In first person, you can share your thoughts and feelings directly. In third person, you have to show those feelings through actions and details instead of just telling. Which version feels more personal? Which feels more cinematic?

Remember from Chapter 3
Mood isn't created by saying "it was scary" or "it was nice." Mood comes from word choice and specific details. "Sunlight pooled on the desks" creates a different mood than "shadows crept across the desks" — even though both describe the same empty classroom.
3

Perspective Detective

About 5 minutes

Label each passage: 1st = first person, 3L = third person limited, 3O = third person omniscient.

Quick reminder
1st person: "I" — narrator is a character in the story.
3rd person limited: "He/she/they" — follows ONE character's thoughts.
3rd person omniscient: "He/she/they" — knows EVERYONE's thoughts and feelings.
  • 1 "I could hear the rain hitting the windows during the test. My mind kept drifting back to the weekend, and I had to force myself to focus on question three." ___
  • 2 "Marcus kicked the ball hard, hoping no one had noticed his mistake. On the other side of the pitch, Elias grinned — he had noticed, and he was already planning how to use it." ___
  • 3 "She stared out the bus window at the flat Danish countryside. The grey sky seemed to stretch on forever, and she wondered if the sun would ever come back." ___
  • 4 "I didn't want to admit it, but the cold was getting to me. My fingers were numb and I couldn't feel my toes inside my trainers." ___
  • 5 "Jake thought the assembly would never end. Next to him, Sofia was secretly relieved — it meant less time in maths. At the front, Mr Hansen could sense the whole room losing patience." ___
  • 6 "He sat alone in the common room, scrolling through his phone. He told himself he wasn't bothered that no one had texted back, but his jaw was tight and his knee wouldn't stop bouncing." ___
The tricky one
Third person limited and third person omniscient both use "he/she/they." The difference? Limited only knows what ONE person thinks. Omniscient can tell you what MULTIPLE characters are thinking or feeling in the same passage. Look for that switch between characters' inner thoughts.

Your Atmosphere Paragraph

About 15 minutes

Now bring it all together. Pick a place at the efterskole or somewhere in Denmark and describe it to create a specific mood or atmosphere. You choose the mood — peaceful, tense, lonely, exciting, nostalgic, whatever fits.

Step 1 — Fill in the template (this is your planning step, not your final answer):

The place was mood adjective.

A sight detail.

A sound detail.

The feeling was emotion because reason.

Step 2 — Write it for real. Use the template ideas but write a flowing paragraph in your own voice. Aim for 80–120 words. Vocabulary Bank ↗

Your paragraph should
• Have a consistent perspective — pick 1st or 3rd person and stick with it
• Create a clear mood through word choice, not by naming it
• Include at least 2 senses (not just sight — add sound, touch, smell)
• Use specific details from a real place you know

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your paragraph from Exercise 4 out loud. Yes, actually out loud — you'll hear awkward phrasing that you won't catch by reading silently. Then check each box honestly.

  • Consistent perspective — Did I stick with one point of view (1st or 3rd) the whole way through?
  • Clear mood/atmosphere — Could a reader name the mood without me telling them directly?
  • Sensory details (2+ senses) — Did I go beyond just sight? Count the senses you used.
  • Specific word choices — No "nice," "good," "really," or "very"? Every word earns its place?
  • Varied sentence structure — Mix of short and long sentences? Read it aloud — does it flow?
  • Reader can feel the mood — Would someone reading this actually feel the atmosphere, not just understand it?
