Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define personification and give one example of it being used to describe the natural world.
2 marks
Answer
Personification is describing something non-human as if it were a person, giving it human qualities [1]. Example: "the angry sea" or "the wind whispered through the trees" or "the branches talked in agony" [1].
Any valid example works. The key: the non-human thing must be given a HUMAN quality (an emotion, action, or ability).

Name the four main purposes of writing.
2 marks
Answer
The four main purposes are: to inform, to argue and persuade, to describe, and to narrate (tell a story) [½ each].
Remember: a single text can have more than one purpose. A travel article might inform AND describe AND persuade.

What do the following Latin-based animal adjectives mean? Feline, canine, aquiline.
2 marks
Answer
Feline = relating to cats [½]. Canine = relating to dogs [½]. Aquiline = relating to eagles; often used to describe a hooked or curved nose [1].
These words come from Latin: felis (cat), canis (dog), aquila (eagle). Knowing the Latin root helps you guess unfamiliar words in the exam.

List three features of informative writing that distinguish it from persuasive writing.
2 marks
Answer
Any three from: informative writing is factual / impersonal and objective / written in third person / uses precise vocabulary / may include data or statistics without bias / does not express personal opinion / sentences are short and clear [1 for first valid point, ½ each for next two].
The core difference: informative = balanced and objective; persuasive = takes a side and tries to convince.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain HOW and WHY, not just name things.
5
Read this sentence: "The river plunges through tight gorges, transforming into angry rapids." Identify one example of personification and explain its effect on the reader.
3 marks
Answer
The word "angry" personifies the rapids by giving them a human emotion [1]. The effect is that the reader feels the river is hostile and threatening, as though it has a will of its own [1]. This creates a sense of danger and power, suggesting that the river is not just flowing but actively aggressive toward anything in its path [1].
One mark for identifying personification, two marks for explaining the effect. "It makes the river sound alive" only gets 1 mark. You need to say WHAT KIND of alive and HOW the reader feels.

6
A news article about flooding uses statistics: "Over one million people will have to move to higher ground." An opinion article about the same topic says: "This devastating project has destroyed countless lives." Compare how each writer uses language differently because of their purpose.
4 marks
Answer
The news article uses a specific statistic ("over one million") because its purpose is to inform. The language is factual and objective — the number gives the reader precise information without telling them how to feel about it [2].

The opinion article uses emotive language ("devastating," "destroyed countless lives") because its purpose is to persuade. "Devastating" is a strong emotional word designed to make the reader feel horror and outrage, while "countless" is deliberately vague to make the problem seem overwhelming [2].
The key insight: same topic, different purpose = different language. Facts inform. Emotive language persuades. The examiner wants you to connect language choices to purpose.

7
You are writing an argumentative essay about whether humans should change the natural environment. Name three persuasive techniques you would use and explain why each one strengthens your argument.
3 marks
Answer
Any three from:
1. Emotive language — using words like "devastation" or "precious" to trigger an emotional response and make the reader care about the issue [1].
2. Counter-arguments — acknowledging the opposing view ("While some argue that...") and then arguing against it, which makes your argument seem fair and well-reasoned [1].
3. Statistics/evidence — using data ("99% of all species that ever lived are now extinct") to support your point with facts, making your argument credible [1].
4. Rhetorical questions — asking "Can we really stand by and watch species disappear?" to engage the reader and make them think [1].
5. Linking phrases — using "Furthermore," "Nevertheless," "In spite of this" to connect ideas logically and guide the reader through your argument [1].
You need to name the technique AND explain why it works. Just listing "rhetorical questions, emotive language, statistics" without explanation gets only half marks.

Advanced — Can You Analyze It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full PEE paragraphs with detailed explanation. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Read the following: "In their fear, despairing flowers nevertheless held bouquets to the grim king; meteors were the tassels of his crown." This is from a poem about a storm. Write a full PEE paragraph analyzing how the writer presents the storm.
5 marks
Answer
P: The writer uses an extended metaphor of a tyrannical king to present the storm as a terrifying, all-powerful force [1]. E: The storm is called "the grim king" with "meteors" as "the tassels of his crown," while flowers hold "bouquets" to him in "fear" and "despair" [1]. E: The metaphor of a "grim king" transforms the storm from a weather event into a cruel ruler who demands submission. The word "grim" suggests something dark, forbidding, and merciless, while "crown" implies absolute authority [1]. The personification of the flowers as "despairing" yet still offering "bouquets" creates a disturbing image of forced tribute — nature itself is terrorized into paying homage to the storm. This creates a sense of helplessness in the reader, as even the natural world cannot resist this force [1]. The meteors as "tassels" elevates the storm to something almost supernatural, suggesting its power reaches beyond Earth into the cosmos itself [1].
Top-band answer: identifies multiple techniques (extended metaphor, personification, imagery), quotes specific words, and explains the effect of individual word choices. The explanation is the longest part of the answer.

9
In Directed Writing (Paper 2), up to 15 marks are for content and up to 25 marks are for quality of writing. Explain what this mark split tells you about how you should prepare your answer. What should you focus on most?
5 marks
Answer
The mark split tells you that how you write matters more than what you write [1]. Quality of writing (25 marks) is worth almost twice as much as content (15 marks), so you should focus most on:

1. Language quality — use varied vocabulary, avoid repetition, choose precise words [1].
2. Sentence structure — vary your sentence lengths and types (simple, compound, complex) [1].
3. Organization — structure your answer with clear paragraphs, each with a topic sentence, linked logically [1].
4. Accuracy — correct spelling, punctuation, and grammar throughout. Careless errors lose marks in the quality section [1].

You should still cover all the content points, but do not sacrifice writing quality to include every possible fact.
Many students focus only on "getting the information in" and ignore how they express it. This is a mistake — the majority of marks are for expression, not content. Write less, but write well.

10
A writer describes a glacier calving: "In the dark of night we are startled awake by a splitting boom, like dynamite being detonated. Great chunks of ice fall from the glacier's side and crash into the lake. Again, and again — all through the night." Analyze how the writer creates tension and awe in this description. Use PEE and comment on at least two language or structural techniques.
6 marks
Answer
Technique 1 — Simile:
P: The writer uses a simile to convey the terrifying power of the glacier [1]. E: The sound is described as "like dynamite being detonated" [1]. E: Comparing a natural event to an explosion suggests extreme violence and danger. "Dynamite" has associations with destruction, warfare, and deliberate force, which makes the reader feel that the glacier is not just breaking apart but actively threatening. The word "detonated" implies something sudden and uncontrollable, heightening the sense of shock [1].

Technique 2 — Repetition and sentence structure:
P: The writer uses repetition and a short, fragmented sentence to create an atmosphere of relentless, exhausting tension [1]. E: "Again, and again — all through the night" [1]. E: The repetition of "again" mimics the repeated crashes of ice, making the reader feel the endless, unrelenting nature of the event. The dash before "all through the night" creates a pause that emphasizes the duration — this is not a single event but hours of noise and fear. The short sentence after the longer descriptive sentences creates a rhythmic contrast that makes the reader feel trapped and overwhelmed, unable to escape the noise [1].
Top marks require two techniques, each with its own PEE. The examiner rewards analysis of specific word choices ("dynamite," "detonated," "again") and structural features (repetition, sentence length, punctuation). Your explanation must connect to the reader's experience.
