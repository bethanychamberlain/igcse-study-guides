Section 1.1 Reading to Identify Information
In most English exams you have to read a passage and answer questions about it under time pressure. The chapter teaches three essential reading strategies that save you time and get you marks: keywording, skimming, and scanning.
W
Write This Down — Keywording

Keywording = finding the key words in a question or task. Before you even look at the passage, underline or circle the important words in the question. This tells you exactly what to look for.

Example: "How does the writer make the setting sound dangerous?" → Key words: how (= what techniques), setting (= descriptions of the place), dangerous (= your focus for evidence).

W
Write This Down — Skimming

Skimming = reading a text quickly to get the general idea of what it is about. Read the title, first and last sentences of each paragraph, and any subheadings. You should skim the passage before you try to answer any questions.

W
Write This Down — Scanning

Scanning = reading to find specific information. Once you know what you are looking for (from keywording), run your eyes over the text looking for that particular word, date, name, or idea. You do NOT re-read the whole passage every time.

?
Why This Matters

In the exam you have limited time. The students who do well are not faster readers — they are smarter readers. The process is: keyword the question → skim the text → scan for specific answers. This three-step process stops you from wasting time reading the whole passage five times.

T
Study Tip — Practice the Process

Every time you read any article (online, in a book, on your phone), practice: first skim it in 30 seconds (what is it about?), then set yourself a question and scan for the answer. Do this with news articles, Wikipedia pages, even game patch notes. The skill transfers directly to exams.

  • I can define keywording, skimming, and scanning
  • I know the order: keyword the question FIRST, then skim, then scan
  • I can keyword a question by underlining the important words
Section 1.2 The SQuEE Technique
The textbook introduces the SQuEE technique for analyzing a writer's style and language choices. This is very similar to PEE paragraphs but with four steps instead of three.
W
Write This Down — SQuEE

S = State the facts or what you believe.
Qu = Quote a relevant word or phrase to support the point.
E = Explain the choice of words.
E = Effect — describe the effect created on the reader.

!
Don't Confuse — SQuEE vs PEE

SQuEE and PEE are very similar frameworks. SQuEE splits the explanation into two parts: explaining the word choice AND describing its effect. PEE combines these into one "Explanation" step. Either framework works in the exam — the key is that you always explain the effect on the reader, not just identify the technique.

?
Why This Matters

The exam gives you a passage and asks something like: "How does the writer make the description effective?" SQuEE gives you a step-by-step formula to answer this. Without a framework, most students just retell the story or define the technique. The framework forces you to explain and analyze.

  • I can name the four steps of SQuEE: State, Quote, Explain, Effect
  • I understand that SQuEE and PEE both require EXPLANATION of effect

THE PEE FRAMEWORK

This is the single most important skill in your English exam. The marks are in the EXPLANATION, not the identification.

PEE = Point, Evidence, Explanation. Every analytical paragraph you write on the exam should follow this structure. Your biggest weakness right now is stopping after the Point — you say what a technique is, but you don't explain how it affects the reader or why the writer used it. That's where the marks are.
P
Point State what the writer does. Name the technique or the choice they made.
"The writer uses sensory imagery to bring the setting to life."
E
Evidence Quote directly from the text. Keep it short — just the relevant words.
"sweet-smelling bread, some sharp, white goat's cheese and a handful of delicious fat, black olives"
E
Explanation Explain the EFFECT on the reader. How does it make them feel? What does it suggest? Why did the writer choose this?
"The use of taste and smell imagery ('sweet-smelling,' 'sharp,' 'delicious') makes the reader feel as if they can experience the food themselves. This creates warmth and comfort, contrasting with the harsh landscape described earlier, and suggests that Asclepius is at peace in this moment."
P
The Golden Rule

Your explanation should be at least twice as long as your Point and Evidence combined. If your explanation is only one sentence, it's not enough. Push yourself: after writing one explanation sentence, ask "So what? What does that make the reader feel?" and write another sentence.

T
Study Tip — Starter Phrases for Explanation

Memorize these and use them as launching pads:

• "This suggests that..."
• "The effect on the reader is..."
• "The word [X] implies..."
• "This creates a sense of..."
• "The reader feels... because..."
• "The writer may have chosen this to..."

Before and After — See the Difference
Weak Response (1-2 marks)

"The writer uses adjectives to describe the food. He says it is 'sweet-smelling' and 'delicious.' This makes the food sound nice."

Problem: This just identifies the technique and summarises. "Makes it sound nice" is not an explanation — it doesn't say HOW or WHY.

Strong PEE Response (4-5 marks)

P: The writer uses sensory language to create a vivid, appealing image of the food. E: The description includes "sweet-smelling bread," "sharp, white goat's cheese" and "delicious fat, black olives." E: The specific sensory details — smell ("sweet-smelling"), taste ("sharp," "delicious") and sight ("white," "black") — allow the reader to experience the meal as if they were there. This creates a moment of warmth and contentment in the middle of a journey, which makes the reader feel the simple pleasure of rest after effort.

Why this works: It names the technique, quotes specific words, then spends MOST of the answer explaining the effect on the reader using multiple senses.

  • I can name the three parts of PEE: Point, Evidence, Explanation
  • I understand that the EXPLANATION is worth the most marks
  • I can write a PEE paragraph about a technique without just defining it
  • I know at least 3 starter phrases for explanations
Section 1.3 Summary Writing Skills
Summary writing is a key exam skill. You will be asked to summarise what a passage says in a limited number of words (usually 100-120). The chapter teaches a clear 7-step process.
W
Write This Down — The 7-Step Summary Process

1. Read the text twice.
2. Read the question and keyword it — underline what you need to find.
3. Find relevant information in the text and underline it. Use different coloured pencils for different parts of the task.
4. Number the separate points you have underlined in order.
5. Make brief notes before you write your summary.
6. Write your summary in your own words — do not copy or "lift" from the text. Use synonyms.
7. Edit and proofread — correct spelling, grammar, and punctuation mistakes.

!
Don't Confuse — Summary vs Analysis

Summary = what the text says. You list the key points in your own words.
Analysis = how the writer says it and why. You discuss techniques and effects.

If the question says "summarise," do NOT start analyzing techniques or giving your opinion. If it says "explain how the writer creates an effect," do NOT just summarise the content. Read the question keyword carefully.

!
Don't Confuse — Own Words vs Copying

Using your own words does NOT mean replacing every simple word with a complicated synonym. A tree is a tree, not "a tall, wooden-stemmed, long-lived plant." Use synonyms only for descriptive or specific phrases. Keep common nouns as they are.

T
Study Tip — The Colour Coding Trick

When a summary task has two parts (e.g. "summarise the ingredients AND the method"), use one colour for each part. This stops you from mixing them up and makes sure you cover both. Works in every subject, not just English.

  • I can list the 7 steps for writing a summary
  • I know the difference between summary and analysis
  • I can write a summary using my own words without adding opinion
Section 1.4 Fact vs Opinion in Travel Writing
Travel writing blends factual information with personal impressions and opinions. The chapter teaches you to distinguish between the two — a key reading comprehension skill.
W
Write This Down

Fact = something that can be verified or proven. "Los Roques is a coral archipelago 150 km off the coast of Venezuela."

Opinion/Impression = the writer's personal view or feeling. "It's a charming place" or "the bread smells very appetizing."

W
Write This Down — Explicit vs Implicit Meaning

Explicit meaning = what is directly stated in the text. You can point to the exact words.

Implicit meaning = what is suggested or hinted at. You have to read between the lines and infer.

Example: If a writer describes a village as having "crumbling walls and empty doorways," the explicit meaning is that the buildings are old and damaged. The implicit meaning might be that the village has been abandoned or is in decline.

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions often ask: "What does the writer imply?" or "What is suggested by...?" These questions test whether you can read beyond the literal words. If you only find explicit information, you'll miss marks on inference questions.

  • I can tell the difference between a fact and an opinion in a passage
  • I understand explicit vs implicit meaning
  • I can infer meaning from a passage (read between the lines)
Section 1.5 The Writer's Craft — Description and Word Choice
Chapter 1 shows how writers make descriptions vivid and effective. You study several travel texts to see how writers use specific word choices, literary devices, and sensory language to bring places to life.
W
Write This Down — Paradox and Oxymoron

Paradox = a statement that appears to contradict itself but contains a truth. Example: "more haste, less speed."

Oxymoron = putting together words that seem to contradict each other. Examples: "bittersweet," "living dead," "organised chaos."

Effect: Both create surprise and force the reader to think more deeply. They suggest complexity — that something can be two contradictory things at the same time.

W
Write This Down — Characteristics of Good Travel Writing

From the textbook's advice on writing travel articles, the best travel writing has:

1. A clear writing style — gets to the point with brevity and clarity
2. A strong sense of the writer's personality — intelligence, wit, style
3. Personal anecdotes and quotations
4. Vivid reporting using the senses
5. High literary quality — accurate grammar and syntax
6. Practical, useful information for the reader

W
Write This Down — Sensory Language
Sense What It Covers Example from Travel Writing
Sight Colour, shape, light, movement "the warm turquoise waters"
Sound Volume, rhythm, tone "the extraordinary dreamlike clamour"
Smell Fragrance, stench, freshness "sweet-smelling bread"
Touch Temperature, texture "the water was refreshingly cold"
Taste Flavour, richness "sharp, white goat's cheese"
P
PEE In Action — Analyzing Word Choice

P: The writer uses carefully chosen sensory details to make the reader feel present in the scene. E: The description includes visual imagery such as "turquoise waters" and tactile details like "refreshingly cold." E: The specific colour "turquoise" creates a vivid, almost photographic image of tropical water, while "refreshingly" conveys not just the temperature but the relief and pleasure of the experience. This combination of senses immerses the reader in the journey, making them feel the appeal of the location rather than simply being told about it.

T
Study Tip — The Five Senses Check

When reading any descriptive passage, make a quick list: which senses does the writer use? If they use three or more, comment on the range of sensory detail. If they focus on just one (e.g. only sight), comment on why that sense dominates — what mood does it create?

  • I can define paradox and oxymoron and explain their effect
  • I can identify which senses a writer appeals to in a passage
  • I know the 6 characteristics of good travel writing
Section 1.6 Writing to Persuade and Describe
Chapter 1 introduces two types of writing you will need for your exams: persuasive writing (holiday brochures, advertisements) and descriptive writing (travel compositions).
W
Write This Down — Persuasive Writing Techniques

Persuasive advertisements combine facts with persuasive details. Key features:

• Short paragraphs — easy to read, punchy
• Subheadings — break up information, let reader scan
• Imperative verbs (commands) — "Visit," "Experience," "Discover"
• Positive adjectives — "stunning," "unforgettable," "pristine"
• Direct address ("you") — makes the reader feel involved
• Rhetorical questions — "Why not treat yourself?"

W
Write This Down — Descriptive Writing Checklist

Before you start a descriptive composition:

• Plan — use a mind map or spidergram before writing
• Use the five senses — don't just describe what things look like
• Be specific — "turquoise waters" beats "nice water"
• Use literary techniques — simile, metaphor, personification
• Vary your sentence length — short for impact, long for flowing description
• Avoid clichés — "bustling markets" and "azure sea" are overused

!
Don't Confuse

Persuasive writing tries to make the reader do something or agree with something. Descriptive writing tries to make the reader experience something. They use overlapping techniques (both use vivid language) but the purpose is different. Always check what the question is asking you to do.

T
Study Tip — Spot the Techniques in Real Life

Look at actual holiday websites or travel ads online. Identify the persuasive techniques: imperative verbs, direct address, positive adjectives, rhetorical questions. This is the same skill the exam tests — just applied to real-world texts.

  • I can list 5 persuasive writing techniques used in advertisements
  • I know to plan my descriptive writing before I start
  • I understand the difference between persuasive and descriptive purposes
