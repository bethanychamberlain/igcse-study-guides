Figurative Language Spotter

About 5 minutes

Each sentence below uses a figurative language device. Label each one by tapping the correct button:

S = Simile (comparison using like/as) M = Metaphor (direct comparison) P = Personification (human qualities given to non-human things) SY = Symbolism (an object represents something bigger)
  • 1 "Time crawled like a wounded animal." ___
  • 2 "The empty chair was a hole in the room." ___
  • 3 "The wind whispered goodbye through the trees." ___
  • 4 "He placed his old jersey in the box — that chapter was closed." ___
  • 5 "Her smile was as sharp as broken glass." ___
  • 6 "The clock on the wall watched them leave, one by one." ___
Quick guide
Simile uses "like" or "as" to compare two things. Metaphor says something is something else. Personification gives human actions or feelings to non-human things. Symbolism uses a concrete object to represent an abstract idea.
2

Flat to Figurative

About 5 minutes

These sentences are flat and boring. Rewrite each one using the figurative language device suggested in the hint.

Flat "I felt sad about leaving."
Try a metaphor — what does sadness feel like physically? What could it be compared to?
Flat "The last day went slowly."
Try a simile or personification — what does time do when it slows down? What does it move like?
Flat "The school looked different on the last day."
Try symbolism or personification — what details looked different? What could they represent?
Key principle
Good figurative language doesn't just decorate a sentence — it makes the reader feel what you felt. "I felt sad" tells the reader nothing new. "Leaving was a stone in my chest" makes them feel the weight.
3

Story Arc Builder

About 5 minutes

Most stories follow Freytag's pyramid — five stages that build tension and then resolve it. Think about your year at efterskole as a story and fill in each stage.

Freytag's Pyramid
Exposition (setup) → Rising Action (tension builds) → Climax (the peak moment) → Falling Action (things settle) → Resolution (how it ends)
1 Exposition — How it started
Your arrival, first impressions, what the place felt like at the beginning.
2 Rising Action — What happened
Challenges, friendships forming, things that built up over the year.
3 Climax — The most important moment
The peak — the most intense, meaningful, or turning-point moment of the year.
4 Falling Action — How things settled
What happened after that peak moment? How did the feeling or situation change?
5 Resolution — How it ends
How the story wraps up. What changed in you? What does the ending feel like?
Why this matters
In Exercise 4 you'll write the ending of this story — the resolution. Having the full arc mapped out helps you write an ending that feels earned, not just stuck on. A good ending connects back to the beginning.

Your Ending Paragraph

About 15 minutes

Step 1 — Fill in the template (planning step, not your final answer):

It was time of day / season.

A sensory detail about the place.

I was what you were doing.

A figurative language moment — simile, metaphor, or symbol.

Your final feeling or realisation.

What makes a satisfying ending
• Don't just stop — "and then it was over" is not an ending
• Echo the beginning — refer back to something from your exposition
• Show change — the character (you) should be different from who arrived
• Use a concrete image — end on something the reader can see, not an abstract statement
• Leave some space — the best endings let the reader feel something, not just understand something

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your ending from Exercise 4 out loud. Does it feel like a real ending — or does it just stop? Then check each box honestly.

  • Figurative language — Did I include at least one simile or metaphor?
  • Sensory details — Did I use at least 2 senses (sight, sound, touch, smell, taste)?
  • Satisfying ending — Does it feel complete, not just "and then it was over"?
  • Natural language — Does the figurative language feel natural, not forced or clichéd?
  • Change and growth — Can the reader tell I'm different from who I was at the start?
  • Varied sentences — Did I mix short and long sentences for rhythm and effect?
