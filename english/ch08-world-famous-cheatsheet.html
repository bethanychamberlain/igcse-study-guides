Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Biography Someone ELSE writes about a person's life. Third person (he/she/they). Can be objective or biased. Autobiography (that's the person writing about themselves)
Autobiography A person writes about their OWN life. First person (I/we). Intimate but may be self-serving. Memoir (similar, but memoir focuses on specific moments, not a whole life)
Tone The writer's attitude toward their subject — admiring, critical, sarcastic, nostalgic, etc. Mood (mood = feeling created in the READER; tone = writer's attitude)
Synthesis Combining information from two or more sources into one seamless response. Summary (that's ONE text shortened). Also not comparison (that highlights differences).
Semi-colon Joins two closely related sentences without a conjunction: "Fame brings opportunity; it also brings scrutiny." Colon (a colon introduces a list or explanation; a semi-colon links equals)
Sensory imagery Language that appeals to the five senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste). Figurative language (imagery uses the senses; figurative language includes metaphor, simile, etc.)
Register The level of formality. Formal = no contractions, complex sentences. Informal = conversational. Tone (register = formality level; tone = writer's attitude)
Reverential Showing deep admiration and respect. A reverential tone treats the subject as heroic or inspiring. Nostalgic (nostalgic = fondly remembering the past; reverential = showing deep respect)

Biography vs Autobiography

Biography Autobiography
Who writes it Someone else The person themselves
Point of view Third person (he/she) First person (I/we)
Strengths Can be more objective; includes outside perspectives Intimate; shares inner thoughts and feelings
Weaknesses Biographer may have own agenda (admiration or dislike) Subject controls the narrative; may hide flaws
Reliability NOT automatically more truthful NOT automatically less truthful
Exam Trap
Don't assume biography = objective and autobiography = biased. BOTH can be biased. The exam rewards you for evaluating reliability, not assuming it.

Fiction vs Non-Fiction Description

Fiction Non-Fiction
Purpose Create atmosphere, build character Help reader picture a real place/person accurately
Techniques Metaphor, simile, personification, symbolism Sensory detail, precise vocabulary, factual imagery
Freedom Can invent and exaggerate Must stay truthful

Summary vs Comparison vs Synthesis

Summary Comparison Synthesis
How many texts ONE TWO TWO+
What you do Shorten to key points Show similarities and differences Blend into one seamless response
Organise by Order of the text Feature (tone, language, purpose) Topic (not by source)
Exam Trap — Synthesis is NOT Two Summaries
Don't write "Text A says... Text B says..." Organise by TOPIC, not by text. Weave information from both sources together so it reads as one unified answer.

Say It in One Sentence

Biography Someone else tells a person's story in third person — can praise, criticise, or both.
Autobiography You tell your own story in first person — intimate but you control the narrative.
Bio structure Hook, background, rise, complications, legacy — it's storytelling about a real person, not a CV.
5 senses Sight, sound, smell, touch, taste — strong description uses at least 3.
Tone vs mood Tone = writer's attitude. Mood = what the reader feels. A sarcastic TONE creates an uncomfortable MOOD.
Article opening Never "In this article I will..." — start with a question, bold statement, or surprising fact.
Semi-colon Joins two related sentences: "Fame brings opportunity; it also brings scrutiny." Free marks for accuracy.

Memory Tricks

PEE Paragraph Template
P = make your POINT (name the technique). E = give EVIDENCE (short quote). E = EXPLAIN the effect (this is where ALL the marks are — make it the longest part). Your explanation should be at least twice as long as point + evidence combined.
The 5 Senses Checklist
Before writing any description, jot down: S-S-S-T-T (Sight, Sound, Smell, Touch, Taste). Tick off at least 3 before you start writing. Smell is the most powerful for evoking memory — use it.
Tone Vocabulary — Go Beyond "Positive/Negative"
Positive: admiring, reverential, celebratory, enthusiastic, affectionate, nostalgic.
Negative: critical, dismissive, sarcastic, contemptuous, cynical, mocking.
Complex: bittersweet, ambivalent, ironic, wistful.
Using precise tone words = higher marks. "The tone is admiring" beats "The tone is positive."
How to Identify Tone — 4 Questions
1. Are the word choices positive, negative, or neutral?
2. Is there humour, exaggeration, or understatement?
3. Is the writer personally involved or distant?
4. How would this sound read aloud? What voice would you use?
Article Opening Hack
Start with a rhetorical question or a bold statistic. "What would you sacrifice for a million followers?" is always better than "In this article I am going to write about fame."
Sentence Starters for Analysis
"The writer uses [technique] to convey [idea]..." / "The word '[X]' suggests/implies/connotes..." / "This creates a sense of [feeling] in the reader because..." / "The writer's [reverential/critical] tone reveals their attitude toward..."

PEE Paragraph Templates — Use These in Every Answer

Your #1 Weakness (Easter Test Result)
You scored 100% on factual/recognition questions but only 46% on analytical writing. The gap is HERE — in PEE paragraphs. These templates close the gap.
Template 1 — Analysing Biographical Writing
P: The [biographer/autobiographer] presents [subject] as [heroic / flawed / complex].
E: For example, "[short quote]"
E: The phrase "[zoom in]" suggests [meaning]. The word "[specific word]" connotes [association], which makes the reader feel [admiration / sympathy / unease]. The writer's choice of [positive/negative] language reveals a [reverential / critical / ambivalent] attitude toward the subject.
Template 2 — Analysing Description
P: The writer uses sensory imagery to [immerse the reader / create atmosphere / convey mood].
E: For example, "[short quote appealing to senses]"
E: The appeal to [smell / touch / sound] makes the reader physically react, as though they are present in the scene. The [simile / metaphor] "[specific words]" suggests [meaning], creating a mood of [feeling]. The overall effect is to make the reader feel [emotion / connection / unease].
Template 3 — Comparing Tones
P: The two writers adopt very different tones toward [topic].
E: Text A uses [language / technique + quote], while Text B uses [language / technique + quote].
E: Text A's [word choice] creates a [admiring / critical] tone, making the reader feel [effect]. In contrast, Text B's [word choice] creates a [different tone], which [different effect]. This reveals that Text A's writer [attitude] while Text B's writer [attitude].

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How does the writer present [person]?" Identify if it's biography or autobiography. Comment on tone (admiring? critical?). Find language choices that reveal the writer's attitude. Use PEE.
"Write a biographical article about..." Headline + hook, then: early life, key achievements, challenges, character traits, impact, closing. Use quotes. It's storytelling, not a list of facts.
"How does the writer use language to describe...?" Find sensory imagery (3+ senses), figurative language (simile, metaphor, personification). Quote short, zoom in on ONE word, explain the mood it creates.
"Compare how the writers convey their attitudes" Identify each writer's tone using precise vocabulary (not just "positive/negative"). Compare by feature. Weave both texts together. Explain HOW language creates tone.
"Using information from both texts, write..." This is synthesis. Organise by TOPIC, not by text. Blend information smoothly. Don't do "Text A says... Text B says..."
"Write an article for a school magazine" Headline, engaging hook (question or bold statement), body paragraphs with evidence, semi-formal register, strong closing line. Never start with "In this article I will..."
"What impression does the reader get of...?" This asks for the overall effect. State the impression, quote evidence, then explain HOW the language creates that impression. Zoom in on word choices.
"Use a semi-colon correctly" Two related sentences, no conjunction: "She had everything; she had nothing." This shows technical accuracy = higher Writing marks.
