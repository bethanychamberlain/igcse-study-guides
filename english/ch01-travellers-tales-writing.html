2

Flat to Vivid

About 5 minutes

These sentences are flat and boring. A travel writer would never write them. Rewrite each one using specific, vivid details — use memories from your trip if you can. Stuck for words? Open the Vocabulary Bank ↗

Flat "The food at the market was good."
Think: What food? What did it look/smell/taste like? Be specific.
Flat "The building was really big and impressive."
Think: How did it make you feel looking up at it? What details caught your eye — the stone, the windows, the height?
Flat "It was hot and we walked around the city all day."
Think: What did the heat feel like on your skin? What sounds surrounded you? What did you notice in the streets?
Remember from Chapter 1
Good travel writing uses specific details, not vague words. "Turquoise waters" is better than "nice water." "Sharp, white goat's cheese" is better than "good cheese." The more specific you are, the more the reader can picture it.
3

Fact or Feeling?

About 5 minutes

Label each statement F (fact — can be verified) or O (opinion — personal feeling or judgment).

  • 1 "Barcelona is located on the northeast coast of Spain." ___
  • 2 "The Sagrada Familia was the most incredible building I have ever seen." ___
  • 3 "Montserrat is a mountain range about 50 km northwest of Barcelona." ___
  • 4 "The chocolate workshop was the best activity on the whole trip." ___
  • 5 "The Cougnac caves contain prehistoric paintings that are over 25,000 years old." ___
  • 6 "French bread tastes so much better than anything you can get in Denmark." ___
  • 7 "The Gothic Quarter in Barcelona has streets dating back to Roman times." ___
  • 8 "Walking through the old streets felt like stepping back in time." ___
Now connect it
Look back at your Senses Grid from Exercise 1. Most of your sensory details are probably opinions (personal impressions). That's normal — travel writing blends facts with feelings. The skill is knowing which is which and using both deliberately.
4

Your Travel Paragraph

About 15 minutes

Now bring it all together. You're going to write a descriptive paragraph about the moment you chose in Exercise 1.

Step 1 — Fill in the template (this is your planning step, not your final answer):

I was in place on a day / time of day.

We were what you were doing.

The first thing I noticed was a sight detail from your grid.

I could hear a sound detail and smell a smell detail.

It felt a touch detail — temperature, texture.

This moment made me feel an emotion because why.

Step 2 — Write it for real. Use the template ideas but write a flowing paragraph in your own voice. Aim for 80–120 words. Vocabulary Bank ↗

Your paragraph should
• Use at least 3 senses from your grid
• Include at least one specific detail (a name, colour, temperature, flavour)
• Have varied sentence lengths — try one short punchy sentence for impact
• End with how the moment made you feel (your personal impression)

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your paragraph from Exercise 4 out loud. Yes, actually out loud — you'll hear awkward phrasing that you won't catch by reading silently. Then check each box honestly.

These are the 6 characteristics of good travel writing from Chapter 1:

  • Clear writing — Could someone who wasn't on the trip follow what you're describing?
  • Your personality — Does it sound like YOU wrote it, not a textbook? Can the reader sense your reaction?
  • At least 3 senses — Count them. Did you go beyond just what things looked like?
  • Specific details — No "nice," "good," "really big," or "amazing." Every adjective should paint a picture.
  • Varied sentences — Do you have at least one short sentence and one longer one? Read it aloud — does it flow?
  • No clichés — No "bustling market," "breathtaking view," "time of my life," or "words can't describe." Find YOUR words.
