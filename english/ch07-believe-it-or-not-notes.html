Section 7.1 Evaluating Sources and Spotting Bias
Not everything you read is true. This skill is about how to evaluate whether a text is trustworthy — and it applies to both exam passages and real life.
W
Write This Down — 8 Steps to Spot Fake News (IFLA Framework)

Use these checks on any text, article, or source:

1. Consider the source — Who published it? Is it a known, reputable outlet?
2. Read beyond the headline — Headlines are designed to grab attention; the article may say something different
3. Check the author — Are they real? Do they have credentials on this topic?
4. Look at supporting sources — Does the article cite evidence? Can you verify those sources?
5. Check the date — Is this current or is old news being recycled?
6. Is it a joke? — Satire sites exist. Check if the source is meant to be humorous.
7. Check your own biases — Are you believing it because you want it to be true?
8. Ask an expert — Consult a librarian, teacher, or fact-checking website

W
Write This Down — What "Significant Absence" Means

A significant absence is when a text deliberately leaves something out. What a writer doesn't say can be just as important as what they do say.

Example: A news report about a protest mentions "angry crowds" but never mentions why they are protesting. The absence of the cause shapes how the reader perceives the protesters — they seem irrational rather than justified.

?
Why This Matters

Paper 2 (Directed Writing) may ask you to write a persuasive article or letter where you need to show awareness of bias. Paper 1 may give you a passage where the writer is not neutral and ask you to identify how they try to influence the reader. Being able to spot bias = higher marks.

T
Study Tip — Practise on Real News

Pick any news headline today. Apply the 8 steps. Ask: Who benefits from this story? What might be missing? This is faster and more useful than just memorising the list.

  • I can list at least 5 of the 8 steps for evaluating sources
  • I can explain what a "significant absence" is and give an example
  • I understand why spotting bias matters for the exam
Section 7.2 Colloquial Language and Cliches
Writers make deliberate choices about the register of their language — formal, informal, or somewhere in between. Spotting these choices and explaining their effect is an exam skill.
W
Write This Down

Colloquial language = informal, everyday speech. Slang, contractions ("don't," "gonna"), and casual expressions.

Effect: Makes the writing feel personal and relatable. Creates a sense of direct conversation with the reader. Often used in articles and opinion pieces to build rapport.

W
Write This Down

Cliche = an overused expression that has lost its impact. "At the end of the day," "the tip of the iceberg," "easier said than done."

In the exam: You might be asked to identify cliches and explain why they are weak. A cliche shows lazy writing — the writer is not thinking originally. Strong writers avoid them or use them deliberately for humour.

!
Don't Confuse

Colloquial language is not always bad. It depends on purpose. In a newspaper feature trying to connect with young readers, colloquial language is effective. In a formal report, it would be inappropriate. The exam asks you to judge whether the writer's choice of register suits their audience and purpose.

  • I can define colloquial language and explain its effect on the reader
  • I can identify a cliche and explain why it weakens writing
Section 7.3 News Writing: Reports, Articles, and Features
Paper 2 may ask you to write a news report or article. Knowing the structure and conventions of each type is essential.
W
Write This Down — Three Types of News Writing
Type Purpose Key Features
News report Inform — tell the reader what happened Factual, third person, past tense, inverted pyramid (most important info first), quotes from witnesses/experts
Article Inform + engage — present information in an interesting way Can be first or third person, uses subheadings, may include opinion, more descriptive than a report
Feature Entertain + explore — go deeper on a topic Longer, uses anecdotes, human interest, descriptive language, may use colloquial language, often a "hook" opening
W
Write This Down — News Report Structure

When the exam asks you to write a news report, use this structure:

1. Headline — short, punchy, present tense ("Fire Destroys Local School")
2. Byline — your name and date ("By [Name], 8 March 2026")
3. Opening paragraph (the hook) — answers WHO, WHAT, WHERE, WHEN in the first 2-3 sentences
4. Body paragraphs — more detail, quotes from witnesses/officials, background information
5. Closing — what happens next, or a final quote

W
Write This Down — Puns in Headlines

Newspaper headlines often use puns (wordplay with double meanings) to grab attention.

Example: "BAKERS IN A JAM" (article about a bakery losing its fruit supply). "Jam" = both a fruit preserve and a difficult situation.

Effect: Puns make headlines memorable and eye-catching. They create humour and make the reader want to find out more.

?
Why This Matters

Paper 2, Question 1 (Directed Writing) often asks you to write a news report, letter, or article based on a passage. You must use the correct format and conventions for the text type — not just dump information. Using a headline, byline, and quotes shows the examiner you know the form.

T
Study Tip — Learn the WHO-WHAT-WHERE-WHEN Formula

Before writing any news report opening, jot down 4 words: WHO, WHAT, WHERE, WHEN. Make sure your first paragraph answers all four. If it doesn't, rewrite it until it does.

  • I can name the 3 types of news writing and explain how they differ
  • I can structure a news report with headline, byline, hook, body, and close
  • I can explain what a pun is and why headlines use them
Section 7.4 Discursive Writing
Discursive writing examines both sides of an argument before reaching a conclusion. This is a key Paper 2 skill.
W
Write This Down — Discursive Writing Structure

1. Introduction — state the topic and explain that there are different viewpoints
2. Arguments FOR — 2-3 paragraphs, each with a clear point + evidence
3. Arguments AGAINST — 2-3 paragraphs, each with a clear point + evidence
4. Conclusion — weigh up both sides and give your own balanced opinion

!
Don't Confuse — Discursive vs Persuasive

Discursive = balanced, explores BOTH sides, then reaches a conclusion.
Persuasive = one-sided, tries to convince the reader of YOUR viewpoint.

If the exam says "discuss," you must show both sides. If it says "argue" or "persuade," you pick a side. Getting this wrong costs you marks on structure.

W
Write This Down — Useful Connectives

Introducing arguments: Firstly, One argument is that, It could be argued that, Supporters claim that
Counter-arguments: However, On the other hand, Conversely, Critics argue that, Nevertheless
Concluding: On balance, Having considered both sides, Ultimately, Taking everything into account

P
PEE in Discursive Writing

Each paragraph in a discursive essay should still follow PEE:

P: "One argument in favour of school uniforms is that they reduce peer pressure."
E: "Studies have shown that students in uniform schools report less bullying based on appearance."
E: "This suggests that uniforms create a more equal environment, which allows students to focus on learning rather than worrying about fashion. This is particularly important for students from lower-income families who might otherwise feel excluded."

  • I can structure a discursive essay with intro, for, against, and conclusion
  • I know the difference between discursive and persuasive writing
  • I can use connectives to signal arguments and counter-arguments
Section 7.5 Emotive Language and Speech in Writing
Writers use emotive language to manipulate how the reader feels. They also choose between direct and indirect speech to control the impact of what people say.
W
Write This Down — Emotive Language

Emotive language = words chosen specifically to provoke an emotional response.

Example: "Defenceless animals are being slaughtered" vs "Animals are being killed." Both describe the same event, but "defenceless" and "slaughtered" make the reader feel outrage and sympathy.

Effect: Emotive language is a tool of persuasion. It bypasses logic and appeals directly to the reader's feelings. It is especially common in news features and opinion pieces.

W
Write This Down — Direct vs Indirect Speech
Type What It Looks Like Effect
Direct speech "I was terrified," said the witness. Feels immediate and real. The reader hears the person's own words. Creates drama and authenticity.
Indirect speech The witness said that she had been terrified. Feels more distant and formal. The reporter is filtering the information. Can feel more objective.
P
PEE In Action — Analysing Emotive Language

P: The writer uses emotive language to create sympathy for the victims. E: The phrase "innocent children torn from their families" E: The word "innocent" emphasises that the children are blameless, which intensifies the reader's sense of injustice. "Torn" is a violent verb that suggests force and pain, making the reader feel the cruelty of the separation. The writer's choice of these words is designed to provoke outrage rather than simply inform.

T
Study Tip — Swap the Words

When you spot emotive language, mentally replace it with a neutral word. "Slaughtered" becomes "killed." "Defenceless" becomes "young." Then ask: what is lost? The difference between the neutral and emotive version IS the effect. That's what you write about in the exam.

  • I can define emotive language and explain its persuasive effect
  • I can tell the difference between direct and indirect speech
  • I can analyse emotive language using PEE
Section 7.6 Comparing Writer Styles Across Texts
The exam may give you two texts on a similar topic and ask you to compare how the writers present their ideas differently. This is a high-mark skill.
W
Write This Down — What to Compare

When comparing two writers, look at these areas:

• Purpose — Are they both trying to inform? Or is one persuading while the other reports?
• Tone — Serious vs humorous? Formal vs informal? Angry vs calm?
• Language choices — Emotive vs neutral? Simple vs complex? Colloquial vs formal?
• Structure — Short punchy paragraphs vs long flowing ones? Does one use quotes more?
• Viewpoint — First person vs third person? Objective vs subjective?

!
Don't Confuse — Content vs Style

Comparing texts is NOT about what each text says — it's about how each writer says it. Don't summarise the content of each text. Instead, compare the techniques, tone, and language choices. The question is always "how does the writer..." not "what does the writer say."

Before and After — Comparison Writing
Weak Comparison (2-3 marks)

"Text A talks about how fake news is dangerous. Text B talks about how people should check their sources. Both texts are about fake news."

Problem: This just summarises content. It doesn't compare HOW the writers present their ideas.

Strong Comparison (5-6 marks)

P: Both writers address fake news, but they adopt very different tones. E: Text A uses emotive language such as "dangerous epidemic of lies," while Text B takes a measured, advisory approach with phrases like "it is worth considering." E: Text A's dramatic language creates urgency and alarm, making the reader feel the problem is immediate and severe. In contrast, Text B's calm register encourages rational reflection, treating the reader as capable of making their own judgements. This difference in tone reflects their different purposes: Text A wants to provoke action, while Text B wants to educate.

Why this works: It compares HOW (tone, language choices), not just WHAT. It weaves both texts together in one paragraph. It explains the EFFECT on the reader.

  • I know the 5 areas to compare when analysing two writers
  • I understand that comparison is about HOW, not WHAT
  • I can write a comparison paragraph that weaves both texts together
Section 7.7 Narrative Voice: Show vs Tell
Chapter 7 includes narrative prose and the concept of the omniscient narrator. Understanding "show vs tell" is critical for both analysing fiction AND for your own creative writing on Paper 2.
W
Write This Down

Show, don't tell is the golden rule of narrative writing.

Telling: "She was scared." (This states the emotion directly.)
Showing: "Her hands trembled as she pressed herself against the wall, her breath coming in short, sharp gasps." (This describes physical signs so the reader feels the fear.)

Effect: "Showing" is always stronger than "telling" because it immerses the reader in the experience. The reader works out the emotion themselves, which makes it more powerful.

W
Write This Down — Omniscient Narrator and Juxtaposition

An omniscient narrator can see into every character's mind. One powerful technique available to this narrator is juxtaposition — placing two contrasting ideas, images, or characters side by side.

Example: "While the king feasted in his golden hall, the children outside huddled in the cold, their stomachs empty."

Effect: Juxtaposition highlights inequality, contrast, or irony. Here, placing wealth next to poverty makes both more vivid and creates moral outrage in the reader.

P
PEE In Action — Narrative Voice Analysis

P: The writer uses an omniscient narrator to create dramatic irony. E: We are told that "she smiled, unaware that the letter in her pocket would change everything." E: The narrator reveals information the character doesn't know, which creates tension for the reader. We want to warn her but can't, which generates a feeling of helplessness and anticipation. This technique is only possible because the narrator can see beyond what the character sees.

T
Study Tip — Use "Show Don't Tell" in Your Own Writing

On Paper 2 (composition), if you write a narrative, you will score higher marks for "showing." Practise by taking any emotion (anger, joy, fear) and writing 2-3 sentences that convey it through actions, body language, and sensory detail WITHOUT naming the emotion.

  • I can explain the difference between showing and telling
  • I can define juxtaposition and explain its effect
  • I can write a PEE paragraph about narrative voice
  • I can use "show don't tell" in my own creative writing

PEE REMINDER FOR CHAPTER 7

This chapter covers many text types (news, discursive, narrative). For EVERY type, the exam asks HOW the writer creates effects. PEE works for all of them.

P
Point State what the writer does. Name the technique or choice.
"The news report uses direct speech to create authenticity."
E
Evidence Quote the text. Keep it short and relevant.
"'I could not believe my eyes,' said local resident Maria Santos."
E
Explanation Explain the EFFECT. How does it make the reader feel? Why this choice?
"The direct quote makes the report feel more trustworthy and immediate, as the reader hears the witness's own words rather than a journalist's summary. The phrase 'could not believe' conveys shock, which draws the reader into the drama of the event."
