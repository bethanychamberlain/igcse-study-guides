Literary Techniques — Quick Reference

Technique What It Means Example Effect on Reader
Simile Comparison using "like" or "as" "The river flowed like liquid silver" Creates a vivid image; helps reader visualise by connecting to something familiar
Metaphor Saying something IS something else (no like/as) "The classroom was a prison" Stronger than simile; states comparison as fact, making the feeling more intense
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things "The trees groaned in the wind" Makes the scene feel alive; can create eerie or emotional atmosphere
Imagery Language appealing to the 5 senses "The air was thick with damp earth and rotting leaves" Immerses the reader in the scene; makes it feel real and physical
Repetition Using the same word or phrase multiple times "Dark. Everything was dark." Emphasises an idea; can feel obsessive, overwhelming, or inescapable
Short sentences Brief, punchy statements "He ran. He didn't look back." Creates tension, urgency, or shock; forces the reader to pause
Rhetorical question Question not expecting an answer "Was there no escape?" Engages the reader; creates reflection or a sense of desperation
Ellipsis (...) Three dots showing something left unsaid "She opened the door and..." Creates suspense; suggests something too awful or important to say directly

PEE Paragraph Template

Fill this in for every analytical paragraph you write. If you follow this pattern, you will score higher. Every. Single. Time.

P (Point): The writer uses technique name to create a sense of mood/feeling.

E (Evidence): This is shown in the phrase "quote from the text".

E (Explanation): The word "specific word" suggests what it implies, which makes the reader feel emotion/reaction. The writer may have chosen this to purpose/intention.

Remember
Your Explanation should be at least TWICE as long as your Point and Evidence combined. The marks are in the Explanation. If your explanation is only one sentence, you haven't said enough.
Common Mistake
Do NOT write: "The writer uses a simile. A simile is when you compare two things using like or as." That's a definition, not an analysis. The examiner already knows what a simile is. Tell them what THIS simile does to THIS reader in THIS text.

Useful Analysis Phrases

Stick these into your explanation sentences. They show the examiner you're analyzing, not just describing.

Phrase When to Use It
"This suggests that..." After quoting a word or phrase, to explain what it implies beyond its surface meaning
"The writer creates a sense of..." To name the mood or atmosphere the writing produces
"The effect on the reader is..." To directly state how the reader is meant to react or feel
"The word [X] implies..." To zoom in on one specific word and explain its connotations
"The reader feels... because..." To connect the technique to a specific reader emotion and explain why
"The writer may have chosen this to..." To speculate on the writer's intention or purpose
"The connotations of [X] are..." To explore the associations a word carries beyond its literal meaning
"This contrasts with... which highlights..." When two ideas or images are placed against each other for effect
Memory Trick
Think of it as S.E.F. — Suggests, Effect, Feels. Every explanation sentence should do one of these: say what the word suggests, describe the effect, or name what the reader feels.

Quick-Fire: If the Question Asks... Then Your Answer Should...

If the Question Says... Your Answer Should...
"How does the writer create a sense of [mood]?" Name techniques + quote evidence + explain how they produce that specific mood. Use PEE. At least 2 techniques.
"What is the effect of [technique/word]?" Do NOT define the technique. Explain what it makes the reader feel and why. Focus entirely on effect.
"How does the writer present [character]?" Look at dialogue, actions, descriptions, others' reactions. Quote specific words and explain what they suggest about the character.
"Compare how the two texts..." Weave texts together. For each point: analyze Text A with PEE, link with a connective, analyze Text B with PEE. Never treat them separately.
"Summarise the main ideas..." Key points only. Own words. No analysis, no opinion. One point per sentence. Be concise.
"What does the word [X] suggest?" Explore connotations. What does the word make you think of? What feeling does it carry? Go beyond the dictionary definition.
"From which point of view is this written? What is the effect?" Name it (first person / third limited / omniscient) AND explain what it allows or restricts. Does it create intimacy? Suspense? A wider view?

Comparison Connectives

Use these to weave two texts together in comparison answers.

Purpose Connectives to Use
Similarity Similarly, Likewise, Both texts, In the same way, This is echoed in, Just as Text A... so too does Text B...
Difference In contrast, However, Whereas, On the other hand, Unlike, While Text A... Text B...
Building on Furthermore, Moreover, Text B takes this further by, This is developed in
Structure Trick
Every comparison paragraph should mention both texts. If you write a paragraph that only discusses one text, you've lost marks. Use the connectives above to force yourself to link them.
