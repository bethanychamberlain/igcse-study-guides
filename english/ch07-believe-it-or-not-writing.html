Bias Detector

About 5 minutes

All six sentences below describe the same event: a school cancelling a trip. Some are neutral and balanced. Some are biased — they use loaded words to push you toward a feeling. Label each one:

N = Neutral / balanced B = Biased / loaded language
  • 1 "The school cancelled the annual trip due to budget cuts." ___
  • 2 "The school cruelly ripped away students' only fun trip of the year." ___
  • 3 "A spokesperson confirmed that the trip would not take place this term." ___
  • 4 "Outraged students slammed the school's ridiculous decision to axe the beloved trip." ___
  • 5 "The trip was cancelled after the school reviewed its spending priorities." ___
  • 6 "The school wisely saved thousands by scrapping a pointless trip nobody needed." ___
How to spot bias
Look for loaded words — words designed to make you feel something. "Cruelly ripped away" pushes anger. "Wisely saved" pushes approval. Neutral writing lets the reader decide how to feel. Biased writing decides for you.
2

Rewrite the Bias

About 5 minutes

Each sentence below is biased. Rewrite it so that it is neutral and balanced — report the facts without pushing an opinion.

Biased "The principal's absurd new phone ban has left students furious and helpless."
Hint: "absurd", "furious", and "helpless" are loaded words. What actually happened? A new rule was introduced.
Biased "The school shamefully replaced real food with disgusting, cheap cafeteria slop."
Hint: "shamefully", "disgusting", and "slop" are loaded. Stick to what changed: the menu was updated.
Biased "Desperate teachers forced exhausted students to sit through a mind-numbing two-hour assembly."
Hint: "desperate", "forced", "exhausted", and "mind-numbing" are all loaded. Just report what happened: an assembly was held.
Neutral writing rule
Replace opinion words with fact words. Instead of "absurd ban", write "new policy". Instead of "disgusting food", write "new menu options". A neutral sentence answers what happened without telling the reader how to feel about it.
3

Inverted Pyramid

About 5 minutes
The inverted pyramid is how journalists structure news stories. The most important information goes first. Details and background come later. Why? Because readers often stop reading partway through — so put the key facts up top.

1. Lead — WHO, WHAT, WHEN, WHERE (the headline facts)
2. Details — HOW, more specifics, quotes
3. Background — context, history, less essential info

Here are 5 pieces of information about an event at an efterskole. Number them 1 to 5 in order of importance for a news story (1 = most important, goes first).

  • ___ A. The talent show was organised by a group of Year 10 students as a fundraiser.
  • ___ B. Greenfield Efterskole held its first-ever talent show on Friday evening, with over 150 people attending.
  • ___ C. The winning act was a comedy skit about daily life at the school, performed by three students from the music line.
  • ___ D. The school has been running for 12 years but had never hosted a student-led event of this size before.
  • ___ E. The event raised 4,200 DKK, which will go toward new equipment for the common room.
Think like a journalist
Ask yourself: if a reader only reads one sentence, which fact is most essential? That goes first (1). Background information that gives context but isn't urgent goes last (5). The lead should answer: who did what, where, and when?

Your News Report

About 15 minutes

Write a short news report about something from your life at the efterskole — real or made up. Use the inverted pyramid: most important facts first. Vocabulary Bank ↗

Pick your event:

A school event or assembly A sports match or competition A new school rule A trip or excursion A visiting speaker or guest Something funny that happened

Step 1 — Plan your 5 Ws:

WHO: who was involved?

WHAT: what happened?

WHEN: when did it happen?

WHERE: where did it take place?

WHY: why did it happen?

Step 2 — Write your news report. Aim for 80–120 words. Remember:

  • Use the inverted pyramid — most important facts in the first 1–2 sentences
  • Keep a neutral tone — no opinion words, no loaded language
  • Answer WHO, WHAT, WHEN, WHERE in your opening sentences
  • No personal opinions ("I think...", "It was amazing...")
  • Use specific facts and details (numbers, names, places)

Reporter's voice
Write as if you're a journalist, not a student. Don't say "I went to..." — say "Students attended..." or "The event took place at..." Third person, past tense, facts only. If you catch yourself writing "amazing", "terrible", or "awesome" — delete it.

Writer's Checklist

About 5 minutes

Read your news report from Exercise 4 out loud. Check each box honestly — if you can't tick it, go back and fix your report.

  • Inverted pyramid — The most important information comes first, background last.
  • Neutral tone — No loaded words, no opinion language, no bias.
  • 5 Ws in the lead — WHO, WHAT, WHEN, and WHERE are answered in the first 2 sentences.
  • No personal opinions — No "I think", "It was great", or "Everyone loved it".
  • Specific facts and details — Includes real numbers, names, or places (not just vague statements).
  • Clear and concise — Every sentence adds information. No waffle or filler.
