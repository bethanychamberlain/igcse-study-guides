Section 2.1 Meet the Elements
In Chapter 1, you learned that everything is made of particles. Now we zoom in: the simplest type of particle is the atom, and a substance made of only one kind of atom is called an element. This section also introduces the Periodic Table, compounds, and mixtures.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Atom: the smallest particle of matter that cannot be broken down further by chemical means.
• Element: a substance that contains only one kind of atom. Examples: sodium (only sodium atoms), carbon (only carbon atoms), sulfur (only sulfur atoms).
• Compound: a substance made of atoms of different elements bonded together. Example: water (H₂O) is hydrogen and oxygen atoms bonded together. A chemical reaction is needed to break them apart.
• Mixture: contains different elements or compounds that are not bonded together. You can usually separate them quite easily (e.g. filtering, distillation).

W
Write This Down — The Periodic Table

The Periodic Table arranges all 118 known elements to show their patterns. Key features:

• Each element has a symbol (Na for sodium, C for carbon, S for sulfur). Some symbols come from Latin names (Na = natrium).
• Columns are called groups (numbered I to VIII). Elements in the same group have similar properties.
• Rows are called periods (numbered 1 to 7).
• A zig-zag line separates metals (left side) from non-metals (right side), except hydrogen which is a non-metal despite being on the left.
• About 94 elements occur naturally; the rest are synthetic (made in labs).

!
Don't Confuse — Compound vs Mixture

This is a classic exam trap. A compound has atoms chemically bonded — you need a chemical reaction to separate them. A mixture has substances just mixed together — you can separate them by physical methods like filtering or evaporation. Water (H₂O) is a compound. Salty water is a mixture.

?
Why This Matters

The definitions of atom, element, compound, and mixture are guaranteed exam marks. They appear almost every year as short-answer questions. If you can write these four definitions from memory, that's easy marks in the bag before you even get to the hard stuff.

T
Study Tip — The "Only One" Test

To tell elements from compounds: if a substance has only one type of atom, it's an element. If it has two or more types bonded together, it's a compound. Look at the chemical formula — H₂ has only H atoms (element), H₂O has H and O atoms (compound). Practise this with 5-6 formulas until it's automatic.

  • I can define atom, element, compound, and mixture without looking
  • I know what groups and periods are on the Periodic Table
  • I can explain the difference between a compound and a mixture
  • I know where the metals and non-metals are on the Periodic Table
Section 2.2 More About Atoms — Subatomic Particles
Atoms aren't the smallest things after all. Inside every atom are three types of even tinier particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons. Understanding these is the key to understanding the whole of chemistry from here on.
W
Write This Down — Copy This Table Into Your Notes
Subatomic Particle Mass (amu) Charge Location
Proton 1 Positive (+1) In the nucleus
Neutron 1 None (0) In the nucleus
Electron ~0 (negligible) Negative (−1) In shells around the nucleus

The nucleus (protons + neutrons) is tiny but contains nearly all the atom's mass. If the atom were the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be the size of a pea.

W
Write This Down — Key Numbers

• Proton number (atomic number): the number of protons in the nucleus. This identifies the element — every element has a unique proton number. Sodium always has 11 protons.
• Nucleon number (mass number): the total number of protons + neutrons. For sodium: 11 + 12 = 23.
• Number of neutrons = nucleon number − proton number. For sodium: 23 − 11 = 12 neutrons.

W
Write This Down — Why Atoms Have No Charge

Every atom has an equal number of protons and electrons. Protons are positive (+), electrons are negative (−). The charges cancel out, so atoms have no overall charge. Sodium has 11 protons (11+) and 11 electrons (11−) = 0 overall.

!
Don't Confuse — Proton Number vs Nucleon Number

The proton number (atomic number) tells you which element it is. The nucleon number (mass number) tells you the total mass. In the shorthand notation, the nucleon number goes on top and the proton number goes on the bottom. For example, sodium is written as ¹¹Na with 23 on top and 11 on the bottom. Remember: big number on top (mass is always bigger because it includes neutrons).

?
Why This Matters

You'll need to read the shorthand notation and work out how many protons, electrons, and neutrons an atom has. This appears on every single IGCSE Chemistry paper. They'll give you something like "the atom has 12 protons and a nucleon number of 24" and expect you to work out everything from that. Practise the formula: neutrons = nucleon number − proton number.

T
Study Tip — Practise With the First 20

The textbook has a table of the first 20 elements with their proton numbers, electrons, neutrons, and nucleon numbers. Cover different columns and try to fill them in from memory. Start with just the first 10 (hydrogen to neon), then add 11–20. If you can do all 20, you'll be fast and confident on the exam.

  • I can name the three subatomic particles and give their mass, charge, and location
  • I can define proton number (atomic number) and nucleon number (mass number)
  • I can calculate the number of neutrons from the nucleon and proton numbers
  • I can explain why atoms have no overall charge
  • I can read the shorthand notation (e.g. the notation for sodium-23) and extract all the information
Section 2.3 How Electrons Are Arranged
Electrons aren't just floating randomly around the nucleus — they're arranged in specific shells (energy levels). The way electrons fill these shells directly links to the Periodic Table and explains why elements in the same group behave similarly.
W
Write This Down — Electron Shell Rules

Electrons fill shells starting from the one closest to the nucleus (lowest energy level):

• 1st shell: holds a maximum of 2 electrons
• 2nd shell: holds a maximum of 8 electrons
• 3rd shell: holds a maximum of 8 electrons (when it's the outer shell — it can hold 18 when it's not the outermost shell, but you don't need that for the first 20 elements)

This arrangement is called the electronic configuration. It's written as numbers separated by commas. For example: sodium = 2,8,1 (2 in the first shell, 8 in the second, 1 in the third).

W
Write This Down — Link to the Periodic Table

This is where it all connects:

• The period number tells you how many electron shells are occupied. Period 2 elements have 2 shells, period 3 have 3, etc.
• The group number (for Groups I–VII) tells you how many outer shell electrons the atom has. Group I elements have 1 outer electron, Group II have 2, and so on.
• The number of outer shell electrons controls how an element reacts. That's why elements in the same group have similar chemical properties.

W
Write This Down — Group VIII: The Noble Gases

Group VIII elements (helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon) have a full outer shell of electrons. Helium has 2 (full 1st shell), neon has 2,8 (full 2nd shell), argon has 2,8,8 (full 3rd shell).

A full outer shell is very stable. These elements are unreactive (they don't need to gain, lose, or share electrons). They're also called the noble gases.

!
Don't Confuse — Shells vs Protons

Remember: the number of electrons equals the number of protons (in a neutral atom). So if they tell you "an element has proton number 17," you know it has 17 electrons and you can work out the electronic configuration: 2, 8, 7. Don't try to fit protons into shells — only electrons go in shells. Protons and neutrons stay in the nucleus.

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves to give you a proton number and ask you to: (1) write the electronic configuration, (2) identify the group and period, or (3) predict how it will react. If you know the shell-filling rules and the link to the Periodic Table, you can answer all three from a single number. This will also help in biology — ion channels and nerve impulses depend on sodium (2,8,1) and potassium (2,8,8,1) losing their outer electrons.

T
Study Tip — Draw All 20

Get a blank piece of paper and draw electron shell diagrams for the first 20 elements (hydrogen to calcium). For each one, write the electronic configuration in 2,8,... format next to it. When you can do all 20 from memory in under 5 minutes, you own this topic. Pay special attention to potassium (2,8,8,1) and calcium (2,8,8,2) — they start a 4th shell even though the 3rd isn't "really" full (it can hold 18), because at this level it fills to 8 and then starts the next one.

  • I can state how many electrons each of the first three shells holds
  • I can write the electronic configuration for any of the first 20 elements
  • I can explain how the period number and group number relate to electron arrangement
  • I can explain why Group VIII elements are unreactive
Section 2.4 Isotopes and Relative Atomic Mass (Aᵣ)
Not all atoms of the same element are identical. Some have extra neutrons, making them heavier. These variations are called isotopes. To deal with this, chemists use an average mass called the relative atomic mass.
W
Write This Down — Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Example — Carbon:
• Carbon-12: 6 protons, 6 neutrons (nucleon number 12) — the most common
• Carbon-13: 6 protons, 7 neutrons (nucleon number 13)
• Carbon-14: 6 protons, 8 neutrons (nucleon number 14) — radioactive

All three are still carbon because they all have 6 protons. The proton number defines the element; the neutron number just changes the mass.

W
Write This Down — Isotopes React the Same Way

All isotopes of an element have the same number of electrons (because they have the same number of protons). Since chemical reactions depend on outer shell electrons, all isotopes of an element have the same chemical properties. Carbon-12, carbon-13, and carbon-14 all react with oxygen to form CO₂ in exactly the same way.

W
Write This Down — Relative Atomic Mass (Aᵣ)

Because isotopes exist, we need an average mass. The relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) of an element is the average mass of its isotopes, compared to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

How to calculate Aᵣ:
Aᵣ = (mass of isotope 1 × its % abundance) + (mass of isotope 2 × its % abundance) + ...

Example — Chlorine:
Chlorine has two isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%).
Aᵣ = (35 × 75/100) + (37 × 25/100) = 26.25 + 9.25 = 35.5

!
Don't Confuse — Nucleon Number vs Relative Atomic Mass

The nucleon number is for one specific atom (always a whole number). The relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) is the average across all isotopes (can be a decimal). Chlorine's nucleon numbers are 35 and 37, but its Aᵣ is 35.5 because it's an average. If the exam asks for nucleon number, give a whole number. If it asks for Aᵣ, it might be a decimal.

?
Why This Matters

You'll use Aᵣ constantly in later chapters when you calculate formula masses, moles, and reacting masses. Getting comfortable with it now saves headaches later. The Aᵣ calculation is also a common Extended syllabus question — it's basically a weighted average, which is the same maths you do in stats class.

T
Study Tip — Sanity-Check Your Aᵣ

Your calculated Aᵣ should always fall between the masses of the lightest and heaviest isotopes, and it should be closer to the more abundant one. For chlorine: 75% is mass 35, 25% is mass 37, so Aᵣ should be between 35 and 37 but closer to 35. Answer: 35.5. If you get a number outside this range, you've made an error.

  • I can define isotopes in one sentence
  • I can explain why isotopes of the same element react the same way
  • I can define relative atomic mass (Aᵣ)
  • I can calculate Aᵣ from isotope masses and percentage abundances
  • I know the difference between nucleon number and relative atomic mass
