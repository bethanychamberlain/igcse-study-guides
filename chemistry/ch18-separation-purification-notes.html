Section 18.1 Making a Substance in the Lab
Practical work in chemistry involves materials, apparatus, and methods. When you make a product in the lab, you need separation methods to remove impurities and obtain a pure substance.
W
Write This Down — The Steps to Make Crystals of Copper(II) Sulfate

1. Add copper(II) oxide (the reactant) to warm dilute sulfuric acid.
2. Filter the excess copper(II) oxide from the solution (it is an impurity).
3. Evaporate most of the water to make a hot saturated solution.
4. Cool the solution — crystallisation takes place and crystals form.
5. Filter the crystals from the remaining solution.
6. Pat the crystals dry with filter paper.

W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Impurity = any unwanted substance mixed with your product. A pure product contains only the particles shown in its formula.

Separation methods are essential because they remove unwanted chemicals and increase the purity of the product.

?
Why This Matters

This copper(II) sulfate procedure is a classic exam question. They can ask you to describe each step, name the apparatus at each stage, or explain why a particular separation method is used. Steps 2–6 are all about purification — each step removes something unwanted.

T
Study Tip — Know Your Apparatus

Learn the names: beaker, conical flask, filter funnel, filter paper, evaporating dish, Bunsen burner, tripod, wire gauze, measuring cylinder, volumetric pipette, burette. The exam expects you to name these from diagrams. A volumetric pipette is more accurate than a measuring cylinder for small precise volumes.

  • I can list the six steps to make copper(II) sulfate crystals
  • I can explain why each step is needed (what it removes)
  • I can define "impurity" and "pure substance"
  • I can name at least six pieces of lab apparatus from diagrams
Section 18.2 Solutions and Solubility
When you mix sugar with water, the sugar seems to disappear — its molecules spread out among the water molecules. Understanding solutions is the key to knowing which separation method to use.
W
Write This Down — Core Definitions (Memorise These)

• Solvent = the substance that dissolves a solute (e.g. water)
• Solute = the substance that is dissolved (e.g. sugar)
• Solution = a mixture of one or more solutes dissolved in a solvent
• Aqueous solution = a solution where water is the solvent
• Saturated solution = a solution that can dissolve no more solute at that temperature
• Solubility = the mass of solute (in g) that dissolves in 100 g of water at a given temperature

W
Write This Down — Solubility Rules

• Solubility is different for every substance. Silver nitrate (241.3 g/100 g) is very soluble; calcium carbonate (0.0013 g/100 g) is effectively insoluble.
• Solubility usually increases with temperature. More solute dissolves in hot water than cold.
• All sodium compounds are soluble in water — useful pattern to remember.
• The solvent matters too: nail polish is insoluble in water but soluble in propanone (acetone). Sugar is soluble in ethanol but salt is not.

!
Don't Confuse — Dissolved vs Suspended

If you mix chalk powder with water, the chalk does not dissolve — you get a cloudy suspension, not a solution. The particles are big enough to see and will eventually sink. A true solution is clear (though it may be coloured, like copper sulfate solution = blue). If you can see particles floating, it's not a solution.

?
Why This Matters — Crystallisation Depends on This

Crystallisation works because solubility decreases as temperature falls. If you make a hot saturated solution of copper(II) sulfate (105 g dissolves at 90°C) and cool it to 25°C (only 34 g dissolves), the extra 71 g must come out of solution as crystals. This is exactly why you heat and then cool in steps 3–4 of making crystals.

T
Study Tip — Solubility Graphs

The exam loves solubility graphs (solubility on the y-axis, temperature on the x-axis). Practice reading them: "How much dissolves at X°C?" and "If I cool a saturated solution from 90°C to 25°C, how many grams of crystals form?" The answer is always: (solubility at high temp) minus (solubility at low temp).

  • I can define solvent, solute, solution, aqueous, saturated, and solubility
  • I know that solubility usually increases with temperature
  • I can explain why crystallisation works using solubility data
  • I can read a solubility graph to calculate crystal yield
Section 18.3 Separating a Solid from a Liquid
There are three main methods: filtration, crystallisation, and evaporation. The method you choose depends on whether the solid is dissolved (in solution) or not, and whether its solubility changes much with temperature.
W
Write This Down — When to Use Each Method
Method Use When How It Works Key Terms
Filtration The solid is not dissolved (insoluble). E.g. chalk from water, excess reactant from a solution. Pour the mixture through filter paper in a funnel. Solid particles are too big to pass through the pores. Residue = solid trapped on the paper. Filtrate = liquid that passes through.
Crystallisation The solid is dissolved AND its solubility changes a lot with temperature. E.g. copper(II) sulfate, potassium nitrate. Heat to evaporate water and concentrate the solution. Cool it — crystals form as solubility drops. Filter and dry. Test readiness: place a drop on a cool microscope slide — if crystals form quickly, it's ready.
Evaporation The solid is dissolved BUT its solubility changes very little with temperature. E.g. sodium chloride (salt). Heat the solution in an evaporating dish. The water evaporates, leaving the solid behind. Crystallisation won't work for salt because cooling doesn't make it come out of solution.
!
Don't Confuse — Crystallisation vs Evaporation

These are not the same thing. Crystallisation = heat to concentrate, then cool so crystals form slowly (gives nice pure crystals). Evaporation = heat until all the water is gone (used when crystallisation won't work). The exam will ask "Why is crystallisation not used for NaCl?" — the answer is that salt's solubility barely changes with temperature, so cooling won't produce crystals.

W
Write This Down — Separating Two Solids

To separate a mixture of two solids, find a solvent that dissolves only one of them.
Example: sugar + salt mixture → add ethanol (sugar dissolves, salt does not) → filter (salt = residue, sugar solution = filtrate) → rinse salt, dry in oven → evaporate ethanol from sugar solution (use a water bath — ethanol is flammable!).

T
Study Tip — The Decision Tree

When the exam says "suggest a method to separate X from Y," ask yourself: (1) Is the solid dissolved? If NO → filtration. If YES → (2) Does its solubility change a lot with temperature? If YES → crystallisation. If NO → evaporation. Write this decision tree in your notes and practice applying it.

  • I can name three methods to separate a solid from a liquid
  • I can explain when to use filtration vs crystallisation vs evaporation
  • I know what residue and filtrate mean
  • I can describe how to separate a mixture of two solids
Section 18.4 Separating by Distillation
Distillation separates substances from a liquid mixture by boiling and then condensing. There are two types: simple distillation (solvent from solute) and fractional distillation (two or more liquids from each other).
W
Write This Down — Simple Distillation

Purpose: Separate the solvent from a solution (e.g. obtain pure water from salt water).

How it works:
1. Heat the solution in a round-bottomed flask with side arm. As it boils, water vapour rises into the condenser, leaving salt behind.
2. The condenser is a cold tube (cold water flows around it). The vapour condenses back to liquid.
3. Pure distilled water drips into a collecting beaker.
4. The salt (or other solute) stays behind as a residue in the flask.

W
Write This Down — Fractional Distillation

Purpose: Separate two or more liquids with different boiling points (e.g. ethanol from water).

How it works:
1. Heat the mixture. Ethanol (b.p. 78°C) boils first. A mixture of vapours rises up the fractionating column (packed with glass beads).
2. The glass beads heat up. Water vapour condenses on them and drips back down. Ethanol vapour keeps rising.
3. When the glass beads reach about 78°C, ethanol vapour no longer condenses on them. Only water vapour condenses — it drips back into the flask.
4. Pure ethanol vapour passes into the condenser, condenses, and is collected.
5. When the thermometer rises above 78°C, all the ethanol has been collected — stop heating.

The fractionating column is the key difference. It provides a surface for repeated condensation and evaporation, giving better separation.

!
Don't Confuse — Simple vs Fractional

Simple distillation = solvent from solute (e.g. water from salt water). Fractional distillation = liquid from liquid (e.g. ethanol from water). If the exam asks "How would you get pure water from seawater?" the answer is simple distillation. If it asks "How would you separate ethanol and water?" the answer is fractional distillation. Simple distillation won't separate two liquids well because their vapours mix.

?
Why This Matters — Industry Examples

Fractional distillation is used in industry to: (1) refine petroleum into fractions (petrol, diesel, kerosene, etc.) in tall fractionating towers, (2) produce ethanol from fermented plant material, and (3) separate nitrogen, oxygen, and noble gases from liquid air (cooled to −200°C, then warmed slowly — each gas boils off at its own boiling point). These are common exam examples.

T
Study Tip — Label the Diagram

Draw both apparatus setups from memory. For simple distillation: round-bottomed flask, condenser (label "water in" at bottom, "water out" at top), collecting beaker. For fractional: same setup plus a fractionating column (with glass beads) and a thermometer. If you can draw and label both, you'll pick up easy marks.

  • I can describe how simple distillation works, step by step
  • I can describe how fractional distillation works, step by step
  • I know when to use simple vs fractional distillation
  • I can give three industrial uses of fractional distillation
Sections 18.5 – 18.6 Paper Chromatography and Rf Values
Paper chromatography is used to: (1) separate substances from a mixture, (2) check whether a substance is pure, and (3) identify unknown substances. It works because different substances travel at different speeds through the paper.
W
Write This Down — How Chromatography Works

1. Draw a pencil line near the bottom of chromatography paper (the origin / base line). Use pencil, not pen, because pen ink would dissolve and interfere.
2. Place a spot of the substance on the line.
3. Stand the paper in a beaker with a small amount of solvent (e.g. ethanol or water). The spot must be above the solvent level.
4. The solvent rises up the paper by capillary action, dissolving the substances and carrying them upward.
5. Different substances travel at different speeds because of their different solubilities in the solvent and attraction to the paper.
6. Remove the paper before the solvent reaches the top. Mark the solvent front (how far the solvent travelled).

The result is called a chromatogram.

Key terms: The paper is the stationary phase (non-moving). The solvent is the mobile phase (carries the mixture along). Substances separate because each has a different level of attraction to each phase.

W
Write This Down — Three Uses of Chromatography
Use How It Tells You
Separating a mixture Different coloured bands appear (e.g. black ink separates into blue, red, yellow). Cut the bands apart to collect each substance.
Checking purity A pure substance gives one spot. If you see two or more spots, the substance contains impurities.
Identifying substances Run the unknown alongside known substances on the same paper. If a spot in the unknown is at the same height as a known substance, they are the same.
W
Write This Down — Rf Values

Rf (retention factor) = distance from origin to spot ÷ distance from origin to solvent front

Rf values are always between 0 and 1. They are unique to each substance for a given solvent under the same conditions. You can look up Rf values in tables to identify unknown substances.

For colourless substances (like amino acids): after running the chromatogram, spray with a locating agent (e.g. ninhydrin) and warm in an oven to make the spots visible. Then measure and calculate Rf.

W
Write This Down — Other Types of Chromatography

Paper chromatography is just one type. The stationary phase can also be a thin coat of adsorbent on a glass plate, or plastic beads packed into a tube. The mobile phase can be a liquid solvent or an inert carrier gas (e.g. helium or nitrogen) — this is called gas chromatography.

Uses of chromatography: identifying amino acids in proteins, checking purity, crime detection (analysing blood samples, fibres, and traces of drugs), identifying pollutants in air or river water, and separating compounds in the petroleum industry.

!
Don't Confuse

Rf values only work if conditions are the same. If you change the solvent, the temperature, or the type of paper, Rf values change. So when comparing an unknown to a table, you must use the same solvent and conditions listed in the table. Also: measure to the centre of each spot, not the edge.

?
Why This Matters

The exam often gives you a chromatogram and asks: "How many substances are in the mixture?" (count the spots), "Is substance X pure?" (one spot = pure), or "Calculate the Rf value" (measure the distances and divide). These are straightforward marks if you know the formula and can use a ruler.

T
Study Tip — Practice Rf Calculations

Draw a fake chromatogram: origin at 0 cm, solvent front at 10 cm, spot at 6 cm. Rf = 6/10 = 0.60. Now do another: origin at 0 cm, solvent front at 8 cm, spot at 3.2 cm. Rf = 3.2/8 = 0.40. Do five of these until the formula is automatic. The exam will give you the distances — all you have to do is divide.

  • I can describe the chromatography procedure step by step
  • I know why you use pencil (not pen) for the base line
  • I can explain the three uses: separating, checking purity, identifying
  • I can calculate Rf values and use them to identify substances
  • I know what a locating agent is and when it is needed
  • I can explain the terms stationary phase and mobile phase
Section 18.7 Checking Purity
A pure substance contains only one type of particle — nothing else. There are two ways to check if something is pure: melting/boiling point analysis and paper chromatography.
W
Write This Down — Using Melting and Boiling Points to Check Purity

• A pure substance has a sharp, definite melting point and boiling point (happens at one exact temperature).
• When a substance contains an impurity:
  – Its melting point falls (lower than expected)
  – Its boiling point rises (higher than expected)
  – It melts and boils over a range of temperatures, not sharply
• The more impurity present, the bigger the change and the wider the range.

W
Write This Down — Melting/Boiling Points as ID

Every pure substance has a unique pair of melting and boiling points — like a fingerprint. If you measure both for an unknown substance and look them up in a data table, you can identify what it is.

Example: A substance melts at 0°C and boils at 100°C = water. If the same substance melts at −0.5°C and boils at 101°C, it contains impurities.

W
Write This Down — How to Increase Purity

• Rinse a solid product with distilled water to wash away soluble impurities.
• Repeat the separation technique (e.g. dissolve crystals in pure solvent, then recrystallise).
• In industry: ultra-fine filtration, repeated crystallisation, and advanced chromatography are used to purify products like vaccines and food additives.

!
Don't Confuse — The Direction of Change

Impurities make the melting point go DOWN and the boiling point go UP. Students often mix these up. Think of it this way: impurities make the lattice easier to break apart (melts sooner = lower m.p.), but make it harder for particles to escape the liquid (boils later = higher b.p.).

?
Why This Matters

The exam commonly gives you two samples: "Sample X melts in the range 332.5–333.5°C. Sample Y melts in the range 331–333.5°C. Pure potassium nitrate melts sharply at 334°C. Which sample contains more impurity?" Y has a wider range and a lower start — so Y has more impurity. Quick marks if you understand the rule.

T
Study Tip — Two Methods, Same Goal

Make a mini flashcard: "Two ways to check purity: (1) Melting/boiling points — sharp and at the correct value = pure. (2) Paper chromatography — one spot = pure." If the exam asks for "a method to check purity," either answer works. But be ready to explain how each method tells you.

  • I can state two methods to check purity
  • I know what happens to melting and boiling points when impurities are present
  • I can explain how m.p./b.p. can be used to identify a substance
  • I can explain how chromatography shows whether a substance is pure
  • I can describe two ways to increase the purity of a product
