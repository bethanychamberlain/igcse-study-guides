Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the term element.
1 mark
Answer
An element is a substance that contains only one kind of atom [1].
The key phrase is "one kind of atom." Don't say "one type of molecule" or "one type of particle" — it must be "atom."

Name the three subatomic particles found in an atom, and state the charge of each.
2 marks
Answer
Proton — positive charge (+1) [1]
Neutron — no charge (0)
Electron — negative charge (−1) [1]
One mark for naming all three, one mark for all charges correct. If you get one charge wrong, you lose the second mark.

3
Define the term isotopes.
2 marks
Answer
Isotopes are atoms of the same element [1] that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons [1].
Both parts are needed for full marks. Saying "same element, different mass" is not enough — you must specifically say "different numbers of neutrons."

4
State the difference between a compound and a mixture.
2 marks
Answer
A compound is made of atoms of different elements chemically bonded together [1]. A mixture contains different elements or compounds that are not bonded together and can usually be separated easily by physical methods [1].
The key distinction is "bonded" vs "not bonded." An example helps: water is a compound (H and O bonded); salt water is a mixture (salt and water not bonded).

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
An atom has a proton number of 15 and a nucleon number of 31. State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in this atom. Identify the element using the Periodic Table.
3 marks
Answer
Protons: 15 (same as proton number) [1]
Electrons: 15 (equal to number of protons in a neutral atom)
Neutrons: 31 − 15 = 16 [1]
The element is phosphorus (P) [1].
The method: protons = proton number, electrons = protons, neutrons = nucleon number − proton number. Always show the subtraction working for the neutrons mark.

6
An element has a proton number of 17. Give its electronic configuration and state which group and period it is in on the Periodic Table.
3 marks
Answer
17 electrons, so the electronic configuration is 2, 8, 7 [1].
There are 3 occupied shells, so the element is in Period 3 [1].
There are 7 outer shell electrons, so it is in Group VII [1].
(The element is chlorine, Cl.)
Fill shells in order: 2 in the first, 8 in the second, that's 10 used, leaving 7 for the third shell. Period = number of shells. Group = number of outer electrons.

7
Explain why atoms have no overall electrical charge.
3 marks
Answer
Atoms contain equal numbers of protons and electrons [1]. Protons have a positive charge (+1) and electrons have a negative charge (−1) [1]. The positive and negative charges cancel out, so the atom has no overall charge [1].
Three marks, three ideas: (1) equal numbers, (2) opposite charges, (3) they cancel. Don't just say "they balance" — explain why they balance.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
The elements lithium (proton number 3), sodium (proton number 11), and potassium (proton number 19) are all in Group I of the Periodic Table. (a) Write the electronic configuration of each. (b) State what is similar about their electronic configurations. (c) Explain why elements in the same group have similar chemical properties.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Lithium: 2, 1. Sodium: 2, 8, 1. Potassium: 2, 8, 8, 1. [1]

(b) They all have 1 electron in their outer shell [1].

(c) The chemical properties of an element depend on the number of outer shell electrons [1]. Since elements in the same group have the same number of outer shell electrons [1], they react in a similar way [1].
The key idea: same group = same number of outer electrons = similar reactions. This is the single most important concept linking electron configuration to chemistry. It explains why the Periodic Table is arranged the way it is.

9
The elements in Group VIII (the noble gases) are described as unreactive. Explain, in terms of electronic configuration, why Group VIII elements do not readily take part in chemical reactions. Include at least one example.
5 marks
Answer
Group VIII elements have a full outer shell of electrons [1]. For example, neon has the electronic configuration 2, 8 — both shells are full [1]. A full outer shell is a very stable arrangement [1]. Atoms react in order to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a full outer shell [1]. Since Group VIII elements already have a full outer shell, they have no tendency to react [1].
Five marks: full outer shell, example with config, stable arrangement, atoms react to get full shells, noble gases already have them. This question tests whether you understand that reactivity is driven by electrons trying to reach a stable configuration.

10
Gallium has two naturally occurring isotopes: gallium-69 and gallium-71. The relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) of gallium is 69.723. (a) Explain what the term relative atomic mass means. (b) Use the Aᵣ value to determine which isotope is more abundant. Explain your reasoning. (c) Calculate the approximate percentage abundance of each isotope.
6 marks
Answer
(a) The relative atomic mass is the average mass of the isotopes of an element [1], compared to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom [1].

(b) The Aᵣ of 69.723 is closer to 69 than to 71 [1], so gallium-69 must be the more abundant isotope, because the average is pulled towards the more common one [1].

(c) Let the percentage of gallium-69 = x. Then gallium-71 = (100 − x).
69x + 71(100 − x) = 69.723 × 100
69x + 7100 − 71x = 6972.3
−2x = −127.7
x = 63.85
So gallium-69 is approximately 64% [1] and gallium-71 is approximately 36% [1].
Part (c) is Extended syllabus. Even if the algebra is tricky, parts (a) and (b) are worth 4 marks on their own. For part (b), the reasoning is simple: the Aᵣ is a weighted average, so it's always closer to the more abundant isotope. For (c), set up the equation carefully and solve step by step.
