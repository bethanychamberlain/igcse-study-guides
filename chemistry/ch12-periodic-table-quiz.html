Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
In the Periodic Table, what is the name for: (a) a vertical column of elements, and (b) a horizontal row of elements?
2 marks
Answer
(a) A group [1]. (b) A period [1].
Simple recall. Don't mix them up — "group" goes down, "period" goes across. Think: a group of people standing in a column.

2
State three physical properties of the alkali metals (Group I) that make them unusual compared to most metals.
3 marks
Answer
They are soft (can be cut with a knife) [1], they have low density (some float on water) [1], and they have low melting points [1].
Three properties, three marks. Most students get two but forget the third. Density is the one most often missed.

3
Describe the appearance of (a) chlorine, (b) bromine, and (c) iodine at room temperature.
3 marks
Answer
(a) Chlorine is a pale yellow-green gas [1].
(b) Bromine is a red-brown liquid [1].
(c) Iodine is a grey-black solid [1].
You need both the colour AND the state for each mark. "Bromine is a liquid" alone would not score — you must say "red-brown liquid."

4
Name two chemical properties that are typical of transition elements but not of Group I metals.
2 marks
Answer
Any two from: they form coloured compounds [1]; they act as catalysts [1]; they form ions with variable charges / variable oxidation numbers [1].
Group I metals form white compounds, are never used as catalysts, and always form 1+ ions. Transition elements do the opposite on all three counts.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY using ideas about electrons and electron shells.
5
Describe what you would see when a small piece of potassium is placed in a trough of water containing universal indicator. Write the word equation for the reaction.
4 marks
Answer
The potassium melts into a ball and moves/shoots across the surface of the water [1]. A gas fizzes off and catches fire, burning with a lilac flame [1]. The indicator changes colour to purple/blue, showing an alkaline solution has formed [1].

Word equation: potassium + water → potassium hydroxide + hydrogen [1].
Four things to mention: melts/moves, fizzes/catches fire, indicator turns purple (alkaline), plus the word equation. Don't forget the lilac flame — examiners love that detail.
Rate your answer:
6
Chlorine water is added to a colourless solution of potassium bromide. (a) Describe what you would observe. (b) Write a word equation for the reaction. (c) Explain why this reaction occurs.
4 marks
Answer
(a) The solution turns orange [1].
(b) chlorine + potassium bromide → potassium chloride + bromine [1].
(c) Chlorine is more reactive than bromine [1], so it displaces bromine from the solution / pushes bromine out of its compound [1].
A more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive one from a solution of its halide. The colour change (orange = bromine released) is the observation. Always state which halogen is more reactive and why that causes displacement.

7
Explain why the elements in Group I all react in a similar way.
3 marks
Answer
All Group I elements have the same number of outer shell electrons [1], which is 1 [1]. Atoms with the same number of outer shell electrons react in a similar way / the outer electrons determine how an element reacts [1].
This pattern applies to any group, not just Group I. The key phrase is "same number of outer shell electrons." The exam wants you to connect group number → outer electrons → similar reactions.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Explain why reactivity increases going down Group I, but decreases going down Group VII.
6 marks
Answer
Group I: Going down the group, atoms have more electron shells [1]. The outer electron is further from the nucleus, so the attraction from the nucleus is weaker [1]. It is therefore easier to lose the outer electron, making the element more reactive [1].

Group VII: Going down the group, atoms also have more electron shells [1]. The outer shell is further from the nucleus, so the nucleus has a weaker pull on incoming electrons [1]. It is therefore harder to gain an extra electron, making the element less reactive [1].
Six marks, six points. The underlying reason is the same (more shells = further from nucleus = weaker attraction), but the effect is opposite because metals LOSE electrons (easier = more reactive) while non-metals GAIN electrons (harder = less reactive). This is one of the most important 6-mark answers in the whole IGCSE course.

9
Rubidium (Rb) is in Group I, below potassium. Predict: (a) how rubidium would react with water (describe what you would see and name the products), and (b) how rubidium would react with chlorine (name the product and describe the reaction).
5 marks
Answer
(a) With water: Rubidium would react even more vigorously than potassium [1], likely exploding on contact with water [1]. The products would be rubidium hydroxide and hydrogen [1].

(b) With chlorine: Rubidium would burn very vigorously / more vigorously than potassium [1] to form rubidium chloride [1].
This is a "predict" question — you use the trend (reactivity increases down Group I) and extend it. Since potassium already catches fire, rubidium would be even more violent. The products follow the same pattern: metal hydroxide + hydrogen (for water), metal chloride (for chlorine).

10
Compare the physical and chemical properties of iron (a transition element) with sodium (a Group I metal). Give at least four differences.
6 marks
Answer
Physical:
• Iron is hard and strong; sodium is soft (can be cut with a knife) [1].
• Iron has a high density (7.9 g/cm³); sodium has a low density (0.97 g/cm³) [1].
• Iron has a high melting point (1535°C); sodium has a low melting point (98°C) [1].

Chemical:
• Iron is much less reactive than sodium — it does not react with cold water, while sodium reacts vigorously [1].
• Iron forms coloured compounds (e.g. rust is red-brown); sodium forms white compounds [1].
• Iron has variable oxidation numbers (Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺); sodium always forms Na⁺ (charge 1+) [1].
Six marks for six clear contrasts. Structure your answer: physical properties first (hardness, density, melting point), then chemical (reactivity, compound colour, ion charge). Each contrast should name both metals and state both sides of the difference.
