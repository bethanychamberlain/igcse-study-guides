Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Particle theory All matter is made of tiny particles that are always moving. Higher temperature = faster movement. —
Diffusion Net movement of particles from high concentration to low concentration (they spread out). Osmosis (water only, through a membrane — that's biology)
Brownian motion Random jerky movement of visible particles (smoke, pollen) caused by collisions with invisible molecules. Diffusion (Brownian motion is evidence FOR particle movement, not the spreading itself)
Melting Solid to liquid. Particles gain energy and overcome some forces. Dissolving (that's mixing into a solvent, not changing state)
Boiling Liquid to gas at a fixed temperature, throughout the liquid. Bubbles form inside. Evaporation (any temp, surface only, no bubbles)
Evaporation Liquid to gas at any temperature, but only at the surface. Fastest particles escape. Boiling (fixed temp, whole liquid, bubbles)
Condensation Gas to liquid. Particles lose energy and forces pull them closer together. Freezing (that's liquid to solid)
Sublimation Solid directly to gas, skipping liquid. Example: dry ice (solid CO₂). Evaporation (that starts from a liquid, not a solid)
Kinetic energy Energy of movement. Higher temperature = particles have more kinetic energy = move faster. —
Gas pressure Force per unit area caused by gas particles colliding with the walls of their container. —
Relative molecular mass (Mᵣ) Sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule. E.g. H₂O = 2(1) + 16 = 18. Number of atoms (CO₂ has 3 atoms but Mᵣ = 44)

Solid vs Liquid vs Gas — Side by Side

Solid Liquid Gas
Arrangement Regular pattern, tightly packed Close but irregular Far apart, random
Movement Vibrate in fixed positions Slide past each other Move fast in all directions
Forces Strong Weaker Very weak / negligible
Spacing Touching Almost touching Far apart
Compressible? No No (almost) Yes (particles far apart)
Example Ice Water Steam
Memory Trick
Solids = Stay put (vibrate only). Liquids = sLide around. Gases = Go everywhere (fast, free, far apart).

Changes of State — What Turns Into What

Solid → Liquid = Melting
Liquid → Gas = Boiling / Evaporation
Gas → Liquid = Condensation
Liquid → Solid = Freezing
Solid → Gas = Sublimation (skips liquid!)
Exam Trap — Heating Curve Flat Sections
If the exam shows a heating curve and asks "Why is the temperature constant here?", the answer is: "Energy is being used to overcome the forces between particles (breaking bonds between molecules), not to increase kinetic energy, so the temperature does not rise." This exact wording is worth 2 marks.
Exam Trap — Boiling vs Evaporation
Boiling = fixed temperature, whole liquid, bubbles. Evaporation = any temperature, surface only, no bubbles. They LOVE asking this difference.

Say It in One Sentence

Particle theory Everything is made of particles that never stop moving, and heating them makes them move faster.
Diffusion Particles randomly spread from where there are lots to where there are few — like perfume filling a room.
Solid Particles packed tight in a pattern, vibrating on the spot — strong forces keep them locked in place.
Liquid Particles close together but free to slide around — forces are weaker so they flow.
Gas Particles far apart, moving fast in every direction — almost no forces between them.
Gas pressure Billions of gas particles smashing into the container walls every second.
Heating curve flat Temperature stops rising because energy is breaking forces between particles, not making them hotter.

Memory Tricks

Diffusion Direction
Think of a packed concert: people spread OUT from the crowd, not into it. Particles do the same — high concentration to low.
Heavy = Slow (Gas Diffusion Rate)
Imagine throwing a bowling ball vs a tennis ball with the same effort. The tennis ball flies further. Lighter molecules (lower Mᵣ) diffuse faster.
Flat Line = Breaking Bonds
On a heating curve, when the line goes flat, picture the energy going into snapping chains between particles instead of speeding them up. Temperature only rises when the chains are already broken.
Chemistry Diffusion vs Biology Diffusion
Same definition (high to low concentration), different examples. Chemistry: perfume, bromine gas, ink in water. Biology: oxygen into blood, CO₂ out of cells. Don't mix up the examples in the wrong exam paper!

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Describe the arrangement of particles in a solid" Regular pattern / lattice, tightly packed, vibrate in fixed positions
"Why can gases be compressed?" Particles are far apart with space between them, so they can be pushed closer
"Why does temperature stay constant during melting?" Energy is used to overcome forces between particles, not to increase kinetic energy
"Explain why perfume can be smelled across a room" Diffusion — perfume particles spread from high to low concentration through the air
"Why does ammonia diffuse faster than HCl?" Ammonia has a lower relative molecular mass (17 vs 36.5), so its particles move faster
"Explain gas pressure using particle theory" Gas particles collide with the walls of the container; more/harder collisions = higher pressure
"What is the difference between boiling and evaporation?" Boiling: fixed temp, whole liquid, bubbles. Evaporation: any temp, surface only, no bubbles
"What is sublimation? Give an example." Solid changes directly to gas (no liquid stage). Example: dry ice (solid CO₂)
