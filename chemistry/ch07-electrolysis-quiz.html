Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define electrolysis.
2 marks
Answer
Electrolysis is the decomposition [1] of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current [1].
Both "decomposition" and "electric current" are needed for full marks. Don't just say "breaking down with electricity" — use "decomposition" and specify "molten or in aqueous solution."

3
Explain why solid sodium chloride does not conduct electricity, but molten sodium chloride does.
2 marks
Answer
In solid sodium chloride, the ions are held in fixed positions and cannot move [1]. When molten, the ions become free to move and can carry the current [1].
The key phrase is "free to move." Do not say "no ions" for the solid — there ARE ions, they just can't move.

4
What does OILRIG stand for? State which process happens at the cathode and which at the anode.
2 marks
Answer
Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons) [1].
Reduction happens at the cathode; oxidation happens at the anode [1].
Remember RAC: Reduction At Cathode. If you get this one fact right, all your half-equations will have the electrons on the correct side.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, predict products, and write half-equations.
5
Molten lead(II) bromide is electrolysed using carbon electrodes. (a) Name the product at each electrode. (b) Write the half-equation for the reaction at the cathode.
4 marks
Answer
(a) At the cathode: lead [1]. At the anode: bromine [1].
(b) Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l) [2: 1 for correct formula and products, 1 for balanced electrons]
For the half-equation: the charge on lead is 2+, so you need 2 electrons. Lead is a metal so it forms at the cathode (reduction = gain of electrons). Don't forget state symbols if the question asks for them.

6
Predict the products at each electrode when a concentrated solution of sodium chloride is electrolysed. Write the half-equation for the reaction at the anode.
4 marks
Answer
At the cathode: hydrogen (because sodium is more reactive than hydrogen, so hydrogen ions are discharged instead) [1].
At the anode: chlorine (because it is a concentrated halide solution) [1].
Anode half-equation: 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻ [2: 1 for correct formula, 1 for balanced electrons]
Two Cl⁻ ions each lose one electron = 2e⁻ released total. They pair up to form Cl₂ (a molecule, not just Cl). The leftover Na⁺ and OH⁻ in solution form sodium hydroxide.

7
A student electrolyses dilute sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) using carbon electrodes. Predict the products at each electrode and explain your reasoning.
4 marks
Answer
At the cathode: hydrogen [1], because H⁺ ions gain electrons / hydrogen is the only positive ion that can be discharged (there is no metal less reactive than hydrogen in solution) [1].
At the anode: oxygen [1], because there is no halide ion present, so OH⁻ ions from water are discharged instead [1].
Dilute sulfuric acid has H⁺ and SO₄²⁻ ions, plus H⁺ and OH⁻ from water. No metal ions, no halide ions — so you get hydrogen and oxygen. This is effectively the decomposition of water.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
A dilute solution of copper(II) sulfate is electrolysed using carbon electrodes. (a) Name the product at each electrode. (b) Write the half-equation for the reaction at the cathode. (c) Explain why the blue colour of the solution fades during electrolysis.
6 marks
Answer
(a) At the cathode: copper [1] (copper is less reactive than hydrogen, so Cu²⁺ ions are discharged). At the anode: oxygen [1] (no halide present, so OH⁻ ions are discharged).

(b) Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s) [2: 1 for correct formula, 1 for balanced electrons and state symbols]

(c) The blue colour is caused by Cu²⁺ ions in solution [1]. As electrolysis proceeds, Cu²⁺ ions are deposited as copper metal at the cathode, so the concentration of Cu²⁺ ions decreases and the blue colour fades [1].
The "colour fading" explanation is a classic exam question. You must link the colour to the specific ion (Cu²⁺) and explain that its concentration is decreasing because it is being discharged at the cathode.

9
The same copper(II) sulfate solution is now electrolysed using copper electrodes instead of carbon. (a) State what happens at each electrode. (b) Explain why the blue colour of the solution does not change. (c) Describe how this principle is used in copper refining.
6 marks
Answer
(a) At the cathode: copper is deposited / the cathode grows larger (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu) [1]. At the anode: the copper electrode dissolves / the anode shrinks (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻) [1].

(b) The blue colour stays the same because Cu²⁺ ions are being removed at the cathode but replaced at the same rate by the dissolving anode [1]. So the concentration of Cu²⁺ ions remains constant [1].

(c) In copper refining, the anode is made of impure copper and the cathode is pure copper [1]. The impure copper dissolves; pure copper is deposited on the cathode. Impurities fall to the bottom as sludge [1].
The key contrast with Q8: carbon electrodes = colour fades (Cu²⁺ removed, not replaced). Copper electrodes = colour stays (Cu²⁺ removed AND replaced). The exam loves comparing these two scenarios.

10
You want to electroplate a steel spoon with silver. (a) State what you would use as the anode, cathode, and electrolyte. (b) Write the half-equation for the reaction at each electrode. (c) Give two reasons why electroplating is carried out.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Anode: silver (the plating metal) [1]. Cathode: the steel spoon (the object to be plated) [1]. Electrolyte: silver nitrate solution (a soluble silver compound).

(b) At the anode: Ag(s) → Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ (silver dissolves, oxidation) [1].
At the cathode: Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Ag(s) (silver deposited, reduction) [1].

(c) Any two of: to make the object look more attractive / shiny [1]; to prevent corrosion / rusting [1]; to make it more hard-wearing / durable.
The general rule: cathode = object, anode = plating metal, electrolyte = solution of a soluble compound of the plating metal. Silver only has a 1+ charge, so only 1 electron is transferred in each half-equation.
