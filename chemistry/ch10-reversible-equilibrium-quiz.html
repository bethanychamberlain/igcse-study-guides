Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
What is a reversible reaction? What symbol is used to show that a reaction is reversible?
2 marks
Answer
A reversible reaction is one where the products can react to re-form the original reactants [1]. The symbol ⇌ (two half-arrows pointing in opposite directions) is used instead of a single arrow [1].
The key phrase is "products can re-form the reactants" or "reaction can go in both directions." Just saying "it goes backwards" is too vague.

2
Hydrated copper(II) sulfate is heated. (a) What colour change occurs? (b) How can the reaction be reversed?
2 marks
Answer
(a) The crystals change from blue to white [1].
(b) Add water to the white anhydrous copper(II) sulfate — it turns blue again (and gets hot) [1].
Don't mix up the colours. Blue = hydrated (has water). White = anhydrous (water removed). This is also used as a test for water.

3
What does dynamic equilibrium mean? State two conditions that must be true when a reversible reaction is at equilibrium.
3 marks
Answer
Dynamic means the forward and reverse reactions are both still occurring [1].
At equilibrium: (1) the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction [1], and (2) the concentrations of reactants and products are no longer changing [1].
You MUST explain both words. "Dynamic" = still happening. "Equilibrium" = rates are equal / no overall change. Miss either word and you lose a mark.

4
Name the industrial process used to make ammonia. State the three conditions used (temperature, pressure, catalyst).
2 marks
Answer
The Haber process [1]. Conditions: temperature of 450°C, pressure of 200 atmospheres, and an iron catalyst [1].
All three conditions for 1 mark — miss any one and you may not get it. The name "Haber process" is a separate mark.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY using Le Chatelier's principle, not just name things.
5
The forward reaction in the Haber process is exothermic. Explain why lowering the temperature increases the yield of ammonia, but the Haber process still uses 450°C.
4 marks
Answer
The forward reaction is exothermic (gives out heat) [1]. Lowering the temperature favours the exothermic direction, so equilibrium shifts to the right and more ammonia is produced [1]. However, at low temperatures the rate of reaction is too slow [1]. 450°C is a compromise — it gives a reasonable rate of reaction while still producing a useful yield [1].
Four ideas: (1) forward is exothermic, (2) low temp favours exothermic = more product, (3) but rate too slow at low temp, (4) 450°C is a compromise. Use the word "compromise."

6
In the Haber process, explain why high pressure increases the yield of ammonia. Refer to the equation: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)
3 marks
Answer
There are 4 molecules of gas on the left (1 N₂ + 3 H₂) and 2 molecules on the right (2 NH₃) [1]. Increasing the pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules [1], so equilibrium shifts to the right, producing more ammonia [1].
Always count the gas molecules and state the numbers. "Fewer molecules on the right" is the key phrase. Don't just say "pressure pushes it right" — explain WHY.

7
In the Contact process, the key reaction is: 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g). The forward reaction is exothermic. State the conditions used and the catalyst, then explain why only 2 atmospheres of pressure is used.
4 marks
Answer
Conditions: 450°C and 2 atmospheres pressure [1]. Catalyst: vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) [1]. There are 3 gas molecules on the left and 2 on the right, so higher pressure would increase the yield [1]. However, the yield at 2 atm is already very high, so increasing pressure further would add cost without significant benefit [1].
The reasoning is different from the Haber process! Here the yield is already high at low pressure, so it's not worth the extra cost. In the Haber process, high pressure is needed because the yield would be too low otherwise.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
The Haber process converter does not operate as a closed system. Explain how the design of the process ensures that the overall yield of ammonia is close to 100%, even though less than 30% is converted each time through the converter.
5 marks
Answer
The gases flow continuously through the converter, so it is not a closed system and equilibrium is never fully reached [1]. After leaving the converter, the mixture is cooled so that ammonia condenses to a liquid and is removed [1]. Removing the product prevents equilibrium from being established and shifts the reaction towards the products side [1]. The unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen are recycled back to the converter for another pass [1]. By repeating steps 3 and 4 continually, the overall yield approaches almost 100% even though each single pass converts less than 30% [1].
Five points: (1) not a closed system, (2) ammonia condensed and removed, (3) removing product shifts equilibrium right, (4) unreacted gases recycled, (5) repeated passes give near-100% overall yield. This is a classic extended-response question.

9
Hydrogen and bromine react reversibly: H₂(g) + Br₂(g) ⇌ 2HBr(g). (a) Explain why increasing the pressure has no effect on the position of equilibrium for this reaction. (b) State two changes that would increase the yield of hydrogen bromide.
5 marks
Answer
(a) There are 2 molecules of gas on the left (1 H₂ + 1 Br₂) and 2 molecules of gas on the right (2 HBr) [1]. Since the number of gas molecules is the same on both sides, changing the pressure does not favour either direction [1], so the position of equilibrium is unaffected [1].

(b) Any two of: add more hydrogen (or add more bromine) [1]; remove the hydrogen bromide as it forms [1]. (If the forward reaction is exothermic, lowering the temperature would also work.)
Part (a) is the tricky one — you must count molecules on both sides AND state that they are equal. For part (b), concentration changes are the safe answers since you don't need to know whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic.

10
Compare the Haber process and the Contact process. For each, state: (a) the product, (b) the balanced equation for the key reversible reaction, (c) the temperature, pressure, and catalyst used, and (d) one reason why the chosen temperature is a compromise.
6 marks
Answer
Haber process:
(a) Ammonia (NH₃) [1]
(b) N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) [1]
(c) 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst
(d) Lower temperature gives higher yield (forward is exothermic) but reaction too slow; 450°C is a compromise between rate and yield [1]

Contact process:
(a) Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) [1]
(b) 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g) [1]
(c) 450°C, 2 atm, vanadium(V) oxide catalyst
(d) Lower temperature gives higher yield (forward is exothermic) but the catalyst is inactive below 400°C; 450°C is a compromise [1]
This is the "if you can answer this, you own the chapter" question. Notice both processes use the same temperature (450°C) and both forward reactions are exothermic. The differences are: pressure (200 vs 2 atm), catalyst (iron vs V₂O₅), and why the compromise temperature is needed (rate vs catalyst activity).
