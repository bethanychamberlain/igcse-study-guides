Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
What is an ore?
1 mark
Answer
A rock from which a metal can be extracted economically (at a profit) [1].
The word "economically" (or "profitably") is key. Just saying "a rock containing metal" is not enough — many rocks contain metals but are not ores.

2
Name the ore and the main compound in it, for: (a) iron, (b) aluminium.
2 marks
Answer
(a) Hematite, mainly iron(III) oxide, Fe₂O₃ [1].
(b) Bauxite, mainly aluminium oxide, Al₂O₃ [1].
Learn these pairs: hematite/Fe₂O₃ and bauxite/Al₂O₃. They come up constantly.

3
Name the three raw materials added to the blast furnace.
2 marks
Answer
Iron ore (hematite), limestone, and coke [1 for all three named]. Hot air is blasted in through the bottom [1].
Know the purpose of each: ore provides the iron, coke is the fuel/reducing agent, limestone removes impurities as slag.

4
Define alloy and give one example.
2 marks
Answer
An alloy is a mixture of a metal with at least one other element [1]. Example: steel (iron + carbon), brass (copper + zinc), bronze (copper + tin), or stainless steel (iron + chromium + nickel) [1].
"Mixture of a metal with at least one other element" is the textbook definition. Don't say "mixture of two metals" — carbon is not a metal, but steel is still an alloy.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain why carbon can be used to extract iron but not aluminium.
3 marks
Answer
Iron is below carbon in the reactivity series [1], so carbon is reactive enough to displace iron from its oxide / reduce it [1]. Aluminium is above carbon in the reactivity series, so carbon is not reactive enough to reduce aluminium oxide. Electrolysis must be used instead [1].
The key concept: a more reactive element can displace a less reactive one. Carbon can only reduce metals below it in the reactivity series.

6
Give the word equation for the reaction in the blast furnace that produces iron. Explain why this is a reduction.
3 marks
Answer
iron(III) oxide + carbon monoxide → iron + carbon dioxide [1].
This is a reduction because the iron(III) oxide loses oxygen [1]. (Carbon monoxide is the reducing agent — it gains oxygen and is oxidised to carbon dioxide [1].)
Remember: reduction = loss of oxygen (or gain of electrons). The reducing agent is the substance that causes the reduction — here it's carbon monoxide.

7
In the extraction of aluminium, explain why cryolite is used.
3 marks
Answer
Aluminium oxide has a very high melting point (2072°C) [1]. Dissolving it in molten cryolite lowers the operating temperature to about 950°C [1], which saves energy/fuel costs. Cryolite also makes the mixture a better electrical conductor [1].
Two reasons: lower temperature (saves energy) and better conductor. Give both for full marks.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Describe the extraction of aluminium by electrolysis. Include: what happens at the cathode and anode, why the carbon anodes must be regularly replaced, and write the overall equation.
6 marks
Answer
Aluminium oxide is dissolved in molten cryolite and electrolysed [1].

At the cathode (−): Aluminium ions gain electrons and are reduced to molten aluminium. Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al [1].

At the anode (+): Oxide ions lose electrons and are oxidised to form oxygen gas. 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ [1].

The oxygen produced at the hot anode reacts with the carbon anode: C + O₂ → CO₂. The anodes gradually burn away, so they must be regularly replaced [1].

Overall equation: 2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂ [1]. The molten aluminium sinks to the bottom of the cell and is tapped off [1].
This is a classic 6-mark question. Hit every point: cryolite, cathode reaction, anode reaction, why anodes burn away, overall equation, how aluminium is collected. Use half-equations if you can for top marks.

9
Explain, in terms of structure, why alloys are harder and stronger than pure metals. Use the example of steel to illustrate your answer.
5 marks
Answer
In a pure metal (like iron), the atoms are all the same size and arranged in regular rows/layers [1]. When a force is applied, these layers can slide over each other easily, so the metal is soft and malleable [1].

In an alloy like steel, atoms of a different size (carbon) are added into the iron lattice [1]. These different-sized atoms disrupt the regular arrangement [1], making it harder for layers to slide past each other. So the alloy is harder and stronger than the pure metal [1].
The examiner wants: (1) pure metal = regular rows, (2) layers slide, (3) alloy = different-sized atoms in lattice, (4) disrupts arrangement, (5) layers can't slide = harder. Draw diagrams if the question allows it.

10
Iron and aluminium are both extracted in large quantities. Compare the two extraction processes, explaining: (a) why different methods are used, (b) the raw materials and conditions for each, and (c) why aluminium costs more per tonne than iron even though it is more abundant.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Iron is below carbon in the reactivity series, so it can be reduced by heating with carbon (coke) in a blast furnace [1]. Aluminium is above carbon, so it is too reactive to be reduced by carbon — electrolysis is needed instead [1].

(b) Iron extraction: hematite (Fe₂O₃) is heated with coke and limestone at about 1900°C. The iron melts and collects at the bottom [1]. Aluminium extraction: bauxite is purified to aluminium oxide, dissolved in molten cryolite, and electrolysed at about 950–1000°C. Aluminium collects at the carbon cathode [1].

(c) Aluminium costs more because electrolysis uses huge amounts of electricity, which is expensive [1]. The carbon anodes also burn away and must be regularly replaced, adding further cost [1].
For comparison questions, deal with both processes in each part — don't describe iron fully then aluminium fully. The examiner is looking for direct comparisons.
