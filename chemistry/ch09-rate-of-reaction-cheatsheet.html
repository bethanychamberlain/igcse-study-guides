The Rate of Reaction

Pin this to your wall

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Rate of reaction How fast reactants are used up or products are formed per unit of time. —
Collision theory Particles must collide with enough energy (≥ activation energy) for a reaction to happen. —
Activation energy (Eₐ) The minimum energy a collision needs to be successful. Overall energy change — Eₐ is the energy barrier, not the final result
Successful collision A collision where particles have enough energy to react. Not every collision is successful. "Collision" alone — many collisions bounce apart with no reaction
Catalyst A substance that increases the rate and is unchanged at the end. Provides a lower-energy pathway. A catalyst does NOT increase the amount of product — only makes it form faster
Enzyme A biological catalyst — a protein made by living cells. Example: catalase. Chemical catalysts like MnO₂ — enzymes are proteins, not metal compounds
Surface area The total exposed area of a solid. Smaller pieces = greater surface area. Mass — small chips and big chips can have the same mass but different surface areas

Five Factors That Increase Rate — How and Why

Factor What You Change Why It Works (Collision Theory)
Concentration More particles per unit volume More frequent collisions → more successful collisions
Temperature Particles move faster Two reasons: (1) more frequent collisions, (2) more particles exceed Eₐ
Surface area Smaller pieces of solid More particles exposed → more frequent successful collisions
Pressure (gases) Squeezes gas into smaller volume More particles per unit volume = higher concentration → more collisions
Catalyst Provides alternative pathway Lower Eₐ → more particles have enough energy → more successful collisions
Exam Trap — Temperature Has TWO Reasons
Most students only write "particles move faster, more collisions." That's one mark. For full marks, also write: "A greater proportion of collisions have energy equal to or greater than the activation energy, so more collisions are successful." Two reasons = two marks.

Reading a Rate Graph — What to Look For

Graph Feature What It Means
Steep curve at the start Reaction is fast — lots of reactant still available
Curve gets less steep Rate is decreasing — reactant is being used up
Line goes flat Reaction is complete — no more product being formed
Two curves, same final height Same amount of reactant used, but different rates
Steeper curve = ___ Faster reaction (higher concentration, temperature, or surface area)

Say It in One Sentence

Rate How much product forms (or reactant disappears) per unit of time — measured in cm³/min or g/s.
Collision theory Particles must collide with enough energy to react — only successful collisions count.
Concentration More particles in the same space means more collisions, so the reaction is faster.
Temperature Faster particles collide more often AND a greater fraction have enough energy to react.
Surface area Smaller pieces expose more surface to the other reactant, so more collisions happen.
Catalyst Lowers the activation energy so more particles can react — but doesn't change the final amount of product.
Average rate Total product ÷ total time — gives one number for the whole reaction.

Memory Tricks

The Five Factors Mnemonic
Cats That Scratch People + Catalyst — Concentration, Temperature, Surface area, Pressure, Catalyst. Five things that increase rate.
Temperature = "Faster AND Harder"
Higher temperature makes particles move faster (more collisions) AND hit harder (more energy per collision). Two effects, two marks. Always write both.
Catalyst = Lower Hump
On a reaction pathway diagram, the catalyst gives a smaller hump (lower Eₐ). Same start and end levels, same ΔH — just an easier route over the hill. Think of it as a tunnel through the mountain instead of climbing over.
Magic Sentence for Collision Theory Questions
"Increasing [factor] means [what happens to particles], so the frequency of successful collisions increases, so the rate increases." Fill in the blanks for each factor — this template gets full marks every time.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Explain using collision theory why increasing concentration increases rate" More particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → more successful collisions per unit of time → rate increases
"Explain why increasing temperature increases rate" Two reasons: (1) particles move faster, collide more frequently; (2) more particles have energy ≥ Eₐ, so a greater proportion of collisions are successful
"Why do small chips react faster than large chips?" Greater surface area exposed → more particles of the solid in contact with the other reactant → more frequent successful collisions
"Why is the final volume of gas the same for both experiments?" The same mass of reactant was used in both — changing the size of pieces doesn't change the total amount
"How can you tell from the graph that the reaction is finished?" The curve goes flat (levels off) — no more gas is being produced / no more mass is being lost
"Define catalyst" A substance that increases the rate of a reaction and is unchanged at the end of the reaction
"How does a catalyst increase the rate?" Provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy → more particles have enough energy for successful collisions
"Calculate the average rate" Average rate = total amount of product ÷ total time. Include units (e.g. cm³ per minute)
"Suggest how you could measure the rate of this reaction" Collect the gas in a gas syringe and record the volume at regular intervals, OR place on a balance and record mass loss over time
