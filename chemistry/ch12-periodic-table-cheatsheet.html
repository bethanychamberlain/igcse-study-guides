Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Group Vertical column. Same number of outer shell electrons. Similar chemical properties. Period (horizontal row — same number of electron shells)
Period Horizontal row. Period number = number of electron shells. Group (vertical column — same outer electrons)
Alkali metals (Group I) Soft metals with 1 outer electron. React with water to form hydroxides. Reactivity increases down the group. Transition metals (hard, dense, high melting point — the opposite!)
Halogens (Group VII) Reactive non-metals with 7 outer electrons. Exist as diatomic molecules (Cl₂, Br₂). Reactivity decreases down the group. Halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻) are the IONS formed when halogens gain an electron
Noble gases (Group VIII) Full outer shell. Unreactive. Monatomic (exist as single atoms). —
Transition elements Middle block of metals. High density, high melting point, coloured compounds, variable ion charges, good catalysts. Alkali metals (Group I) — opposite in almost every physical property
Displacement A more reactive element pushes a less reactive element out of its compound. —

Group I vs Group VII — Reactivity Trends

Group I (Alkali Metals) Group VII (Halogens)
Type Metals Non-metals
Outer electrons 1 (they LOSE it) 7 (they GAIN 1 more)
Reactivity going down Increases Decreases
Why? More shells → outer electron further from nucleus → easier to lose More shells → outer shell further from nucleus → harder to attract extra electron
Ion formed Positive (Na⁺, K⁺) Negative (Cl⁻, Br⁻)
Most reactive Caesium (bottom) Fluorine (top)
Exam Trap — Opposite Trends
The same reason (more shells = outer electron further away) causes opposite effects. Group I: easier to lose → more reactive down. Group VII: harder to gain → less reactive down. The exam loves testing this. Always state which way the trend goes AND explain why.

Alkali Metals + Water — What Happens

Metal What You See Key Word
Lithium Floats, fizzes gently, moves on surface Fizzes
Sodium Melts to a ball, shoots across water, vigorous fizzing Shoots
Potassium Melts, shoots across water, catches fire, lilac flame Burns

Word equation: sodium + water → sodium hydroxide + hydrogen

Violence increases down the group. Rubidium/caesium: predict "even more vigorous, may explode."

Rule: A halogen only displaces one below it in the group (less reactive one). Higher = more reactive = wins.

Transition Elements vs Group I Metals

Property Group I (e.g. Na) Transition (e.g. Fe, Cu)
Hardness Soft (cut with a knife) Hard and strong
Density Low (Li, Na, K float on water) High (Fe = 7.9 g/cm³)
Melting point Low (Na = 98°C) High (Fe = 1535°C)
Reactivity Very reactive Much less reactive
Compound colour White only Coloured (blue, green, red-brown)
Ion charge Always 1+ Variable (Fe²⁺ or Fe³⁺)
Catalytic use None Many (Fe in Haber, Pt in converters)

Say It in One Sentence

Group number Tells you how many outer shell electrons — Group II = 2 outer electrons.
Period number Tells you how many electron shells — Period 3 = 3 shells.
Group I trend More shells → outer electron easier to lose → more reactive going down.
Group VII trend More shells → harder to attract an extra electron → less reactive going down.
Displacement A more reactive halogen kicks out a less reactive one from its salt solution.
Transition metals Hard, dense, high melting point, coloured compounds, variable charges, good catalysts.
Noble gases Full outer shell = no need to react = the most stable elements.

Memory Tricks

Group I Water Reactions: Fizz, Shoot, Burn
Lithium fizzes. Sodium shoots. Potassium burns. Violence increases down the group. If they ask about rubidium, say "even more vigorous, may explode."
Halogen Colours: Light to Dark
Going down Group VII, colours get darker: chlorine = pale yellow-green gas, bromine = red-brown liquid, iodine = grey-black solid. Gas → liquid → solid. Boiling point increases.
Displacement Rule: "Higher Beats Lower"
A halogen only displaces one below it in the group. Cl₂ beats Br⁻ and I⁻. Br₂ beats I⁻ only. I₂ beats nobody. Think of it as a tournament — higher rank always wins.
Ion Charge from Group Number
Groups I, II, III: metals lose 1, 2, 3 electrons → charge 1+, 2+, 3+. Groups VI, VII: non-metals gain 2, 1 electrons → charge 2−, 1−. Group VIII: full shell, no ion, no reaction.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Element X is in Group II, Period 3. Predict its properties." 2 outer electrons, 3 shells, metal (left of zig-zag), forms 2+ ions, reacts with acids to form salts
"Why do Group I metals get more reactive going down?" More electron shells → outer electron is further from the nucleus → weaker attraction → easier to lose the electron → more reactive
"Why do halogens get less reactive going down?" More electron shells → outer shell is further from the nucleus → harder to attract an incoming electron → less reactive
"Write the word equation for potassium reacting with water" potassium + water → potassium hydroxide + hydrogen
"Chlorine is added to potassium bromide solution. What happens?" Chlorine displaces bromine. Solution turns orange. Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂
"Bromine is added to potassium chloride solution. What happens?" No reaction. Bromine is less reactive than chlorine, so it cannot displace it.
"Compare iron with sodium" (4–6 marks) (1) Iron is hard, sodium is soft. (2) Iron has high melting point, sodium has low. (3) Iron forms coloured compounds, sodium forms white. (4) Iron has variable oxidation states, sodium is always 1+. (5) Iron is a catalyst; sodium is not.
"Why are noble gases unreactive?" They have a full outer shell of electrons, so they do not need to lose, gain, or share electrons
"What does the Roman numeral in copper(II) oxide mean?" The oxidation number of copper is 2 — it has lost 2 electrons to form Cu²⁺
