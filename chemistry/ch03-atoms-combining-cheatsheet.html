Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Ionic bond Strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, formed by electron transfer (metal + non-metal). Covalent bond (sharing, not transferring)
Covalent bond A shared pair of electrons between two non-metal atoms. Both nuclei attract the shared pair. Ionic bond (transfer, not sharing)
Metallic bond Attraction between positive metal ions and a sea of delocalised electrons. Ionic bond (metallic has no anions — just + ions and free electrons)
Ion A charged particle — atom that has gained or lost electrons. Proton count does NOT change. Atom (an atom is neutral; an ion has a charge)
Cation Positive ion — atom that lost electrons (metals). E.g. Na⁺, Mg²⁺. Anion (negative — gained electrons)
Anion Negative ion — atom that gained electrons (non-metals). E.g. Cl⁻, O²⁻. Cation (positive — lost electrons)
Molecule Two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds. E.g. H₂O, CO₂. Lattice (ionic compounds form lattices, NOT molecules)
Allotropes Different physical forms of the same element. E.g. diamond and graphite are both carbon. Isotopes (same element, different neutrons — that's Chapter 2)
Delocalised electrons Electrons that are free to move through the whole structure (not attached to one atom). —

Ionic vs Covalent vs Metallic — Side by Side

Ionic Covalent (molecular) Metallic
Who bonds? Metal + non-metal Non-metal + non-metal Metal + metal
What happens? Electrons transferred Electrons shared Electrons delocalised
Structure Giant ionic lattice Small molecules Giant metallic lattice
Melting point High Low High
Conducts electricity? Only when melted or dissolved No (never) Yes (always)
Why conducts / doesn't? Ions free to move only when melted/dissolved No free ions or electrons Delocalised electrons free to move
Exam Trap — Ionic Solids Don't Conduct
Solid ionic compounds do NOT conduct — the ions are locked in the lattice. They only conduct when melted or dissolved because the ions become free to move. Write the exact phrase: "ions are free to move to carry the charge."

Diamond vs Graphite — Both Carbon!

Diamond Graphite
Bonds per C atom 4 (tetrahedral) 3 (flat hexagonal layers)
Structure Rigid 3D network Layers with weak forces between
Hardness Very hard (hardest natural substance) Soft and slippery (layers slide)
Conducts electricity? No (all electrons in bonds) Yes (4th electron is delocalised)
Melting point Very high (3550°C) Very high
Exam Trap — "Weak Bonds" in Graphite
Never say graphite is soft "because it has weak bonds." The covalent bonds within the layers are strong. It's the forces between the layers that are weak, allowing them to slide.

Say It in One Sentence

Why atoms bond To get a full outer shell of electrons, like the noble gases (8 electrons, or 2 for helium).
Ionic bonding Metal gives its outer electrons to a non-metal, creating positive and negative ions that attract each other.
Covalent bonding Two non-metals share electron pairs so both atoms end up with full outer shells.
Metallic bonding Metal atoms release outer electrons into a "sea" — positive ions sit in a sea of free electrons, held by attraction.
Why metals conduct Delocalised electrons are free to move through the lattice, carrying charge as a current.
Why metals are malleable Layers of ions slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond — the electron sea moves with them.
Diatomic elements Elements whose molecules are pairs of atoms: H₂, O₂, N₂, Cl₂, I₂.

Memory Tricks

Which Bonding? 3-Second Test
Metal + non-metal = ionic. Non-metal + non-metal = covalent. Metal + metal = metallic. Just check which side of the zig-zag line each element sits on. Done.
Positive Cat, Negative An
Cation = positive ion. Anion = negative ion. Remember: a cat is positive (friendly!). "An" = a negative.
How Many Bonds? Count What's Missing
Number of covalent bonds = electrons needed to fill the outer shell. H needs 1 = 1 bond. O needs 2 = 2 bonds. N needs 3 = 3 bonds. C needs 4 = 4 bonds. Use this to check your dot-and-cross diagrams.
Ionic Formula Shortcut
Write the ion charges, then criss-cross: Al³⁺ and O²⁻ → Al₂O₃. The charge of one becomes the subscript of the other. Total charge must be zero.
NaCl Is NOT "Molecules"
Never say "sodium chloride molecules." NaCl is ionic — it forms a lattice of ions, not individual molecules. Only covalent substances form molecules.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Describe how an ionic bond forms in NaCl" Sodium loses 1 electron to form Na⁺. Chlorine gains 1 electron to form Cl⁻. Both get full outer shells. The strong electrostatic attraction between the oppositely charged ions is the ionic bond
"Why does a Na⁺ ion have a charge of 1+?" It has 11 protons (+) and only 10 electrons (−), so the overall charge is 1+ (number of protons NEVER changes)
"What is a covalent bond?" A shared pair of electrons between two atoms
"Explain why NaCl has a high melting point" NaCl has a giant ionic lattice with strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions. A lot of energy is needed to overcome these forces
"Why does NaCl conduct when dissolved but not as a solid?" Solid: ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move. Dissolved: ions are free to move and carry the charge
"Why do covalent substances have low melting points?" The intermolecular forces (forces between molecules) are weak and easy to overcome. Note: the covalent bonds themselves are strong — it's the forces between molecules that are weak
"Why is diamond hard but graphite soft?" Diamond: each C bonded to 4 others in a rigid 3D network. Graphite: C bonded to 3 others in layers — weak forces between layers let them slide
"Why does graphite conduct electricity?" Each carbon uses only 3 of its 4 outer electrons for bonding. The 4th electron is delocalised (free to move), carrying charge as a current
"Explain why metals are good conductors" Metal atoms lose outer electrons, which become delocalised. These delocalised electrons are free to move through the lattice, carrying charge as a current
"Data table: identify the type of bonding" High melting point + conducts when melted/dissolved = ionic. Low melting point + never conducts = molecular covalent. High melting point + always conducts = metallic
