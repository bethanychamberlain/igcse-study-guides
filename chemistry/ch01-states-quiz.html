Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the three states of matter and give one example of each.
2 marks
Answer
Solid (e.g. ice), liquid (e.g. water), gas (e.g. steam/water vapour) [1 for all three named, 1 for correct examples].
Any valid examples accepted. Don't use the same substance in different states unless you name the state clearly.

2
Define diffusion.
2 marks
Answer
The net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration [1] to a region of lower concentration [1].
Examiner wants: direction (high to low) + "particles" or "molecules." Do NOT mention membranes here — that's osmosis (biology).

3
What is sublimation? Give one example.
2 marks
Answer
Sublimation is when a solid changes directly into a gas without becoming a liquid first [1]. Example: dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) [1].
"Without becoming a liquid" is the key phrase. Other examples: iodine crystals turning to purple vapour.

4
State one difference between boiling and evaporation.
1 mark
Answer
Any one of: Boiling happens at a fixed temperature (boiling point) while evaporation can happen at any temperature [1]. Or: Boiling happens throughout the liquid while evaporation only happens at the surface [1].
Either difference gets the mark. For 2-mark versions, give both.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY using particle theory, not just name things.
5
A student heats a solid substance from room temperature until it becomes a gas. Describe what happens to the arrangement and movement of particles at each stage.
4 marks
Answer
Solid: Particles are in a regular pattern and vibrate in fixed positions [1].
When melting: Particles gain energy, overcome some forces, and start to slide past each other — arrangement becomes irregular [1].
Liquid: Particles are close together but move around freely with no fixed pattern [1].
When boiling: Particles gain enough energy to completely overcome the forces between them, move far apart, and travel quickly in all directions [1].
The key words: "regular pattern" (solid), "vibrate" (solid), "slide past" (liquid), "far apart / all directions" (gas). Use them.

6
A heating curve shows that the temperature of a substance stays constant at 80°C for several minutes, even though heating continues. Explain why the temperature does not rise during this time.
3 marks
Answer
At 80°C the substance is changing state (melting or boiling) [1]. The energy being supplied is used to overcome/break the forces between particles [1], rather than to increase the kinetic energy of the particles, so the temperature does not rise [1].
Three marks, three ideas: (1) it's changing state, (2) energy goes into breaking inter-particle forces, (3) not into kinetic energy so temp stays flat. This exact pattern comes up almost every year.

7
A bottle of perfume is opened in the corner of a room. After a few minutes, a person at the other side of the room can smell it. Explain this observation using particle theory.
3 marks
Answer
The perfume particles evaporate and become a gas [1]. The gas particles move randomly in all directions and spread out from a region of higher concentration (near the bottle) to a region of lower concentration (across the room) [1]. This process is called diffusion [1].
Name the process (diffusion), explain the direction (high to low concentration), and mention random particle movement. Don't just say "the smell travels" — explain WHY it travels.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
In an experiment, cotton wool soaked in ammonia solution (NH₃, Mᵣ = 17) is placed at one end of a glass tube, and cotton wool soaked in hydrochloric acid (HCl, Mᵣ = 36.5) is placed at the other end. A white ring of ammonium chloride forms inside the tube, closer to the HCl end. Explain these observations.
5 marks
Answer
Both ammonia and HCl gases diffuse along the tube from opposite ends [1]. The white ring forms where the two gases meet and react to form ammonium chloride [1]. Ammonia has a lower relative molecular mass (17) than HCl (36.5) [1]. At the same temperature, lighter particles move faster [1], so ammonia diffuses further along the tube and the ring forms closer to the HCl end [1].
5 points: both gases diffuse, they react where they meet, ammonia is lighter, lighter = faster, so ammonia travels further. This is one of the most common 5-mark questions in IGCSE Chemistry.

9
A sealed container of gas is heated from 25°C to 100°C. The volume of the container does not change. Explain, using particle theory, why the pressure inside the container increases.
5 marks
Answer
When the temperature increases, the gas particles gain more kinetic energy [1] and move faster [1]. The particles collide with the walls of the container more frequently (more often) [1] and with greater force (harder) [1]. Since pressure depends on the number and force of collisions with the walls, the pressure increases [1]. The volume is fixed, so the particles cannot spread out to reduce the pressure.
The chain: heat → more kinetic energy → faster movement → more frequent collisions + harder collisions → higher pressure. Each link in the chain is a mark.

10
Compare the three states of matter in terms of: (a) particle arrangement, (b) particle movement, (c) forces between particles. Then explain why gases can be compressed but solids cannot.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Arrangement: In a solid, particles are in a regular, fixed pattern [1]. In a liquid, particles are close together but arranged randomly [1]. In a gas, particles are far apart with no pattern.

(b) Movement: Solid particles vibrate in fixed positions. Liquid particles slide past each other. Gas particles move quickly and randomly in all directions [1].

(c) Forces: Solids have strong forces between particles. Liquids have weaker forces. Gases have very weak or negligible forces [1].

Compression: Gases can be compressed because the particles are far apart with lots of empty space between them, so they can be pushed closer together [1]. Solids cannot be compressed because the particles are already touching / tightly packed with no space to move closer [1].
This is the "if you can answer this, you own the chapter" question. For each part (a-c), describe all three states in one clear sentence. Then the compression explanation needs both sides: why gases CAN and why solids CANNOT.
