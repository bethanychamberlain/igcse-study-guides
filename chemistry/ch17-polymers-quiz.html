Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the terms: (a) polymer, (b) monomer, (c) polymerisation.
3 marks
Answer
(a) A polymer is a very large molecule made by joining together many small molecules [1].
(b) A monomer is the small molecule that is the repeating unit in a polymer [1].
(c) Polymerisation is the reaction in which monomers join to form a polymer [1].
These three definitions come up almost every year. Learn them word for word. "Very large molecule" is key for polymer — don't just say "big."

Name the two types of polymerisation reaction.
2 marks
Answer
Addition polymerisation [1] and condensation polymerisation [1].
Spelling counts. "Condensation" not "condensing." "Addition" not "adding."

Give three general properties of plastics.
3 marks
Answer
Any three from: they are electrical/thermal insulators [1]; they are unreactive (resistant to water, acids, chemicals) [1]; they are lightweight [1]; they are strong and hard to tear [1]; they are not brittle (do not shatter) [1]; they do not catch fire easily [1].
Be specific. "Useful" is not a property. "Lightweight" or "does not conduct electricity" are properties.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Ethene undergoes addition polymerisation to form poly(ethene). (a) What feature of the ethene molecule makes this possible? (b) Explain why it is not possible to give an exact molecular formula for poly(ethene).
3 marks
Answer
(a) Ethene has a C=C double bond [1]. The double bond breaks open, allowing the molecules to add on to each other.

(b) During polymerisation, the chains that form have different lengths [1] — the number of monomer units varies from molecule to molecule. So we can only give a general formula using brackets and n, not an exact molecular formula [1].
For (a), you must name the C=C double bond specifically. For (b), the key idea is "different chain lengths." Don't just say "it's too big."

6
State three differences between addition polymerisation and condensation polymerisation.
3 marks
Answer
• In addition polymerisation, monomers must have a C=C double bond; in condensation polymerisation, monomers have two functional groups (no double bond needed) [1].
• Addition polymerisation produces only one product (the polymer); condensation polymerisation produces two products (the polymer + a small molecule, usually water) [1].
• Addition polymerisation uses one type of monomer; condensation polymerisation typically uses two different monomers [1].
Each difference needs BOTH sides stated clearly. Don't just describe one type — compare them directly.

7
Nylon is a condensation polymer. (a) Name the type of polymer nylon is. (b) Name the two types of monomer used to make nylon. (c) Name the small molecule eliminated during the reaction. (d) Name the linkage formed.
4 marks
Answer
(a) Nylon is a polyamide [1].
(b) A dicarboxylic acid and a diamine [1].
(c) Water (H₂O) [1].
(d) The amide linkage (–CONH–) [1].
Don't confuse polyamide with polyester. Amide = acid + amine (think: am-ide = am-ine). Ester = acid + alcohol.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Plastics are very difficult to dispose of. Describe the environmental problems caused by plastic waste: (a) in landfill sites, (b) in the ocean, and (c) when burned in the open air.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Landfill: Plastics decompose very slowly, so they take up landfill space for hundreds of years [1]. As they break down, they may release toxic chemicals that leak into soil and water supplies [1].

(b) Ocean: Marine animals mistake plastic for food and eat it; the plastic fills their guts and they starve to death [1]. Plastics break into tiny microparticles that accumulate in food chains and damage marine life [1].

(c) Burning in open air: Burning plastic releases toxic gases [1] that can cause serious health problems including damage to the heart, lungs, and nervous system [1].
Six marks, so you need two clear points for each location. Don't just say "pollution" — name the specific problem (toxic chemicals, microparticles, toxic gases) and the specific harm (contamination, starvation, health damage).

9
Proteins are natural polymers. (a) Name the monomers of proteins. (b) Name two functional groups present in each monomer. (c) What type of polymerisation forms proteins? (d) Name the linkage formed. (e) Explain why proteins are sometimes called "natural polyamides."
5 marks
Answer
(a) Amino acids [1].
(b) The carboxyl group (–COOH) and the amine group (–NH₂) [1].
(c) Condensation polymerisation [1].
(d) The amide linkage (–CONH–) [1].
(e) Proteins contain the same amide linkage as synthetic polyamides like nylon. Because they are made by nature (not in a factory) and contain amide links, they are called natural polyamides [1].
This question connects Chapter 17 to biology. The key insight: the amide linkage in nylon is chemically the same as the one in every protein in your body.

10
Suggest four different ways to reduce the amount of plastic waste in the environment. For two of your suggestions, explain how they work.
6 marks
Answer
Any four approaches [1 each, max 4]:
• Reduce/ban single-use plastics (e.g. straws, bags, cups)
• Recycle plastics (e.g. PET bottles can be shredded, melted, and remoulded)
• Reuse plastic items (e.g. refill bottles instead of buying new ones)
• Use biodegradable plastics / bioplastics (e.g. PLA from maize, which can be broken down by microbes)
• Burn in incinerators (where toxic gases are trapped and heat generates electricity)

Explanations [1 each for any two]:
• Recycling works because plastic like PET can be collected, cleaned, shredded into pellets, melted, and reformed into new products — reducing the amount going to landfill [1].
• Biodegradable plastics work because their polymer chains contain links that can be attacked by microbes. The microbes break down the plastic into CO₂ and water, so it does not persist in the environment for centuries [1].
Four approaches = 4 marks. Two explained = 2 marks. Don't just list — the explanations must say HOW or WHY the approach reduces waste.
