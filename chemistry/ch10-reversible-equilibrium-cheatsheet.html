Reversible Reactions & Equilibrium

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Reversible reaction A reaction that can go in both directions — products can re-form the original reactants. Symbol: ⇌ Irreversible reactions (single arrow →, products can't go back)
Dynamic equilibrium Forward and reverse reactions happen at the same rate. Concentrations stay constant. Both reactions are still happening. "Equilibrium" does NOT mean equal amounts — it means the amounts have stopped changing
Closed system Nothing can enter or leave. Equilibrium can only be reached in a closed system. Open system — products escape and equilibrium is never reached
Le Chatelier's principle If you change conditions at equilibrium, the system shifts to oppose the change. —
Hydrated Contains water molecules in the crystal structure (e.g. blue CuSO₄·5H₂O). Anhydrous — water-free form (e.g. white CuSO₄)
Compromise Industrial conditions chosen to balance yield, rate, cost, and safety. —

Le Chatelier's Principle — Master Table

Change You Make Equilibrium Shifts To... Why
Increase temperature The endothermic direction System absorbs the extra heat
Decrease temperature The exothermic direction System releases heat to oppose cooling
Increase pressure Side with fewer gas molecules Fewer molecules = lower pressure
Decrease pressure Side with more gas molecules More molecules = higher pressure
Add more reactant Towards products System uses up the extra reactant
Remove product Towards products System makes more to replace it
Add a catalyst No shift — yield unchanged Speeds up BOTH directions equally
Exam Trap — "A Catalyst Increases the Yield"
This is WRONG and costs marks every time. A catalyst speeds up the rate (gets to equilibrium faster) but does NOT change the yield (position of equilibrium is the same). Temperature and pressure change yield. Catalyst does not.

Colour Changes to Memorise

Substance Hydrated (with water) Anhydrous (without water)
Copper(II) sulfate Blue crystals White powder
Cobalt(II) chloride Pink Blue
Water Test
Add a liquid to anhydrous copper(II) sulfate: white → blue = water is present. Add a liquid to cobalt(II) chloride paper: blue → pink = water is present. These only show water is present, not that it is pure.

Say It in One Sentence

Reversible Products can react to re-form reactants — use the ⇌ symbol instead of →.
Dynamic Both forward and reverse reactions are still happening — it's not stopped, just balanced.
Equilibrium Forward and reverse rates are equal, so concentrations stay constant (but NOT necessarily equal).
Le Chatelier Push the system one way and it pushes back — but settles at a new balance point.
Compromise 450°C is used because lower temp = better yield but reaction is too slow; it's a trade-off.
Haber process N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ at 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst — makes ammonia for fertilisers.
Contact process 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ at 450°C, 2 atm, V₂O₅ catalyst — makes sulfuric acid.

Memory Tricks

Temperature → "The System Fights Back"
If you heat it up, the system tries to cool down by going endothermic. If you cool it down, the system fights back by going exothermic. The system always opposes you.
Pressure → Count the Molecules
Write the numbers above the equation: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ = 4 molecules ⇌ 2 molecules. High pressure favours the smaller number. If both sides have the same number, pressure has no effect.
Haber vs Contact — Same Temp, Different Pressure
Both use 450°C. The Haber process needs 200 atm (big pressure difference needed). The Contact process only needs 2 atm (yield is already high). Remember: Haber = High pressure. Contact = Calm pressure.
CuSO₄ Colours
Blue = Big crystals (hydrated, with water). White = Without water (anhydrous). Adding water turns it back to blue.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What is meant by dynamic equilibrium?" The forward and reverse reactions are occurring at the same rate, so there is no overall change in the concentrations of reactants and products
"The forward reaction is exothermic. What happens to the yield if temperature increases?" Yield decreases. Higher temperature favours the endothermic (reverse) direction, making less product.
"What happens to the yield if pressure increases?" (with more gas moles on left) Yield increases. Higher pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules (the products side).
"Why is 450°C used in the Haber process?" It is a compromise. Lower temperature gives higher yield (forward is exothermic), but the rate would be too slow. 450°C gives a reasonable rate with a useful yield.
"What effect does a catalyst have on the yield?" None. A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally. It reaches equilibrium faster but does NOT change the position of equilibrium.
"Why is ammonia removed as soon as it is formed?" Removing product shifts equilibrium towards products (Le Chatelier's). Unreacted gases are recycled, giving an overall yield close to 100%.
"Name the catalyst used in the Haber process" Iron
"Name the catalyst used in the Contact process" Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅)
"Why is SO₃ dissolved in H₂SO₄ and not added directly to water?" SO₃ reacts violently with water, producing a dangerous acid mist. It is safer to dissolve it in concentrated sulfuric acid first (making oleum), then carefully dilute.
"How can you test for the presence of water?" Add the liquid to anhydrous copper(II) sulfate — turns from white to blue if water is present
