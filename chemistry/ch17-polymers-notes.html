Section 17.1 Introducing Polymers
Polymers are everywhere — you eat them (starch), wear them (nylon, polyester), and you are partly made of them (proteins, DNA). This section introduces the key vocabulary you need for the whole chapter.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Polymer: A very large molecule made by joining together thousands of small molecules.
• Monomer: The small repeating molecule that joins to make a polymer.
• Polymerisation: The reaction in which monomers join to form a polymer.
• Poly- means "many." So poly(ethene) = many ethene units joined together.

W
Write This Down — Natural vs Synthetic

Natural polymers are made by living things:

• Starch — made from glucose monomers (in rice, bread, potatoes)
• Cellulose — also from glucose (in cotton, wood, paper)
• Proteins — made from amino acid monomers (in skin, hair, muscles)
• Keratin (hair, nails), collagen (skin, bones) — both proteins

Synthetic polymers are made in factories from chemicals obtained from petroleum:

• Poly(ethene) (polythene), nylon, polyester, PVC, polystyrene, Teflon (PTFE), acrylic

W
Write This Down — Two Types of Polymerisation

There are two different types of polymerisation reaction:
• Addition polymerisation — monomers with C=C double bonds add on to each other
• Condensation polymerisation — monomers with two functional groups join, losing a small molecule (usually water)

?
Why This Matters

Polymer questions appear on almost every IGCSE Chemistry exam. You need to know the difference between the two types of polymerisation, be able to draw diagrams for each, and discuss environmental problems. This chapter is high-value for marks.

  • I can define polymer, monomer, and polymerisation
  • I can give three examples of natural polymers and name their monomers
  • I can give three examples of synthetic polymers
  • I can name the two types of polymerisation
Section 17.2 Addition Polymerisation
Addition polymerisation is how most plastics are made. The monomer must have a C=C double bond. The double bonds break open and the molecules link together in a long chain. Only one product is formed.
W
Write This Down — How It Works

In addition polymerisation, the C=C double bonds in the monomers break, and the molecules add on to each other to form a long chain.

Key features:
• The monomer must have a C=C double bond (it must be unsaturated)
• Only one product is formed (the polymer) — no small molecule is lost
• The polymer has only single bonds in the main chain (all double bonds have opened)

W

W
Write This Down — Short-Form Equation

The polymerisation equation is shown using brackets and n:

n CH₂=CH₂ → –(CH₂–CH₂)ₙ–

The double bond in the monomer becomes a single bond in the polymer. n stands for a very large number (many thousands).

W
Write This Down — Identifying the Monomer from the Polymer

To find the monomer from a polymer chain:
• Find the repeating unit (two carbon atoms side by side in the main chain, with their attached groups)
• Draw brackets around it
• Replace the single bond between the two carbons with a C=C double bond
• That is your monomer

!
Don't Confuse — No Exact Formula

Poly(ethene) does not have an exact molecular formula because the chains have different lengths. That is why we use the general formula with brackets and n. Don't try to write a molecular formula for a polymer — the exam will mark it wrong.

T
Study Tip — Practice the Drawing

The exam often gives you a monomer and asks you to draw part of the polymer chain, or gives you a polymer and asks for the monomer. Practice going both directions: monomer → polymer (break the double bond, link the carbons) and polymer → monomer (find the repeat unit, restore the double bond). Do at least three examples each way.

  • I can describe how addition polymerisation works
  • I know the monomer must have a C=C double bond
  • I can draw the polymer from the monomer (and vice versa)
  • I can name 4 addition polymers and their monomers
Section 17.3 Condensation Polymerisation
Condensation polymerisation is different: it uses two different monomers, each with two functional groups, and a small molecule (usually water) is eliminated each time two monomers join. Nylon and polyester are made this way — and so are your proteins.
W
Write This Down — How It Differs From Addition
Addition Condensation
Monomers One type, must have C=C Two different types, each with two functional groups
Products Polymer only Polymer + small molecule (usually water)
Bond type C=C breaks → C–C forms Functional groups react, losing H₂O
Examples Poly(ethene), PVC, PTFE Nylon, polyester (PET), proteins
W
Write This Down — Making a Polyamide (Nylon)

Two monomers are used:
• Monomer A: a dicarboxylic acid (has –COOH at each end)
• Monomer B: a diamine (has –NH₂ at each end)

The –COOH of one monomer reacts with the –NH₂ of the other. A water molecule is eliminated and a C–N bond forms. The link formed is called an amide linkage (–CONH–).

The polymer is called a polyamide. Nylon is a polyamide.

W
Write This Down — Making a Polyester (PET)

Two monomers are used:
• Monomer C: a dicarboxylic acid (has –COOH at each end)
• Monomer D: a diol (has –OH at each end)

The –COOH of one monomer reacts with the –OH of the other. A water molecule is eliminated and a C–O bond forms. The link formed is called an ester linkage (–COO–).

The polymer is called a polyester. PET (used for drinks bottles and clothing) is a polyester.

W
Write This Down — PET Can Be Recycled

PET can be broken down to its monomers by reaction with steam at high temperature and pressure. This is called hydrolysis (the reverse of condensation — water is added back). The monomers can then be re-polymerised to make new PET.

!
Don't Confuse — Amide vs Ester Linkage

Amide linkage (–CONH–): found in polyamides (nylon) and proteins. Formed from –COOH + –NH₂.
Ester linkage (–COO–): found in polyesters (PET). Formed from –COOH + –OH.
The difference is which functional group the acid reacts with: an amine gives an amide, an alcohol gives an ester.

T
Study Tip — Block Diagrams

The textbook shows monomers as coloured blocks with functional groups at each end. Copy these diagrams — they make it much easier to see where the water molecule is lost and where the new bond forms. Circle the linkage (–CONH– or –COO–) in your drawing. The exam often asks you to identify or circle the linkage.

  • I can list the differences between addition and condensation polymerisation
  • I can name the two monomers for making a polyamide and describe the reaction
  • I can name the two monomers for making a polyester and describe the reaction
  • I can identify amide and ester linkages in a polymer chain
  • I can explain how PET can be recycled (hydrolysis + re-polymerisation)
Section 17.4 Plastics
Plastics are synthetic polymers. They are incredibly useful because of their properties, but their unreactivity makes them very difficult to dispose of. This section covers properties, uses, and the link between structure and properties.
W
Write This Down — General Properties of Plastics

Plastics are synthetic polymers. Their general properties:

• Do not conduct electricity or heat (good insulators)
• Unreactive — resistant to water, air, acids, and other chemicals
• Lightweight — much lighter than metals, glass, or wood
• Strong — long molecules attract each other, making them hard to tear
• Do not break easily (not brittle like glass)
• Do not catch fire easily, but soften and melt when heated

W

W
Write This Down — HDPE vs LDPE

Different conditions during polymerisation change the properties:
• HDPE (50 °C, 4 atm, catalyst): long straight chains packed closely → rigid and strong
• LDPE (200 °C, 2000 atm, oxygen): branched chains that can't pack closely → flexible and soft

?
Why This Matters

The exam can ask you to explain why a particular plastic is chosen for a particular use. For example: "Why is PVC used for water pipes?" Answer: it is unreactive (won't corrode or react with water), waterproof, strong, lightweight, and can be moulded into shape. Always link the property to the use.

  • I can list at least 5 general properties of plastics
  • I can name 4 plastics and give a use for each
  • I can explain the difference between HDPE and LDPE
Sections 17.5 – 17.6 The Plastics Problem and Solutions
The same property that makes plastics so useful — their unreactivity — is exactly what makes them an environmental disaster. They do not decompose for hundreds of years. This section covers the problems and the solutions.
W
Write This Down — The Problem

By 2015, the world had produced 8.3 billion tonnes of plastic. Of this: 60% in landfill or the environment, 24% still in use, 9% burned, 7% recycled.

Plastics are so stable that they decompose very slowly — a plastic bottle could last 450 years in a landfill.

W
Write This Down — Problems in Three Places

In landfill: takes up space; animals eat plastic and die; as it breaks down it releases toxic chemicals into soil and rivers.

In the ocean: marine animals eat plastic and starve; plastics break into microparticles that accumulate in food chains; ocean currents create huge "plastic islands."

When burned: releases toxic gases that damage health (heart, lungs, nervous system). Must be burned in incinerators with gas-trapping systems, not in the open air.

W
Write This Down — Five Solutions

• 1. Reduce single-use plastics — ban straws, bags, disposable cups
• 2. Burn with care — use incinerators to trap toxic gases; heat can generate electricity. Also: pyrolysis (melting plastic without oxygen to break it down into new products)
• 3. Make plastics biodegradable — design polymers with links that microbes can attack. Bioplastics (e.g. PLA from maize) are an alternative
• 4. Recycle — PET bottles can be shredded, melted, and re-moulded into new products (bottles, carpet fibres, clothing)
• 5. Reuse — refill bottles, reuse containers

?
Why This Matters

Environmental questions about plastics are very popular on IGCSE papers. A typical 4-mark question: "Explain the environmental problems caused by plastic waste." You need to cover landfill, ocean, and/or burning, with a specific detail for each (not just "it's bad for the environment"). Name the problem AND explain why it is a problem.

!
Don't Confuse — Biodegradable vs Photodegradable

Biodegradable = broken down by microbes (bacteria).
Photodegradable = broken down by sunlight.
Some plastics are one, both, or neither. Even photodegradable plastics may only break into microparticles, which is still a problem.

  • I can describe problems with plastic waste in landfill, oceans, and when burned
  • I can name five approaches to tackling the plastics problem
  • I know the difference between biodegradable and photodegradable
  • I can explain what bioplastics are and give an example (PLA)
Section 17.7 Proteins
Proteins are natural polymers made by condensation polymerisation of amino acids. They are essentially natural polyamides — the same amide linkage found in nylon is found in every protein in your body.
W
Write This Down — Amino Acids

Amino acids are the monomers of proteins. All amino acids contain:

• A carboxyl group (–COOH) — the acid part
• An amine group (–NH₂) — the amino part
• A side chain (R group) — different in each of the 22 amino acids

Both functional groups are attached to the same carbon atom. General formula: NH₂CH(R)COOH

W
Write This Down — How Amino Acids Join

The –COOH of one amino acid reacts with the –NH₂ of another. A water molecule is eliminated and an amide linkage (–CONH–) forms. This is condensation polymerisation — the same reaction used to make nylon.

Proteins are therefore called natural polyamides.

If 5 amino acids join, 4 water molecules are eliminated (one less than the number of monomers).

W
Write This Down — Why Proteins Are So Varied

There are 22 different amino acids, and they can join in any combination and any order. Protein chains range from 60 to over 6000 amino acids long. This gives an enormous number of possible proteins — over 100,000 different ones in your body.

Because each amino acid has a different R group, you cannot draw a simple repeating unit for a protein (unlike nylon or PET). The sequence matters.

W
Write This Down — How Your Body Makes Proteins

• You eat protein (e.g. eggs, meat, beans)
• Proteases (enzymes) in your digestive system break proteins down into amino acids (hydrolysis)
• Amino acids travel to the liver and then to body cells
• Cells reassemble amino acids into the specific proteins you need
• DNA provides the instructions; RNA carries them out
• Your body can make many amino acids, but there are 9 essential amino acids you must get from food

T
Study Tip — Connect to Biology

This overlaps with your biology chapter on enzymes and digestion! Proteins = polymers made from amino acid monomers by condensation polymerisation. Digestion = hydrolysis (breaking them back down by adding water). If you understand it in chemistry, you understand it in biology too — double the benefit.

  • I can describe the structure of an amino acid (COOH + NH₂ + R group)
  • I can explain how amino acids join by condensation polymerisation
  • I can identify the amide linkage in a protein chain
  • I can explain why proteins are called natural polyamides
