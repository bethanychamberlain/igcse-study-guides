Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the compounds formed from these pairs of elements: (a) sodium and chlorine, (b) hydrogen and bromine, (c) magnesium and oxygen.
3 marks
Answer
(a) Sodium chloride [1] — metal first, non-metal ending in -ide.
(b) Hydrogen bromide [1] — hydrogen is always named first when paired with another non-metal.
(c) Magnesium oxide [1] — metal first, non-metal ending in -ide.
The -ide ending tells you there are only two elements. If you see -ate or -ite, there's oxygen in there too (that's a later chapter).

2
Write the chemical formulae for: (a) water, (b) carbon dioxide, (c) ammonia, (d) magnesium chloride.
4 marks
Answer
(a) H₂O [1]
(b) CO₂ [1]
(c) NH₃ [1]
(d) MgCl₂ [1] — Mg has valency 2, Cl has valency 1, so you need two Cl to balance one Mg.
Water, CO₂, and ammonia are "just memorise them" formulae. MgCl₂ you can work out from valencies: Mg is Group II (valency 2), Cl is Group VII (valency 1), so criss-cross gives MgCl₂.

3
What do these state symbols mean? (a) (s)   (b) (l)   (c) (g)   (d) (aq)
2 marks
Answer
(a) Solid, (b) Liquid, (c) Gas, (d) Aqueous solution (dissolved in water) [1 for all four correct, 1 for explaining aqueous].
The tricky one is (aq). "Aqueous" just means "dissolved in water." If salt is dissolved in water, you write NaCl (aq), not NaCl (l). It's still a solid substance, just dissolved.

4
Define relative atomic mass (Ar) and state the standard atom it is compared to.
2 marks
Answer
The relative atomic mass is the average mass of the atoms of an element [1], compared to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom [1].
Two key parts: (1) it's an average (because of isotopes) and (2) it's relative to carbon-12. If you forget the "average" part, you lose a mark. If you just say "compared to carbon" without specifying 1/12 of carbon-12, you lose the other mark.

Intermediate — Can You Calculate It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. Show your working clearly — method marks matter even if the final answer is wrong.
5
Balance the following equations:
(a) Na + Cl₂ → NaCl
(b) H₂ + O₂ → H₂O
(c) Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃
3 marks
Answer
(a) 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl [1]
Cl is balanced (2 each side). Na needs a 2 on the left to match the 2 on the right.

(b) 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O [1]
O: 2 on left, 1 on right → put 2 in front of H₂O. Now H: 2 on left, 4 on right → put 2 in front of H₂.

(c) 4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃ [1]
Al₂O₃ has 2 Al and 3 O. Put 2 in front of Al₂O₃: 4 Al and 6 O on right. So 4Al on left, and 3O₂ gives 6 O on left. Balanced!
Remember: never change the subscript numbers inside a formula. Only add big numbers in front. For part (c), this is one of the trickier ones — the trick is to balance Al₂O₃ by doubling it first so you get even numbers to work with.

6
Calculate the relative molecular mass (Mr) of: (a) sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄   (b) calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂
(Ar values: H = 1, S = 32, O = 16, Ca = 40)
4 marks
Answer
(a) H₂SO₄
H: 2 × 1 = 2
S: 1 × 32 = 32
O: 4 × 16 = 64
Mr = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 [2]

(b) Ca(OH)₂
Ca: 1 × 40 = 40
O: 2 × 16 = 32  (the 2 outside the bracket multiplies both O and H)
H: 2 × 1 = 2
Mr = 40 + 32 + 2 = 74 [2]
For (b), the bracket is the key. (OH)₂ means 2 oxygen atoms AND 2 hydrogen atoms. If you wrote O = 16 and H = 1, you forgot the bracket and your answer would be 57 — wrong. Always expand the brackets first.

7
Calculate the percentage of oxygen in magnesium oxide, MgO.
(Ar values: Mg = 24, O = 16)
3 marks
Answer
Step 1: Mr of MgO = 24 + 16 = 40 [1]
Step 2: Mass of oxygen in formula = 16 [1]
Step 3: % oxygen = (16 ÷ 40) × 100 = 40% [1]
Always show these three steps: (1) find Mr, (2) identify the element's mass, (3) divide and multiply by 100. Even if you get the final number wrong, you can get 2 out of 3 marks for correct method. Also: you can check by calculating % Mg = (24 ÷ 40) × 100 = 60%. Does 40% + 60% = 100%? Yes. Good.

Advanced — Can You Apply It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need multi-step calculations with full working. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Iron reacts with excess sulfuric acid: Fe + H₂SO₄ → FeSO₄ + H₂. If 5 g of iron gives 13.6 g of iron(II) sulfate, calculate: (a) How much iron(II) sulfate can be obtained from 10 g of iron? (b) How much iron is needed to make 136 g of iron(II) sulfate?
4 marks
Answer
(a) 5 g Fe gives 13.6 g FeSO₄
10 g Fe is ×2, so: 13.6 × 2 = 27.2 g of iron(II) sulfate [2]

(b) 5 g Fe gives 13.6 g FeSO₄
136 g FeSO₄ is ×10, so: 5 × 10 = 50 g of iron [2]
The ratio method: find the scale factor (how many times bigger or smaller), then apply it to the other quantity. Always write down the original ratio first, then show the multiplication. Two lines of working = two marks.

9
Calculate the percentage composition by mass of each element in calcium carbonate, CaCO₃.
(Ar values: Ca = 40, C = 12, O = 16)
5 marks
Answer
Step 1: Mr of CaCO₃ = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100 [1]

Step 2: % Calcium = (40 ÷ 100) × 100 = 40% [1]

Step 3: % Carbon = (12 ÷ 100) × 100 = 12% [1]

Step 4: % Oxygen = (48 ÷ 100) × 100 = 48% [1]

Check: 40% + 12% + 48% = 100% ✓ [1]
This is a very clean example because Mr = 100, which makes the percentages obvious. On the exam, the numbers won't always be this nice, so always show division × 100. The "check" that they add to 100% is worth doing — it catches errors and some mark schemes give credit for it.

10
A sample of lead(II) bromide weighed 15 g. It was found to contain only 13.5 g of lead(II) bromide, with the rest being impurity. (a) Calculate the percentage purity of the sample. (b) What mass of impurity did the sample contain? (c) If a factory needs 54 g of pure lead(II) bromide, what mass of this impure sample must they use?
5 marks
Answer
(a) % purity = (mass of pure substance ÷ total mass) × 100
= (13.5 ÷ 15) × 100 = 90% [2]

(b) Mass of impurity = total mass − pure mass = 15 − 13.5 = 1.5 g [1]

(c) The sample is 90% pure, so only 90/100 of any sample is usable.
We need 54 g pure, so: mass of impure sample = 54 ÷ 0.9 = 60 g [2]
(Check: 90% of 60 g = 54 g ✓)
Part (c) is the reverse calculation — they give you the pure amount and ask for the impure total. Divide by the fraction (0.9), don't multiply. A common error is doing 54 × 0.9 = 48.6, which gives you LESS than what you need, not more. If the sample is impure, you always need MORE of it, so the answer must be bigger than 54.
