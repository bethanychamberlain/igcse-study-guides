Sections 3.1 – 3.2 Compounds, Mixtures, and Why Atoms Form Bonds
Before we get into types of bonding, you need to know the difference between elements, compounds, and mixtures — and understand why atoms bother to bond in the first place.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• An element contains only one kind of atom. E.g. sodium contains only sodium atoms.
• A compound is made of atoms of different elements, bonded together. It is described by a formula, e.g. H₂O, CO₂.
• A mixture contains different substances that are not bonded together. You can usually separate them easily (filtering, distilling, etc.).

W
Write This Down — Signs of a Chemical Change

You can tell a chemical change (chemical reaction) has occurred by these signs:
• One or more new substances are formed (they look or behave differently)
• Energy is taken in or given out (heat, light, sound)
• The change is usually difficult to reverse

A physical change forms no new substance and is usually easy to reverse (e.g. mixing iron filings and sulfur, dissolving, filtering).

W
Write This Down — Why Atoms Bond

The noble gases (Group VIII) do not form bonds because their atoms already have a full outer shell of electrons. A full outer shell makes an atom stable.

All other atoms bond in order to obtain a full outer shell of electrons, like the noble gases. That means 8 electrons in the outer shell (or 2, if the atom only has one shell, like helium).

!
Don't Confuse — Compound vs Mixture

A mixture of iron and sulfur can be separated with a magnet. But the compound iron(II) sulfide (FeS) cannot — the atoms are chemically bonded. The exam loves asking: "Is this a mixture or a compound? Explain." If the substances are bonded together and you can't easily separate them, it's a compound.

?
Why This Matters

The idea that "atoms bond to get a full outer shell" is the single most important idea in this chapter. Every type of bonding — ionic, covalent, metallic — is just a different strategy atoms use to achieve that same goal. If you understand this, the rest of the chapter makes sense.

T
Study Tip

Draw the electron structures of helium (2), neon (2,8), and argon (2,8,8). These are the "targets" that all other atoms are trying to match. When you see any bonding diagram, ask yourself: "Has every atom ended up with a full outer shell?" If yes, the diagram is correct.

  • I can define element, compound, and mixture
  • I can list three signs of a chemical change
  • I can explain why noble gases don't form bonds
  • I can state why atoms form bonds (to get a full outer shell)
Sections 3.3 – 3.4 Ionic Bonding and Ions
When metals react with non-metals, the metal atoms transfer electrons to the non-metal atoms. This creates charged particles called ions, which attract each other and form ionic bonds.
W
Write This Down — How Ionic Bonds Form

Ionic bonding happens between a metal and a non-metal.
• The metal atom loses its outer shell electron(s) → becomes a positive ion (cation)
• The non-metal atom gains those electron(s) → becomes a negative ion (anion)
• Both ions now have full outer shells

An ionic bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.

W
Write This Down — Sodium Chloride Example

• Sodium (2,8,1) loses 1 electron → Na⁺ ion (2,8) — charge is 1+ because 11 protons, 10 electrons
• Chlorine (2,8,7) gains 1 electron → Cl⁻ ion (2,8,8) — charge is 1− because 17 protons, 18 electrons
• The ions attract each other and form a lattice — a regular 3D arrangement of alternating Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions
• Formula: NaCl (one Na⁺ for each Cl⁻, total charge = 0)

W
Write This Down — More Ionic Examples

Magnesium oxide (MgO): Mg (2,8,2) loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺. Oxygen (2,6) gains 2 electrons → O²⁻. One Mg²⁺ for each O²⁻, so formula = MgO.

Magnesium chloride (MgCl₂): Mg loses 2 electrons, but each Cl only needs 1. So each Mg gives 1 electron to each of 2 Cl atoms. Formula = MgCl₂.

W
Write This Down — Ions and Naming

• An ion is a charged particle with an unequal number of protons and electrons
• Positive ions = cations (remember: positive cat). Negative ions = anions (remember: negative an)
• Metal ions keep the element name (sodium ion, Na⁺). Non-metal ions end in -ide (chloride, Cl⁻; oxide, O²⁻)
• Transition metals can form more than one ion — use Roman numerals: iron(II) = Fe²⁺, iron(III) = Fe³⁺
• Compound ions are groups of bonded atoms with an overall charge: OH⁻, NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻, NH₄⁺

!
Don't Confuse — Atoms vs Ions

A sodium atom (Na) has 11 protons and 11 electrons — no charge. A sodium ion (Na⁺) has 11 protons and 10 electrons — charge of 1+. The number of protons never changes. The charge comes from gaining or losing electrons only. If the exam asks "Why does a sodium ion have a charge of 1+?", write: "It has 11 protons (+) and only 10 electrons (−), so the overall charge is 1+."

?
Why This Matters

Drawing dot-and-cross diagrams for ionic bonding is one of the most common exam questions. Use dots for electrons from one atom and crosses for electrons from the other. Show the electron transfer with an arrow, and put square brackets around each ion with the charge outside. Practice NaCl, MgO, and MgCl₂ until you can draw them from memory.

T
Study Tip — Working Out Formulae

To find the formula of an ionic compound: (1) Write the ions and their charges. (2) Balance charges to zero. For example, aluminium chloride: Al³⁺ and Cl⁻. You need 3 Cl⁻ to balance 1 Al³⁺, so the formula is AlCl₃. This is a pattern you can use for any ionic compound.

  • I can describe how an ionic bond forms (electron transfer, attraction between ions)
  • I can draw dot-and-cross diagrams for NaCl, MgO, and MgCl₂
  • I can work out the formula of an ionic compound from the ion charges
  • I can define ion, cation, and anion
  • I know what compound ions are and can name three examples
Sections 3.5 – 3.6 Covalent Bonding and Covalent Compounds
When two non-metal atoms need electrons to fill their outer shells, they don't transfer — they share. This sharing creates covalent bonds, and the bonded groups of atoms are called molecules.
W
Write This Down — What Is a Covalent Bond?

A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share a pair of electrons. Both nuclei attract the shared electrons, and this attraction holds the atoms together.

Covalent bonding happens between non-metal atoms. The result is a molecule — two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds.

W
Write This Down — Covalent Elements

Many non-metal elements exist as molecules. Learn these:
• Single bond (one shared pair): H₂ (H–H), Cl₂ (Cl–Cl)
• Double bond (two shared pairs): O₂ (O=O)
• Triple bond (three shared pairs): N₂ (N≡N)

Elements with molecules of 2 atoms are called diatomic: H₂, O₂, N₂, Cl₂, I₂.

W
Write This Down — Covalent Compounds

When atoms of different non-metal elements share electrons, they form covalent compounds. Key examples:

Compound Formula Bonds Formed
Hydrogen chloride HCl H shares 1 electron with Cl — single bond
Water H₂O O shares 1 electron with each of 2 H atoms — two single bonds
Ammonia NH₃ N shares 1 electron with each of 3 H atoms — three single bonds
Methane CH₄ C shares 1 electron with each of 4 H atoms — four single bonds
Carbon dioxide CO₂ C shares 2 electrons with each of 2 O atoms — two double bonds (O=C=O)
!
Don't Confuse — Ionic vs Covalent

Ionic bonding = electron transfer (metal + non-metal). Covalent bonding = electron sharing (non-metal + non-metal). A huge exam mistake is saying atoms in a covalent bond "give" electrons to each other. They share — both atoms use the same pair of electrons. Also, covalent compounds form molecules, not ions. Never say "sodium chloride molecules" — NaCl is ionic and forms a lattice of ions, not molecules.

?
Why This Matters

You need to be able to draw dot-and-cross diagrams for covalent molecules. The exam will ask for specific ones: H₂, Cl₂, O₂, N₂, HCl, H₂O, NH₃, CH₄, CO₂, and C₂H₄ (ethene). In each diagram, check that every atom ends up with a full outer shell (8 electrons, or 2 for hydrogen).

T
Study Tip — Count the Bonds

Here's a shortcut: the number of bonds an atom forms = the number of electrons it needs to fill its outer shell. Hydrogen needs 1, so it forms 1 bond. Oxygen needs 2 → 2 bonds. Nitrogen needs 3 → 3 bonds. Carbon needs 4 → 4 bonds. Use this to check your diagrams make sense.

  • I can define a covalent bond (shared pair of electrons)
  • I can draw dot-and-cross diagrams for H₂, Cl₂, O₂, N₂, HCl, H₂O, NH₃, CH₄, and CO₂
  • I know the difference between single, double, and triple bonds
  • I can define molecule and give examples of diatomic elements
Section 3.7 Comparing Ionic and Covalent Compounds
The type of bonding in a compound determines its properties. Ionic and covalent compounds behave very differently — and the exam loves asking you to explain why.
W
Write This Down — Copy This Comparison Table
Property Ionic Compounds Molecular Covalent Compounds
Melting/boiling points High (e.g. NaCl melts at 801°C) Low (e.g. CO melts at −199°C)
Why? Strong ionic bonds between ions require lots of energy to break Weak forces between molecules (intermolecular forces) are easy to overcome
Solubility in water Usually soluble Usually insoluble
Electrical conductivity Conducts when melted or dissolved (ions free to move), but NOT as a solid Does not conduct (no charged particles to carry current)
Structure Giant ionic lattice of ions Small molecules with weak forces between them
State at room temp Solid Often liquid or gas
!
Don't Confuse — Why Ionic Solids Don't Conduct

Students often say "ionic compounds conduct electricity" and leave it at that. Wrong. Solid ionic compounds do not conduct because the ions are locked in the lattice and cannot move. They only conduct when melted (ions free to move) or dissolved in water (ions separated and free to move). The key phrase is: "the ions must be free to move to carry the charge."

?
Why This Matters

A classic exam question gives you a table of data (melting point, conductivity, solubility) and asks you to identify the type of bonding. If something has a high melting point, conducts when melted but not as a solid, and is soluble — it's ionic. If it has a low melting point and never conducts — it's molecular covalent. Learn the pattern and you can answer any data question.

T
Study Tip

Make a flashcard with the comparison table on one side and "ionic vs covalent" on the other. Test yourself until you can list all the differences from memory. Focus especially on the reasons for each difference — the exam rarely just asks "which has a higher melting point?" It asks "explain why."

  • I can list the key property differences between ionic and covalent compounds
  • I can explain why ionic compounds have high melting points (strong bonds in lattice)
  • I can explain when ionic compounds conduct and why (ions free to move)
  • I can explain why covalent compounds have low melting points (weak intermolecular forces)
Section 3.8 Giant Covalent Structures
Not all covalent substances are small molecules with low melting points. Some form enormous networks of covalently bonded atoms called giant covalent structures. Diamond, graphite, and silicon(IV) oxide are the ones you need to know.
W
Write This Down — Diamond

Diamond is a giant covalent structure made of carbon atoms only.
• Each carbon atom is bonded to 4 other carbon atoms by strong covalent bonds
• The structure is tetrahedral and extends in all directions
• Properties: very hard (hardest natural substance), very high melting point (3550°C), does not conduct electricity (no free electrons or ions)
• Uses: cutting and drilling tools

W
Write This Down — Graphite

Graphite is also a giant covalent structure of carbon atoms, but with a very different arrangement.
• Each carbon atom bonds to 3 others, forming flat layers (sheets) of hexagonal rings
• The layers are held together by weak forces, so they can slide over each other
• Each carbon has 4 outer electrons but only uses 3 for bonds — the 4th electron is delocalised (free to move)
• Properties: soft and slippery (layers slide), conducts electricity (delocalised electrons), high melting point
• Uses: lubricant, pencil lead, electrodes

W
Write This Down — Silicon(IV) Oxide

Silicon(IV) oxide (SiO₂), found naturally as quartz and sand, has a structure similar to diamond.
• Each silicon bonds to 4 oxygen atoms; each oxygen bonds to 2 silicon atoms
• Properties: hard, high melting point (1710°C), does not conduct electricity
• Uses: sandpaper, bricks for lining furnaces

!
Don't Confuse — Diamond and Graphite Are Both Carbon!

Diamond and graphite are allotropes of carbon — different physical forms of the same element. Don't say they are "different elements." They have completely different properties because their structures are different, even though they contain the same atoms. Also, don't say graphite is soft "because it has weak bonds." The covalent bonds within the layers are strong. It's the forces between the layers that are weak.

?
Why This Matters

The exam will ask why diamond is hard but graphite is soft, even though they're both carbon. It will also ask why graphite conducts electricity but diamond doesn't. The answer to both is about structure: diamond has 4 bonds per carbon in a rigid 3D network; graphite has 3 bonds per carbon in layers with delocalised electrons between them.

T
Study Tip — Sketch the Structures

Draw a small section of each structure: diamond (tetrahedral, 4 bonds per C), graphite (flat hexagonal layers with gaps between them), SiO₂ (alternating Si and O). Label the bonds and forces. Being able to sketch these from memory is worth easy marks.

  • I can describe the structure of diamond and explain its properties
  • I can describe the structure of graphite and explain why it's soft and conducts electricity
  • I can describe the structure of silicon(IV) oxide
  • I know that diamond and graphite are allotropes of carbon
Section 3.9 The Bonding in Metals
Metals are a third type of giant structure. Their bonding is different from both ionic and covalent — and it explains why metals are such good conductors and why they can be bent and shaped.
W
Write This Down — Metallic Bonding

In metals, atoms are packed tightly in a regular lattice. The outer electrons separate from their atoms and become delocalised — free to move throughout the whole structure.

The result is a lattice of positive metal ions surrounded by a “sea” of delocalised electrons.

A metallic bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between the positive metal ions and the sea of delocalised electrons.

W
Write This Down — Properties Explained by Structure

• High melting points: It takes a lot of energy to overcome the strong metallic bonds (e.g. copper 1083°C, iron 1535°C)
• Malleable and ductile: Layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond, because the sea of electrons moves with them. New bonds re-form in the new position.
• Good conductors of electricity: Delocalised electrons are free to move through the lattice, carrying charge as a current
• Good conductors of heat: Free electrons transfer kinetic energy quickly through the structure

!
Don't Confuse — Three Types of Bonding

Make sure you keep the three types of bonding straight:
• Ionic = transfer of electrons → ions in a lattice (metal + non-metal)
• Covalent = sharing of electrons → molecules or giant covalent structures (non-metal + non-metal)
• Metallic = delocalised electrons → positive ions in a sea of electrons (metal + metal)
The exam can ask you to identify the type of bonding in any substance. If it's a metal, it's metallic. If it's a metal with a non-metal, it's ionic. If it's non-metals only, it's covalent.

?
Why This Matters

A favourite exam question: "Explain why metals are good conductors of electricity." You must mention delocalised electrons. Simply saying "metals have free electrons" gets 1 mark. For full marks, say: "Metal atoms lose their outer electrons, which become delocalised. These delocalised electrons are free to move through the lattice, carrying charge as an electric current."

T
Study Tip — Summary Diagram

Draw three labelled diagrams side by side: (1) Ionic — lattice of + and − ions with arrows showing electron transfer. (2) Covalent — two atoms with overlapping shells and shared electron pairs. (3) Metallic — rows of + ions with dots scattered around them representing the electron sea. This one page covers the entire chapter.

  • I can describe the metallic bond (attraction between positive ions and delocalised electrons)
  • I can explain why metals are malleable (layers slide, bonds re-form)
  • I can explain why metals conduct electricity and heat (delocalised electrons)
  • I can identify whether a substance has ionic, covalent, or metallic bonding
