Section 11.1 Acids and Bases
Acids and bases are two huge groups of chemicals. You need to know what they are, how to identify them, and the common ones you'll meet in the lab.
W
Write This Down — What Are Acids?

Acids are non-metal compounds dissolved in water. They are corrosive (especially when concentrated) and can eat away metals, cloth, and skin.

The four main lab acids and their formulae:

AcidFormulaGives these salts
Sulfuric acidH₂SO₄ (aq)Sulfates
Hydrochloric acidHCl (aq)Chlorides
Nitric acidHNO₃ (aq)Nitrates
Ethanoic acidCH₃COOH (aq)Ethanoates

Other everyday acids: citric acid (lemons), lactic acid (yoghurt), carbonic acid (fizzy drinks).

W
Write This Down — What Are Bases?

Bases are oxides and hydroxides of metals. They react with acids to give a salt and water.

Examples: calcium oxide (CaO), magnesium oxide (MgO), sodium hydroxide (NaOH), magnesium hydroxide Mg(OH)₂.

Most bases are insoluble in water. But the soluble ones have a special name: alkalis.

W
Write This Down — Alkalis

Alkalis are soluble bases. They are hydroxides.

The three main lab alkalis:

• Calcium hydroxide — Ca(OH)₂ (aq) — its solution is called limewater
• Sodium hydroxide — NaOH (aq)
• Potassium hydroxide — KOH (aq)

Alkalis are also corrosive. They attack skin and some metals. They can do more harm than acids.

W
Write This Down — Indicators
IndicatorIn AcidIn Alkali
LitmusRedBlue
Methyl orangeRedYellow
ThymolphthaleinColourlessBlue
!
Don't Confuse — Base vs Alkali

All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis. A base is any metal oxide or hydroxide. An alkali is specifically a base that dissolves in water. Copper oxide is a base but NOT an alkali (it's insoluble).

T
Study Tip — Memory Trick for Litmus

Think: "Acids turn litmus Red" — both "acid" and "red" are short, punchy words. If you remember that one, the other is automatic (alkalis turn litmus blue). For the exam: acid = red, alkali = blue.

  • I can name the four main lab acids and write their formulae
  • I can define a base and give examples
  • I can define an alkali and explain how it differs from a base
  • I know the colour changes for litmus, methyl orange, and thymolphthalein
Section 11.2 The pH Scale, Strong and Weak Acids
What makes an acid acidic? What makes an alkali alkaline? It all comes down to ions. This section also covers the pH scale and the difference between strong and weak acids.
W
Write This Down — The Ions That Matter

All acids contain hydrogen ions, H⁺. That is what makes them acidic.

For example, hydrochloric acid dissociates in water:
HCl (aq) → H⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq)

All alkalis contain hydroxide ions, OH⁻. That is what makes them alkaline.

For example, sodium hydroxide dissolves and separates:
NaOH (aq) → Na⁺ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq)

W
Write This Down — The pH Scale

The pH scale runs from 0 to 14 and measures how acidic or alkaline a solution is.

pH RangeTypeIon Responsible
Below 7 (0–6)AcidicH⁺ ions — more H⁺ = lower pH = stronger acid
Exactly 7NeutralEqual H⁺ and OH⁻
Above 7 (8–14)AlkalineOH⁻ ions — more OH⁻ = higher pH = stronger alkali

Pure water has pH 7 (neutral). A sugar solution is also neutral. Stomach acid has a pH of about 2.

W
Write This Down — Universal Indicator

Universal indicator is a mixture of dyes that changes colour across the full pH range. It tells you the actual pH, not just "acid or alkali."

Colour sequence: red (pH 1–2) → orange (3) → yellow (4–5) → yellowish-green (6) → green (7) → greenish-blue (8–9) → blue (10–11) → violet (12–13)

W
Write This Down — Strong vs Weak Acids

A strong acid is completely dissociated into ions in solution.
Example: HCl (aq) → H⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq) — 100% dissociation. All molecules split into ions.

A weak acid is only partially dissociated into ions in solution.
Example: CH₃COOH (aq) ⇌ H⁺ (aq) + CH₃COO⁻ (aq) — the reaction is reversible. At equilibrium, many molecules remain intact. So there are fewer H⁺ ions, which means a higher pH (less acidic) than a strong acid at the same concentration.

Strong acids: hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid, nitric acid.
Weak acids: ethanoic acid, citric acid, carbonic acid.

!
Don't Confuse — Strong/Weak vs Concentrated/Dilute

Strong/weak = how much the acid dissociates (splits into ions). This is a property of the acid itself.
Concentrated/dilute = how much acid is dissolved in water (amount per volume). You CAN have a concentrated weak acid (lots of ethanoic acid in a small volume) or a dilute strong acid (a tiny amount of HCl in lots of water).

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves to give you two acids at the same concentration and ask why they have different pH values. The answer: one is a strong acid (fully dissociated, more H⁺ ions, lower pH) and the other is a weak acid (partially dissociated, fewer H⁺ ions, higher pH). Learn this pattern.

  • I know that H⁺ ions cause acidity and OH⁻ ions cause alkalinity
  • I can describe the pH scale (0–14) and what each region means
  • I can describe what universal indicator does and its colour range
  • I can define strong and weak acids and give examples of each
  • I know the difference between strong/weak and concentrated/dilute
Section 11.3 The Reactions of Acids and Bases
Acids react with metals, bases, and carbonates. Each reaction follows a predictable pattern with a word equation AND a chemical equation. This is the section you must absolutely nail — word equations come up on every exam paper.
W
Write This Down — Which Acid Gives Which Salt?

The name of the salt depends on the acid you start with:

• Hydrochloric acid → gives chlorides
• Sulfuric acid → gives sulfates
• Nitric acid → gives nitrates

The first part of the salt name comes from the metal or base. So: hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide → sodium chloride.

W
Write This Down — Reaction 1: Acid + Metal

Word equation:
acid + metal → salt + hydrogen

Example (word):
magnesium + sulfuric acid → magnesium sulfate + hydrogen

Example (chemical):
Mg (s) + H₂SO₄ (aq) → MgSO₄ (aq) + H₂ (g)

The metal displaces the hydrogen from the acid. You see bubbling (hydrogen gas).

W
Write This Down — Reaction 2: Acid + Base

Word equation:
acid + base → salt + water

Example with an alkali (soluble base):
hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide → sodium chloride + water
HCl (aq) + NaOH (aq) → NaCl (aq) + H₂O (l)

Example with an insoluble base:
sulfuric acid + copper(II) oxide → copper(II) sulfate + water
H₂SO₄ (aq) + CuO (s) → CuSO₄ (aq) + H₂O (l)

W
Write This Down — Reaction 3: Acid + Carbonate

Word equation:
acid + carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide

Example (word):
calcium carbonate + hydrochloric acid → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide

Example (chemical):
CaCO₃ (s) + 2HCl (aq) → CaCl₂ (aq) + H₂O (l) + CO₂ (g)

You see fizzing/bubbling (CO₂ gas being released).

!
Don't Confuse — Products of Each Reaction

• Acid + metal = salt + hydrogen (just two products, and hydrogen gas bubbles off)
• Acid + base = salt + water (just two products)
• Acid + carbonate = salt + water + carbon dioxide (three products!)
The carbonate reaction is the only one with three products. Don't forget the CO₂.

W
Write This Down — Neutralisation

Neutralisation is a reaction between an acid and a base (or carbonate) that produces water.

Acid + base = neutralisation (produces water). Acid + carbonate = also neutralisation (produces water + CO₂).
But acid + metal is NOT a neutralisation — it does not produce water.

?
Why This Matters — Neutralisation in Real Life

• Toothpaste contains bases to neutralise acids made by mouth bacteria (prevents tooth decay)
• Indigestion tablets contain bases (e.g. magnesium hydroxide) to neutralise excess stomach acid
• Lime (calcium oxide) is spread on soil to neutralise acidic soil so crops can grow

T
Study Tip — Write Equations From Scratch

Practice writing 6 different word equations from memory: pick an acid (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) and react it with (a) a metal, (b) a base, (c) a carbonate. Follow the pattern: acid name tells you the salt. If you can do this for all three acids, you own the exam questions. Cover the answers and try right now.

  • I can write the word equation for acid + metal
  • I can write the word equation for acid + base
  • I can write the word equation for acid + carbonate
  • I know which acid gives chlorides, sulfates, and nitrates
  • I can define neutralisation and explain why acid + metal is NOT neutralisation
  • I can give three real-life examples of neutralisation
Sections 11.4 – 11.5 Ionic Equations, Proton Transfer, and Oxides
Now we look deeper at what actually happens during neutralisation (at the ion level), and classify the different types of oxides.
W
Write This Down — The Ionic Equation for Neutralisation

When an acid reacts with a soluble base (alkali), the key reaction is:

H⁺ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq) → H₂O (l)

This is the ionic equation. The other ions (e.g. Na⁺ and Cl⁻) are just floating in solution — they don't react. They are called spectator ions.

When an acid reacts with an insoluble base (like MgO), the ionic equation is:

2H⁺ (aq) + O²⁻ (s) → H₂O (l)

W
Write This Down — Proton Donors and Acceptors

An H⁺ ion is just a proton (a hydrogen atom that has lost its one electron).

In neutralisation, the acid donates protons (H⁺) and the base accepts them.

This gives us a more precise definition:
• Acids are proton donors
• Bases are proton acceptors

W
Write This Down — Types of Oxides
TypeMade FromReacts WithExamples
Basic oxidesMetals + oxygenAcids (to give salt + water)CaO, CuO, MgO, Fe₂O₃
Acidic oxidesNon-metals + oxygenWater (to give acids) / AlkalisCO₂, SO₂, P₄O₁₀
Amphoteric oxidesCertain metalsBoth acids AND alkalisAl₂O₃, ZnO
Neutral oxidesSome non-metalsNeither acids nor basesCO, N₂O (laughing gas)
!
Don't Confuse — Amphoteric

Amphoteric means it reacts with both acids and alkalis. The two examples to remember are aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) and zinc oxide (ZnO). The exam will expect you to name both.

?
Why This Matters — Acid Rain

Non-metal oxides like SO₂ and NO₂ are released by burning fossil fuels. They dissolve in rainwater to form acids (sulfuric acid, nitric acid), creating acid rain. This is a direct application of "non-metals form acidic oxides" and comes up regularly in exams.

T
Study Tip — How to Write Ionic Equations

Three steps: (1) Write the full balanced equation. (2) Split all aqueous ionic compounds into their ions. (3) Cross out any ion that appears on both sides — those are spectator ions. What's left is the ionic equation. Practice with HCl + NaOH, then try H₂SO₄ + KOH.

  • I can write the ionic equation for acid + alkali: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O
  • I can define spectator ions
  • I know that acids are proton donors and bases are proton acceptors
  • I can classify oxides as basic, acidic, amphoteric, or neutral
  • I can name two amphoteric oxides
Sections 11.6 – 11.7 Making Salts — Methods and Solubility Rules
How do you actually make a specific salt in the lab? The method depends on whether the salt is soluble or insoluble, and what starting materials you use. This is one of the most heavily examined practical topics.
W
Write This Down — Solubility Rules
SolubleInsoluble
All sodium, potassium, and ammonium salts—
All nitrates—
All chlorides, bromides, iodidesExcept silver and lead halides
All sulfatesExcept calcium, barium, and lead sulfate
Sodium, potassium, and ammonium carbonatesAll other carbonates are insoluble
W
Write This Down — Method 1: Making a Soluble Salt from a Metal

Steps (e.g. making zinc sulfate from zinc + dilute sulfuric acid):

1. Add excess zinc to the acid in a beaker — it dissolves and hydrogen bubbles off
2. When bubbling stops, all the acid is used up. Filter off the excess zinc
3. Heat the solution to evaporate some water, then cool — crystals form

Zn (s) + H₂SO₄ (aq) → ZnSO₄ (aq) + H₂ (g)

This works for Mg, Al, Zn, Fe. NOT for Na, K, Ca (too reactive/violent) or Cu, Ag, Au (too unreactive).

W
Write This Down — Method 2: Making a Soluble Salt from an Insoluble Base or Carbonate

Steps (e.g. making copper(II) sulfate from copper(II) oxide + dilute sulfuric acid):

1. Warm dilute acid. Add excess insoluble base (or carbonate) — it dissolves, solution turns blue
2. When no more dissolves, all the acid is used up. Filter off excess solid
3. Heat filtrate to get a saturated solution, then cool — blue crystals of CuSO₄.5H₂O form

CuO (s) + H₂SO₄ (aq) → CuSO₄ (aq) + H₂O (l)

W
Write This Down — Method 3: Making a Soluble Salt from an Alkali (Titration)

Both reactants are soluble, so you cannot filter off excess. You need a titration.

Steps (e.g. making sodium chloride from NaOH + HCl):

1. Pipette 25 cm³ of alkali into a flask. Add indicator (e.g. thymolphthalein — blue in alkali)
2. Add acid from a burette until indicator changes colour (blue → colourless) — the end-point. Record the volume.
3. Repeat without indicator, using the exact volume found. (Indicator would be an impurity in the salt.)
4. Evaporate the solution to get salt crystals

NaOH (aq) + HCl (aq) → NaCl (aq) + H₂O (l)

W
Write This Down — Method 4: Making an Insoluble Salt by Precipitation

Precipitation occurs when you mix two solutions and an insoluble product (the precipitate) forms.

Steps (e.g. making barium sulfate):

1. Mix solutions of barium chloride and magnesium sulfate
2. A white precipitate of barium sulfate forms immediately
3. Filter to collect the precipitate
4. Rinse with distilled water, then dry in a warm oven

BaCl₂ (aq) + MgSO₄ (aq) → BaSO₄ (s) + MgCl₂ (aq)

Ionic equation: Ba²⁺ (aq) + SO₄²⁻ (aq) → BaSO₄ (s)

Rule: To make an insoluble salt, mix a solution containing its positive ion with a solution containing its negative ion.

W
Write This Down — Hydrated and Anhydrous Salts

Hydrated salts have water molecules bonded into their crystals. This water is called water of crystallisation.

Example: CuSO₄.5H₂O (blue crystals — hydrated copper(II) sulfate).

Heating drives off the water, leaving the anhydrous (water-free) salt — CuSO₄ (white powder).

!
Don't Confuse — When to Use Which Method

• Starting with a metal, insoluble base, or insoluble carbonate? Add excess, then filter.
• Starting with an alkali (soluble base)? You must do a titration because both reactants are solutions — you can't filter off excess.
• Need an insoluble salt? Precipitation — mix two solutions.

T
Study Tip — The Solubility Rules Are Exam Gold

Memorise the solubility table. A quick trick: Nitrates, Sodium, Potassium, Ammonium are always soluble (think "N-SPA"). For the exceptions, remember "SLP" for insoluble halides/sulfates: Silver, Lead, (and for sulfates) Barium + Calcium.

  • I can list the solubility rules for salts
  • I can describe how to make a soluble salt from a metal + acid (excess, filter, crystallise)
  • I can describe how to make a soluble salt from an insoluble base/carbonate + acid
  • I can explain why titration is needed when using an alkali
  • I can describe how to make an insoluble salt by precipitation
  • I can define hydrated, anhydrous, and water of crystallisation
Section 11.8 Finding Concentration by Titration
Titration isn't just a method for making salts — it's also used to calculate the concentration of an unknown acid or alkali. Follow the four steps carefully, and the calculation becomes routine.
W
Write This Down — Key Formulae

Concentration (mol/dm³) = number of moles ÷ volume in dm³

To convert cm³ to dm³: divide by 1000. So 25 cm³ = 0.025 dm³.

Number of moles = concentration × volume (in dm³).

W
Write This Down — The Four-Step Titration Calculation

Worked example: 27.8 cm³ of HCl neutralises 25 cm³ of 1 mol/dm³ Na₂CO₃. Find the concentration of HCl.

Step 1: Calculate moles of the known substance.
25 cm³ of 1 mol/dm³ Na₂CO₃ = 25/1000 × 1 = 0.025 mol

Step 2: Find the molar ratio from the equation.
2HCl + Na₂CO₃ → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂
Ratio: 2 moles HCl to 1 mole Na₂CO₃

Step 3: Calculate moles of the unknown.
0.025 mol Na₂CO₃ × 2 = 0.05 mol HCl

Step 4: Calculate concentration.
Volume of HCl = 27.8 cm³ = 0.0278 dm³
Concentration = 0.05 ÷ 0.0278 = 1.8 mol/dm³

?
Why This Matters

Titration calculations are worth 4–6 marks on the exam and follow the same four steps every time. If you practise the method, it's free marks. The solution with the known concentration is called the standard solution.

!
Don't Confuse — cm³ vs dm³

The most common mistake is forgetting to convert cm³ to dm³. Always divide by 1000 before using the formula. 25 cm³ = 0.025 dm³. 27.8 cm³ = 0.0278 dm³. Write out the conversion every time until it becomes automatic.

T
Study Tip — Always Write the Four Steps

Even if you can do the maths in your head, write out all four steps on the exam paper. Each step earns a mark. If your final answer is wrong but your method is right, you still get most of the marks. Label each step clearly: "Step 1: Moles of ...", "Step 2: Ratio from equation...", etc.

  • I can convert cm³ to dm³ by dividing by 1000
  • I can calculate moles from concentration and volume
  • I can use a molar ratio from a balanced equation
  • I can follow all four steps of a titration calculation to find concentration
