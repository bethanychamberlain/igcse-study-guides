Section 10.1 Reversible Reactions
Most reactions you've studied so far go in one direction — reactants turn into products and that's that. But some reactions can go both ways. These are called reversible reactions, and understanding them is the key to this entire chapter.
W
Write This Down — Definition

A reversible reaction is a reaction that can go in both directions — the products can react to re-form the original reactants. You can change the direction of the reaction by changing the conditions.

We use the symbol ⇌ (two half-arrows) instead of a single arrow (→) to show that a reaction is reversible.

• The reaction from left to right is the forward reaction
• The reaction from right to left is the reverse reaction

W
Write This Down — Key Examples

Example 1: Copper(II) sulfate
Blue hydrated copper(II) sulfate → heat → white anhydrous copper(II) sulfate + water
Add water back → turns blue again
CuSO₄·5H₂O (s) ⇌ CuSO₄ (s) + 5H₂O (l)
     blue                          white

Example 2: Cobalt(II) chloride
Pink hydrated cobalt(II) chloride → heat → blue anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride + water
Add water back → turns pink again
CoCl₂·6H₂O (s) ⇌ CoCl₂ (s) + 6H₂O (l)
     pink                          blue

W
Write This Down — Testing for Water

Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate and anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride can be used to test for the presence of water. If the liquid contains water:

• Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate turns from white to blue
• Anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride paper turns from blue to pink

But these tests only show that water is present — they do not tell you whether it is pure water.

W
Write This Down — Dynamic Equilibrium

When a reversible reaction takes place in a closed system (nothing can escape), it reaches a state of dynamic equilibrium.

"Equilibrium" means no overall change — the amounts of reactants and products remain steady.

"Dynamic" means both reactions are still happening at the particle level — the forward and reverse reactions are both taking place, but at the same rate.

A reversible reaction in a closed system is at equilibrium when:
• The forward and reverse reactions take place at the same rate
• The concentrations of reactants and products are no longer changing

!
Don't Confuse — Equilibrium Does Not Mean Equal

"Equilibrium" does not mean that the amounts of reactants and products are equal. It means the amounts have stopped changing. There could be much more product than reactant, or vice versa — the key is that the ratio is now constant because both reactions happen at the same rate.

!
Don't Confuse — Closed System Required

Equilibrium can only be reached in a closed system where no particles can escape. In an open system, products leave and the reaction just keeps going one way. This is actually what factories do on purpose — they remove the product so equilibrium is never reached and the forward reaction keeps going.

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves to ask: "What is meant by dynamic equilibrium?" You need both words explained: dynamic = forward and reverse reactions are still occurring; equilibrium = at the same rate, so no overall change in concentrations. Miss either part and you lose a mark.

T
Study Tip — Colour Pairs

Make flashcards for the two colour-change examples. One side: "Hydrated copper(II) sulfate — colour?" Other side: "Blue. Anhydrous = white." Do the same for cobalt(II) chloride (pink ↔ blue). These come up constantly in exams, and mixing up the colours is an easy way to throw away marks.

  • I can define a reversible reaction and name the symbol used
  • I can write the equations for copper(II) sulfate and cobalt(II) chloride reversible reactions
  • I can describe the colour changes in both examples
  • I can explain what "dynamic equilibrium" means (both words)
  • I know that equilibrium requires a closed system
Section 10.2 Shifting the Equilibrium
Reversible reactions never go to completion — they reach equilibrium. But industry needs high yields. The solution: change the conditions to shift the equilibrium towards the products side. This idea has a name: Le Chatelier's principle.
W
Write This Down — Le Chatelier's Principle

When a reversible reaction is at equilibrium and you make a change, the system acts to oppose the change and restore equilibrium. A new equilibrium mixture forms.

In simpler terms: if you push the system one way, it pushes back. But the pushback is never complete — you end up with a new balance point, shifted in the direction you wanted.

W
Write This Down — Copy This Table Into Your Notes
Change You Make Effect on Equilibrium Why
Increase temperature Favours the endothermic direction System absorbs the extra heat to oppose the change
Decrease temperature Favours the exothermic direction System releases heat to oppose the cooling
Increase pressure (gases only) Favours the side with fewer gas molecules Fewer molecules = lower pressure, opposing the increase
Decrease pressure (gases only) Favours the side with more gas molecules More molecules = higher pressure, opposing the decrease
Increase concentration of a reactant Shifts towards products System uses up the extra reactant
Remove product Shifts towards products System makes more product to replace what was removed
Add a catalyst No shift — no change in yield Catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse equally; equilibrium reached faster, but position unchanged
W
Write This Down — Applying It to Ammonia

N₂ (g) + 3H₂ (g) ⇌ 2NH₃ (g)    forward reaction is exothermic

Temperature: Lower temperature favours the exothermic (forward) direction → more ammonia. But too low = reaction is too slow.

Pressure: Left side has 4 gas molecules (1 + 3), right side has 2. Higher pressure favours fewer molecules → shifts right → more ammonia.

Concentration: Removing ammonia as it forms shifts equilibrium towards products → more ammonia made.

Catalyst: Iron catalyst gets to equilibrium faster, but does not change the yield.

!
Don't Confuse — Rate vs Yield

A catalyst speeds up the rate (how fast equilibrium is reached) but does not change the yield (how much product you get at equilibrium). Temperature changes both — it changes the rate AND the yield. Students often say "a catalyst increases the yield" — that's wrong and costs marks every time.

?
Why This Matters

This is one of the highest-mark topics on the exam. They'll give you a reaction equation, tell you it's exothermic or endothermic, and ask: "What happens to the yield if you increase the temperature / pressure?" You need to (1) state which way the equilibrium shifts, (2) explain why using Le Chatelier's principle, and (3) say whether yield increases or decreases. Three steps, three marks.

T
Study Tip — Count the Molecules

For pressure questions, always count the gas molecules on each side of the equation. Write the numbers above the equation: e.g. "4 molecules ⇌ 2 molecules." High pressure favours the smaller number. If both sides have the same number of gas molecules, pressure has no effect on the yield.

  • I can state Le Chatelier's principle in my own words
  • I can predict the effect of changing temperature on yield (need to know exo/endothermic)
  • I can predict the effect of changing pressure on yield (count gas molecules)
  • I can explain why changing concentration or removing product shifts equilibrium
  • I know that a catalyst does NOT change the yield
Section 10.3 The Haber Process
The Haber process is the industrial method for making ammonia (NH₃) from nitrogen and hydrogen. It's the most famous example of Le Chatelier's principle applied in the real world — and it comes up on almost every IGCSE Chemistry exam.
W
Write This Down — The Reaction

N₂ (g) + 3H₂ (g) ⇌ 2NH₃ (g)    forward reaction is exothermic

Sources of reactants:
• Nitrogen: from the air (air is ~80% nitrogen; oxygen is removed by burning hydrogen: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O)
• Hydrogen: from reacting natural gas (methane) with steam: CH₄ + 2H₂O → CO₂ + 4H₂, or from cracking hydrocarbons

W
Write This Down — The Five Steps

1. Nitrogen and hydrogen are mixed and scrubbed (cleaned) to remove impurities
2. The mixture is compressed to 200 atmospheres by pumps
3. The compressed gases pass through the converter — a round tank with beds of iron catalyst at 450°C. Less than 30% is converted to ammonia
4. The mixture is cooled. Ammonia condenses to a liquid and is removed. Unreacted N₂ and H₂ are recycled back to the converter
5. Ammonia is stored as a liquid under pressure

W
Write This Down — Why These Conditions?
Condition Chosen Value Why Not the "Ideal" Value?
Temperature 450°C (moderate) Lower temp = higher yield, but reaction is too slow. 450°C is a compromise between yield and rate.
Pressure 200 atmospheres (high) Higher pressure = higher yield (4 molecules → 2 molecules). 400 atm gives better yield but needs expensive, dangerous equipment. 200 atm is a compromise between yield, safety, and cost.
Catalyst Iron Speeds up the reaction (gets to equilibrium faster). Does NOT change the yield.
Removal Ammonia condensed and removed; unreacted gases recycled Removing product shifts equilibrium to the right. Recycling means the final overall yield is almost 100%.
?
Why This Matters

Most of the world's ammonia is used to make fertilisers. The rest goes into dyes, pesticides, and other chemicals. Without the Haber process, modern agriculture could not feed the world's population. Exam questions often ask you to explain why the conditions are chosen, not just state them — so always include the compromise reasoning.

!
Don't Confuse — Converter vs Closed System

The converter in the Haber process is not a closed system. Gases flow through it continuously, ammonia is removed, and unreacted gases are recycled. Equilibrium is never fully reached in the converter — that's the whole point. The system is designed to keep the forward reaction going.

T
Study Tip — The "Compromise" Word

If the exam asks "Why is 450°C used?" your answer must include the word compromise. Say: "450°C is a compromise. A lower temperature would give a higher yield because the forward reaction is exothermic, but the rate would be too slow. 450°C gives a reasonable rate while still producing a useful yield." That's a full-mark answer.

  • I can write the equation for the Haber process with state symbols
  • I know where the nitrogen and hydrogen come from
  • I can state the conditions: 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst
  • I can explain why each condition is chosen (using Le Chatelier's + compromise)
  • I can explain how removing ammonia and recycling gases pushes yield towards 100%
Section 10.4 The Contact Process
The Contact process makes sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) — the world's most-produced chemical. Like the Haber process, it's built around a reversible reaction and uses Le Chatelier's principle to maximise the yield.
W
Write This Down — The Key Steps

Raw materials: sulfur (or sulfur dioxide from roasting metal sulfide ores), air, and water

Step 1: Burn sulfur in air → sulfur dioxide
S (s) + O₂ (g) → SO₂ (g)

Step 2: Mix sulfur dioxide with more air

Step 3 (KEY REACTION): Pass over a catalyst of vanadium(V) oxide at 450°C
2SO₂ (g) + O₂ (g) ⇌ 2SO₃ (g)    forward reaction is exothermic

Step 4: Dissolve sulfur trioxide in concentrated sulfuric acid → oleum (thick fuming liquid)

Step 5: Mix oleum carefully with water → concentrated sulfuric acid
H₂O (l) + SO₃ (g) → H₂SO₄ (l)

Unreacted SO₂ from step 3 is recycled back over the catalyst beds. Final yield is over 99%.

W
Write This Down — Why These Conditions?
Condition Chosen Value Why?
Temperature 450°C Forward reaction is exothermic, so lower temp = higher yield. But the catalyst is inactive below 400°C. 450°C is the compromise.
Pressure 2 atmospheres (low) 3 gas molecules → 2, so higher pressure helps yield. But the yield at 2 atm is already very high, so raising pressure further is not worth the cost.
Catalyst Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) Speeds up the reaction. Does NOT change the yield. Multiple beds of catalyst give maximum contact.
Removal SO₃ dissolved in H₂SO₄; unreacted SO₂ recycled Removing product shifts equilibrium right. Recycling gives over 99% final yield.
!
Don't Confuse — Why Not Add Water Directly to SO₃?

Sulfur trioxide reacts violently with water, producing a thick, deadly acid mist. For safety, the SO₃ is dissolved in concentrated sulfuric acid first (making oleum), then the oleum is carefully diluted with water. The exam sometimes asks about safety precautions in the Contact process — this is the answer.

!
Don't Confuse — Haber vs Contact

Students mix these up all the time. Keep them straight:

• Haber = ammonia (NH₃), iron catalyst, 200 atm, 450°C
• Contact = sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), vanadium(V) oxide catalyst, 2 atm, 450°C

Both use 450°C. The big difference is the pressure (200 atm vs 2 atm) and the catalyst (iron vs V₂O₅).

?
Why This Matters

Sulfuric acid is used in fertilisers, paints, detergents, plastics, dyes, and car batteries. The exam may ask you to compare the Haber and Contact processes — same principle (Le Chatelier + compromise), different details. If you can explain one, you can explain the other.

T
Study Tip — Make a Side-by-Side Table

Draw a two-column table: Haber on the left, Contact on the right. Row headings: Product, Equation, Temperature, Pressure, Catalyst, Why not higher/lower temp, Uses. Fill it in from memory. If you can complete both columns without looking, you're ready for the exam. Both processes follow the exact same logic — learn the pattern, not just the facts.

  • I can write the key equation for the Contact process with state symbols
  • I can name the raw materials and the catalyst (vanadium(V) oxide)
  • I can state the conditions: 450°C, 2 atm, V₂O₅
  • I can explain why each condition is chosen (exothermic + compromise)
  • I can explain the safety reason for dissolving SO₃ in acid (not water)
  • I can compare the Haber and Contact processes from memory
