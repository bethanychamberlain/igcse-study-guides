Section 8.1 Energy Changes in Reactions
Every chemical reaction involves an energy change. Energy is either given out or taken in, usually as heat (thermal energy). This divides all reactions into two groups: exothermic and endothermic.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Exothermic reaction: a reaction that gives out thermal energy to the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings rises.

Endothermic reaction: a reaction that takes in thermal energy from the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings falls.

Surroundings = everything around the reaction that is not a reactant or product (e.g. the water in the beaker, the container walls, the air).

W
Write This Down — Examples
Exothermic (gives out energy) Endothermic (takes in energy)
Burning fuels (combustion) Thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate
Neutralisation (acid + alkali) Sherbet reacting on your tongue (citric acid + sodium hydrogen carbonate)
Iron reacting with sulfur Barium hydroxide + ammonium chloride (freezes water underneath!)
Sodium reacting with water Cooking (reactions in food when heated)
W
Write This Down — Describing Energy Changes

Exothermic: reactants → products + thermal energy

The products have less energy than the reactants. Energy has been given out.

Endothermic: reactants + thermal energy → products

The products have more energy than the reactants. Energy has been taken in.

!
Don't Confuse — The Temperature Trick

Exothermic makes the surroundings hotter (temperature goes up). Endothermic makes the surroundings colder (temperature goes down). Students often get this backwards because they think "taking in energy = getting hotter." No — it's taking energy away from the surroundings, so the surroundings cool down.

T
Study Tip — Memory Trick

Exo means out (think "Exit" — energy exits the reaction). Endo means in (energy enters the reaction from the surroundings). If you remember "exit" you'll never mix them up.

?
Why This Matters

The exam will give you a scenario — "the beaker gets warmer" or "the temperature drops" — and ask you to identify whether it's exothermic or endothermic. If the surroundings heat up, the reaction gave out energy = exothermic. If the surroundings cool down, the reaction took in energy = endothermic. Always explain in terms of energy transfer, not just "it gets hot."

  • I can define exothermic and endothermic reactions
  • I can give two examples of each type
  • I can explain what "surroundings" means
  • I can describe how each type affects temperature
  • I can write the general energy equation for each type
Section 8.2 A Closer Look at Energy Changes
To understand why reactions give out or take in energy, we need to look at what happens to bonds. Every reaction involves breaking old bonds and making new ones — and each step has its own energy cost.
W
Write This Down — The Two Steps in Every Reaction

Step 1: Breaking bonds in the reactants — this takes in energy (endothermic process). The energy level rises.

Step 2: Making bonds in the products — this gives out energy (exothermic process). The energy level falls.

Remember: Bond breaking = endothermic. Bond making = exothermic. This is always true, no exceptions.

W
Write This Down — What Decides Exo vs Endo?

The overall energy change depends on which step is bigger:

• If energy out from making bonds > energy in to break bonds → exothermic overall (net energy given out)

• If energy in to break bonds > energy out from making bonds → endothermic overall (net energy taken in)

W
Write This Down — Activation Energy and Enthalpy

Activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy that colliding particles must have in order to react. It's the energy needed to start breaking bonds. Every reaction needs it — this is why the line on a reaction pathway diagram always goes up first.

Enthalpy change (ΔH) is the overall energy change of the reaction.

• ΔH is negative for exothermic reactions (energy is released)

• ΔH is positive for endothermic reactions (energy is absorbed)

W
Write This Down — Energy Level Diagrams

You need to be able to draw and label two diagrams. Both have "energy" on the y-axis and "progress of reaction" on the x-axis:

Exothermic: Reactants are drawn high, products are drawn low. The arrow pointing down between them shows ΔH (negative). The hump above the reactants shows Ea.

Endothermic: Reactants are drawn low, products are drawn high. The arrow pointing up between them shows ΔH (positive). The hump above both shows Ea.

!
Don't Confuse — Energy Level Diagram vs Reaction Pathway Diagram

A simple energy level diagram (Section 8.1) just shows reactants and products at two levels with an arrow between them. A reaction pathway diagram (Section 8.2) shows the full curve with the hump (activation energy). The exam might ask for either — read the question carefully. If it says "include Ea and ΔH," draw the pathway diagram with the hump.

?
Why This Matters

Drawing and labelling reaction pathway diagrams is one of the most common exam questions. They'll ask you to label the axes, mark Ea, mark ΔH, identify reactants and products, and say whether it's exothermic or endothermic. The line always curves up first (because you always need activation energy to break bonds first) — don't forget this.

T
Study Tip — Draw Both Diagrams From Memory

Practice drawing both reaction pathway diagrams until you can do them with your eyes closed. For exothermic: start high (reactants), hump up (Ea), come down low (products), mark ΔH as the drop. For endothermic: start low (reactants), hump up (Ea), end high (products), mark ΔH as the rise. If you can sketch both in under 30 seconds, you own it.

  • I know that bond breaking is endothermic and bond making is exothermic
  • I can explain what determines whether a reaction is exo or endo overall
  • I can define activation energy (Ea) and enthalpy change (ΔH)
  • I can draw and label a reaction pathway diagram for an exothermic reaction
  • I can draw and label a reaction pathway diagram for an endothermic reaction
Section 8.3 Calculating Enthalpy Changes
You can calculate the overall energy change of a reaction if you know the bond energies of all the bonds broken and formed. This section is where the maths comes in — but it's the same simple formula every time.
W
Write This Down — The Formula

Enthalpy change (ΔH) = Energy in (to break bonds) − Energy out (from making bonds)

The unit of energy is the joule (J). 1000 J = 1 kilojoule (kJ). Bond energies and enthalpy changes are given in kJ/mol.

Bond energy is the energy needed to break a bond, or released when the same bond forms. It is measured in kJ/mol.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example 1 (Exothermic)

Hydrogen + Chlorine → Hydrogen chloride

H2 + Cl2 → 2HCl

Show all bonds: H—H + Cl—Cl → 2 H—Cl

Energy in (break bonds in reactants):
• 1 × H—H = 436 kJ
• 1 × Cl—Cl = 242 kJ
• Total energy in = 678 kJ

Energy out (make bonds in products):
• 2 × H—Cl = 2 × 431 = 862 kJ

ΔH = 678 − 862 = −184 kJ

The negative sign means exothermic — 184 kJ of energy is given out.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example 2 (Endothermic)

Decomposition of ammonia

2NH3 → N2 + 3H2

Energy in (break bonds in reactants):
• 6 × N—H = 6 × 391 = 2346 kJ

Energy out (make bonds in products):
• 1 × N≡N = 946 kJ
• 3 × H—H = 3 × 436 = 1308 kJ
• Total energy out = 2254 kJ

ΔH = 2346 − 2254 = +92 kJ

The positive sign means endothermic — 92 kJ of energy is taken in.

!
Don't Confuse — The Order Matters!

The formula is energy in MINUS energy out. NOT the other way around. If you flip it, your sign will be wrong and you'll call an exothermic reaction endothermic. Think of it this way: the formula follows the progress of the reaction — first you put energy in (to break bonds), then energy comes out (from making bonds). So "in" comes first.

T
Study Tip — The Three-Step Method

Every bond energy calculation follows the same three steps:
1. Draw out all the bonds (write the equation showing every single bond).
2. Add up energy in (bonds broken) and energy out (bonds made) separately.
3. Subtract: ΔH = energy in − energy out.
If ΔH is negative, it's exothermic. If positive, it's endothermic. Practice on at least three different reactions until this feels automatic.

?
Why This Matters

Bond energy calculations are worth 4–6 marks on the exam and they are easy marks if you know the method. They'll give you the equation and a table of bond energies. All you do is count the bonds, multiply, add, subtract. Show your working clearly — even if you get the final answer wrong, you can pick up marks for each correct step.

  • I can write the formula: ΔH = energy in − energy out
  • I can identify all bonds broken and formed from a structural equation
  • I have worked through Example 1 (H2 + Cl2) and got −184 kJ
  • I have worked through Example 2 (2NH3) and got +92 kJ
Section 8.4 The Hydrogen-Oxygen Fuel Cell
Hydrogen and oxygen react to produce water — you already know this is exothermic. But instead of just making heat, a fuel cell converts this chemical energy directly into electrical energy. This is a big deal for clean transport.
W
Write This Down — What Is a Fuel Cell?

A hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell uses hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity. The only chemical product is water, which is harmless.

Overall reaction: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l) + electrical energy + some thermal energy

W
Write This Down — How It Works (Simplified)

• The cell has two electrodes (anode and cathode) coated with a platinum catalyst, with a special membrane between them.
• At the anode (+): Hydrogen gas gives up electrons to form H+ ions. (2H2 → 4H+ + 4e−) — this is oxidation.
• The electrons flow through an external circuit — this is the electric current that powers the motor.
• The H+ ions pass through the membrane to the cathode.
• At the cathode (−): Oxygen reacts with the H+ ions and electrons to form water. (O2 + 4H+ + 4e− → 2H2O) — this is reduction.
• The overall reaction is a redox reaction.

W
Write This Down — Advantages and Disadvantages
Hydrogen Fuel Cell Petrol Engine
Advantages
Only product is water — harmless Petrol stations widely available
Hydrogen can be made from water by electrolysis Petrol is relatively cheap
Disadvantages
Hydrogen must be stored under high pressure Burning petrol produces CO2 (climate change)
Hydrogen filling stations are rare Also produces CO, NOx, unburnt hydrocarbons
Most hydrogen currently comes from fossil fuels Petrol is from oil — a non-renewable resource
Fuel cell cars cost more (for now)
!
Don't Confuse — Fuel Cell vs Electrolysis

A fuel cell looks a bit like an electrolysis cell, but they do the opposite thing. In electrolysis, you put electricity in to force a reaction to happen. In a fuel cell, a reaction happens and produces electricity. The fuel cell acts like a battery, not like electrolysis. Don't mix them up — the exam loves this trick.

?
Why This Matters

Fuel cell questions often appear in the "extended" section of the exam and are worth easy marks if you know the advantages/disadvantages table. They also connect to environmental chemistry — expect questions like "Give one reason why hydrogen fuel cells are better for the environment than petrol engines" (answer: the only product is water, which is harmless, whereas petrol produces CO2 which causes climate change).

T
Study Tip — Think "Clean but Not Easy"

The fuel cell sounds perfect — only makes water, no pollution. So why doesn't everyone use it? Because the hydrogen has to come from somewhere. If you use fossil fuels to make the hydrogen, you're still polluting. The future depends on using renewable energy (solar, wind) to split water by electrolysis. If the exam asks "Why isn't hydrogen widely used as a fuel?", hit these points: storage is difficult (high pressure), filling stations are rare, and the hydrogen is usually made from fossil fuels.

  • I can state what a hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell produces (electricity + water)
  • I can outline how the fuel cell works (anode, cathode, membrane)
  • I can give two advantages and two disadvantages vs petrol engines
  • I know the difference between a fuel cell and electrolysis
