Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define: (a) solute, (b) solvent, (c) saturated solution.
3 marks
Answer
(a) A solute is a substance that is dissolved in a solvent [1].
(b) A solvent is a substance that dissolves a solute [1].
(c) A saturated solution is one that can dissolve no more solute at that temperature [1].
"At that temperature" is essential for the saturated solution definition. Without it you lose the mark.

2
When a mixture is filtered, what is the name given to: (a) the solid trapped in the filter paper, and (b) the liquid that passes through?
2 marks
Answer
(a) The solid is called the residue [1].
(b) The liquid is called the filtrate [1].
These two terms are tested constantly. Residue = what stays behind. Filtrate = what flows through.

3
Name the separation technique you would use to obtain pure water from a solution of salt water.
1 mark
Answer
Simple distillation [1].
Not evaporation — that would give you the salt, not the water. Not fractional distillation — that separates two liquids. You want the solvent (water) from the solute (salt).

4
State two ways of checking whether a substance is pure.
2 marks
Answer
1. Check its melting point and boiling point — a pure substance melts and boils sharply at the expected temperature [1].
2. Carry out paper chromatography — a pure substance gives only one spot [1].
Both are valid. The examiner wants two distinct methods. Don't just say "test it" — name the specific method.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
23 g of potassium nitrate dissolves in 100 g of water at 25°C. 169 g dissolves at 80°C. Outline the steps to obtain crystals of potassium nitrate from its aqueous solution.
4 marks
Answer
1. Heat the solution to evaporate some of the water, making the solution more concentrated [1].
2. Test that the solution is saturated by placing a drop on a cold glass slide — if crystals form quickly, it is ready [1].
3. Allow the hot saturated solution to cool slowly — crystals of potassium nitrate will form as the solubility decreases [1].
4. Filter the crystals from the remaining solution and dry them with filter paper [1].
This is crystallisation, NOT evaporation. It works because potassium nitrate's solubility changes a lot with temperature (169 g vs 23 g). Cooling forces the excess solute out of solution.

6
36 g of sodium chloride dissolves in 100 g of water at 20°C. 38 g dissolves at 80°C. Explain why crystallisation is not a suitable method to separate sodium chloride from water, and suggest a better method.
3 marks
Answer
The solubility of sodium chloride changes very little with temperature (36 g at 20°C vs 38 g at 80°C) [1]. So cooling a hot saturated solution would cause very little salt to crystallise out [1]. A better method is evaporation — heat the solution in an evaporating dish until all the water has evaporated, leaving solid salt behind [1].
The key insight: crystallisation relies on a BIG change in solubility with temperature. Salt barely changes, so crystallisation is useless for it. Evaporation removes ALL the water instead.

7
In paper chromatography, a student draws the base line in pen instead of pencil. Explain why this is a mistake. Then define Rf and state the formula.
3 marks
Answer
The ink from the pen would dissolve in the solvent and travel up the paper, mixing with the substances being tested and ruining the chromatogram [1].

Rf (retention factor) = distance from origin to centre of spot ÷ distance from origin to solvent front [1 for definition, 1 for formula].
Pencil is insoluble in the solvents used, so it stays put. This is a very common exam question — "Why pencil, not pen?"

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A student wants to separate a mixture of ethanol and water. (a) Name a suitable separation technique and describe the apparatus used. (b) Explain how the technique works, referring to boiling points. (c) Explain why simple distillation would not be suitable.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Fractional distillation [1]. Apparatus: round-bottomed flask, fractionating column packed with glass beads, thermometer, condenser, collecting beaker [1].

(b) The mixture is heated. Ethanol (boiling point 78°C) boils first [1]. A mixture of ethanol and water vapours rises up the fractionating column. Water vapour condenses on the glass beads and drips back; ethanol vapour continues to rise, enters the condenser, condenses, and is collected as pure liquid [1].

(c) Simple distillation does not have a fractionating column, so both ethanol and water vapours would pass into the condenser together [1]. The collected liquid would still be a mixture, not pure ethanol [1].
Six marks, six ideas: name the method, describe the apparatus, explain boiling point difference, explain the role of the column, say why simple distillation fails, and state the result would be impure.

9
Two samples of potassium nitrate, X and Y, are tested. Pure potassium nitrate melts sharply at 334°C. Sample X melts in the range 332.5–333.5°C. Sample Y melts in the range 331–333.5°C. (a) Which sample contains more impurity? Explain your answer. (b) Describe what happens to the boiling point when impurities are present. (c) State one other method that could confirm whether a sample is pure.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Sample Y contains more impurity [1]. Its melting point range is wider (2.5°C vs 1°C) and starts lower (331°C vs 332.5°C) [1]. Impurities cause the melting point to fall and the substance to melt over a range rather than sharply [1].

(b) When impurities are present, the boiling point increases (rises above the expected value) and the substance boils over a range of temperatures rather than at one sharp temperature [1].

(c) Paper chromatography — a pure substance gives one spot; if impurities are present, additional spots will appear [1].
Remember the direction: impurities make m.p. go DOWN and b.p. go UP. Both change from a sharp point to a range.

10
A food scientist needs to check whether a yellow food colouring (substance Z) is a single pure dye or a mixture. Describe how paper chromatography could be used to investigate this. Include: the procedure, how to interpret the results, and how Rf values could help identify the dyes present.
6 marks
Answer
Procedure: Draw a pencil line (the origin) near the bottom of chromatography paper [1]. Place a spot of substance Z on the line, and spots of known dyes alongside it. Stand the paper in a beaker containing a small amount of suitable solvent (with the origin above the solvent level). Allow the solvent to rise up the paper, then remove and mark the solvent front [1].

Interpreting results: If Z produces only one spot, it is a single pure dye [1]. If Z produces two or more spots, it is a mixture of dyes [1].

Using Rf values: Measure the distance from the origin to the centre of each spot, and the distance from the origin to the solvent front. Calculate Rf = distance to spot ÷ distance to solvent front [1]. Compare the Rf values to a table of known dyes (using the same solvent) to identify which dyes are present in Z [1].
Full procedure + interpretation + Rf calculation + identification. The examiner wants to see that you understand the whole process, not just one step. Mention pencil (not pen), solvent level below the origin, and same conditions for Rf comparison.
