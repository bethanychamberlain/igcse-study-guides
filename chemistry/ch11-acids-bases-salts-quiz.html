Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the four common laboratory acids and give the formula for each one.
2 marks
Answer
Sulfuric acid — H₂SO₄ [1 for any two correct pairs]
Hydrochloric acid — HCl
Nitric acid — HNO₃
Ethanoic acid — CH₃COOH [1 for all four correct]
You must match the name to the right formula. These come up constantly. Learn them cold.

2
What is a base? What is an alkali? Give one example of each.
2 marks
Answer
A base is an oxide or hydroxide of a metal [1]. Example: copper(II) oxide, CuO (or magnesium oxide, MgO).
An alkali is a soluble base [1]. Example: sodium hydroxide, NaOH (or potassium hydroxide, KOH).
The key difference is solubility. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis. Use this exact phrasing on the exam.

3
A solution has a pH of 3. (a) Is this solution acidic, alkaline, or neutral? (b) What colour would universal indicator turn in this solution?
2 marks
Answer
(a) Acidic [1] — any pH below 7 is acidic.
(b) Universal indicator would turn orange [1] at pH 3.
pH below 7 = acidic. pH above 7 = alkaline. pH exactly 7 = neutral. Universal indicator at pH 3 is orange (red at 1-2, orange at 3, yellow at 4-5).

4
Write the word equation for the reaction between dilute hydrochloric acid and calcium carbonate.
2 marks
Answer
calcium carbonate + hydrochloric acid → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide [1 for correct salt name, 1 for all three products]
Remember: acid + carbonate = salt + water + carbon dioxide (three products). Hydrochloric acid gives chlorides, so the salt is calcium chloride. Never forget the CO₂!

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, write equations, and apply the patterns.
5
Write the word equation and the balanced chemical equation for the reaction between dilute sulfuric acid and magnesium.
3 marks
Answer
Word equation:
magnesium + sulfuric acid → magnesium sulfate + hydrogen [1]

Chemical equation:
Mg (s) + H₂SO₄ (aq) → MgSO₄ (aq) + H₂ (g) [1 for correct formulae, 1 for balanced with state symbols]
Pattern: acid + metal = salt + hydrogen. Sulfuric acid gives sulfates. The equation is already balanced (1:1:1:1). Always include state symbols if the question asks for a "balanced chemical equation."

6
Two acid solutions have the same concentration (0.1 mol/dm³). Solution A is hydrochloric acid (pH 1.1). Solution B is ethanoic acid (pH 2.9). Explain why their pH values are different.
3 marks
Answer
Hydrochloric acid is a strong acid — it is completely dissociated into ions in solution [1], so it has a high concentration of H⁺ ions, giving a low pH [1].
Ethanoic acid is a weak acid — it is only partially dissociated [1], so it has fewer H⁺ ions in solution and therefore a higher pH.
Three marks, three ideas: (1) HCl is strong = fully dissociated, (2) ethanoic acid is weak = partially dissociated, (3) fewer H⁺ ions = higher pH. The word "dissociated" is essential.

7
Describe how you would make pure, dry crystals of copper(II) sulfate starting from copper(II) oxide and dilute sulfuric acid.
4 marks
Answer
1. Warm the dilute sulfuric acid and add excess copper(II) oxide [1]. The black powder dissolves and the solution turns blue.
2. When no more copper(II) oxide dissolves (excess remains), all the acid has been used up. Filter to remove the excess solid [1].
3. Heat the blue filtrate to evaporate some water until a saturated solution is obtained [1].
4. Allow the solution to cool. Blue crystals of copper(II) sulfate form [1].
Four key steps: (1) add excess, (2) filter, (3) heat to saturated solution, (4) cool to crystallise. The word "excess" is critical — it ensures all the acid is used up. This is the standard practical method for insoluble base + acid.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
A student wants to make pure, dry sodium chloride from sodium hydroxide solution and dilute hydrochloric acid. (a) Explain why a titration is needed. (b) Describe the steps the student should follow, including the indicator used and why it must be removed.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Both sodium hydroxide and hydrochloric acid are soluble, so you cannot add one in excess and filter it off [1]. A titration is needed to find the exact volume of acid required to neutralise the alkali [1].

(b) Steps:
1. Pipette 25 cm³ of NaOH into a flask. Add a few drops of thymolphthalein indicator (blue in alkali) [1].
2. Add HCl from a burette until the indicator turns colourless (the end-point). Record the volume of acid used [1].
3. Repeat the experiment using the same volumes but without the indicator — because the indicator would be an impurity in the salt [1].
4. Evaporate the solution to obtain dry sodium chloride crystals.
The reason for repeating without indicator is a classic exam mark. Also accept methyl orange as the indicator (red in acid, yellow in alkali). The key concept: both reactants are soluble, so you can't just filter off excess.

9
Barium sulfate is an insoluble salt. (a) Choose two soluble starting compounds you could use to make barium sulfate by precipitation. (b) Write the full balanced equation. (c) Write the ionic equation and name the spectator ions.
5 marks
Answer
(a) You need one solution containing Ba²⁺ ions and one containing SO₄²⁻ ions. Choose: barium chloride (BaCl₂) and magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) [1]. (Or sodium sulfate, zinc sulfate, etc. — any soluble sulfate.)

(b) BaCl₂ (aq) + MgSO₄ (aq) → BaSO₄ (s) + MgCl₂ (aq) [1 for formulae, 1 for balanced with state symbols]

(c) Ionic equation: Ba²⁺ (aq) + SO₄²⁻ (aq) → BaSO₄ (s) [1]
Spectator ions: Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ [1]
The ionic equation only shows the ions that actually form the precipitate. Everything else is a spectator ion. To pick starting compounds: one must contain the positive ion (Ba²⁺) and the other must contain the negative ion (SO₄²⁻), and both must be soluble.

10
In a titration, 25 cm³ of sodium hydroxide solution (concentration 1 mol/dm³) was neutralised by 20 cm³ of sulfuric acid. Calculate the concentration of the sulfuric acid. Show all your working.
Equation: H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
4 marks
Answer
Step 1: Moles of NaOH = concentration × volume = 1 × (25 ÷ 1000) = 0.025 mol [1]

Step 2: Molar ratio from equation: H₂SO₄ : NaOH = 1 : 2
So moles of H₂SO₄ = 0.025 ÷ 2 = 0.0125 mol [1]

Step 3: Volume of H₂SO₄ = 20 cm³ = 20 ÷ 1000 = 0.020 dm³ [1]

Step 4: Concentration = moles ÷ volume = 0.0125 ÷ 0.020 = 0.625 mol/dm³ [1]
Follow the four steps every time: (1) moles of the known, (2) use the ratio, (3) convert volume to dm³, (4) calculate concentration. Each step earns a mark even if your final number is wrong. Don't skip the working!
