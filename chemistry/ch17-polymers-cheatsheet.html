Polymers

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Polymer A very large molecule made by joining together thousands of small molecules (monomers). Monomer (the small repeating unit that builds the polymer)
Monomer The small molecule that repeats to form a polymer chain. Polymer (the finished long chain)
Addition polymerisation Monomers with C=C double bonds join up. Only one product (the polymer). No small molecule lost. Condensation polymerisation (two products: polymer + water)
Condensation polymerisation Two different monomers join, losing a small molecule (usually water) each time. Addition polymerisation (only one product, no water lost)
Amide linkage –CONH– bond found in polyamides (nylon) and proteins. Formed from –COOH + –NH₂. Ester linkage (–COO–, formed from –COOH + –OH)
Ester linkage –COO– bond found in polyesters (PET). Formed from –COOH + –OH. Amide linkage (–CONH–, uses –NH₂ not –OH)
Biodegradable Can be broken down by microbes (bacteria). Photodegradable (broken down by sunlight, not microbes)
Hydrolysis Breaking a polymer back into monomers by adding water. The reverse of condensation. Condensation (joining monomers by removing water)

Addition vs Condensation Polymerisation

Addition Condensation
Monomer requirement Must have C=C double bond Two different monomers, each with two functional groups
Products Polymer only Polymer + water (or HCl)
Bond change C=C opens → C–C forms Functional groups react, lose H₂O
Linkage type C–C backbone Amide (–CONH–) or Ester (–COO–)
Examples Poly(ethene), PVC, PTFE Nylon, PET, proteins

Key Plastics and Their Uses

Polymer Monomer Type Use
Poly(ethene) / polythene Ethene Addition Bags, bottles, bowls
PVC Chloroethene Addition Pipes, cable covers
PTFE / Teflon Tetrafluoroethene Addition Non-stick pans
Nylon Diamine + diacid Condensation Ropes, tents, clothing
PET Diol + diacid Condensation Bottles, clothing

Say It in One Sentence

Addition polymer C=C bonds break open and monomers snap together in a long chain — only one product, no waste.
Condensation polymer Two different monomers join, kicking out a water molecule each time they link up.
Polyamide (nylon) Dicarboxylic acid + diamine → –CONH– links + water. Same linkage as in your proteins.
Polyester (PET) Dicarboxylic acid + diol → –COO– links + water. Drinks bottles and polyester clothing.
Proteins Natural polyamides made from amino acid monomers by condensation — same chemistry as nylon!
Plastics problem Plastics are so unreactive they take hundreds of years to decompose — landfill, ocean, toxic when burned.
HDPE vs LDPE HDPE: straight chains, rigid. LDPE: branched chains, flexible. Same atoms, different arrangement.

Memory Tricks

Addition = One Product Only
Think of ADDING bricks to a wall — you only get a wall, nothing left over. No water, no waste. Just the polymer.
Condensation = "Crying" Water
Condensation on a window = water drops. Condensation polymerisation = water drops out each time two monomers join. If you see water as a product, it's condensation.
Amide vs Ester — "AN" Rule
Amide = uses Amine (–NH₂). Ester = uses alcohol (–OH, which has an "O" — think "Ester = OH"). Both react with –COOH.
Monomer from Polymer — "Find and Restore"
To find the monomer: (1) Find the repeating unit (2 carbons in the backbone + what's attached). (2) Put brackets around it. (3) Restore the C=C double bond. Done!
Proteins = Natural Nylon
Amino acids have –COOH and –NH₂ on the same molecule. They join by condensation, forming –CONH– amide links — exactly like nylon. Your hair is basically natural nylon!

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What type of polymerisation makes poly(ethene)?" Addition — ethene has a C=C double bond that opens up
"Draw the monomer from this polymer chain" Find 2-carbon repeat unit, put brackets around it, restore the C=C double bond
"What is the small molecule lost in condensation polymerisation?" Water (H₂O)
"Identify the linkage –CONH–" Amide linkage — found in polyamides (nylon) and proteins
"Why are plastics an environmental problem?" They are unreactive/non-biodegradable — take centuries to decompose. Harm animals, release toxins
"How can PET be recycled?" Hydrolysis (steam breaks it into monomers), then re-polymerisation into new PET
"Why are proteins called natural polyamides?" Amino acids join by condensation forming –CONH– amide linkages — same as nylon
"Explain the difference between HDPE and LDPE" HDPE: straight chains, pack closely, rigid. LDPE: branched chains, can't pack, flexible
