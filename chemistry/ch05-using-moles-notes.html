Section 5.1 The Mole — Chemistry's Counting Unit
Atoms are far too tiny to count one by one. Chemists use a special number called the mole to count huge quantities of particles at once — the same way you buy eggs by the dozen, chemists count atoms by the mole.
W
Write This Down — What Is a Mole?

One mole of any substance contains 6.02 × 10²³ particles. This number is called the Avogadro constant.

The symbol for the mole is mol (just like g is the symbol for grams).

The particles can be atoms (e.g. 1 mol of helium = 6.02 × 10²³ helium atoms), molecules (e.g. 1 mol of water = 6.02 × 10²³ water molecules), or ions (e.g. 1 mol of NaCl = 6.02 × 10²³ "units" of Na⁺ and Cl⁻).

W
Write This Down — The Molar Mass Shortcut

One mole of a substance has a mass equal to its Aᵣ or Mᵣ, expressed in grams.

Substance Formula Aᵣ or Mᵣ Mass of 1 mole
Carbon C Aᵣ = 12 12 g
Water H₂O Mᵣ = 18 18 g
Sodium chloride NaCl Mᵣ = 58.5 58.5 g
Ethanol C₂H₅OH Mᵣ = 46 46 g

To find Mᵣ, add up the Aᵣ of every atom in the formula. For ethanol (C₂H₅OH): (2×12) + (6×1) + (1×16) = 24 + 6 + 16 = 46.

W
Write This Down — The Key Formulas
moles = mass (g) ÷ Mᵣ
mass (g) = moles × Mᵣ
Mᵣ = mass (g) ÷ moles

These three are just rearrangements of the same equation. Use the calculation triangle: put mass on top, and moles × Mᵣ on the bottom. Cover the one you want to find, and what's left tells you what to calculate.

!
Don't Confuse — Atoms vs Molecules

0.5 mol of bromine molecules (Br₂, Mᵣ = 160) has mass = 0.5 × 160 = 80 g.
0.5 mol of bromine atoms (Br, Aᵣ = 80) has mass = 0.5 × 80 = 40 g.
Always check: does the question ask about atoms or molecules? This changes which mass you use.

?
Why This Matters

The mole lets you link the invisible world (how many atoms react) to the real world (how many grams you weigh on a balance). Every single calculation in the rest of this chapter — and most of IGCSE chemistry — starts with converting between mass and moles.

T
Study Tip — Drill the Triangle

Write the triangle (mass on top, moles × Mᵣ on bottom) on a sticky note and put it on your desk. Do 10 quick conversions every day until it's automatic: "46 g of ethanol → how many moles?" (46 ÷ 46 = 1 mol). "3 mol of water → how many grams?" (3 × 18 = 54 g). Speed and confidence come from repetition.

  • I can define the mole and state the Avogadro constant
  • I can calculate Mᵣ for any compound by adding up Aᵣ values
  • I can convert mass to moles using moles = mass ÷ Mᵣ
  • I can convert moles to mass using mass = moles × Mᵣ
  • I know the difference between atoms and molecules when calculating
Section 5.2 Calculations from Equations
A balanced equation does not just show you what reacts and what forms — it tells you the exact ratio of moles (and therefore grams) of every substance involved.
W
Write This Down — Reading Moles from an Equation

Consider: C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)

The coefficients (big numbers in front) tell you the mole ratio:
• 1 mole of carbon reacts with 1 mole of oxygen to give 1 mole of carbon dioxide
• Using Aᵣ/Mᵣ: 12 g of carbon + 32 g of oxygen → 44 g of carbon dioxide

For 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l):
• 2 mol H₂ + 1 mol O₂ → 2 mol H₂O
• 4 g + 32 g → 36 g

W
Write This Down — Step-by-Step Method for Mass Calculations

To calculate masses from an equation, follow these four steps every time:

Step 1: Write the balanced equation.
Step 2: Write down Aᵣ or Mᵣ for each substance you need.
Step 3: Use Mᵣ to convert the moles in the equation to grams (theoretical masses).
Step 4: Scale up or down to match the actual mass given in the question.

Worked Example — Masses from Equations

Question: What mass of oxygen is needed to burn 1 g of hydrogen, and what mass of water is produced?

Step 1: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)
Step 2: Mᵣ: H₂ = 2, O₂ = 32, H₂O = 18
Step 3: From the equation: 2×2 g H₂ + 32 g O₂ → 2×18 g H₂O, so 4 g + 32 g → 36 g
Step 4: We only have 1 g of H₂ (not 4 g), so divide everything by 4:
  1 g H₂ needs 32÷4 = 8 g of O₂ and gives 36÷4 = 9 g of H₂O
W
Write This Down — Conservation of Mass

The total mass does not change during a chemical reaction. No atoms disappear; they are just rearranged. In the example above: 1 g + 8 g = 9 g. Mass in = mass out.

!
Don't Confuse — Coefficients Are Moles, Not Grams

In 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, the "2" in front of H₂ means 2 moles of hydrogen, not 2 grams. You must multiply by Mᵣ to get grams. This is where most mistakes happen.

?
Why This Matters

Almost every extended calculation on the IGCSE paper uses this method. If you can follow these four steps consistently, you can answer questions about any reaction — even ones you have never seen before.

T
Study Tip — Always Write All Four Steps

Even if you can do it in your head, write every step. The examiner awards marks for method, not just the final number. If you get the arithmetic wrong but show the right method, you still pick up 2 or 3 of the marks. Skip steps and you get zero for a wrong answer.

  • I can read the mole ratio from a balanced equation
  • I can convert moles to grams using Mᵣ and scale to any given mass
  • I can check my answer using conservation of mass
  • I understand that coefficients mean moles, not grams
Section 5.3 Reactions Involving Gases
Gases are hard to weigh, so chemists measure them by volume instead. A beautifully simple rule — Avogadro's Law — makes gas calculations surprisingly easy.
W
Write This Down — Avogadro's Law

One mole of any gas occupies the same volume at the same temperature and pressure.

At room temperature and pressure (rtp) — defined as 20 °C and 1 atmosphere — the molar gas volume is 24 dm³ (which is 24,000 cm³).

This means 1 mol of hydrogen (2 g) and 1 mol of carbon dioxide (44 g) both take up exactly 24 dm³ at rtp. It doesn't matter what gas it is.

W
Write This Down — Gas Volume Formulas
volume (dm³) = moles × 24
moles = volume (dm³) ÷ 24

If the volume is in cm³, convert first: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³.
Or use: volume (cm³) = moles × 24,000 and moles = volume (cm³) ÷ 24,000.

Worked Example — Gas Volume from Mass

Question: What volume does 22 g of CO₂ occupy at rtp?

Step 1: Find Mᵣ of CO₂ = 12 + (2×16) = 44
Step 2: Find moles: 22 ÷ 44 = 0.5 mol
Step 3: Find volume: 0.5 × 24 = 12 dm³
Worked Example — Gas Volume from an Equation

Question: What volume of hydrogen reacts with 24 dm³ of oxygen?
Equation: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

Step 1: From the equation, 2 volumes of H₂ react with 1 volume of O₂
Step 2: So 2 × 24 dm³ react with 24 dm³
Answer: 48 dm³ of hydrogen
!
Don't Confuse — Units! dm³ vs cm³

The most common mistake in gas questions is mixing up dm³ and cm³. Always check the units in the question. If they give you cm³, divide by 1000 to get dm³ before using the formula, or use 24,000 instead of 24. Write the conversion as a separate line so you don't lose marks.

T
Study Tip — Gas Questions Are Two-Step

Every gas volume question is: (1) find moles (from mass, or from an equation), then (2) multiply by 24 to get volume. If they give you volume, do it backwards: divide by 24 to get moles. That's it. Two steps. Practise until it's automatic.

  • I can state Avogadro's Law and the molar gas volume at rtp (24 dm³)
  • I can calculate volume from moles and moles from volume
  • I can convert between dm³ and cm³ without errors
  • I can calculate gas volumes from balanced equations
Section 5.4 – 5.6 Concentration, Empirical Formula, and Molecular Formula
This section covers three related skills: calculating the concentration of solutions, working out the simplest formula of a compound from experimental data, and converting that to the real molecular formula.
W
Write This Down — Concentration of a Solution

Concentration = the amount of solute dissolved in 1 dm³ of solution.

• Solute = the substance you dissolve (e.g. salt)
• Solvent = the liquid you dissolve it in (usually water)
• Solution = solute + solvent mixed together

concentration (mol/dm³) = amount of solute (mol) ÷ volume of solution (dm³)
amount of solute (mol) = concentration (mol/dm³) × volume (dm³)

Concentration can also be given in g/dm³. The units in the question tell you which formula to use.

Volume conversion: if volume is in cm³, divide by 1000 to get dm³.

Worked Example — Concentration

Question: 25 cm³ of a solution contains 0.05 mol of NaOH. What is its concentration?

Step 1: Convert volume: 25 cm³ ÷ 1000 = 0.025 dm³
Step 2: concentration = 0.05 ÷ 0.025 = 2 mol/dm³
!
Don't Confuse — cm³ and dm³ (Again!)

This is the single most common error in concentration calculations. The formula uses dm³. If the question says "250 cm³", you must divide by 1000 to get 0.25 dm³ before plugging in. Write the conversion as a visible step. Every time.

W
Write This Down — Empirical Formula

The empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.

To find it from experimental data, follow this method every time:

Step What to Do
1 Write down the mass of each element (given in the question, or from percentages — assume 100 g)
2 Divide each mass by its Aᵣ to get moles of atoms
3 Divide every mole value by the smallest mole value → this gives the ratio
4 If the ratio is not whole numbers, multiply through to make it whole (e.g. 1:1.5 → 2:3)
5 Write the formula using the ratio
Worked Example — Empirical Formula

Question: A compound is 80% carbon and 20% hydrogen by mass. Find its empirical formula.

Step 1: Assume 100 g → 80 g C, 20 g H
Step 2: Moles: C = 80÷12 = 6.67 mol; H = 20÷1 = 20 mol
Step 3: Divide by smallest (6.67): C = 1, H = 20÷6.67 = 3
Step 4: Ratio is 1:3 → empirical formula = CH₃
W
Write This Down — From Empirical to Molecular Formula

The molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms in one molecule. It is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

n = Mᵣ (given) ÷ empirical formula mass
molecular formula = empirical formula × n

Example: If the empirical formula is CH₃ (mass = 15) and Mᵣ = 30, then n = 30 ÷ 15 = 2, so the molecular formula is C₂H₆ (ethane).

For ionic compounds, the empirical formula is the formula. There is no "molecular formula" because ionic compounds don't form individual molecules.

?
Why This Matters

Empirical formula questions appear on almost every IGCSE paper. They look hard but they always follow the same table method. If you can set up the table (element / mass / Aᵣ / moles / ratio), you will get the marks. The molecular formula is just one extra division step at the end.

T
Study Tip — Use the Table Every Time

Draw a table with rows: element, mass, Aᵣ, moles, ratio, formula. Fill it in mechanically. Even when it feels "obvious", the table keeps your working organized and prevents silly mistakes. Practise with: sulfur + oxygen → SO₂ (32 g S + 32 g O), and magnesium + oxygen → MgO (2.4 g Mg + 1.6 g O).

  • I can define concentration and use the formula (mol ÷ dm³)
  • I always convert cm³ to dm³ before calculating concentration
  • I can find the empirical formula from masses or percentages
  • I can convert an empirical formula to a molecular formula using Mᵣ
  • I know that for ionic compounds, empirical = actual formula
Section 5.7 Percentage Yield and Percentage Purity
In real life, chemical reactions never produce exactly as much product as the equation predicts. Some product is lost or contaminated. These two calculations tell you how good the reaction was.
W
Write This Down — % Yield

The yield is the amount of product you actually obtain from a reaction.

The theoretical mass is the mass you would get if the reaction were perfect (calculated from the equation).

% yield = (actual mass of product ÷ theoretical mass of product) × 100%

% yield is always less than or equal to 100%. If your answer is over 100%, you've made an error.

Worked Example — % Yield

Question: 1 mole of salicylic acid (Mᵣ = 138) should give 1 mole of aspirin (Mᵣ = 180). A trial using 100.0 g of salicylic acid produced 121.2 g of aspirin. What is the % yield?

Step 1: Moles of salicylic acid = 100.0 ÷ 138 = 0.725 mol
Step 2: Theoretical mass of aspirin = 0.725 × 180 = 130.5 g
Step 3: % yield = (121.2 ÷ 130.5) × 100 = 92.9%
W
Write This Down — % Purity

When a product has impurities mixed in (e.g. unreacted substances), we calculate % purity.

% purity = (mass of pure product ÷ mass of impure sample) × 100%
Worked Example — % Purity

Question: 10 g of chalk (mostly CaCO₃) is reacted with excess HCl. 2280 cm³ of CO₂ is collected at rtp. Find the % purity of the chalk.

Equation: CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Step 1: Moles of CO₂ = 2280 ÷ 24000 = 0.095 mol
Step 2: From equation: 1 mol CaCO₃ gives 1 mol CO₂, so 0.095 mol CaCO₃ reacted
Step 3: Mass of pure CaCO₃ = 0.095 × 100 = 9.5 g
Step 4: % purity = (9.5 ÷ 10) × 100 = 95%
!
Don't Confuse — % Yield vs % Purity

% yield answers: "How much product did I get compared to what I expected?" (actual vs theoretical).
% purity answers: "How much of my sample is the substance I actually want?" (pure vs impure).
They are different formulas with different purposes. Read the question carefully to know which one they want.

?
Why This Matters

In factories making medicines, a yield of 92% means 8% of expensive starting material was wasted. A purity of 78% in a drug means 22% is impurities — dangerous for patients. These calculations are used every day in real chemistry. On the exam, they are worth 3-5 marks and combine multiple skills (moles, equations, and percentages).

T
Study Tip — Two Different Starting Points

For % yield: start by finding the theoretical mass from the equation (this is the hard part), then the formula is easy. For % purity: start by working out the mass of pure substance from the experimental data (often via gas volume or titration), then the formula is easy. In both cases, the formula itself is just a fraction × 100. The real skill is figuring out what number goes on top and what goes on bottom.

  • I can define % yield and use the formula
  • I can calculate theoretical mass from a balanced equation
  • I can define % purity and use the formula
  • I know the difference between % yield and % purity
  • I can work backwards from gas volumes or titration data to find mass of pure substance
