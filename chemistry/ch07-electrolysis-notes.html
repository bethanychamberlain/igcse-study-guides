Section 7.1 Conductors and Non-Conductors
Electricity is a flow of charged particles. Some substances let it through (conductors), others block it (insulators). Understanding which substances conduct — and why — is the foundation for understanding electrolysis.
W
Write This Down

An electric current is a flow of electrons. A battery acts as an electron pump: electrons leave the negative terminal, travel around the circuit, and re-enter through the positive terminal.

W
Write This Down — Conductors vs Insulators

A conductor is a substance that allows electricity to pass through it. An insulator (non-conductor) does not allow electricity to pass through it.

Type Conducts? Why? Examples
Metals Yes (solid and liquid) Have free electrons that can move through the structure Copper, aluminium, iron, tin
Carbon (graphite) Yes Has free electrons (like a metal) Graphite rods, pencil lead
Molecular substances No (never) No free electrons or ions Plastic, ethanol, sugar, petrol, sulfur
Ionic compounds (solid) No Ions are locked in fixed positions Solid NaCl, solid PbBr₂
Ionic compounds (molten or dissolved) Yes Ions are free to move and carry the current Molten NaCl, NaCl solution
!
Don't Confuse — Two Types of Conduction

Metals conduct using electrons (electronic conduction). Ionic compounds (when molten or dissolved) conduct using ions (ionic conduction). The exam often asks "What carries the current?" — make sure you say electrons for metals, ions for electrolytes.

?
Why This Matters

When ionic compounds conduct, they break down at the same time. This is the whole basis of electrolysis: passing electricity through a molten or dissolved ionic compound decomposes it. That's why solid ionic compounds don't work — the ions can't move, so there's no current and no decomposition.

T
Study Tip

If the exam gives you a substance and asks "Will it conduct?", ask yourself two questions: (1) Does it have free electrons? (metals/graphite = yes). (2) Does it have free ions? (molten or dissolved ionic = yes). If neither, it's an insulator.

  • I can define conductor and insulator
  • I can explain why metals conduct (free electrons)
  • I can explain why ionic compounds only conduct when molten or dissolved (free ions)
  • I know that molecular substances never conduct
  • I can explain why ionic compounds decompose when they conduct
Section 7.2 The Principles of Electrolysis
Electrolysis means using electricity to break down a compound. This section covers the key definitions, the apparatus, and how to predict the products you get — both for molten compounds and aqueous solutions.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Electrolysis: the decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current.
• Electrolyte: the liquid (molten or dissolved ionic compound) that undergoes electrolysis.
• Electrodes: the rods (usually carbon or platinum) that carry current into and out of the electrolyte. They are inert — they carry the current but are not changed.
• Anode: the positive electrode (connected to + terminal of battery).
• Cathode: the negative electrode (connected to − terminal of battery).
• Electrolytic cell: the whole apparatus (electrodes + electrolyte + power supply).

!
Don't Confuse — Anode vs Cathode

This is the single biggest mix-up in electrolysis. Two memory tricks:
• PA: Positive = Anode
• Think of a cat: a cat is negative (grumpy!) = cathode is the negative electrode
The exam will test this in every electrolysis question. Get it wrong and every other part of your answer will be backwards.

W
Write This Down — Electrolysis of Molten Compounds

When you electrolyse a molten ionic compound (only two elements present), the pattern is simple:

• The metal forms at the cathode (negative electrode)
• The non-metal forms at the anode (positive electrode)

Example — molten lead(II) bromide (PbBr₂):
• At the cathode: silvery bead of lead forms
• At the anode: brown bromine gas bubbles off
• Overall: lead(II) bromide → lead + bromine

W
Write This Down — Why Do Ions Move?

Opposites attract. During electrolysis:
• Positive ions (cations, e.g. Pb²⁺, Na⁺) are attracted to the negative electrode (cathode)
• Negative ions (anions, e.g. Br⁻, Cl⁻) are attracted to the positive electrode (anode)

So: electrons carry the current through the wires and electrodes, but ions carry the current through the liquid.

W
Write This Down — Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions (The Rules)

When the electrolyte is a solution (dissolved in water), there are extra ions from water: H⁺ and OH⁻. So the products may be different from the molten compound.

At the cathode (−), either a metal or hydrogen forms:
• If the metal is more reactive than hydrogen (e.g. sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium, zinc, iron, lead), the metal stays in solution and hydrogen gas bubbles off.
• If the metal is less reactive than hydrogen (e.g. copper, silver), the metal is deposited.

At the anode (+), a non-metal other than hydrogen forms:
• If the solution is a concentrated halide (contains Cl⁻, Br⁻, or I⁻), the halogen (chlorine, bromine, or iodine) forms.
• If the solution is dilute, or there is no halide, then oxygen forms.

?
Why This Matters

The exam will give you an electrolyte and ask you to predict the products at each electrode. If you learn the two rules (reactivity for the cathode, halide concentration for the anode) and this table, you can answer any prediction question. This is one of the most common 4-6 mark questions on the paper.

T
Study Tip — Practice the Predictions

Write out 5 electrolytes on flashcards (e.g. "concentrated CuCl₂ solution"). On the back, write the cathode product and anode product. Test yourself. The key is: (1) Is the metal above or below hydrogen in the reactivity series? (2) Is there a concentrated halide? If you can answer those two questions, you can predict any electrolysis result.

  • I can define electrolysis, electrolyte, electrode, anode, and cathode
  • I know that positive ions go to the cathode and negative ions go to the anode
  • I can predict the products of electrolysis of a molten ionic compound
  • I can state the rules for predicting products of aqueous electrolysis (cathode: reactivity; anode: halide or not)
  • I can predict products for at least 5 different electrolytes from memory
Section 7.3 The Reactions at the Electrodes
This is the hardest part of the chapter, but also the most important for your exam. You need to understand oxidation and reduction in terms of electron transfer, and be able to write half-equations for what happens at each electrode.
W
Write This Down — OILRIG

Oxidation Is Loss of electrons.
Reduction Is Gain of electrons.

During electrolysis:
• At the cathode (−): ions gain electrons → reduction
• At the anode (+): ions lose electrons → oxidation

Memory trick: RAC — Reduction At Cathode.

Overall, electrolysis is a redox reaction (both reduction and oxidation happen).

!
Don't Confuse — Oxidation vs Reduction at Electrodes

Students constantly get this backwards. Here's how to keep it straight:
• The cathode is negative, so it has extra electrons to give away. Ions at the cathode gain electrons = reduction.
• The anode is positive, so it pulls electrons away from ions. Ions at the anode lose electrons = oxidation.
If you write "oxidation at the cathode" on an exam, every half-equation mark after that will be wrong too. Get OILRIG + RAC locked in.

W
Write This Down — Worked Half-Equations (Molten Lead(II) Bromide)

At the cathode (reduction — ions gain electrons):
Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb
Each lead ion gains 2 electrons and becomes a lead atom. A silvery bead of lead forms.

At the anode (oxidation — ions lose electrons):
2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻
Each bromide ion loses 1 electron. Two bromide ions pair up to form one bromine molecule. Brown bromine gas bubbles off.

W
Write This Down — Worked Half-Equations (Concentrated NaCl Solution)

The solution contains Na⁺, Cl⁻, H⁺, and OH⁻ (the last two from water).

At the cathode (reduction):
2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)
Hydrogen ions gain electrons (not sodium, because sodium is more reactive and "prefers" to stay as ions). Hydrogen gas bubbles off.

At the anode (oxidation):
2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻
Chloride ions lose electrons (concentrated solution, so plenty of Cl⁻ ions present). Chlorine gas bubbles off.

The Na⁺ and OH⁻ ions remain in solution, so you end up with a solution of sodium hydroxide (NaOH).

W
Write This Down — Worked Half-Equations (Dilute NaCl Solution)

Same ions present, but now Cl⁻ concentration is low.

At the cathode: 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻ → 2H₂(g) — hydrogen still forms.
At the anode: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ — oxygen forms instead (not enough Cl⁻).

Overall result: water has been decomposed (hydrogen + oxygen produced). The sodium chloride solution is still there.

T
Study Tip — How to Write Half-Equations

Follow these steps every time:
1. Name the ions present and the products.
2. Write the ion with its correct charge (e.g. Pb²⁺, Cl⁻).
3. Positive ions go to cathode, negative ions go to anode.
4. Write the correct element symbol for the product (Cl₂ not Cl, Br₂ not Br).
5. Balance the electrons: the number of electrons must equal the total charge on the ion(s). E.g. Pb²⁺ needs 2e⁻; two Cl⁻ ions means 2e⁻ are released.
6. Add state symbols if needed: (l) for molten, (aq) for solution, (s) for solid, (g) for gas.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example: Molten Magnesium Chloride

Step 1: Name the ions and products.
Magnesium ions (Mg²⁺) and chloride ions (Cl⁻) are present. Products: magnesium and chlorine.

Step 2: Write each half-equation correctly.
• Reduction at the cathode: Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Mg
• Oxidation at the anode: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
(Two Cl⁻ ions have a total charge of 2−, so 2 electrons are released. Write Cl₂ not Cl, since chlorine exists as molecules.)

Step 3: Add state symbols.
• Mg²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Mg(s)
• 2Cl⁻(l) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻

?
Why This Matters

Half-equations are worth 2-3 marks each on the exam and appear in almost every electrolysis question. If you can write half-equations for lead bromide, magnesium chloride, sodium chloride (concentrated and dilute), and copper sulfate, you can handle anything the exam throws at you.

  • I can state what OILRIG stands for
  • I know that reduction happens at the cathode and oxidation at the anode (RAC)
  • I can write the half-equations for molten lead(II) bromide
  • I can write the half-equations for molten magnesium chloride
  • I can write the half-equations for concentrated sodium chloride solution
  • I can explain why dilute NaCl gives oxygen at the anode but concentrated gives chlorine
Section 7.4 Electroplating
When you use electrodes made of the same metal as the solution (instead of inert carbon/platinum electrodes), something different happens: the anode dissolves and the cathode gets coated. This is the basis of electroplating and metal refining.
W
Write This Down — Copper Sulfate with Different Electrodes

A. Carbon (inert) electrodes:
• Cathode: copper is deposited (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu)
• Anode: oxygen bubbles off (4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻)
• Blue colour fades because Cu²⁺ ions are being removed from solution

B. Copper electrodes (not inert):
• Cathode: copper is deposited (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu) — cathode grows larger
• Anode: copper dissolves into the solution (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻) — anode shrinks
• Blue colour stays the same because Cu²⁺ ions are being replaced as fast as they are removed
• Net effect: copper moves from the anode to the cathode

W
Write This Down — Electroplating

Electroplating means using electrolysis to coat one metal with another, to make it look better or to prevent corrosion.

The set-up for electroplating an object with metal X:
• Cathode = the object to be plated (e.g. steel jug)
• Anode = pure metal X (e.g. silver bar)
• Electrolyte = a solution of a soluble compound of X (e.g. silver nitrate solution)

Examples:
• Steel taps plated with chromium (looks shiny, prevents rust)
• Steel food cans plated with tin (prevents corrosion)
• Cheap jewellery plated with silver (looks good, cheaper than solid silver)

W
Write This Down — Copper Refining

Metal refining uses the same principle as electroplating to purify metals.
• Anode = impure copper
• Cathode = pure copper
• Electrolyte = dilute copper(II) sulfate solution

The impure copper dissolves from the anode. Pure copper deposits on the cathode. Impurities (including valuable metals like platinum, gold, silver, and selenium) fall to the bottom as sludge and are recovered. The final copper is over 99.9% pure — essential for electrical wiring.

!
Don't Confuse — Which Is the Cathode in Electroplating?

In electroplating, the object you want to coat is always the cathode (because metal is deposited at the cathode). The plating metal is the anode (because it dissolves). Students sometimes get these backwards. Remember: you want the metal to go onto the object, and metal goes to the cathode.

?
Why This Matters

Electroplating and copper refining are common exam application questions. They'll describe a set-up and ask you to identify the anode, cathode, and electrolyte, or ask you to design a set-up to electroplate a given object. If you know the three rules (cathode = object, anode = plating metal, electrolyte = solution of plating metal compound), you can answer all of them.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Set-Up

Draw and label the electroplating set-up from memory: battery at the top, two electrodes dipping into a solution. Label the anode (plating metal), cathode (object), and electrolyte (solution of plating metal compound). Draw arrows showing: electrons flow in the wire, metal ions move through the solution from anode to cathode. If you can draw this without looking, you understand electroplating.

  • I can explain why the blue colour fades with carbon electrodes but stays with copper electrodes
  • I can define electroplating and give two reasons it is done
  • I know which is the anode, cathode, and electrolyte in any electroplating set-up
  • I can describe how copper is purified by electrolysis
