Section 12.1 The Periodic Table: An Overview
The Periodic Table arranges all 118 known elements in order of proton number (atomic number). The way it's organised reveals patterns and trends that let you predict how elements will behave.
W
Write This Down — Key Vocabulary

• Proton number (atomic number): the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. Elements are arranged in the Periodic Table in order of increasing proton number.
• Group: a vertical column of elements. Elements in the same group have the same number of outer shell electrons and similar chemical properties.
• Period: a horizontal row. The period number tells you how many electron shells the atoms have. Period 2 = 2 shells, Period 3 = 3 shells.
• Periodicity: the pattern where elements with similar properties appear at regular intervals when arranged by proton number.

W
Write This Down — Groups and Outer Electrons

The group number = the number of outer shell electrons (for Groups I–VII).
• Group I elements have 1 outer electron
• Group VII elements have 7 outer electrons
• Group VIII (noble gases) have a full outer shell — that's why they are unreactive and monatomic (exist as single atoms)

W
Write This Down — Metals vs Non-Metals

• The zig-zag line on the Periodic Table separates metals (left) from non-metals (right). Hydrogen is an exception — it sits on the left but behaves like a non-metal.
• Over 80% of the elements are metals.
• Transition elements are the large block of metals in the middle (between Groups II and III).
• 94 elements occur naturally. The rest are synthetic — made in labs, radioactive, and often decay within fractions of a second.

?
Why This Matters

If you know an element's position in the Periodic Table, you can predict its properties. The exam will ask things like "Element X is in Group II, Period 3. Predict its properties." You use: group number = outer electrons, period = shells, left of zig-zag = metal. That's 3 marks from just knowing where it sits.

!
Don't Confuse — Groups vs Periods

Groups go down (vertical columns) — same outer electrons, similar properties. Periods go across (horizontal rows) — same number of shells, but properties change from metal to non-metal. The exam loves to test whether you know which is which.

T
Study Tip

Draw a blank Periodic Table outline. Label Groups I, II, III–VII, VIII. Draw the zig-zag line. Mark "metals" on the left, "non-metals" on the right, and "transition elements" in the middle. If you can sketch this from memory, you understand the layout.

  • I can state what "group" and "period" mean
  • I know that group number = number of outer shell electrons
  • I know that period number = number of electron shells
  • I can describe where metals, non-metals, and transition elements sit on the table
  • I know why Group VIII elements are unreactive
Section 12.2 Group I: The Alkali Metals
The alkali metals — lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, caesium — are soft, silvery metals with 1 outer shell electron. They are the most reactive metals in the Periodic Table, and reactivity increases as you go down the group.
W
Write This Down — Physical Properties

The alkali metals are not typical metals. They are:
• Soft — you can cut them with a knife
• Low density — lithium, sodium, and potassium float on water (density less than 1 g/cm³ for Li and K)
• Low melting points compared to most metals

W
Write This Down — Trends in Physical Properties (Copy This Table)
Metal Density (g/cm³) Melting Point (°C)
Lithium, Li 0.53 181
Sodium, Na 0.97 98
Potassium, K 0.86 63
Rubidium, Rb 1.53 39
Caesium, Cs 1.88 29

Trends down Group I: density increases, melting point decreases.

W
Write This Down — Reactions with Water

All alkali metals react vigorously with water to produce a metal hydroxide (an alkali) and hydrogen gas.

Word equation: sodium + water → sodium hydroxide + hydrogen

• Lithium: floats, fizzes gently, moves around on the surface
• Sodium: melts into a silver ball, shoots across the water, fizzes vigorously
• Potassium: melts, shoots across the water, catches fire, burns with a lilac flame

The indicator changes colour because the hydroxide produced is an alkali — that's why they're called alkali metals.

W
Write This Down — Reactions with Chlorine and Oxygen

• With chlorine: alkali metals burn brightly to form chlorides (e.g. sodium + chlorine → sodium chloride). Potassium burns most vigorously.
• With oxygen: they burn fiercely to form oxides. These dissolve in water to give alkaline solutions. Again, potassium reacts most vigorously.

Key point: Reactivity increases as you go down Group I.

W
Write This Down — Why They React the Same Way

All Group I metals have 1 outer shell electron. Atoms with the same number of outer shell electrons react in a similar way. When they react, they lose that 1 electron to form ions with a charge of 1+ (e.g. Na⁺, K⁺). The products are ionic compounds — all white solids that dissolve in water to give colourless solutions.

?
Why This Matters — Why Reactivity Increases Down the Group

As you go down Group I, atoms have more electron shells. The outer electron is further from the nucleus, so the pull from the nucleus is weaker. That makes it easier to lose the outer electron, so the element is more reactive. This explanation is worth 3 marks on the exam — learn the chain: more shells → outer electron further away → weaker attraction → easier to lose → more reactive.

!
Don't Confuse — Potassium's Density

Look at the density table: the general trend is that density increases down the group, but potassium (0.86) is actually less dense than sodium (0.97). This breaks the trend. The exam sometimes asks you to spot the anomaly. Remember: the overall trend is increasing, even though K doesn't follow it perfectly.

T
Study Tip — Memorise the Water Reactions

Think of the violence increasing: lithium fizzes, sodium shoots, potassium catches fire. If the exam gives you rubidium and asks you to predict, just go one step further — "it would react even more vigorously, possibly exploding." They're stored under oil because they react with air and water.

  • I can list three physical properties of alkali metals
  • I know the trends in density and melting point down Group I
  • I can describe how Li, Na, and K react with water
  • I can write the word equation for an alkali metal reacting with water
  • I can explain why reactivity increases down Group I (using electron shells)
  • I know that Group I metals form 1+ ions and ionic compounds
Section 12.3 Group VII: The Halogens
The halogens — fluorine, chlorine, bromine, and iodine — are a group of reactive non-metals. They all have 7 outer shell electrons and exist as diatomic molecules (e.g. Cl₂, Br₂). Unlike the alkali metals, reactivity decreases going down Group VII.
W
Write This Down — Physical Properties (Copy This Table)
Halogen Appearance at Room Temperature Boiling Point (°C)
Chlorine, Cl₂ Pale yellow-green gas −35
Bromine, Br₂ Red-brown liquid 59
Iodine, I₂ Grey-black solid 184

Trends down Group VII: boiling point increases, density increases, colour gets darker. The halogens go from gas to liquid to solid.

W
Write This Down — Chemical Properties

The halogens react with metals to form compounds called halides. For example, with iron wool:
• Chlorine: hot iron wool glows brightly → iron(III) chloride, FeCl₃ (yellow solid)
• Bromine: glows less brightly → iron(III) bromide, FeBr₃ (red-brown solid)
• Iodine: faint red glow → iron(III) iodide, FeI₃ (black solid)

Key point: Reactivity decreases as you go down Group VII. This is the opposite of the trend in Group I.

W
Write This Down — Why They React the Same Way

All halogens have 7 outer shell electrons. They need only 1 more electron to fill their outer shell. When they react:
• With metals: halogen atoms accept electrons to form halide ions (e.g. Cl⁻, Br⁻). The products are ionic compounds.
• With non-metals: halogen atoms share electrons, forming molecules with covalent bonds (e.g. HCl).

W
Write This Down — Displacement Reactions

A more reactive halogen will displace a less reactive halogen from a solution of its halide. This is the key exam topic:

• Chlorine + potassium bromide → potassium chloride + bromine (solution turns orange)
• Chlorine + potassium iodide → potassium chloride + iodine (solution turns red-brown)
• Bromine + potassium iodide → potassium bromide + iodine (solution turns red-brown)

But bromine + potassium chloride → no reaction (bromine is less reactive than chlorine, so it cannot displace it).

?
Why This Matters — Why Reactivity Decreases Down the Group

Going down Group VII, atoms have more electron shells. The outer shell is further from the nucleus. It is therefore harder for the atom to attract the one extra electron it needs. So the element is less reactive. Chain: more shells → outer shell further from nucleus → weaker attraction for incoming electron → less reactive. Chlorine is more reactive than bromine, which is more reactive than iodine.

!
Don't Confuse — Group I vs Group VII Reactivity Trends

This is a classic exam trap. In Group I, reactivity increases going down (easier to lose electrons). In Group VII, reactivity decreases going down (harder to gain electrons). The reason is the same — more shells means the outer shell is further from the nucleus — but the effect is opposite because metals lose electrons while non-metals gain them.

T
Study Tip — Displacement Table

Draw a 3×3 grid: rows = halogen added (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂), columns = solution it's added to (chloride, bromide, iodide). Fill in "displaces" or "no reaction." The rule is simple: a halogen only displaces one below it in the group. If you can fill this grid from memory, you own the displacement topic.

  • I can describe the appearance and state of chlorine, bromine, and iodine
  • I know that boiling point and density increase down Group VII
  • I know that reactivity decreases down Group VII (opposite of Group I)
  • I can explain the displacement reaction rule
  • I can predict the colour change when a halogen displaces another
  • I can explain why halogens are reactive (need 1 electron to fill outer shell)
Section 12.4 More About the Trends
This section ties everything together: how group number links to ion charge, why reactivity trends work the way they do, and what happens as you go across a period (not just down a group).
W
Write This Down — Group Number and Ion Charge (Copy This Table)
Group Outer Shell Electrons Electrons Lost or Gained Ion Charge Examples
I 1 1 lost 1+ Na⁺, K⁺
II 2 2 lost 2+ Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺
III 3 3 lost 3+ Al³⁺, Ga³⁺
VI 6 2 gained 2− O²⁻, S²⁻
VII 7 1 gained 1− Cl⁻, Br⁻

Rule: Metals lose electrons to form positive ions. Non-metals gain electrons to form negative ions. A full outer shell makes an atom stable — that's why Group VIII elements are unreactive.

W
Write This Down — Why Electron Shells Affect Reactivity

The nucleus is positive and pulls on the outer electrons. But more shells = more distance = weaker pull. This means:
• Group I (metals): more shells → outer electron further from nucleus → easier to lose → more reactive going down
• Group VII (non-metals): more shells → outer shell further from nucleus → harder to attract an extra electron → less reactive going down

Across Period 3: elements change from metals → metalloid → non-metals. Reactivity is high at both ends (Na on the left, Cl on the right) and low in the middle. Oxides change from basic → amphoteric → acidic.

W
Write This Down — Metalloids

Silicon is a metalloid — it has properties of both metals and non-metals. It can conduct electricity under certain conditions (semiconductor) but forms covalent bonds with oxygen like a non-metal. Other periods also have metalloids at the boundary between metals and non-metals.

?
Why This Matters

The ion charge table is a gift on the exam. If they ask "What charge does a magnesium ion have?" you just look at the group: Group II → loses 2 electrons → charge 2+. The exam also loves "Explain why sodium is more reactive than magnesium." Answer: sodium has 1 outer electron to lose (less energy needed) while magnesium has 2 (more energy needed), so sodium reacts more easily.

!
Don't Confuse — Number of Electrons and Reactivity Across a Period

The fewer electrons to lose or gain, the less energy is needed, so the more reactive the element. That's why Na (lose 1) is more reactive than Mg (lose 2), and Cl (gain 1) is more reactive than S (gain 2). The least reactive elements in a period are in the middle, where atoms would need to lose or gain 3–4 electrons — too much energy.

T
Study Tip

The exam might give you a new element you haven't seen (like rubidium, Rb, in Group I, Period 5). Don't panic. Use what you know: Group I → 1 outer electron → forms 1+ ions → reacts with water to give hydroxide + hydrogen → more reactive than potassium because it's further down. Position tells you everything.

  • I can predict the ion charge from the group number
  • I can explain why more electron shells means different reactivity for metals vs non-metals
  • I know the main trends across Period 3 (metal → metalloid → non-metal)
  • I can explain why sodium is more reactive than magnesium
  • I know what a metalloid is and can give an example
Section 12.5 The Transition Elements
The transition elements sit in the middle of the Periodic Table, between Groups II and III. They include everyday metals like iron, copper, nickel, zinc, silver, and gold. They are very different from the alkali metals.
W
Write This Down — Physical Properties
Element Symbol Density (g/cm³) Melting Point (°C)
Iron Fe 7.9 1535
Copper Cu 8.9 1083
Nickel Ni 8.9 1455
Sodium (Group I) Na 0.97 98

Compared to alkali metals, transition elements have:
• High density (iron is over 8× heavier than sodium)
• High melting points (mercury is the exception — it's a liquid at room temperature)
• Hard and strong (not soft like Group I metals)
• Good conductors of heat and electricity (silver is the best conductor, then copper)

W
Write This Down — Chemical Properties

Transition elements are very different from Group I metals chemically:

• Much less reactive — copper and nickel do not react with water or catch fire in air (though iron rusts in damp air)
• No clear trend in reactivity (unlike Group I or VII)
• Form coloured compounds (e.g. copper sulfate is blue, iron(III) oxide is red-brown) — Group I metals only form white compounds
• Many act as catalysts (e.g. iron is used as a catalyst in making ammonia; platinum is used in catalytic converters)
• Form ions with variable charges (e.g. copper can be Cu⁺ or Cu²⁺; iron can be Fe²⁺ or Fe³⁺) — Group I metals always form 1+ ions
• Can form more than one compound with another element because of their variable charges (e.g. copper(I) oxide Cu₂O and copper(II) oxide CuO)

W
Write This Down — Oxidation Numbers

The Roman numeral in a compound name tells you the oxidation number — how many electrons the metal atom has lost.
• copper(I) oxide = Cu₂O (copper lost 1 electron each)
• copper(II) oxide = CuO (copper lost 2 electrons)
• iron(II) oxide = FeO (iron lost 2 electrons)
• iron(III) oxide = Fe₂O₃ (iron lost 3 electrons)

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves comparing transition elements with Group I metals. A classic 4–6 mark question: "Compare the properties of iron and sodium." You need to give at least three contrasts: (1) iron is hard/strong vs sodium is soft, (2) iron has a high melting point vs sodium is low, (3) iron forms coloured compounds vs sodium forms white ones, (4) iron has variable oxidation numbers vs sodium is always 1+. These are easy marks if you learn the comparisons.

!
Don't Confuse — "Unreactive" vs "Less Reactive"

Transition elements are less reactive than Group I metals, but they are not unreactive. Iron rusts (reacts with oxygen and water). Zinc reacts with acids. Don't say they don't react at all. The truly unreactive elements are the noble gases (Group VIII).

T
Study Tip — The Comparison List

Make a two-column table: "Group I Metals" vs "Transition Elements." Compare: hardness, density, melting point, reactivity, compound colour, ion charge (fixed vs variable), catalytic use. If you can write 6 differences from memory, you're exam-ready for this topic.

  • I can state three chemical differences between transition elements and Group I metals
  • I understand what variable oxidation numbers mean
  • I know that transition elements form coloured compounds and act as catalysts
