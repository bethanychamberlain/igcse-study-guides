Acids, Bases, and Salts

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Acid Produces H⁺ ions in solution. pH below 7. Proton donor. Alkali (produces OH⁻ ions, pH above 7)
Base A metal oxide or hydroxide that neutralises acids. Proton acceptor. Alkali — an alkali is a SOLUBLE base. Not all bases are alkalis.
Alkali A soluble base. Produces OH⁻ ions in solution. Examples: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂. Base — CuO is a base but NOT an alkali (it's insoluble)
Neutralisation Acid + base → salt + water. The H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to make H₂O. Acid + metal is NOT neutralisation (no water is produced)
Strong acid Completely dissociates into ions (100%). Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃. Concentrated — that's about AMOUNT, not dissociation
Weak acid Partially dissociates (reversible ⇌). Fewer H⁺ ions. Examples: ethanoic acid, citric acid. Dilute — a concentrated weak acid is still weak
Amphoteric Reacts with both acids and alkalis. Two examples: Al₂O₃ and ZnO. Basic oxide (reacts with acids only) or acidic oxide (reacts with alkalis only)
Precipitation Mixing two solutions to form an insoluble solid (the precipitate). —

The Three Acid Reactions — Word Equations

Reaction Word Equation Products
Acid + Metal acid + metal → salt + hydrogen 2 products (H₂ bubbles off)
Acid + Base acid + base → salt + water 2 products (neutralisation)
Acid + Carbonate acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂ 3 products (fizzing = CO₂)
Exam Trap — Which Acid Gives Which Salt?
Hydrochloric acid → chlorides. Sulfuric acid → sulfates. Nitric acid → nitrates. The first part of the salt name comes from the metal/base. Example: sulfuric acid + sodium hydroxide → sodium sulfate + water.
Exam Trap — Three Products for Carbonates!
Acid + carbonate gives three products: salt + water + CO₂. Students often forget the CO₂. If you see fizzing/bubbling in a reaction with acid, think carbonate.

Say It in One Sentence

Acid Produces H⁺ ions in water — turns litmus red, pH below 7.
Alkali A soluble base that produces OH⁻ ions — turns litmus blue, pH above 7.
Neutralisation H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O — that's the ionic equation, and it's always the same.
pH scale 0–6 = acidic, 7 = neutral, 8–14 = alkaline. Lower number = more H⁺ ions.
Salt naming Metal name from the base + ending from the acid (chloride, sulfate, or nitrate).
Titration Used when both reactants are solutions — measure exact volumes, then repeat without indicator.
Precipitation Mix two solutions to make an insoluble salt — filter, wash, dry.

Memory Tricks

Acids Turn Litmus Red
"Acid" and "Red" are both short, punchy words. Remember that one and the other is automatic: alkalis turn litmus blue.
Acid + Metal = Salt + Hydrogen (Not Neutralisation!)
Neutralisation always makes water. Acid + metal makes hydrogen instead. No water = not neutralisation. The exam tests this distinction every year.
Solubility Rules: "N-SPA Always Dissolves"
Nitrates, Sodium, Potassium, Ammonium salts are ALWAYS soluble. For exceptions, remember SLP: Silver and Lead halides are insoluble; Barium, Calcium, and Lead sulfates are insoluble.
Titration Four-Step Method
1. Calculate moles of the known substance. 2. Find the molar ratio from the equation. 3. Calculate moles of the unknown. 4. Calculate concentration. Write all four steps = full marks, even if the final number is wrong.
Amphoteric = "Both Ways"
Only two oxides to remember: Al₂O₃ and ZnO. They react with acids AND alkalis. "A-Z both ways" — Aluminium and Zinc.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What ion do all acids produce in solution?" H⁺ (hydrogen ions)
"What ion do all alkalis produce in solution?" OH⁻ (hydroxide ions)
"Write the ionic equation for neutralisation" H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)
"Why does a strong acid have a lower pH than a weak acid at the same concentration?" The strong acid is fully dissociated (more H⁺ ions), while the weak acid is only partially dissociated (fewer H⁺ ions)
"Write the word equation for acid + carbonate" acid + carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide (three products!)
"What salt does hydrochloric acid form?" Chlorides (e.g. sodium chloride, magnesium chloride)
"What salt does sulfuric acid form?" Sulfates (e.g. copper sulfate, zinc sulfate)
"How would you make copper(II) sulfate crystals?" Add excess copper(II) oxide to warm dilute sulfuric acid. Filter off excess solid. Heat filtrate to get saturated solution, then cool to crystallise. CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O
"Why must you use a titration to make sodium chloride?" Both NaOH and HCl are soluble — you cannot filter off excess. You need to find the exact volume needed to neutralise, then repeat without indicator.
"Name two amphoteric oxides" Aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) and zinc oxide (ZnO)
"Give a real-life example of neutralisation" Indigestion tablets (base) neutralise excess stomach acid. Lime (CaO) neutralises acidic soil. Toothpaste (base) neutralises mouth acids.
