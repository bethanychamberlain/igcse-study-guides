Section 6.1 Oxidation and Reduction
Thousands of reactions happen every day — in labs, factories, and in nature. One huge group is redox reactions, where oxidation and reduction happen at the same time. This section covers the simplest way to understand them: in terms of oxygen gain and loss.
W
Write This Down — The Basic Definitions

Oxidation is a gain of oxygen. The substance has been oxidised.
Reduction is a loss of oxygen. The substance has been reduced.

Examples:
• Magnesium burns in air: magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide. The magnesium gains oxygen, so it is oxidised.
• Copper(II) oxide + hydrogen → copper + water. The copper(II) oxide loses oxygen, so it is reduced.

W
Write This Down — Oxidation and Reduction Always Happen Together

You cannot have oxidation without reduction. They always take place together. That is why we call the reaction a redox reaction (reduction + oxidation = redox).

In the CuO + H₂ reaction:
• CuO loses oxygen → it is reduced
• H₂ gains oxygen → it is oxidised
Both happen in the same reaction — that makes it a redox reaction.

!
Don't Confuse — Which One Is Which?

Students constantly mix up oxidation and reduction. Here is the single most important thing to remember: Oxidation = gain of Oxygen. The "O" in oxidation matches the "O" in oxygen. If something gains oxygen, it is oxidised. If it loses oxygen, it is reduced. Always check: where did the oxygen go?

?
Why This Matters — Real World

Rusting is oxidation: iron reacts with oxygen and water to form hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust), Fe₂O₃·2H₂O. Combustion (burning) is also oxidation: when you burn methane on a gas stove, the carbon and hydrogen in methane gain oxygen. Every burning reaction is a redox reaction. So is respiration in your body — glucose reacts with oxygen to give you energy.

T
Study Tip — Label the Equations

For every equation in this chapter, draw curved arrows above and below to show which substance is being oxidised and which is being reduced. For example:
• 2Ca + O₂ → 2CaO: calcium is oxidised (gains O), oxygen is reduced
• 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O: hydrogen is oxidised (gains O), oxygen is reduced
Practise this on every equation until it feels automatic.

  • I can define oxidation as a gain of oxygen
  • I can define reduction as a loss of oxygen
  • I can explain why oxidation and reduction always happen together
  • I can identify what is oxidised and what is reduced in a given equation
  • I can explain what the term "redox" means
Section 6.2 Redox and Electron Transfer
The oxygen definition works, but it is limited. There is a broader, more powerful way to define oxidation and reduction: in terms of electrons. This is the definition the exam cares about most.
W
Write This Down — OIL RIG (Learn This Forever)

OIL RIG:
• Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons)
• Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

When magnesium burns in oxygen:
• Each Mg atom loses 2 electrons → Mg is oxidised: Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻
• Each O atom gains 2 electrons → O is reduced: O₂ + 4e⁻ → 2O²⁻

W
Write This Down — Half-Equations

Half-equations show the electron transfer in a redox reaction. One half-equation shows oxidation (electron loss), the other shows reduction (electron gain).

How to write them (3 steps):
1. Write each reactant with the electrons it gains or loses
2. Make sure each substance is in its correct form (e.g. O₂ not O, Cl₂ not Cl)
3. Balance so the number of electrons is the same in both half-equations

Example — Sodium + Chlorine:
• Oxidation: 2Na → 2Na⁺ + 2e⁻
• Reduction: Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻
Both have 2 electrons — balanced!

W
Write This Down — Ionic Equations

When you add the two balanced half-equations together, the electrons cancel out, and you get the ionic equation. The ionic equation shows only the ions that take part in the reaction.

Example — Chlorine displacing bromine from potassium bromide:
• Reduction: Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻
• Oxidation: 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻
• Add them: Cl₂ + 2Br⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + Br₂ (electrons cancel)

!
Don't Confuse — "Loss" and "Gain" Are Backwards From What You Expect

Your instinct says "gaining something = good = oxidation." Wrong. Oxidation is loss of electrons. This trips up almost everyone. The mnemonic OIL RIG exists specifically because the words feel backwards. Write OIL RIG at the top of every redox question on the exam. Every. Single. Time.

!
Don't Confuse — Redox Does NOT Require Oxygen

The reaction between sodium and chlorine (2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl) is a redox reaction even though there is no oxygen anywhere. Any reaction involving electron transfer is redox. The electron-transfer definition is broader than the oxygen definition. If the exam asks "Is this a redox reaction?" check for electron transfer, not just oxygen.

?
Why This Matters

Half-equations come up in almost every extended-answer chemistry question about reactions. The exam will give you a full equation and ask you to write the half-equations, or give you half-equations and ask you to combine them into an ionic equation. If you can do this confidently, you pick up easy marks that most students drop.

T
Study Tip — Practise With Flashcards

Make flashcards: front side has a reaction equation, back side has the two half-equations labelled "oxidation" and "reduction." Test yourself until you can write the half-equations without looking. Start with: Mg + O₂, Na + Cl₂, Cl₂ + KBr. These three cover most exam patterns.

  • I can state OIL RIG from memory and explain what it means
  • I can define oxidation and reduction in terms of electron transfer
  • I can write balanced half-equations for a redox reaction
  • I can combine half-equations to form an ionic equation
  • I know that redox reactions do not have to involve oxygen
Section 6.3 Redox and Oxidation Numbers
Oxidation numbers are a way of tracking electrons in compounds. They let you spot redox reactions just by looking at the numbers — no need to draw electron diagrams every time.
W
Write This Down — What Is an Oxidation Number?

The oxidation number tells you how many electrons each atom of an element has lost, gained, or shared in forming a compound. It is usually written as a Roman numeral (I, II, III, etc.).

W
Write This Down — The Rules (Copy This Table)
Rule Detail Example
1. Uncombined elements Oxidation number = 0 Fe = 0, O₂ = 0, Na = 0
2. Simple (monatomic) ions Oxidation number = the charge Na⁺ = +I, Cl⁻ = −I, Mg²⁺ = +II
3. Hydrogen Usually +I H in H₂O = +I
4. Oxygen Usually −II (except in peroxides) O in H₂O = −II
5. Group I metals Always +I Na in NaCl = +I
6. Group II metals Always +II Ca in CaO = +II
7. Aluminium (Group III) Always +III Al in Al₂O₃ = +III
8. Compound total All oxidation numbers add up to zero MgCl₂: (+II) + 2(−I) = 0
W
Write This Down — Transition Metals Have Variable Oxidation Numbers

Most transition elements can have different oxidation numbers. The oxidation number is shown in the compound's name as a Roman numeral.

Element Common Oxidation Numbers
Iron +II and +III (e.g. iron(II) chloride, iron(III) oxide)
Copper +I and +II (e.g. copper(I) oxide, copper(II) oxide)
Manganese +II, +IV, and +VII
W
Write This Down — How Oxidation Numbers Identify Redox

• If an element's oxidation number increases (rises) during a reaction → it has been oxidised
• If an element's oxidation number decreases (falls) during a reaction → it has been reduced
• If oxidation numbers change, it is a redox reaction

Worked Example: Fe + S → FeS
• Fe: 0 → +II (rises = oxidised)
• S: 0 → −II (falls = reduced)
Oxidation numbers changed, so this is a redox reaction.

Non-example: NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl
• N: −III → −III (no change), H: +I → +I (no change), Cl: −I → −I (no change)
No change in oxidation numbers, so this is not a redox reaction.

!
Don't Confuse — "Increase" vs "Decrease"

An oxidation number going from 0 to +II is an increase (oxidation). An oxidation number going from 0 to −II is a decrease (reduction). Think of a number line: moving right (towards positive) = oxidation. Moving left (towards negative) = reduction. Up = oxidised, down = reduced.

?
Why This Matters

Oxidation numbers let you answer the exam question "Is this a redox reaction? Explain your answer" in a systematic way. Write the oxidation number below each element before and after the reaction. If any numbers changed, it is redox. If none changed, it is not. This method works on any equation, even complicated ones where you cannot easily see electron transfer.

T
Study Tip — Practise the Method

Take these three equations and assign oxidation numbers to every element, then state if it is redox or not:
1. 2FeCl₂ + Cl₂ → 2FeCl₃
2. C + O₂ → CO₂
3. NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O
(Answers: 1 = yes, Fe goes +II to +III. 2 = yes, C goes 0 to +IV. 3 = no, nothing changes.)

  • I can define oxidation number
  • I can state the rules for assigning oxidation numbers
  • I can work out the oxidation number of any element in a compound
  • I can use oxidation number changes to identify a redox reaction
Section 6.4 Oxidising and Reducing Agents
In every redox reaction, one substance causes oxidation and another causes reduction. These are the oxidising agent and reducing agent. The exam loves asking you to identify them and explain lab tests that use them.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Oxidising agent: a substance that oxidises another substance. It does this by taking electrons from the other substance. The oxidising agent is itself reduced.

Reducing agent: a substance that reduces another substance. It does this by giving electrons to the other substance. The reducing agent is itself oxidised.

Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
• CuO is the oxidising agent (it oxidises the hydrogen, and is itself reduced)
• H₂ is the reducing agent (it reduces the copper oxide, and is itself oxidised)

!
Don't Confuse — The Agent Does the Opposite of What Happens to It

This is the trickiest part of the whole chapter. The oxidising agent gets reduced. The reducing agent gets oxidised. It sounds contradictory, but think of it this way: the oxidising agent is the one causing oxidation in the other substance, like a bully taking someone's electrons. The bully (oxidising agent) gains electrons (is reduced), and the victim (reducing agent) loses electrons (is oxidised).

W
Write This Down — Common Oxidising and Reducing Agents
Strong Oxidising Agents Strong Reducing Agents
Oxygen (O₂) Hydrogen (H₂)
Chlorine (Cl₂) Reactive metals (Na, Mg, Zn)
Potassium manganate(VII), KMnO₄ Carbon (C) and carbon monoxide (CO)
Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) Potassium iodide (KI)
W
Write This Down — Lab Tests

Test for a reducing agent: Add acidified potassium manganate(VII) solution (purple). If a reducing agent is present, the purple colour fades to colourless (Mn goes from +VII to +II).

Test for an oxidising agent: Add potassium iodide solution (colourless). If an oxidising agent is present, the solution turns red-brown (iodide ions are oxidised to iodine: 2I⁻ → I₂).

?
Why This Matters — Oxidising Agents in Everyday Life

Strong oxidising agents are everywhere outside the lab:
• Bleach and household cleaners use oxidising agents to kill bacteria and remove colour
• Chlorine is added to water treatment plants to kill bacteria and make water safe to drink
• Hydrogen peroxide is used to bleach hair
The exam sometimes asks for everyday uses of oxidising agents — these three examples cover it.

T
Study Tip — The "Itself" Trick

Whenever the exam asks you to identify the oxidising and reducing agent, use this trick: find what is oxidised and what is reduced first (using OIL RIG or oxidation numbers). Then flip:
• The substance that is reduced = the oxidising agent
• The substance that is oxidised = the reducing agent
Write "is reduced = oxidising agent" on a sticky note and put it on your wall.

  • I can define oxidising agent and reducing agent
  • I can explain that the oxidising agent is itself reduced (and vice versa)
  • I can describe the test for a reducing agent using potassium manganate(VII)
  • I can describe the test for an oxidising agent using potassium iodide
