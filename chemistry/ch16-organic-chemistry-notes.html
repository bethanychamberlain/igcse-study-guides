Section 16.1 Petroleum: A Fossil Fuel
Petroleum (crude oil), coal, and natural gas are the three fossil fuels. They formed from the remains of ancient organisms buried under heat and pressure for millions of years. Petroleum is the most important because of its many uses.
W
Write This Down — Fossil Fuels

The three fossil fuels are petroleum (crude oil), coal, and natural gas (mainly methane). They formed from the remains of plants and animals that lived millions of years ago.

W
Write This Down — What Is Petroleum?

Petroleum is a mixture of hundreds of hydrocarbons. Hydrocarbons are compounds that contain only carbon and hydrogen.

Organic compounds are compounds that contain carbon atoms, usually in chains or rings, with hydrogen attached. Most compounds in petroleum are organic.

W
Write This Down — Displayed Formulae

Displayed formulae show all the atoms and all the bonds in a molecule. Each short line represents a covalent bond (a shared pair of electrons).

Carbon can form straight chains (e.g. pentane, C₅H₁₂), branched chains (e.g. 3-methylpentane, C₆H₁₄), or rings (e.g. cyclohexane, C₆H₁₂).

Carbon atoms form the spines of organic molecules. They can bond to 1–70+ other carbon atoms.

?
Why This Matters

Petroleum provides fuel for transport (over 50%), heat and electricity for homes and industry, and about 16% goes to make feedstock — the starting chemicals for plastics, detergents, drugs, and fabrics. The modern world runs on it.

!
Don't Confuse — Hydrocarbon vs Organic Compound

A hydrocarbon contains ONLY carbon and hydrogen (e.g. methane CH₄). An organic compound contains carbon but may also have oxygen, nitrogen, etc. (e.g. ethanol C₂H₅OH). All hydrocarbons are organic, but not all organic compounds are hydrocarbons.

  • I can name the three fossil fuels
  • I can define hydrocarbon and organic compound
  • I can explain what a displayed formula shows
  • I know the three uses of petroleum (fuel, heat, feedstock)
Section 16.2 Refining Petroleum
Petroleum is useless as a raw mixture. It must be separated into groups of compounds with similar chain lengths. This process is called fractional distillation, and it happens in huge towers at refineries.
W
Write This Down — How Fractional Distillation Works

Fractional distillation separates petroleum into fractions (groups of hydrocarbons with similar boiling points).

How it works:
• Petroleum is heated in a furnace until it vaporises
• The vapour enters a tall fractionating tower (hot at bottom, cool at top)
• As vapour rises, it cools. Each fraction condenses at the height where the temperature matches its boiling point
• Fractions are collected in trays at different heights

W
Write This Down — The Trends

As chain length increases (going down the tower):

• Boiling point increases (longer chains need more energy to separate)
• Viscosity increases (thicker, stickier liquids)
• Flammability decreases (harder to catch fire)
• Volatility decreases (evaporates less easily)

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves to ask you to explain trends. For example: "Why is petrol more flammable than diesel?" Answer: petrol has shorter chains, a lower boiling point, and is more volatile, so it catches fire more easily. Always link your answer back to chain length.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Tower

Draw a fractionating tower from memory: hot bottom, cool top. Label at least five fractions from top to bottom. Write one use next to each. Add arrows showing the trends (boiling point, viscosity up; flammability, volatility down). If you can draw this from scratch, you own the topic.

  • I can describe fractional distillation in 4 steps
  • I can name at least 5 fractions and give a use for each
  • I can state the 4 trends as chain length increases
  • I can draw a labelled fractionating tower from memory
Section 16.3 Four Families of Organic Compounds
The rest of organic chemistry is about four families: alkanes, alkenes, alcohols, and carboxylic acids. Each family has a specific naming system and a functional group that controls how it reacts.
W
Write This Down — The Naming System

Name endings tell you the family:

W
Write This Down — Three Types of Formula

You must know three ways to show a formula:

• Molecular formula — just the numbers of each atom (e.g. C₃H₈)
• Structural formula — shows each unit but not every bond (e.g. CH₃CH₂CH₃)
• Displayed formula — shows every atom and every bond (drawn out with lines)

W
Write This Down — Functional Groups

A functional group is an atom or group of atoms in a molecule that dictates how the compound will react.

• Alkenes: C=C (carbon-carbon double bond)
• Alcohols: –OH (hydroxyl group)
• Carboxylic acids: –COOH (carboxyl group)
• Alkanes have no functional group — only single C–C bonds

W
Write This Down — Homologous Series

A homologous series is a family of similar compounds that have the same functional group and therefore similar chemical properties. In a homologous series:

• All members fit the same general formula
• Each member differs by one CH₂ unit
• Members show a trend in physical properties (melting/boiling points rise, viscosity rises, flammability falls with chain length)

!
Don't Confuse — This Is Critical For You

Alkanes vs alkenes: The names look almost identical! Alkane = single bonds only (saturated). Alkene = has a double bond (unsaturated). One letter changes everything about how the compound reacts.

T
Study Tip — Flashcard Drill

Make flashcards: one side has the name (e.g. "propanol"), the other side has the family, molecular formula, structural formula, and functional group. Drill these until you can do them instantly. The naming system is the skeleton key to every organic chemistry question.

  • I can name a compound from its formula (and vice versa) for all four families
  • I know the prefix for 1–5 carbons (meth, eth, prop, but, pent)
  • I can write molecular, structural, and displayed formulae
  • I can define functional group and name the one for each family
  • I can give the four characteristics of a homologous series
Section 16.4 The Alkanes
Alkanes are the simplest organic family. They come straight from petroleum, they burn well as fuels, and they are the starting point for making the other three families. But they are generally unreactive.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts About Alkanes

• General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
• Contain only carbon and hydrogen — they are hydrocarbons
• All bonds are single covalent bonds — they are saturated
• Generally unreactive (no functional group to attack)

W
Write This Down — The First Four Alkanes
NameFormulaCarbonsBoiling Pt
MethaneCH₄1−164 °C
EthaneC₂H₆2−87 °C
PropaneC₃H₈3−42 °C
ButaneC₄H₁₀4−0.5 °C

All four are gases at room temperature. Boiling points increase with chain length.

W
Write This Down — Combustion of Alkanes

Complete combustion (plenty of oxygen): alkane + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + heat

Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen): produces carbon monoxide (CO) — a poisonous gas — and/or carbon (soot), plus water and less heat.

Example: 2CH₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO + 4H₂O

W
Write This Down — Substitution Reactions

Alkanes react with chlorine in sunlight (a photochemical reaction). A hydrogen atom is replaced by a chlorine atom. This is a substitution reaction.

Example: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (chloromethane)

Further substitution gives: CH₂Cl₂ (dichloromethane), CHCl₃ (trichloromethane), CCl₄ (tetrachloromethane).

W
Write This Down — Structural Isomers

Structural isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural formulae.

Example: butane and 2-methylpropane both have the formula C₄H₁₀, but butane is a straight chain while 2-methylpropane is branched. Branched isomers have lower boiling points because their molecules cannot pack as closely together.

!
Don't Confuse — Substitution vs Addition

Alkanes do substitution (swap one atom for another). Alkenes do addition (atoms add across the double bond). If the exam asks which type of reaction alkanes do, it's substitution. If it says alkenes, it's addition. Don't mix these up!

T
Study Tip — Draw All Four From Memory

Practice drawing the displayed formulae for methane, ethane, propane, and butane. Remember: each carbon makes 4 bonds, each hydrogen makes 1 bond. If you can draw them correctly every time, you will get these marks on the exam.

  • I can give the general formula for alkanes and explain "saturated"
  • I can draw displayed formulae for the first four alkanes
  • I can write equations for complete and incomplete combustion of methane
  • I can describe the substitution reaction of methane with chlorine
  • I can define structural isomers and give an example
Section 16.5 Cracking Alkanes
There is high demand for short-chain hydrocarbons (petrol) and for alkenes (to make plastics). Cracking breaks long, less useful molecules into shorter, more useful ones.
W
Write This Down — What Is Cracking?

Cracking breaks large hydrocarbon molecules into smaller, more useful ones.

Method: liquid hydrocarbons are sprayed through a very hot catalyst (a porous mineral called a zeolite). The liquid vaporises on the catalyst and the molecules break apart. This is called catalytic cracking.

W
Write This Down — Why Crack?

Two key reasons:

• To meet demand for shorter-chain fractions — e.g. there is more naphtha (C₆–C₁₀) than needed but not enough petrol (C₅–C₆). Cracking naphtha gives petrol-length molecules.
• To produce alkenes — alkenes have C=C double bonds, making them reactive. They are the starting materials for plastics, alcohols, and many other products.

W
Write This Down — Examples

Cracking decane: C₁₀H₂₂ → C₅H₁₂ + C₃H₆ + C₂H₄

(decane → pentane + propene + ethene)

Cracking ethane: C₂H₆ → C₂H₄ + H₂

(ethane → ethene + hydrogen) — needs steam and over 800 °C

Notice: cracking always produces at least one alkene (with a C=C bond).

?
Why This Matters

Cracking is one of the most important industrial processes. Without it, we would not have enough petrol for cars or ethene for making plastics. The exam often asks: "Give two reasons why cracking is important." Make sure you can write both reasons clearly.

  • I can define cracking and describe how catalytic cracking works
  • I can give two reasons why cracking is important
  • I can write an equation for cracking decane or ethane
Section 16.6 The Alkenes
Alkenes are the reactive family. Their C=C double bond makes them much more useful than alkanes because it can open up and add on other atoms — this is the basis of the entire plastics industry.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts About Alkenes

• General formula: CₙH₂ₙ
• Contain a C=C double bond — this is their functional group
• They are unsaturated (they have a double bond that can open up)
• They are not found in petroleum — they are made by cracking alkanes
• Much more reactive than alkanes

W

NameFormulaCarbonsBoiling Pt
EtheneC₂H₄2−102 °C
PropeneC₃H₆3−47 °C
But-1-eneC₄H₈4−6.5 °C
W
Write This Down — Addition Reactions

An addition reaction turns an unsaturated alkene into a saturated compound. There is only one product. The double bond opens and atoms add across it.

Key addition reactions of ethene:

• + hydrogen (Ni catalyst, heat/pressure) → ethane (an alkane)

• + steam (acid catalyst, heat/pressure) → ethanol (an alcohol)

• + bromine → 1,2-dibromoethane (the test for unsaturation)

W
Write This Down — Test for Unsaturation

Add bromine water (orange) to the compound. If the compound is unsaturated (has a C=C bond), the bromine water decolourises (turns from orange to colourless). If saturated, no colour change.

!
Don't Confuse — Saturated vs Unsaturated

Saturated = all single bonds (alkanes). Unsaturated = contains at least one C=C double bond (alkenes). The bromine water test distinguishes them. This is a very common exam question — don't get the colour change backwards. Orange → colourless = unsaturated.

T
Study Tip — Think "Double Bond = Door"

Think of the C=C double bond as a door that can open up to let new atoms in. That is why alkenes do addition reactions — atoms add on through the open door. Alkanes have no door (no double bond), so they can only substitute (swap) atoms instead.

  • I can give the general formula for alkenes and explain "unsaturated"
  • I can name and draw the first three alkenes
  • I can describe three addition reactions of ethene (H₂, steam, Br₂)
  • I can describe the bromine water test for unsaturation
Section 16.7 The Alcohols
Alcohols contain the –OH (hydroxyl) group. Ethanol is the most important member — it is used as a solvent, a fuel, and in hand sanitisers. There are two ways to make it, and the exam loves to compare them.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts About Alcohols

• General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH
• Functional group: –OH (hydroxyl group)
• They are not hydrocarbons (they contain oxygen)
• All liquid at room temperature (first four members)
• Names end in -ol: methanol, ethanol, propan-1-ol, butan-1-ol

Write This Down — Ethanol as a Fuel

Ethanol burns cleanly: C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O + heat

Ethanol from fermentation is a biofuel — made from renewable plant material. It is increasingly mixed with petrol. It still produces CO₂ when burned, but the plants absorbed CO₂ while growing, so the net environmental impact is lower than fossil fuels.

Why This Matters

The exam will ask you to "compare the two methods of making ethanol, giving advantages and disadvantages of each." This is a classic 4–6 mark question. Learn two advantages and two disadvantages for each method. The table above has everything you need.

Section 16.8 The Carboxylic Acids
Carboxylic acids contain the –COOH (carboxyl) group. Ethanoic acid is the one you need to know best — it is the acid in vinegar. These acids behave like typical acids, but they are weak acids.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts About Carboxylic Acids

• General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH
• Functional group: –COOH (carboxyl group)
• Names end in -oic acid: methanoic acid (HCOOH), ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH), propanoic acid (C₂H₅COOH), butanoic acid (C₃H₇COOH)
• They are not hydrocarbons (they contain oxygen)
• Ethanoic acid is a weak acid (only partially dissociates in water)

W
Write This Down — Two Ways to Make Ethanoic Acid

• Chemical method: Oxidise ethanol using potassium manganate(VII) — the purple solution turns colourless
• Biological method: Leave ethanol standing in air — bacteria oxidise it to ethanoic acid (acid fermentation). This is how vinegar is made.

W
Write This Down — Typical Acid Reactions

Ethanoic acid behaves like any acid:

• + metal: 2CH₃COOH + Mg → (CH₃COO)₂Mg + H₂ (fizzing, hydrogen gas)
• + base: CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
• + carbonate: 2CH₃COOH + K₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COOK + CO₂ + H₂O (fizzing)

W
Write This Down — Esters

Carboxylic acid + alcohol → ester + water (with an acid catalyst, e.g. conc. H₂SO₄)

This is a condensation reaction (two molecules join, losing a small molecule — water).

Esters have pleasant smells and tastes. They are used in perfumes, food flavourings, and cosmetics. The ester trade is worth over 1 billion dollars a year.

Naming: the alcohol part goes first in the ester name. E.g. ethanoic acid + propanol → propyl ethanoate + water.

!
Don't Confuse — Weak vs Dilute

Weak acid means only some molecules dissociate (split into ions) in water. Dilute acid means there isn't much acid dissolved in the water. You can have a concentrated weak acid or a dilute strong acid — they are completely different ideas.
