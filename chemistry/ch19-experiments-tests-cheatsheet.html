Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Independent variable The thing you deliberately change in an experiment. Dependent variable (the thing you measure)
Dependent variable The thing you measure — it depends on what you changed. Independent variable (the thing you change on purpose)
Control variables Everything you keep the same to make the test fair. —
Hypothesis A testable explanation or prediction that can be tested by experiment. Observation (just noticing something — not yet a testable idea)
Cation A positive ion (e.g. Na⁺, Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺). Goes to the cathode. Anion (negative ion — goes to the anode)
Anion A negative ion (e.g. Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻). Goes to the anode. Cation (positive ion — goes to the cathode)
Precipitate An insoluble solid that forms when two solutions are mixed. Residue (solid left after filtration — similar but different context)
Limewater Calcium hydroxide solution, Ca(OH)₂. Used to test for CO₂ (turns milky/cloudy). Lime juice (completely different — limewater is a chemistry reagent)

Gas Tests — The Complete Table

Gas Test Procedure Positive Result
Hydrogen (H₂) Hold a lighted splint to the gas Burns with a squeaky pop
Oxygen (O₂) Hold a glowing splint to the gas Splint relights
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Bubble through limewater Turns cloudy / milky
Ammonia (NH₃) Hold damp red litmus paper in the gas Turns blue (+ sharp smell)
Chlorine (Cl₂) Hold damp indicator paper in the gas Bleached white
Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) Hold filter paper soaked in acidified KMnO₄ in the gas Purple turns colourless
Exam Trap — Lighted vs Glowing Splint
Lighted splint (has a flame) = test for hydrogen (squeaky pop). Glowing splint (ember, no flame) = test for oxygen (relights). Mix them up and you lose the mark every time.

Flame Tests — Cation Colours

Cation Flame Colour
Lithium, Li⁺ Red
Sodium, Na⁺ Yellow
Potassium, K⁺ Lilac
Calcium, Ca²⁺ Orange-red
Barium, Ba²⁺ Light green
Copper(II), Cu²⁺ Blue-green
Flame Test Procedure
(1) Clean wire in conc. HCl + hot flame. (2) Dip in acid, then into the solid. (3) Hold in the clear blue part of the Bunsen flame. (4) Observe the colour.

Cation Tests — NaOH and Ammonia Precipitates

Cation Precipitate Colour Excess NaOH? Excess Ammonia?
Fe²⁺ (iron II) Pale green No change No change
Fe³⁺ (iron III) Red-brown No change No change
Cu²⁺ (copper II) Pale blue No change Dissolves → deep blue solution
Zn²⁺ (zinc) White Dissolves Dissolves
Al³⁺ (aluminium) White Dissolves Does NOT dissolve
Ca²⁺ (calcium) White No change Very slight / none
Cr³⁺ (chromium III) Green Dissolves No change
Exam Trap — Al³⁺ vs Zn²⁺
Both give a white precipitate that dissolves in excess NaOH. To tell them apart, use aqueous ammonia: Zn²⁺ dissolves in excess ammonia, Al³⁺ does NOT. This is the most common "distinguish" question.
Don't Forget — Ammonium Ion Test
To test for NH₄⁺: add NaOH, heat gently. Ammonia gas is released. Test with damp red litmus paper — it turns blue.

Anion Tests — The Complete Table

Anion Test Procedure Positive Result
Chloride, Cl⁻ Add dilute HNO₃, then AgNO₃ White precipitate
Bromide, Br⁻ Add dilute HNO₃, then AgNO₃ Cream precipitate
Iodide, I⁻ Add dilute HNO₃, then AgNO₃ Yellow precipitate
Sulfate, SO₄²⁻ Add dilute HNO₃, then Ba(NO₃)₂ White precipitate
Sulfite, SO₃²⁻ Add acidified KMnO₄ Purple turns colourless
Carbonate, CO₃²⁻ Add dilute HCl Fizzes; gas turns limewater milky
Nitrate, NO₃⁻ Add NaOH + Al foil, heat Ammonia gas (damp red litmus turns blue)
Exam Trap — Halide Colours Get Darker
Going down Group VII: Cl⁻ = white, Br⁻ = cream, I⁻ = yellow. They get darker. You MUST add nitric acid first to remove carbonates/sulfates that would also precipitate with silver and give a false result.

Say It in One Sentence

Independent var. The one thing you change on purpose — everything else stays the same.
Hydrogen test Lighted splint → squeaky pop (H₂ is flammable).
Oxygen test Glowing splint → relights (O₂ supports combustion).
CO₂ test Bubble through limewater → goes cloudy (calcium carbonate precipitate forms).
Flame tests Li = red, Na = yellow, K = lilac, Ca = orange-red, Ba = light green, Cu = blue-green.
Fe²⁺ vs Fe³⁺ Iron(II) = pale green precipitate. Iron(III) = red-brown precipitate. Neither dissolves in excess.
Halide test Add nitric acid + silver nitrate. White = Cl⁻, cream = Br⁻, yellow = I⁻.
Carbonate test Add acid → fizzes → test gas with limewater → cloudy = CO₃²⁻ confirmed.

Memory Tricks

Gas Tests — First Letter Clues
Hydrogen = Hear it (squeaky pop). Oxygen = Oh! It relights! CO₂ = Cloudy limewater. Ammonia = turns litmus Alkaline (blue). Cl₂ = bleaches white. SO₂ = decolourises (goes See-through).
Cation Precipitate Colours
Iron(II) = "Fe-Two = green" (both have double-e). Iron(III) = "rusty iron = brown" (think rust). Copper(II) = "Cu = sky-blue." Al/Zn/Ca = all white (use excess NaOH/ammonia to tell apart).
CAP and AN
Cations Are Positive. Anions are Negative. Cations go to the Cathode. Anions go to the Anode.
Halide Colours — Traffic Light Backwards
Going down Group VII: Cl⁻ = white, Br⁻ = cream (getting warmer), I⁻ = yellow. Getting progressively darker. Think of the colours warming up as you go down the group.
Three Groups of Anion Tests
Group 1: Silver nitrate tests for halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻). Group 2: Barium nitrate tests for sulfate. Group 3: Acid tests for carbonate (fizz + limewater). Then two specials: sulfite (KMnO₄) and nitrate (NaOH + Al).
Variables — "I Change, Depends Measures, Controls Stay"
Independent = what I change. Dependent = what Depends on the change (what I measure). Control = what stays Constant.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How would you confirm the gas is hydrogen?" Hold a lighted splint — it burns with a squeaky pop
"A gas turns limewater milky. Name the gas." Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
"A white precipitate forms with NaOH and dissolves in excess. What could the cation be?" Zinc (Zn²⁺) or aluminium (Al³⁺). Use ammonia to distinguish: Zn dissolves, Al does not
"A pale green precipitate forms with NaOH. Name the cation." Iron(II), Fe²⁺
"A cream precipitate forms with silver nitrate. Name the anion." Bromide, Br⁻
"A solid fizzes with acid and the gas turns limewater milky. Name the anion." Carbonate, CO₃²⁻
"How do you test for ammonium ions?" Add NaOH, heat gently. Ammonia gas given off — turns damp red litmus blue
"How do you test for sulfate ions?" Add dilute nitric acid, then barium nitrate — white precipitate (BaSO₄)
"Name the flame colour for potassium" Lilac. (K cannot be identified by NaOH precipitate — must use flame test)
"What is the independent variable in this experiment?" The thing the experimenter deliberately changes. Look for what varies between trials
