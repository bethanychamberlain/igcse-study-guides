Extracting and Using Metals

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Ore A rock from which a metal can be extracted economically (profitably). Mineral (a mineral is any naturally occurring compound in rock — not all minerals are ores)
Native metal An unreactive metal found as the pure element in rock (e.g. gold, silver, platinum). Ore (native metals don't need extraction — they're already uncombined)
Reduction Loss of oxygen (or gain of electrons). The metal ore is reduced during extraction. Oxidation (gain of oxygen / loss of electrons — the opposite)
Electrolysis Using electricity to break down a compound. Used for very reactive metals (above carbon). Heating with carbon (that only works for metals below carbon in the reactivity series)
Alloy A mixture of a metal with at least one other element, made by melting and mixing. Compound (an alloy is a mixture, not a chemical compound)
Slag Calcium silicate waste from the blast furnace. Forms when limestone removes impurities. Coke (coke is the carbon fuel; slag is the waste product)
Cryolite Sodium aluminium fluoride. Aluminium oxide is dissolved in molten cryolite to lower the operating temperature. Bauxite (bauxite is the ore; cryolite is the solvent)
Galvanising Coating iron or steel with a layer of zinc to prevent rusting (sacrificial protection). Electroplating (coating with any metal using electrolysis — galvanising is specifically zinc)

Iron Extraction vs Aluminium Extraction

Iron (Blast Furnace) Aluminium (Electrolysis)
Ore Hematite (Fe₂O₃) Bauxite (impure Al₂O₃)
Method Heat with carbon/CO Electrolysis in molten cryolite
Temperature ~1400 °C ~950 °C
Reducing agent Carbon monoxide Electricity (electrons)
Relative cost Cheaper 3× more expensive
Key waste Slag (calcium silicate) Carbon anodes burn away

Key Alloys — What's In Them and Why

Alloy Made From Key Property
Mild steel Iron + carbon (<0.5%) Hard and strong
Stainless steel Iron + chromium + nickel Rustproof
Brass Copper + zinc Hard, attractive
Bronze Copper + tin Hard, sonorous
Why Alloys Are Harder
Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular lattice, preventing layers from sliding over each other. Pure metal = neat rows that slide. Alloy = mixed sizes that jam.

Say It in One Sentence

Ore A rock containing enough metal compound to make extraction worthwhile.
Reactivity rule Above carbon = electrolysis, below carbon = heat with carbon, unreactive = found as element.
Blast furnace Iron ore + coke + limestone go in the top; carbon monoxide reduces the ore to molten iron at the bottom.
Why cryolite? It lowers the melting point of aluminium oxide from 2072 °C to ~950 °C, saving huge energy costs.
Anodes burn away Oxygen produced at the anode reacts with the hot carbon, forming CO₂ — so anodes must be regularly replaced.
Alloy A metal mixed with another element; different-sized atoms block layers from sliding, making it harder and stronger.
Aluminium resists corrosion A thin layer of aluminium oxide forms on the surface and protects the metal underneath from further attack.

Memory Tricks

Reactivity Series Mnemonic
Please Stop Calling Me A Cute Zebra, I'm Completely Silly Going Pink = Potassium, Sodium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, Carbon, Zinc, Iron, Copper, Silver, Gold, Platinum. Draw a line after Carbon — everything above needs electrolysis!
Blast Furnace — 3 In, 3 Out
IN: Iron ore + Coke + Limestone. OUT: Molten iron + Slag + Waste gases. Three things enter the top, three things come out.
OILRIG for Electrolysis
Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). At the cathode: Al³⁺ gains electrons = reduction. At the anode: O²⁻ loses electrons = oxidation.
Brass vs Bronze
Brass = copper + zinc (A to Z). Bronze = copper + tin (think "bronze age" — ancient, made with tin).

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How would you extract aluminium?" Electrolysis — it is above carbon in the reactivity series, so carbon cannot reduce it
"Why is cryolite used?" It lowers the melting point of aluminium oxide, reducing energy costs, and it is a better conductor
"Name the three raw materials in the blast furnace" Iron ore (hematite), coke (carbon), limestone (calcium carbonate)
"What is the reducing agent in the blast furnace?" Carbon monoxide — it removes oxygen from the iron ore
"Why do the carbon anodes need replacing?" Oxygen produced at the anode reacts with the hot carbon: C + O₂ → CO₂, so the anodes burn away
"Explain why alloys are harder than pure metals" Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular arrangement, preventing layers from sliding
"Why is aluminium used for aircraft?" Low density (light) + resists corrosion (protective oxide layer) + can be made into strong alloys
"Why is stainless steel used for cutlery?" It is hard, strong, and does not rust (contains chromium and nickel)
