Section 15.1 Our Environment and Us
We depend on air, water, and soil every second of our lives. But human activity pollutes all three. This chapter is about the chemistry of that pollution and what we can do about it.
W
Write This Down — Key Environmental Problems

Human activities harm the environment in many ways:
• Harmful gases from power stations, factories, and engines (CO₂, CO, SO₂, nitrogen oxides) → global warming, acid rain
• Methane from farm animals → global warming
• Particulates (tiny solid particles) from factories and diesel engines → respiratory problems, cancer
• Untreated sewage in water → harmful microbes (cholera, typhoid)
• Fertilisers draining into rivers → eutrophication (algae growth, fish die)
• Toxic metals (lead, mercury, arsenic) from mines/factories → contaminate water

?
Why This Matters

This section is an overview — the rest of the chapter fills in the details. The exam often asks "Describe two ways human activity harms the environment." Being able to name specific pollutants and their effects (not vague statements like "pollution") is what gets marks.

  • I can name at least four types of pollution caused by human activity
  • I can link each type to a specific harmful effect
Section 15.2 What Is in River Water?
River water is never pure. It contains helpful substances (dissolved oxygen, minerals) and harmful ones (toxic metals, sewage, fertilisers, plastic waste).
W
Write This Down — Helpful Substances

• Dissolved oxygen — essential for fish and aquatic life (they breathe it through their gills)
• Essential minerals — calcium (bones, teeth), magnesium (heart), sodium, potassium, iron, copper, zinc, fluorine — dissolved from rocks and soil

W
Write This Down — Harmful Substances

• Toxic metals — lead (brain damage), mercury (damages nervous system), arsenic (cancer risk). From mines, factories, and natural rock.
• Nitrates and phosphates — from fertilisers. They promote algae growth, leading to eutrophication.
• Microbes from sewage — bacteria causing cholera and typhoid; viruses causing hepatitis.
• Plastic waste — animals mistake it for food; it clogs their guts and they starve.

T
Study Tip

For the exam, always give specific examples: don't just say "toxic metals" — say "lead, mercury, and arsenic." Don't just say "diseases" — say "cholera and typhoid." Specific names = more marks.

  • I can name two helpful and four harmful types of substance in river water
  • I can explain why untreated sewage is dangerous
Section 15.3 Our Water Supply
River water must be treated before we can drink it safely. There are five key steps to providing a clean water supply.
W
Write This Down — The Five Steps of Water Treatment

1. Choose a clean source — a clean stretch of river, or an aquifer (underground water in rock).
2. Remove solid particles — three methods:
  • Filtration: pass water through gravel or sand
  • Sedimentation: let particles settle to the bottom of a tank
  • Flocculation: add a coagulant (e.g. iron(III) sulfate) to clump fine particles together; skim them off
3. Remove dissolved compounds — filter through carbon (activated charcoal). This removes bad tastes, smells, and some toxic metals.
4. Kill microbes — add chlorine.
5. Store safely — in a clean covered reservoir, ready to pump to homes.

W
Write This Down — Testing for Water

Test that a liquid contains water:
• Add to white anhydrous copper(II) sulfate → turns blue
• Add to blue cobalt(II) chloride paper → turns pink

Test that water is pure:
• It will boil at exactly 100°C and freeze at exactly 0°C.

!
Don't Confuse — "Contains Water" vs "Is Pure Water"

Turning copper(II) sulfate blue only proves the liquid contains water — it could be a mixture. To prove it is pure water, you must check the boiling point (100°C) and freezing point (0°C). Treated tap water is NOT pure — it still contains dissolved salts and chlorine. Distilled water is used in labs when pure water is needed.

?
Why This Matters

The water treatment steps come up frequently on the exam. A common question is "Describe the steps in treating water for drinking" (4-5 marks). Know the order and the purpose of each step. Also, the tests for water (copper sulfate + cobalt chloride) are classic practical questions.

  • I can list the five steps of water treatment in order
  • I know the difference between filtration, sedimentation, and flocculation
  • I can describe both tests for water and the test for pure water
Section 15.4 Fertilisers
Plants need nutrients from the soil. When crops use them up, farmers add fertilisers. But overuse of fertilisers causes serious problems in rivers and lakes.
W
Write This Down — What Plants Need

Plants need three main elements (besides carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen from CO₂ and water):
• Nitrogen (N) — for making chlorophyll and proteins
• Potassium (K) — for making proteins and resisting disease
• Phosphorus (P) — for root growth and helping crops ripen

Plants absorb nitrogen as nitrate ions and ammonium ions through their roots.

W
Write This Down — Fertilisers

• A fertiliser is any substance added to soil to make it more fertile.
• Natural fertilisers: animal manure.
• Synthetic fertilisers: salts made in factories, e.g. ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃), ammonium phosphate ((NH₄)₃PO₄).
• An NPK fertiliser is a mixture of salts that provides all three elements: nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.

W
Write This Down — Eutrophication (The Big Problem)

When fertilisers wash into rivers (especially in wet weather), they cause eutrophication:

1. Fertilisers (nitrates/phosphates) enter the river [1]
2. They promote rapid growth of algae on the water surface [1]
3. The algae block light to plants below, which die [1]
4. Bacteria feed on the dead plants and algae, using up dissolved oxygen [1]
5. Fish and other aquatic life suffocate (die from lack of oxygen) [1]

?
Why This Matters

Eutrophication is a classic 4-5 mark exam question. Learn the chain of events in order: fertilisers → algae bloom → plants die → bacteria decompose → oxygen used up → fish die. Each step in the chain is a mark. This question appears almost every year in some form.

T
Study Tip

Think of eutrophication as a domino chain: each event causes the next. Write the chain on a flashcard: Fertiliser → Algae → Block light → Plants die → Bacteria decompose → O₂ used up → Fish die. If you can recite this chain from memory, you can answer any eutrophication question.

  • I can name the three elements plants need and why
  • I can define fertiliser and explain what NPK means
  • I can describe the full chain of eutrophication (5 steps)
Section 15.5 Air — The Gas Mixture We Live In
We live in the troposphere, the lowest layer of the atmosphere. Clean dry air has a fixed composition that you need to learn.
W
Write This Down — Composition of Clean Dry Air

• Nitrogen: 78%
• Oxygen: 21%
• Argon: ~1% (the remaining 1% is mainly argon, plus small amounts of carbon dioxide, and traces of other noble gases like helium, neon, krypton, xenon)

Air also contains variable amounts of water vapour and may contain pollutants from human activities.

W
Write This Down — Measuring Oxygen in Air

Experiment: Pass 100 cm³ of air back and forth over heated copper wire in a tube between two gas syringes.
• The oxygen reacts with the hot copper: copper + oxygen → copper oxide (the copper turns black).
• Repeat until the volume stops decreasing.
• Final volume: ~79 cm³. Volume of oxygen used = 100 − 79 = 21 cm³.
• Percentage of oxygen = 21/100 × 100 = 21%.

!
Don't Confuse

The exam might ask "What happens to the copper?" — it turns black (copper oxide forms). Don't say it "dissolves" or "melts." Also, remember: the air must be pushed back and forth several times to make sure all the oxygen has reacted. Stopping too early gives a wrong result.

  • I can state the composition of clean dry air (78% N₂, 21% O₂, ~1% Ar)
  • I can describe the experiment to find the percentage of oxygen in air
Section 15.6 Air Pollution from Fossil Fuels
Burning fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas) releases pollutant gases into the atmosphere. Each pollutant has different sources and causes different harm.
W
Write This Down — The Five Main Pollutants
Pollutant How It Forms Harm It Does
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Complete combustion of carbon-containing fuels Greenhouse gas → global warming → climate change
Carbon monoxide (CO) Incomplete combustion (limited air supply, e.g. car engines) Poisonous: binds to haemoglobin in blood, prevents oxygen transport → death
Particulates (soot) Incomplete combustion (especially diesel engines) Respiratory problems, cancer
Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) Burning fuels that contain sulfur impurities (coal, some petroleum) Dissolves in rain → acid rain. Damages limestone/marble buildings, kills trees, acidifies lakes
Nitrogen oxides (NO, NO₂) Nitrogen and oxygen in air react inside hot engines/furnaces Acid rain, respiratory problems, form photochemical smog in sunlight
!
Don't Confuse — Complete vs Incomplete Combustion

Complete combustion (plenty of air): fuel → CO₂ + H₂O. Produces carbon dioxide.
Incomplete combustion (limited air): fuel → CO + C (soot) + H₂O. Produces carbon monoxide and particulates.
Carbon monoxide is colourless and odourless — you can't detect it without a detector. That's what makes it so dangerous.

W
Write This Down — Tackling Acid Rain

Three ways to reduce acid rain:
• Use low-sulfur fuels — remove sulfur from fuels before burning them
• Flue gas desulfurisation — treat waste gases from power stations with calcium oxide (lime): SO₂ + CaO → CaSO₃ (calcium sulfite)
• Catalytic converters in cars — convert nitrogen oxides to harmless nitrogen, and carbon monoxide to carbon dioxide

?
Why This Matters

The pollutant table is one of the most-tested topics. The exam will ask "Name a pollutant that causes acid rain" or "Explain how carbon monoxide is harmful." Learn the table: for each pollutant, know the source, the type of combustion, and the harm it does.

T
Study Tip

Make flashcards with each pollutant on the front and its source + harm on the back. Pay special attention to the distinction between CO₂ (global warming) and CO (poisonous) — the exam likes to test whether you know the difference. Also remember: nitrogen oxides come from the air itself reacting in a hot engine, not from the fuel.

  • I can name the five main pollutants from fossil fuels
  • I can state the source and harm of each pollutant
  • I can explain the difference between complete and incomplete combustion
  • I can describe three ways to reduce acid rain
Section 15.7 Two Greenhouse Gases
Carbon dioxide and methane are greenhouse gases. They trap heat in the atmosphere, keeping Earth warm. But we are adding too much, causing global warming and climate change.
W
Write This Down — The Greenhouse Effect

How it works:
1. The Sun sends energy to Earth as light and UV rays [1]
2. Earth's surface absorbs this energy and re-emits it as heat (thermal/infrared energy) [1]
3. Some heat escapes to space, but some is absorbed by greenhouse gases (CO₂ and CH₄) in the atmosphere [1]
4. This trapped heat warms the atmosphere and Earth's surface [1]

We need the greenhouse effect — without it, Earth would freeze at night. The problem is that we are adding too much CO₂ and CH₄, so more heat is being trapped than before.

W
Write This Down — Sources of These Gases

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) sources:
• Burning fossil fuels (coal, gas, petrol, diesel, kerosene) for electricity and transport
• Burning/clearing forests (deforestation)

Methane (CH₄) sources:
• Grazing livestock (cattle, sheep, goats) — microbes in their stomachs produce methane
• Rice paddy fields and boglands (microbes break down organic matter without oxygen)
• Leaks from natural gas wells

There is more CO₂ in the air than methane, but methane is about 28 times more powerful at trapping heat per molecule.

W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Global warming: the increase in Earth's average temperature due to rising levels of greenhouse gases.
• Climate change: the changes in weather patterns (rainfall, storms, temperatures) caused by global warming.

W
Write This Down — Predicted Impacts of Climate Change

• More frequent droughts in some areas; more flooding in others
• Melting polar ice and glaciers → sea level rise → flooding of low-lying countries
• More severe storms, heatwaves, and wildfires
• Farmers unable to grow their usual crops
• Species extinction (animals that cannot adapt)
• Spread of tropical diseases to new areas
• Climate refugees — people forced to move

!
Don't Confuse — Greenhouse Effect vs Global Warming

The greenhouse effect is natural and necessary — it keeps Earth warm enough for life. Global warming is the enhanced greenhouse effect caused by humans adding extra CO₂ and CH₄. The exam penalises students who say "the greenhouse effect is bad" — it's not. The increase in greenhouse gases is the problem.

  • I can explain how the greenhouse effect works (4 steps)
  • I can name the two main greenhouse gases and their sources
  • I can define global warming and climate change
  • I can list at least four predicted impacts of climate change
Section 15.8 Tackling Climate Change
The two main greenhouse gases are CO₂ and methane. Tackling climate change means reducing emissions of both.
W
Write This Down — Reducing Carbon Dioxide

1. Use non-carbon fuels for electricity:
• Wind, waves, tides, fast-flowing rivers (hydroelectricity) — spin turbines
• Nuclear power — nuclear fission heats water to steam to spin turbines
• Solar power — photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight to electricity directly
These are all forms of renewable energy (they do not run out).

2. Use non-carbon power for transport:
• Electric vehicles (batteries charged using non-carbon electricity)
• Hydrogen-oxygen fuel cells (produce only water as a by-product)

3. Plant more trees:
Trees absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis:
carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen
Deforestation must be stopped — dead/burned trees release their stored carbon as CO₂.

4. Carbon capture: trapping CO₂ from power stations and storing it underground (in old oil/gas wells).

W
Write This Down — Reducing Methane

The main source of methane is livestock (cattle, sheep, goats). To reduce methane emissions:
• Rear fewer grazing animals
• Eat less meat and dairy — eat more fish, chicken, or plant-based foods instead

?
Why This Matters

The exam often asks: "Suggest two ways to reduce carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere." The best answers are: (1) use renewable energy instead of fossil fuels, and (2) plant more trees (they absorb CO₂ by photosynthesis). If you can also write the word equation for photosynthesis, that's a bonus mark.

T
Study Tip

Group the solutions into categories: electricity (wind, solar, nuclear, hydro), transport (electric cars, hydrogen fuel cells), biology (plant trees, photosynthesis), diet (eat less meat). Having solutions in categories makes them easier to recall under exam pressure.

  • I can name four non-carbon ways to generate electricity
  • I can explain how planting trees helps reduce CO₂ (with the word equation)
  • I can suggest how methane emissions can be reduced
  • I can define renewable energy and give examples
