Section 4.1 The Names and Formulae of Compounds
Before you can do any chemistry calculations, you need to be able to name compounds and write their formulae. There are clear rules for this — once you know them, you can name almost any compound on sight.
W
Write This Down — Naming Rules

Rule 1: Metal + non-metal (ionic compound):
• Name the metal first, then the non-metal with its ending changed to -ide.
• Examples: sodium chloride, magnesium oxide, iron sulfide.

Rule 2: Two non-metals:
• If one is hydrogen, name hydrogen first.
• Otherwise, name the element with the lower group number first.
• The second element ends in -ide.
• Examples: hydrogen chloride, carbon dioxide (carbon is Group IV, oxygen is Group VI).

W
Write This Down — Three Ways to Find a Formula

There are three ways to work out a formula: from the structure of the compound, using valency, and by balancing charges.

1. From the structure:
• For a molecular compound, the formula tells you the type and number of each atom in one molecule. E.g. a molecule of water has 1 oxygen and 2 hydrogen atoms, so H₂O.
• For an ionic compound, the formula tells you the type and ratio of the ions in the compound. E.g. potassium iodide has one K⁺ ion for every I⁻ ion, so KI.

W
Write This Down — 2. Finding a Formula Using Valency

The valency of an element is the number of electrons its atoms lose, gain or share, in forming compounds. It is linked to the group number:

Elements In forming a compound, the atoms … Valency Example compounds
Group I lose 1 electron 1 sodium chloride, NaCl
Group II lose 2 electrons 2 magnesium chloride, MgCl₂
Group III lose 3 electrons 3 aluminium chloride, AlCl₃
Group IV share 4 electrons 4 methane, CH₄
Group V gain or share 3 electrons 3 ammonia, NH₃
Group VI gain or share 2 electrons 2 magnesium oxide, MgO; water, H₂O
Group VII gain or share 1 electron 1 sodium chloride, NaCl; hydrogen chloride, HCl
Group VIII (do not form compounds) – none
Hydrogen lose or share 1 electron 1 hydrogen bromide, HBr
W
Write This Down — How to Write a Formula Using Valency

Step 1: Find the valency of each element in the compound, from the table.
Step 2: Write their symbols in the same order as the elements in the name.
Step 3: If the elements have different valencies, write numbers after the symbols to balance the valencies.

Example 1: What is the formula for hydrogen sulfide?
1. Valencies: hydrogen, 1; sulfur 2 (Group VI); they are different.
2. HS
3. The formula is H₂S (the valencies are balanced: 2 × 1 and 2).

Example 2: What is the formula for aluminium oxide?
1. Valencies: aluminium 3 (Group III); oxygen 2 (Group VI); different.
2. AlO
3. The formula is Al₂O₃ (the valencies are balanced: 2 × 3 and 3 × 2).

W
Write This Down — 3. Finding a Formula by Balancing Charges

In an ionic compound, the total charge is zero. So you can also work out the formula by balancing the charges on its ions.

For example, the magnesium ion is Mg²⁺ and the chloride ion is Cl⁻. A magnesium ion needs two chloride ions to balance its charge. So the formula for magnesium chloride is MgCl₂. The charges add up to zero.

!
Don't Confuse — Common Names vs Chemical Names

Some compounds have everyday names that tell you nothing about their elements: water (H₂O), ammonia (NH₃), methane (CH₄). You just have to memorise these formulae — you can’t work them out from the name alone. The exam expects you to know them.

?
Why This Matters

Every single calculation question in chemistry starts with a formula. If you write the wrong formula, the entire calculation is wrong from line one — even if your maths is perfect. Getting formulae right is the foundation for everything that follows in this chapter.

T
Study Tip — The Criss-Cross Method

To remember how valencies become subscripts, think of it as a criss-cross: the valency of element A becomes the subscript of element B, and vice versa. Practice with 5 compounds (MgO, Al₂O₃, CaCl₂, Na₂O, FeCl₃) until you can write the formula in under 10 seconds each.

  • I can name a compound from its elements (metal + non-metal, or two non-metals)
  • I know the difference between a molecular formula (type and number of atoms) and an ionic formula (type and ratio of ions)
  • I know the valency for each group (I through VIII and hydrogen)
  • I can write a formula by balancing valencies
  • I can write a formula by balancing ionic charges (e.g. Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ gives MgCl₂)
  • I know the formulae for water, ammonia, and methane from memory
Section 4.2 Equations for Chemical Reactions
Chemical equations are the language of chemistry. They show what reacts, what forms, and in what proportions. You need to be able to write them, balance them, and add state symbols — this comes up on every single exam paper.
W
Write This Down — Word Equations vs Symbol Equations

A word equation uses names:
carbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide

A symbol equation uses formulae:
C + O₂ → CO₂

The things on the left of the arrow are reactants. The things on the right are products. The “+” means “reacts with” and the arrow means “produces” or “gives.”

W
Write This Down — The 4-Step Method for Writing Equations

Step 1: Write the word equation.
Step 2: Replace names with correct formulae.
Step 3: Balance it — make sure you have the same number of each type of atom on both sides. Never change a formula to balance — only add numbers in front.
Step 4: Add state symbols.

Example 1: Calcium burns in chlorine gas to form calcium chloride.
1. calcium + chlorine → calcium chloride
2. Ca + Cl₂ → CaCl₂
3. Ca: 1 atom on the left and 1 on the right. Cl: 2 atoms on the left and 2 on the right. Balanced.
4. Ca (s) + Cl₂ (g) → CaCl₂ (s)

Example 2: Hydrogen chloride is formed by burning hydrogen in chlorine.
1. hydrogen + chlorine → hydrogen chloride
2. H₂ + Cl₂ → HCl
3. H: 2 on the left, 1 on the right (not balanced). Cl: 2 on the left, 1 on the right (not balanced). Put a 2 in front of HCl: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl. Now balanced.
4. H₂ (g) + Cl₂ (g) → 2HCl (g)

Example 3: Magnesium burns in oxygen to form magnesium oxide.
1. magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide
2. Mg + O₂ → MgO
3. Mg: 1 on each side (good). O: 2 on the left, 1 on the right (not balanced). Put a 2 in front of MgO: Mg + O₂ → 2MgO. Now O is balanced, but Mg is 1 vs 2. Put a 2 in front of Mg: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO. Balanced!
4. 2Mg (s) + O₂ (g) → 2MgO (s)

W
Write This Down — State Symbols

Add these after each formula to show what state each substance is in:

Symbol Meaning Example
(s) Solid Mg (s), CaCO₃ (s)
(l) Liquid H₂O (l)
(g) Gas O₂ (g), CO₂ (g)
(aq) Aqueous solution (dissolved in water) NaCl (aq), HCl (aq)
!
Don't Confuse — Balancing vs Changing Formulae

When balancing, you can only put big numbers in front of a formula (like 2MgO). You must never change the small subscript numbers inside a formula. Changing MgO to MgO₂ to “balance” the oxygen would create a completely different (and wrong) substance. The formula is set by the compound’s structure — you don’t get to change it.

?
Why This Matters

Atoms don’t appear or disappear during a reaction — they just rearrange. That’s why equations must balance. This is also the law of conservation of mass: total mass of reactants = total mass of products. Every calculation you do later in this chapter depends on balanced equations being correct.

T
Study Tip — Balance in This Order

When balancing, work through one element at a time. A useful order: metals first, then non-metals, and leave oxygen and hydrogen for last (they’re often in multiple compounds so they’re trickier). Check every element at the end. Practice by balancing at least 5 equations every study session until it feels automatic.

  • I can write a word equation for a reaction
  • I can convert a word equation into a symbol equation with correct formulae
  • I can balance a symbol equation without changing any formulae
  • I know all four state symbols and when to use each one
Section 4.3 The Masses of Atoms, Molecules, and Ions
Atoms are too small to weigh on a scale, so chemists use a relative scale. Everything is compared to a carbon-12 atom. Once you understand this, you can calculate the mass of any molecule or ionic compound.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

¹²C — the standard atom: The carbon-12 atom (¹²C) has 12 nucleons (6 protons + 6 neutrons). It is taken as the standard, and assigned an atomic mass of 12 units. The mass of a nucleon is taken as 1 — that is, 1/12 of the mass of the atom. All other atoms are compared to this standard, so their masses are relative masses.

Isotopes are atoms of the same element, with the same number of protons, but different numbers of neutrons. So each isotope has a different mass.

The relative atomic mass of an element, Aᵣ, is the average mass of its isotopes, compared to 1/12 the mass of the ¹²C atom. Aᵣ values are usually rounded to the nearest whole number — except for chlorine, which has a value halfway between two whole numbers.

Key Aᵣ values to know: H = 1, C = 12, N = 14, O = 16, Na = 23, Mg = 24, S = 32, Cl = 35.5, Zn = 65, I = 127

W
Write This Down — Relative Molecular Mass (Mᵣ) and Relative Formula Mass

For a molecule: add up the Aᵣ values of the atoms in the molecule. This gives the relative molecular mass, or Mᵣ for short.

For an ionic compound: an ion has the same mass as its atom, so add up the Aᵣ values of the atoms in its formula. This gives the relative formula mass, which is also shortened to Mᵣ.

Worked Examples:

Substance Formula Atoms in formula Aᵣ of atoms Mᵣ
Hydrogen H₂ 2H H = 1 2 × 1 = 2
Water H₂O 2H, 1O H = 1, O = 16 (2 × 1) + 16 = 18
Oxygen O₂ 2O O = 16 2 × 16 = 32
Ammonia NH₃ 1N, 3H N = 14, H = 1 14 + (3 × 1) = 17
Sodium chloride NaCl 1Na, 1Cl Na = 23, Cl = 35.5 23 + 35.5 = 58.5
Magnesium nitrate (ionic) Mg(NO₃)₂ 1Mg, 2N, 6O Mg = 24, N = 14, O = 16 24 + (2 × 14) + (6 × 16) = 148
!
Don't Confuse — Brackets in Formulae

In Mg(NO₃)₂, the subscript 2 outside the bracket means everything inside the bracket is multiplied by 2. So that’s 2 nitrogen atoms and 6 oxygen atoms (not 2 and 3). This is the number one mistake in Mᵣ calculations. If there are brackets, multiply every element inside by the subscript outside.

!
Don't Confuse — Ions Have the Same Mass as Their Atoms

When an atom becomes an ion, it gains or loses electrons. But electrons have virtually no mass. So a Na atom (Aᵣ = 23) and a Na⁺ ion both have a mass of 23. Don’t try to subtract or add anything for the charge.

?
Why This Matters

Mᵣ is the starting point for every mass calculation in chemistry. Percentage composition, reacting masses, moles (which you’ll meet later) — they all begin with Mᵣ. If you can calculate Mᵣ quickly and accurately, the harder questions become much easier. Get this skill locked in.

T
Study Tip — Write Out Every Step

When calculating Mᵣ, always write: (1) the formula, (2) how many of each atom, (3) the Aᵣ of each, (4) the multiplication, (5) the total. Even if you can do it in your head, writing the steps gets you marks for “showing working” and catches bracket errors. Practice with: CaCO₃, H₂SO₄, Ca(OH)₂, (NH₄)₂SO₄.

  • I can define relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) and explain why it’s relative to carbon-12
  • I can define isotopes and explain why Aᵣ is an average
  • I can calculate Mᵣ for a molecular compound (e.g. H₂O, CO₂, NH₃)
  • I can calculate Mᵣ for an ionic compound with brackets (e.g. Mg(NO₃)₂)
  • I know that an ion has the same mass as its atom
Section 4.4 Calculations About Mass and Percentage
This is where the maths kicks in. You’ll use two laws of chemistry plus the Mᵣ skills you just learned to calculate reacting masses, percentage composition, and percentage purity. Every step is straightforward — just follow the method.
W
Write This Down — The Two Laws You Need

Law 1: Elements always react in the same ratio, to form a given compound.
For example, when carbon burns in oxygen to form carbon dioxide: 6 g of carbon combines with 16 g of oxygen, so 12 g of carbon will combine with 32 g of oxygen, and so on. The ratio stays the same.

Law 2: The total mass does not change, during a chemical reaction.
So total mass of reactants = total mass of products.
So 6 g of carbon and 16 g of oxygen give 22 g of carbon dioxide. 12 g of carbon and 32 g of oxygen give 44 g of carbon dioxide.

W
Write This Down — Calculating Reacting Masses (Ratio Method)

64 g of copper reacts with 16 g of oxygen to give the black compound copper(II) oxide.

a. What mass of copper will react with 32 g of oxygen?
64 g of copper reacts with 16 g of oxygen, so 2 × 64 g or 128 g of copper will react with 32 g of oxygen.

b. What mass of oxygen will react with 32 g of copper?
16 g of oxygen reacts with 64 g of copper, so 16÷2 or 8 g of oxygen will react with 32 g of copper.

c. What mass of copper(II) oxide will be formed, in b?
40 g of copper(II) oxide will be formed. (32 + 8 = 40)

d. How much copper and oxygen will give 8 g of copper(II) oxide?
64 g of copper and 16 g of oxygen give 80 g of copper(II) oxide, so 64÷10 and 16÷10 g will give 8 g of copper(II) oxide, so 6.4 g of copper and 1.6 g of oxygen are needed.

W
Write This Down — Percentage Composition

The percentage composition of a compound tells you how much of each element it contains, as a percentage of the total mass. This is how to work it out:

1 Write down the formula of the compound.
2 Using Aᵣ values, work out its molecular or formula mass (Mᵣ).
3 Write the mass of the element as a fraction of the Mᵣ.
4 Multiply the fraction by 100, to give a percentage.

Example: Calculate the percentage of oxygen in sulfur dioxide.
1. The formula of sulfur dioxide is SO₂.
2. Aᵣ values: S = 32, O = 16. So its Mᵣ is 64. (32 + 2 × 16)
3. Mass of oxygen as a fraction of the total = 32 ÷ 64
4. Mass of oxygen as a percentage of the total = (32 ÷ 64) × 100 = 50% oxygen.
This means it is also 50% sulfur (100% − 50% = 50%).

W
Write This Down — Percentage Purity

A pure substance has nothing else mixed with it. But substances often contain unwanted substances, or impurities. Purity is usually given as a percentage:

% purity of a substance = (mass of pure substance in it ÷ total mass) × 100

Example: Impure copper is refined (purified), to obtain pure copper for use in computers. 20 tonnes of copper gave 18 tonnes of pure copper, on refining.

a. What was the % purity of the copper before refining?
% purity = (18 tonnes ÷ 20 tonnes) × 100 = 90%. So the copper was 90% pure.

b. How much pure copper will 50 tonnes of the impure copper give?
The impure copper is 90% pure. 90% is 90÷100.
So 50 tonnes of it will give (90 ÷ 100) × 50 tonnes or 45 tonnes of pure copper.

!
Don't Confuse — Common Calculation Errors

Error 1: Forgetting to multiply by subscripts. In CaCO₃, the oxygen mass is 3 × 16 = 48, not just 16. Always count all the atoms.
Error 2: Dividing the wrong way round. For % composition, it’s element mass ÷ total Mᵣ, not the other way round. The bigger number should be on the bottom.
Error 3: Forgetting to × 100. A fraction of 0.5 is not 0.5%. It’s 50%. Always multiply by 100 at the end.

?
Why This Matters

These calculations are worth 3-6 marks on the exam and they’re free marks if you follow the steps. The chemistry is straightforward — it’s really just multiplication and division. The examiner gives you Aᵣ values, so you don’t even need to memorise them. Just follow the method carefully and show your working.

T
Study Tip — The Sanity Check

After every calculation, ask yourself: “Does this make sense?” If you get a percentage over 100%, something went wrong. If you get a reacting mass that’s negative, something went wrong. If the mass of product is bigger than the mass of all reactants combined, something went wrong. Catching your own errors is a skill that wins marks.

  • I can state both laws (constant ratio, conservation of mass)
  • I can calculate reacting masses using the ratio method
  • I can calculate percentage composition of an element in a compound
  • I can calculate percentage purity and use it in reverse
