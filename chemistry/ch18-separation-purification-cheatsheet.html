Separation and Purification

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Solvent The substance that dissolves a solute (e.g. water). Solute (the substance that gets dissolved)
Solute The substance that dissolves in the solvent (e.g. sugar). Solvent (the liquid doing the dissolving)
Saturated solution A solution that can dissolve no more solute at that temperature. Concentrated solution (has a lot of solute, but may not be at maximum)
Residue The solid left on the filter paper after filtration. Filtrate (the liquid that passes through the filter paper)
Filtrate The liquid that passes through the filter paper. Residue (the solid trapped on top)
Rf value Distance spot travels ÷ distance solvent front travels. Unique for each substance in a given solvent. —
Solubility The mass of solute (g) that dissolves in 100 g of water at a given temperature. "Soluble" (a yes/no description, not a number)
Locating agent A chemical (e.g. ninhydrin) sprayed on a chromatogram to make colourless spots visible. —

Separation Methods — Which One and When?

Method Use When How It Works
Filtration Solid is NOT dissolved (insoluble) Pour through filter paper; solid = residue, liquid = filtrate
Crystallisation Solid IS dissolved, solubility changes a lot with temperature Heat to concentrate, cool to form crystals, filter, dry
Evaporation Solid IS dissolved, solubility barely changes with temperature Heat until all water is gone (e.g. NaCl)
Simple distillation Separate solvent from solute (e.g. water from salt water) Boil, condense vapour, collect pure solvent
Fractional distillation Separate two or more liquids with different boiling points Fractionating column separates vapours; lowest b.p. collected first
Chromatography Identify or separate components of a mixture Solvent carries substances up paper at different speeds
Exam Trap — Crystallisation vs Evaporation
These are NOT the same! Crystallisation = heat then cool (for substances whose solubility changes with temp, like CuSO₄). Evaporation = heat until dry (for NaCl, whose solubility barely changes). If the exam asks "Why not use crystallisation for NaCl?" — cooling won't produce crystals because salt's solubility barely changes.

Simple vs Fractional Distillation

Simple Distillation Fractional Distillation
Purpose Solvent from solute Liquid from liquid
Example Pure water from salt water Ethanol from water
Key apparatus Condenser Fractionating column + condenser
Industry use Desalination Petroleum refining, liquid air separation

Say It in One Sentence

Filtration Separates an insoluble solid from a liquid — the solid stays on the paper (residue), the liquid passes through (filtrate).
Crystallisation Make a hot saturated solution, cool it down, crystals form because solubility drops with temperature.
Chromatography Solvent carries substances up paper at different speeds — one spot = pure, multiple spots = mixture.
Rf formula Rf = distance to spot centre ÷ distance to solvent front. Always between 0 and 1.
Purity test (m.p.) Pure substance: sharp melting point at the correct value. Impure: melting point drops and range widens.
Impurity effect Impurities make melting point go DOWN and boiling point go UP.
Pencil not pen Use pencil for the chromatography baseline because pen ink dissolves in the solvent and interferes.

Memory Tricks

Decision Tree for Separation
Ask yourself: (1) Is the solid dissolved? NO → filtration. YES → (2) Does solubility change a lot with temperature? YES → crystallisation. NO → evaporation. Memorise these two questions and you can answer any "suggest a method" question.
Residue vs Filtrate
Residue = Remains on top (the solid). Filtrate = Flows through (the liquid). R stays, F flows.
Impurity Direction — "M Down, B Up"
Impurities make Melting point go Down and Boiling point go Up. Think: "MuD BUbbles" — Melting Down, Boiling Up.
Simple vs Fractional — "S for Solute, F for Friends"
Simple = separate from a Solute (solid from liquid). Fractional = separate Friends (two liquids from each other). If it's liquid from liquid, you need the fractionating column.
Rf Calculation Shortcut
"Spot over Front" — measure from the origin to the spot centre, divide by origin to front. Always less than 1. Practice: spot at 4 cm, front at 10 cm → Rf = 0.40.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How would you obtain pure water from seawater?" Simple distillation — boil, condense the steam, collect the distillate
"How would you separate ethanol from water?" Fractional distillation — ethanol (b.p. 78 °C) boils first and is collected
"How would you get salt from salt solution?" Evaporation — heat until all water is gone (salt's solubility barely changes with temp)
"How would you get copper sulfate crystals from solution?" Crystallisation — heat to concentrate, cool to form crystals, filter, pat dry
"Is substance X pure?" Check: does it give one spot on chromatography? Does it have a sharp melting point at the correct value?
"Calculate the Rf value" Rf = distance to spot centre ÷ distance to solvent front. Measure from the origin line
"Why use pencil for the baseline?" Pencil is insoluble in the solvent — pen ink would dissolve and interfere with results
"What happens to melting point with impurities?" Melting point decreases (goes down) and melts over a range instead of sharply
