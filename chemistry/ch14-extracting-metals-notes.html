Section 14.1 Metal Ores and Metal Extraction
Most metals are found in Earth's crust as compounds in rocks. The rocks we dig up to get metals are called ores. How you extract a metal depends on how reactive it is.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Ore: a rock from which a metal can be extracted economically (profitably).
• Native metals: unreactive metals like gold, silver, platinum, and mercury that occur as the element itself (uncombined) in rock.
• Extraction: the process of obtaining a metal from its ore. It involves reduction — the metal compound loses oxygen (or gains electrons) to become the metal.

W
Write This Down — Earth's Crust Composition

The two most common elements in Earth's crust are oxygen (45%) and silicon (27%). Together they make up nearly three-quarters of the crust. The most abundant metals are aluminium (8%) and iron (6%). All six most common metals occur as compounds because they are reactive.

W
Write This Down — Reactivity Decides the Method
Reactivity Metals Extraction Method
Very reactive Potassium, sodium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium Electrolysis (powerful but expensive)
Medium reactive (below carbon) Zinc, iron, copper Heating with carbon or carbon monoxide in a furnace
Unreactive Silver, gold Found as elements — no reduction needed
!
Don't Confuse — Carbon's Position

Carbon is not a metal, but it sits in the reactivity series between aluminium and zinc. Metals below carbon can be extracted by heating with carbon. Metals above carbon (like aluminium) need electrolysis because carbon is not reactive enough to displace them.

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves to give you a metal and ask "How would you extract it?" The answer is always: check where it sits in the reactivity series relative to carbon. Above carbon = electrolysis. Below carbon = heat with carbon. Unreactive = found as element. This one rule answers many exam questions.

T
Study Tip

Learn the reactivity series with the mnemonic: Please Stop Calling Me A Cute Zebra, I'm Completely Silly Going Pink (Potassium, Sodium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, Carbon, Zinc, Iron, Copper, Silver, Gold, Platinum). Draw a line after Carbon — everything above needs electrolysis.

  • I can define "ore" and "native metal"
  • I can name the two most common metals in Earth's crust
  • I can explain why the extraction method depends on reactivity
  • I can state which metals need electrolysis vs heating with carbon
Section 14.2 Extracting Iron — The Blast Furnace
Iron is the world's most-used metal. It is extracted from its ore (hematite) in a blast furnace — a huge oven about 30 metres tall that runs 24 hours a day.
W
Write This Down — The Charge (What Goes In)

Three raw materials are loaded into the top of the blast furnace. This mixture is called the charge:

• Iron ore (hematite) — mainly iron(III) oxide, Fe₂O₃
• Limestone — mainly calcium carbonate, CaCO₃ (removes impurities)
• Coke — almost pure carbon, made from coal (the fuel and reducing agent)

Hot air is blasted in through pipes near the bottom — this is why it is called a blast furnace.

W
Write This Down — The Four Stages of Reactions

Stage 1: Coke burns in hot air → carbon dioxide + heat
carbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide (exothermic, heats the furnace)

Stage 2: CO₂ reacts with more coke → carbon monoxide
carbon + carbon dioxide → carbon monoxide (endothermic)

Stage 3: Carbon monoxide reduces the iron ore → iron
iron(III) oxide + carbon monoxide → iron + carbon dioxide
This is the key reaction! The iron(III) oxide is reduced (loses oxygen). Carbon monoxide is the reducing agent (it is oxidised to CO₂).

Stage 4: Limestone removes impurities as slag
calcium carbonate → calcium oxide + carbon dioxide (thermal decomposition)
calcium oxide + silicon(IV) oxide → calcium silicate (slag)

W
Write This Down — Products and Waste

• Molten iron collects at the bottom and is tapped off. It still contains some carbon and impurities.
• Slag (calcium silicate) floats on the iron and is drained off. It is used for road building.
• Waste gases (hot CO₂ and N₂) exit from the top. Their heat is used to warm the incoming air blast.

!
Don't Confuse — Reduction vs Oxidation in the Furnace

The iron ore is reduced (it loses oxygen). The carbon/carbon monoxide is oxidised (it gains oxygen). This makes it a redox reaction. On the exam, if they ask "Why is this a reduction?", say: "The iron(III) oxide loses oxygen" or "The iron ions gain electrons."

?
Why This Matters

The blast furnace is one of the most-tested topics in IGCSE Chemistry. You need to know: the three raw materials and their purposes, the word equations for each stage, what slag is and how it forms, and why the iron produced is not pure. This can easily be a 5-6 mark question.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Furnace

Sketch the blast furnace from memory: chimney shape, charge goes in the top, hot air blasted in at the bottom, molten iron at the very bottom, slag floating above it. Label the temperature zones: 1400°C at the bottom (coke burning), 800°C in the middle (CO forms), 400°C higher up (iron forms and trickles down). If you can draw it and label all four stages, you own this topic.

  • I can name the three raw materials and the purpose of each
  • I can give the word equation for each of the four stages
  • I can explain why this is a reduction reaction
  • I can explain what slag is and how it forms
Section 14.3 Extracting Aluminium — Electrolysis
Aluminium is the most abundant metal in Earth's crust, but it is too reactive to extract with carbon. It must be extracted by electrolysis — a powerful but expensive method.
W
Write This Down — The Process

• The ore is bauxite (impure aluminium oxide, Al₂O₃, mixed with sand and iron oxides).
• Bauxite is purified to give white aluminium oxide.
• Aluminium oxide has a very high melting point (2072°C) — too expensive to melt.
• So it is dissolved in molten cryolite (sodium aluminium fluoride), which melts at only ~1012°C. This lowers the operating temperature to about 950°C and saves fuel costs. Cryolite is also a better conductor than pure aluminium oxide.

W
Write This Down — The Electrolytic Cell

• The cell is a steel container lined with carbon (graphite), which acts as the cathode (negative electrode).
• Large blocks of carbon hang into the cell as anodes (positive electrodes).
• When electricity flows, the aluminium oxide breaks down into aluminium and oxygen.

At the cathode (−): aluminium ions gain electrons → molten aluminium
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (reduction)

At the anode (+): oxide ions lose electrons → oxygen gas
2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ (oxidation)

Overall: aluminium oxide → aluminium + oxygen
2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂

W
Write This Down — Why Anodes Need Replacing

The oxygen gas produced at the anodes reacts with the hot carbon anodes:
carbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide
The carbon anodes gradually burn away and must be regularly replaced. This adds to the cost.

!
Don't Confuse — Melted vs Dissolved

The aluminium oxide is not melted — it is dissolved in molten cryolite. If the exam asks "Why is cryolite used?", give two reasons: (1) it lowers the melting point so the operating temperature is lower, saving energy/fuel costs, and (2) it acts as a better conductor.

?
Why This Matters

Aluminium extraction costs over three times as much per tonne as iron, even though aluminium is more abundant. This is because electrolysis uses huge amounts of electricity. The exam often asks you to compare the extraction of iron and aluminium — know both processes, their costs, and why different methods are used.

T
Study Tip

Remember: Cathode = Cation goes there = aluminium ions (positive) = Reduction. Anode = Anion goes there = oxide ions (negative) = Oxidation. Use "OILRIG" (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain of electrons) to check your half-equations.

  • I can name the ore and the purified compound used
  • I can explain why cryolite is used (two reasons)
  • I can state what happens at the cathode and the anode
  • I can explain why the carbon anodes must be replaced
Section 14.4 Making Use of Metals
Different metals have different properties — density, strength, conductivity, resistance to corrosion. We match the properties to the use.
W
Write This Down — Properties Dictate Uses
Metal Used For Key Properties
Aluminium Overhead cables, aircraft, drinks cans, cooking foil Low density (light), good conductor, ductile, resists corrosion
Copper Electrical wiring, water pipes Excellent conductor, ductile, malleable, does not corrode easily
Zinc Galvanising (coating iron/steel), batteries Prevents rusting (sacrificial protection), reactive enough for batteries
Gold Jewellery, electronics Unreactive, lustrous, ductile
W
Write This Down — Aluminium's Special Properties

• Light (much lower density than iron) — ideal for vehicles, planes, spacecraft
• Not as strong as iron, but can be made into strong alloys
• More malleable and ductile than iron — can be rolled into thin sheets or drawn into cables
• Conducts electricity (not as well as copper, but lighter and cheaper for overhead cables)
• A thin layer of aluminium oxide forms on its surface, which prevents further corrosion

?
Why This Matters

The exam will ask: "Explain why aluminium is used for aircraft bodies" or "Why is copper used for electrical wiring?" You must link the property to the use. For example: "Aluminium is used for aircraft because it has a low density (is light) so less fuel is needed, and it resists corrosion so it does not weaken over time." Always give the property AND the reason it matters.

  • I can link properties of aluminium, copper, and zinc to their uses
  • I can explain why aluminium resists corrosion
Section 14.5 Alloys
Pure metals are often too soft for practical use. Mixing a metal with one or more other elements creates an alloy with improved properties.
W
Write This Down — Definition and Why Alloys Work

An alloy is a mixture of a metal with at least one other element (which can be a metal or a non-metal).

In a pure metal, atoms are in regular rows. When force is applied, the layers slide over each other easily, so the metal is soft. In an alloy, the added atoms are a different size and fit into the lattice. This disrupts the regular arrangement, making it harder for layers to slide. So the alloy is harder and stronger.

W
Write This Down — Key Alloys
Alloy Made From Properties Uses
Mild steel Iron + small amount of carbon (<0.5%) Hard and strong Buildings, ships, car bodies, machinery
Stainless steel Iron + chromium + nickel Hard, rustproof Cutlery, surgical instruments, kitchens
Brass Copper + zinc Hard, attractive, does not corrode Musical instruments, door knobs, keys
Bronze Copper + tin Hard, sonorous, resists corrosion Coins, statues, bells, propellers
Aluminium alloy Aluminium + zinc, magnesium, copper Light but very strong, does not corrode Aircraft, spacecraft
!
Don't Confuse — Alloy vs Mixture of Metals

An alloy is not just metals mixed together in a bowl. The new atoms must enter the metal lattice. To make an alloy, you melt the main metal first, dissolve the other element in it, then let it cool and harden. The atoms of different sizes inside the lattice are what change the properties.

?
Why This Matters

Pure iron is almost never used because it is too soft and rusts easily. Most iron from the blast furnace is turned into steel (an alloy). The exam may show you diagrams of a pure metal lattice vs an alloy lattice and ask you to explain why the alloy is harder. The answer: different-sized atoms prevent layers sliding.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Two Lattices

Draw two diagrams side by side: (1) Pure metal: neat rows of same-size circles. Arrow shows layers sliding. (2) Alloy: mostly same-size circles with a few bigger/smaller ones mixed in. Arrow shows layers blocked. Label each and write: "Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular structure, preventing layers from sliding easily." This is a classic 3-mark exam answer.

  • I can define what an alloy is
  • I can explain why alloys are harder than pure metals (using lattice diagrams)
  • I can name the components and uses of mild steel, stainless steel, brass, and bronze
  • I can explain why pure iron is not useful for construction
