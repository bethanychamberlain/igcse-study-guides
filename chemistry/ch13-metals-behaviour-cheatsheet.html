Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Malleable Can be hammered into shape without breaking. Ductile (drawn into wires — different from hammering)
Ductile Can be drawn (stretched) into thin wires. Malleable (hammered into sheets — different process)
Sonorous Makes a ringing sound when struck. —
Reactivity series A list of metals ranked by how easily they lose electrons to form positive ions. —
Displacement A more reactive metal pushes a less reactive metal out of its compound. Only works "downhill" — less reactive cannot displace more reactive
Reducing agent A substance that removes oxygen from another substance (it gets oxidised itself). Oxidising agent (adds oxygen to something)
Rusting Iron reacts with both oxygen and water to form hydrated iron(III) oxide. Corrosion in general — rusting specifically means iron + oxygen + water
Galvanising Coating iron with zinc. Gives both barrier AND sacrificial protection. Painting (barrier only — if paint chips, iron rusts immediately)
Sacrificial protection Attaching a more reactive metal (Mg or Zn) that corrodes instead of the iron. Barrier methods (just keep out air/water; no chemical protection)

Exam Trap — Hydroxide vs Oxide
Cold water → hydroxide (NaOH). Steam → oxide (MgO). The exam checks whether you know which product goes with which condition. Only the most reactive metals (K, Na, Ca) make hydroxides with water.

Rusting — Conditions and Prevention

Barrier Methods Sacrificial Protection
How it works Keeps out oxygen and water More reactive metal corrodes instead
Examples Painting, oiling, plastic coating, tin plating Magnesium blocks on ship hulls, zinc bars on oil rigs
If scratched? Iron rusts immediately underneath Still protected (the reactive metal sacrifices itself)
Special case Galvanising (zinc coating) gives BOTH barrier AND sacrificial protection — this is why it's the best method

Rusting equation: iron + oxygen + water → hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust)

Both oxygen AND water must be present. Remove either one = no rust.

Say It in One Sentence

Reactivity series K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, (C), Zn, Fe, (H), Cu, Ag, Au — most reactive to least reactive.
Displacement A more reactive metal kicks out a less reactive metal from its compound — only works downhill.
Carbon line Metals above carbon need electrolysis; metals below carbon can be reduced by heating with carbon.
Hydrogen line Metals above hydrogen displace it from dilute acids; metals below hydrogen cannot.
Rusting Iron + oxygen + water = rust. Remove either oxygen or water and rusting stops.
Galvanising Zinc coating on iron — barrier protection PLUS sacrificial protection even if scratched.
Aluminium puzzle High in the series but seems unreactive because its oxide layer protects it from further corrosion.

Memory Tricks

Reactivity Series Mnemonic
Please Stop Calling Me A Cute Zebra, I Hate Copying Silly Games — Potassium, Sodium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, Carbon, Zinc, Iron, Hydrogen, Copper, Silver, Gold. Write it out five times from memory.
Displacement = "Higher Wins the Fight"
Think of the reactivity series as a ranking. Iron vs copper? Iron is higher → iron wins → iron displaces copper. Copper vs iron? Copper is lower → copper loses → no reaction. Simple.
Three Test Tubes for Rusting
Tube 1: dry air only (no water) → no rust. Tube 2: boiled water + oil (no air) → no rust. Tube 3: air + water → rust. Both must be present. Sketch this from memory.
Galvanising = "Double Defence"
Zinc is a barrier (keeps out air/water) AND a sacrifice (if scratched, zinc corrodes instead of iron). Painting is only a barrier. That's why the exam always asks about galvanising — it's the best of both worlds.
Aluminium's Secret Armour
Aluminium IS reactive (it's above zinc!) but its oxide layer is like invisible armour — thin, tough, and sticks tight. Scratch through it and aluminium reacts vigorously (thermit reaction = molten iron!).

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Give three differences between metals and non-metals" (1) Metals are malleable, non-metals are brittle. (2) Metals conduct electricity, non-metals don't (except graphite). (3) Metal oxides are basic, non-metal oxides are acidic.
"Put these metals in order of reactivity: Cu, Mg, Fe, Zn" Most reactive first: Mg, Zn, Fe, Cu (follow the reactivity series)
"Will zinc react with copper sulfate solution?" Yes. Zinc is above copper in the series → zinc displaces copper. Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. Copper coats the zinc; blue solution fades.
"Will copper react with zinc sulfate solution?" No reaction. Copper is below zinc — it cannot displace a more reactive metal.
"Write the word equation for iron + copper(II) sulfate" iron + copper(II) sulfate → iron(II) sulfate + copper
"What two conditions are needed for iron to rust?" Oxygen (from air) and water. Both must be present.
"Why does galvanised iron not rust even when scratched?" Zinc is more reactive than iron, so it corrodes instead of the iron (sacrificial protection). The zinc gives up electrons in place of the iron.
"Why can carbon reduce iron oxide but not aluminium oxide?" Carbon is above iron but below aluminium in the reactivity series. Carbon can only reduce the oxide of a metal below it.
"Why does aluminium appear unreactive despite being high in the series?" Aluminium forms a thin, tough layer of aluminium oxide on its surface that acts as a barrier, preventing further reaction.
"Metal X reacts slowly with steam but not with cold water. Suggest what X might be." Magnesium, zinc, or iron — they react with steam (making oxide + hydrogen) but not cold water.
"Why are gold and copper found as native elements?" They are very unreactive (low in the reactivity series), so they do not combine easily with other elements and exist uncombined in the Earth's crust.
