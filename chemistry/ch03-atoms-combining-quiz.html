Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
What is a compound? How is it different from a mixture?
2 marks
Answer
A compound is made of atoms of different elements bonded together [1]. A mixture contains different substances that are not bonded/chemically joined together and can usually be separated easily [1].
The key word is "bonded." A compound = bonded, a mixture = not bonded. If you only say "a compound has different elements" without mentioning bonding, you lose a mark.

2
Explain why atoms of Group VIII (the noble gases) do not form bonds.
1 mark
Answer
Their atoms already have a full outer shell of electrons, so they are stable and do not need to gain, lose, or share electrons [1].
"Full outer shell" is the key phrase. Don't just say "they are stable" without explaining why.

3
Define the term ion. What is the difference between a cation and an anion?
2 marks
Answer
An ion is a charged particle (atom or group of atoms) that has an unequal number of protons and electrons [1]. A cation is a positive ion (formed by losing electrons). An anion is a negative ion (formed by gaining electrons) [1].
Remember: positive cat(ion), negative an(ion). Metals form cations; non-metals form anions.

4
What is a covalent bond? What is a molecule?
2 marks
Answer
A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share a pair of electrons [1]. A molecule is two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds [1].
Don't say "atoms give electrons to each other" — that describes ionic bonding, not covalent. Sharing is the key word.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY and HOW, not just name things.
5
Describe how an ionic bond forms between sodium and chlorine. Include what happens to the electrons, the charges on the ions formed, and the formula of the compound.
4 marks
Answer
Sodium has 1 electron in its outer shell. It loses/transfers this electron to a chlorine atom [1]. Sodium becomes a positive ion, Na⁺, and chlorine becomes a negative ion, Cl⁻ [1]. Both ions now have a full outer shell of electrons [1]. The formula of the compound is NaCl (one Na⁺ for each Cl⁻ so the charges balance to zero) [1].
Four marks, four ideas: (1) electron transfer from Na to Cl, (2) charges on both ions, (3) both achieve full outer shells, (4) formula. Don't forget to say both ions get full outer shells — that's the whole reason bonding happens.

6
Explain why solid sodium chloride does not conduct electricity, but sodium chloride solution does conduct.
3 marks
Answer
In solid sodium chloride, the ions are held in a fixed/regular lattice and cannot move [1]. To conduct electricity, charged particles must be free to move [1]. When dissolved in water, the lattice breaks apart and the ions (Na⁺ and Cl⁻) are free to move through the solution, carrying the electric charge [1].
Three marks: (1) ions fixed in solid, (2) must be free to move to conduct, (3) dissolving frees the ions. This question comes up very frequently. The same explanation applies to melted ionic compounds.

7
Diamond and graphite are both made of carbon atoms, yet diamond is very hard while graphite is soft and slippery. Explain this difference in terms of their structures.
4 marks
Answer
In diamond, each carbon atom is bonded to 4 other carbon atoms by strong covalent bonds [1], forming a rigid 3D giant covalent structure, which makes it very hard [1]. In graphite, each carbon atom is bonded to 3 others, forming flat layers/sheets [1]. The forces between the layers are weak, so the layers can slide over each other, making graphite soft and slippery [1].
Key distinction: it's the forces BETWEEN the layers in graphite that are weak, not the covalent bonds. The bonds within the layers are just as strong as in diamond. If you say "graphite has weak bonds," that's wrong and you'll lose the mark.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
The table below shows data for four substances. For each one, identify the type of structure and bonding. Explain your reasoning.

SubstanceMelting point / °CConducts as solid?Conducts when melted?
A801NoYes
B−78NoNo
C1083YesYes
D3550NoNo
6 marks
Answer
A: Giant ionic — high melting point (strong ionic bonds), does not conduct as a solid (ions fixed in lattice) but conducts when melted (ions free to move) [2 — 1 for identification, 1 for reasoning].

B: Molecular covalent (simple molecules) — very low melting point (weak intermolecular forces between molecules), does not conduct in either state (no charged particles/ions) [1].

C: Metallic — high melting point (strong metallic bonds), conducts as a solid and when melted (delocalised electrons free to move and carry charge in both states) [2 — 1 for identification, 1 for reasoning].

D: Giant covalent — very high melting point (many strong covalent bonds to break), does not conduct in either state (no free electrons or ions) [1].
This is one of the most common exam question types. The trick is matching the pattern: ionic = high mp, conducts only when melted/dissolved. Metallic = high mp, always conducts. Molecular = low mp, never conducts. Giant covalent = very high mp, never conducts (except graphite).

9
Magnesium (Group II) reacts with oxygen (Group VI) to form magnesium oxide. (a) Describe what happens to the electrons when magnesium reacts with oxygen. (b) State the charges on the ions formed and explain how you can work out the charge on each ion. (c) Write the formula of magnesium oxide.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Each magnesium atom loses/transfers 2 electrons from its outer shell to an oxygen atom [1]. The oxygen atom gains these 2 electrons [1].

(b) The magnesium ion has a charge of 2+ (Mg²⁺) because it has 12 protons but only 10 electrons (12+ and 10− = 2+) [1]. The oxide ion has a charge of 2− (O²⁻) because it has 8 protons but 10 electrons (8+ and 10− = 2−) [1].

(c) The formula is MgO — one Mg²⁺ balances one O²⁻, giving a total charge of zero [1].
The ion charges come from proton count minus electron count. Magnesium is in Group II so it loses 2 electrons. Oxygen is in Group VI so it needs 2 more to fill its shell. That's why the charges match and you only need one of each.

10
Copper is a metal used for electrical wiring and water pipes. Using your knowledge of metallic bonding and the structure of metals, explain: (a) why copper is a good conductor of electricity, (b) why copper can be drawn into thin wires (it is ductile), and (c) why copper has a high melting point (1083°C).
6 marks
Answer
(a) In copper, the outer electrons are delocalised (free to move throughout the structure) [1]. When a voltage is applied, these delocalised electrons can move through the metal lattice, carrying charge as an electric current [1].

(b) The metal ions are arranged in layers. When a force is applied, the layers can slide over each other [1] without the metallic bond breaking, because the sea of delocalised electrons moves with the ions and new bonds re-form in the new position [1].

(c) There is a strong electrostatic attraction between the positive copper ions and the sea of delocalised electrons (the metallic bond) [1]. It takes a large amount of energy to overcome these strong attractions, so the melting point is high [1].
Six marks, six ideas. Notice every answer comes back to the same model: positive ions in a sea of delocalised electrons. If you understand that model, you can answer any question about metals. Always mention "delocalised electrons" — that's the phrase the examiner is looking for.
