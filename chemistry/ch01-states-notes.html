Section 1.1 Everything Is Made of Particles
The big idea in chemistry: everything is made of very tiny pieces called particles. You can't see them, but there is evidence all around you that they exist.
W
Write This Down — The Big Idea

All matter is made of very tiny pieces called particles. Most are far too small to see. When you look at a jar of a chemical, you are looking at a collection of countless trillions of tiny particles.

W
Write This Down — Evidence for Particles

We can't see particles directly, but there is evidence they exist:

• Dust in sunlight: dust specks dance because they are being bombarded by tiny air particles, too small to see
• Cooking smells spread: smell particles are bombarded by air particles and spread by diffusion
• Potassium manganate(VII) crystal in water: the purple colour spreads through the water as particles leave the crystal and mix among the water particles — it dissolves
• Bromine vapour spreads upwards: brown bromine particles mix among colourless air particles by diffusion, even moving against gravity

W
Write This Down — Diffusion

Diffusion is the mixing and spreading of particles by colliding with other particles and bouncing off in all directions. It takes place in liquids and gases. Particles spread from where they are more concentrated to where they are less concentrated, until they are evenly mixed.

W
Write This Down — Three Types of Particles

• Atoms — the smallest particles that cannot break down further in chemical reactions (e.g. argon gas is single atoms)
• Molecules — two or more atoms joined together (e.g. water, nitrogen, oxygen, bromine)
• Ions — atoms or groups of atoms that carry a charge (e.g. the particles in potassium manganate(VII))

W
Write This Down — Evidence from Robert Brown

In 1827, Robert Brown saw pollen granules jiggling in water under a microscope. 78 years later, Einstein explained why: the granules were being struck by tiny invisible water particles. This proved water is made of particles.

!
Don't Confuse — Chemistry vs Biology Diffusion

In biology, you also learn about osmosis and active transport. Here in chemistry, diffusion means the same thing: particles spreading from high to low concentration. But chemistry diffusion doesn't involve membranes or living cells — it's about gases and liquids spreading out in open space. Same definition, different context.

?
Why This Matters

Diffusion explains why cooking smells spread from the kitchen to your room. On the exam, they want you to explain using particle theory: "The smell particles collide with air particles, bouncing in all directions, and gradually spread from where they are concentrated to where they are not."

T
Study Tip

When asked to "explain" diffusion, don't just say "particles move." Say why they move: "Particles move randomly. Because there are more particles in the area of high concentration, more move away from it than towards it, so there is a net movement from high to low concentration." That's a 3-mark answer.

  • I can state the big idea: all matter is made of particles
  • I can give four pieces of evidence that particles exist
  • I can define diffusion using the textbook's wording
  • I can name the three types of particle (atoms, molecules, ions)
  • I can explain how Robert Brown's observation proved particles exist
Section 1.2 Solids, Liquids, and Gases
Before we look at particles, we need to know the big visible differences between the three states of matter and the names for changing between them.
W
Write This Down — Properties of Each State

• A solid has a fixed shape and a fixed volume. It does not flow. It cannot be compressed into a smaller space.
• A liquid flows easily. It has a fixed volume, but its shape changes — it takes the shape of its container.
• A gas does not have a fixed volume or shape. It spreads out to fill its container. It is much lighter than the same volume of solid or liquid.

W
Write This Down — Changes of State

• Melting: solid → liquid (e.g. ice melts at 0°C)
• Boiling: liquid → gas at a fixed temperature throughout the liquid (e.g. water boils at 100°C)
• Evaporation: liquid → gas at the surface, at any temperature (slower than boiling)
• Condensing: gas → liquid (the opposite of evaporating)
• Freezing (solidifying): liquid → solid (the opposite of melting)

The melting point and freezing point of a substance are the same temperature. For water, both are 0°C.

?
Why This Matters

The exam may give you melting/boiling point data and ask what state a substance is in at a given temperature. Room temperature = 20°C. If the melting point is below 20°C and the boiling point is above 20°C, the substance is a liquid at room temperature (e.g. mercury: melts at −39°C, boils at 357°C → liquid).

!
Don't Confuse — Boiling vs Evaporation

Boiling happens at a fixed temperature throughout the liquid (bubbles form inside).
Evaporation happens at any temperature but only at the surface. It's why a puddle dries up even though it's not 100°C outside. The textbook lists three differences — make sure you know them.

T
Study Tip

Cover the "Boiling point" column in the table and try to recall whether each substance is a solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature (20°C). Then check. This is the kind of question that comes up on exams.

  • I can give two properties each of solids, liquids, and gases
  • I can name the five changes of state
  • I know the difference between boiling and evaporation
  • I can work out the state of a substance at a given temperature from its melting and boiling points
Section 1.3 The Particles in Solids, Liquids, and Gases
Section 1.2 told you the visible differences. Now we look at why — the differences are all due to how particles are arranged, how they move, and how strong the forces between them are.
W
Write This Down — Copy This Table Into Your Notes
Property Solid Liquid Gas
Arrangement Regular pattern (lattice), tightly packed Close together but no fixed pattern Far apart, no pattern
Movement Vibrate in fixed positions Slide past each other Move quickly in all directions
Forces Strong forces hold particles in place Weaker forces — particles can move around Very weak / negligible forces
Spacing Very close (touching) Close (almost touching) Very far apart
Energy Lowest kinetic energy Medium kinetic energy Highest kinetic energy
Example Ice, iron, salt Water, mercury, oil Steam, oxygen, helium
?
Why This Matters

This table is worth easy marks. The exam loves questions like "Describe the arrangement of particles in a liquid" or "Explain why gases can be compressed but solids cannot." The answer is always about spacing — gas particles are far apart (room to push closer), solid particles are already touching (no room).

!
Don't Confuse

Solid particles are not completely still. They vibrate in fixed positions. If the exam says "Do particles in a solid move?" the answer is yes — they vibrate, they just don't change position.

W
Write This Down — The Kinetic Particle Theory

The textbook summarises the key ideas you've now met as the kinetic particle theory (kinetic means "about motion"):

• A substance can be a solid, a liquid, or a gas, and change from one state to another.
• It has different characteristics in each state (e.g. solids do not flow).
• The differences are due to the way its particles are arranged and move in each state.

W
Write This Down — Changing State

Substances change state because particles take in heat energy, which changes how they move:

• Melting: a solid is heated → particles vibrate more and the solid expands → at the melting point, particles vibrate so much they break away from their positions → liquid forms
• Boiling: a liquid is heated → particles move faster, collide more, bounce further apart → at the boiling point, particles get enough energy to overcome the forces between them → gas forms
• Evaporating: even below the boiling point, some particles at the surface have enough energy to escape as gas

Cooling reverses all of this: particles lose energy, move more slowly, and forces of attraction pull them closer together.

T
Study Tip — Draw It

Draw particle diagrams for each state. Solid = neat rows of circles touching. Liquid = circles touching but jumbled. Gas = circles far apart with arrows showing movement. If you can draw all three from memory, you know it.

  • I can describe the arrangement, movement, and forces in solids
  • I can describe the arrangement, movement, and forces in liquids
  • I can describe the arrangement, movement, and forces in gases
  • I can explain why gases are compressible but solids are not
  • I can state the kinetic particle theory in my own words
  • I can explain what happens to particles during melting and boiling
Section 1.4 Heating and Cooling Curves
A heating curve is a graph of temperature vs time that shows exactly when state changes happen. The key insight: temperature stays constant during melting and boiling, even though you keep heating.
W
Write This Down — The Heating Curve

A heating curve has sloping sections (temperature rises) and flat sections (temperature stays constant).

• Flat at melting point: energy is used to break forces between particles, not to raise temperature
• Flat at boiling point: same idea — energy breaks remaining forces so particles escape as gas
• Sloping sections: energy increases kinetic energy of particles → temperature rises

W
Write This Down — Cooling Curves

A cooling curve is the mirror image of a heating curve. It shows the temperature as a substance cools from gas to solid. The flat sections appear at the same temperatures (boiling/condensing point and melting/freezing point) because energy is being released as forces of attraction form between particles.

!
Don't Confuse — Pure vs Impure on Heating Curves

A pure substance has sharp, flat horizontal lines at its melting and boiling points. An impure substance (mixture) melts and boils over a range of temperatures, so the flat sections become tilted/sloped. This is how you can test whether a substance is pure from its heating curve.

?
Why This Matters

The exam will show you a heating curve and ask: "Why is the temperature constant at this section?" The answer is always: "Energy is being used to overcome the forces between particles, not to increase kinetic energy, so the temperature does not change." Learn this sentence — it's worth 2 marks every time.

T
Study Tip — Sketch the Curve

Draw a heating curve from scratch: x-axis = time, y-axis = temperature. Draw the slope-flat-slope-flat-slope pattern. Label each section: solid, melting, liquid, boiling, gas. Write what particles are doing at each stage. If you can draw and label this from memory, you'll ace any heating curve question.

  • I can explain why temperature stays constant during melting and boiling
  • I can draw and label a heating curve from memory (slope-flat-slope-flat-slope)
  • I can describe a cooling curve and how it relates to the heating curve
  • I can explain how to tell if a substance is pure from its heating curve
Section 1.5 A Closer Look at Gases
Gas particles move fast, hit the walls of their container, and create pressure. How gases behave when you change the temperature or pressure — and why lighter gases diffuse faster.
W
Write This Down — Gas Pressure

Gas pressure is caused by gas particles colliding with the walls of their container. All gases exert pressure on the walls of their containers in this way.

W
Write This Down — Temperature and Gas Volume

At constant pressure:
• Heat a gas → particles move faster, collide with walls more often and with more force → gas expands (volume increases)
• Cool a gas → particles slow down, strike walls less often and with less force → gas contracts (volume decreases)

W
Write This Down — Pressure and Gas Volume

At constant temperature:
• Increase pressure (push piston down) → particles squeezed into smaller space → volume decreases
• Decrease pressure (release piston) → particles spread out → volume increases

If you increase the pressure enough, you can push gas particles so close together that the gas turns into a liquid.

W
Write This Down — Rate of Diffusion

The lower the mass of its particles, the faster a gas will diffuse. When particles collide and bounce away, lighter particles bounce further and faster.

The mass of a molecule is called its relative molecular mass. So: the lower the relative molecular mass, the faster a gas will diffuse.

W
Write This Down — The Ammonia-HCl Experiment

Cotton wool soaked in ammonia solution is placed at one end of a glass tube (A). Cotton wool soaked in hydrochloric acid is placed at the other end (B). Both give off gas. The gases diffuse along the tube and where they meet, white smoke (ammonium chloride) forms.

The white smoke forms closer to the HCl end (B). This shows ammonia particles have travelled further — they diffuse faster because they are lighter than hydrogen chloride particles.

?
Why This Matters

The ammonia-HCl tube experiment is a classic exam question. They'll show you the tube and ask "Why does the white ring form closer to one end?" You need to explain that ammonia particles have a lower mass, so they diffuse faster and travel further along the tube. The textbook also notes that the experiment must be at room temperature for a valid test, since temperature also affects how fast particles move.

T
Study Tip — Think "Light = Fast"

The textbook uses a marble analogy: imagine a light marble and a heavy marble colliding — the lighter one bounces further and faster. Same with gas molecules. If the exam asks which gas diffuses faster, just think: lighter particles = faster diffusion.

  • I can explain what happens to gas volume when you heat or cool it
  • I can explain what happens to gas volume when you change the pressure
  • I can explain why lighter gases diffuse faster
  • I can describe the ammonia-HCl tube experiment and explain the result
