Section 1.1 Everything Is Made of Particles
All matter — solids, liquids, and gases — is made of tiny particles (atoms, molecules, or ions). They're constantly moving, and the way they move explains almost everything in chemistry.
W
Write This Down

Particle theory (the kinetic model) says: (1) all matter is made of tiny particles, (2) particles are always moving, and (3) higher temperature = faster movement = more kinetic energy.

W
Write This Down — Diffusion

Diffusion is the spreading out of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration. It happens because particles move randomly in all directions.

Examples:
• Perfume sprayed in a corner eventually smells across the whole room
• Bromine gas (brown) spreads to fill a gas jar when the lid is removed
• A drop of ink spreads through a beaker of water without stirring

!
Don't Confuse — This Is Your Biggest Mix-Up

In biology, you also learn about osmosis and active transport. Here in chemistry, diffusion means the same thing: particles spreading from high to low concentration. But chemistry diffusion doesn't involve membranes or living cells — it's about gases and liquids spreading out in open space. Same definition, different context.

?
Why This Matters

Diffusion explains why you can smell food cooking from another room. The food particles spread through the air from high concentration (kitchen) to low concentration (your room). On the exam, they want you to explain using particle theory, not just say "it spreads."

W
Write This Down — Brownian Motion

Brownian motion is the random, jerky movement of small visible particles (like smoke or pollen) caused by collisions with invisible gas or liquid molecules. It provides evidence that particles are constantly moving.

T
Study Tip

When asked to "explain" diffusion, don't just say "particles move." Say why they move: "Particles move randomly. Because there are more particles in the area of high concentration, more move away from it than towards it, so there is a net movement from high to low concentration." That's a 3-mark answer.

  • I can state the three key ideas of particle theory
  • I can define diffusion without looking
  • I can give two examples of diffusion in chemistry
  • I can explain Brownian motion and why it matters
Section 1.3 Particles in Solids, Liquids, and Gases
The three states of matter differ in how their particles are arranged, how they move, and how strong the forces between them are.
W
Write This Down — Copy This Table Into Your Notes
Property Solid Liquid Gas
Arrangement Regular pattern (lattice), tightly packed Close together but no fixed pattern Far apart, no pattern
Movement Vibrate in fixed positions Slide past each other Move quickly in all directions
Forces Strong forces hold particles in place Weaker forces — particles can move around Very weak / negligible forces
Spacing Very close (touching) Close (almost touching) Very far apart
Energy Lowest kinetic energy Medium kinetic energy Highest kinetic energy
Example Ice, iron, salt Water, mercury, oil Steam, oxygen, helium
?
Why This Matters

This table is worth easy marks. The exam loves questions like "Describe the arrangement of particles in a liquid" or "Explain why gases can be compressed but solids cannot." The answer is always about spacing — gas particles are far apart (room to push closer), solid particles are already touching (no room).

!
Don't Confuse

Solid particles are not completely still. They vibrate in fixed positions. If the exam says "Do particles in a solid move?" the answer is yes — they vibrate, they just don't change position.

T
Study Tip — Draw It

Draw particle diagrams for each state. Solid = neat rows of circles touching. Liquid = circles touching but jumbled. Gas = circles far apart with arrows showing movement. If you can draw all three from memory, you know it.

  • I can describe the arrangement, movement, and forces in solids
  • I can describe the arrangement, movement, and forces in liquids
  • I can describe the arrangement, movement, and forces in gases
  • I can explain why gases are compressible but solids are not
Section 1.4 Heating and Cooling Curves
When you heat a substance, it changes state. A heating curve is a graph of temperature vs time that shows exactly when those changes happen — and what the particles are doing at each stage.
W
Write This Down — Changes of State

• Melting: solid → liquid (particles gain energy, overcome some forces, start sliding)
• Boiling / Evaporation: liquid → gas (particles gain enough energy to escape forces completely)
• Condensation: gas → liquid (particles lose energy, forces pull them closer)
• Freezing: liquid → solid (particles lose energy, lock into fixed positions)
• Sublimation: solid → gas directly (skips liquid), e.g. dry ice (solid CO₂)

W
Write This Down — The Heating Curve

A heating curve has sloping sections (temperature rises) and flat sections (temperature stays constant).

• Flat at melting point: energy is used to break forces between particles, not to raise temperature
• Flat at boiling point: same idea — energy breaks remaining forces so particles escape as gas
• Sloping sections: energy increases kinetic energy of particles → temperature rises

!
Don't Confuse — Boiling vs Evaporation

Boiling happens at a fixed temperature throughout the liquid (bubbles form inside).
Evaporation happens at any temperature but only at the surface. It's why a puddle dries up even though it's not 100°C outside.

?
Why This Matters

The exam will show you a heating curve and ask: "Why is the temperature constant at this section?" The answer is always: "Energy is being used to break/overcome the forces between particles, not to increase kinetic energy, so the temperature does not change." Learn this sentence — it's worth 2 marks every time.

T
Study Tip — Sketch the Curve

Draw a heating curve from scratch: x-axis = time, y-axis = temperature. Draw the slope-flat-slope-flat-slope pattern. Label each section: solid, melting, liquid, boiling, gas. Write what particles are doing at each stage. If you can draw and label this from memory, you'll ace any heating curve question.

  • I can name all five changes of state
  • I can explain why temperature stays constant during melting and boiling
  • I know the difference between boiling and evaporation
  • I can draw and label a heating curve from memory
Section 1.5 A Closer Look at Gases
Gas particles move fast, hit the walls of their container, and create pressure. Heavier gas molecules move more slowly, which affects how quickly gases diffuse.
W
Write This Down

Gas pressure is caused by gas particles colliding with the walls of their container. More collisions (or harder collisions) = higher pressure.

Pressure increases when you: (1) heat the gas (particles move faster, hit harder), (2) decrease the volume (particles hit walls more often), or (3) add more gas (more particles = more collisions).

W
Write This Down — Diffusion Rate and Molecular Mass

Lighter gas molecules diffuse faster than heavier ones. This is because at the same temperature, all gas particles have the same kinetic energy, but lighter particles must move faster to have the same energy.

Example: Hydrogen (Mᵣ = 2) diffuses much faster than hydrogen chloride (Mᵣ = 36.5). In the classic experiment, ammonia (NH₃, Mᵣ = 17) and HCl (Mᵣ = 36.5) diffuse along a tube — the white ring of ammonium chloride forms closer to the HCl end because ammonia is lighter and travels further.

!
Don't Confuse

Relative molecular mass (Mᵣ) is not the same as the number of atoms. CO₂ has 3 atoms but Mᵣ = 44 (12 + 16 + 16). H₂ has 2 atoms but Mᵣ = 2 (1 + 1). Always add up the actual atomic masses, not count atoms.

?
Why This Matters

The ammonia-HCl tube experiment is a classic exam question. They'll show you the tube and ask "Why does the white ring form closer to one end?" You need to explain that ammonia has a lower molecular mass, so it diffuses faster and travels further along the tube.

T
Study Tip — Think "Heavy = Slow"

Imagine throwing a tennis ball vs a bowling ball with the same energy. The tennis ball goes much further and faster. Same with gas molecules: lighter = faster diffusion. If the exam asks which gas diffuses faster, just compare their Mᵣ values — the smaller number wins.

  • I can explain what causes gas pressure using particle theory
  • I know three ways to increase gas pressure
  • I can explain why lighter gases diffuse faster
  • I can describe the ammonia-HCl tube experiment and explain the result
