Foundation — Can You Define and Calculate the Basics?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. These are your free marks — don't drop them.
1
Define the term mole and state the value of the Avogadro constant.
2 marks
Answer
A mole is the amount of substance that contains 6.02 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) [1]. The Avogadro constant is 6.02 × 10²³ [1].
You can also say: "One mole is the number of particles obtained by weighing out the Aᵣ or Mᵣ of a substance in grams." Either definition works.

2
Calculate the relative molecular mass (Mᵣ) of calcium carbonate, CaCO₃. (Aᵣ: Ca = 40, C = 12, O = 16.)
1 mark
Answer
Mᵣ = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100 [1].
Show the working even for a 1-mark question. The "3 × 16" is where people go wrong — don't forget there are three oxygens.

3
How many moles of molecules are in 64 g of oxygen gas, O₂? (Aᵣ: O = 16.)
2 marks
Answer
Mᵣ of O₂ = 2 × 16 = 32 [1].
Moles = 64 ÷ 32 = 2 mol [1].
Remember: oxygen exists as O₂ molecules, not single O atoms. Mᵣ = 32, not 16.

4
State the molar gas volume at room temperature and pressure (rtp). What volume would 0.5 mol of any gas occupy at rtp?
2 marks
Answer
The molar gas volume at rtp is 24 dm³ (or 24,000 cm³) [1].
0.5 mol occupies 0.5 × 24 = 12 dm³ [1].
rtp = 20°C and 1 atmosphere. The 24 dm³ value is given in some exams, but you should memorise it just in case.

Intermediate — Can You Work Through Multi-Step Calculations?

Worth 3-4 marks each. Show every step — method marks matter even if the final number is wrong.
5
Hydrogen burns in oxygen to form water: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l). Calculate the mass of water produced when 1 g of hydrogen burns completely. (Aᵣ: H = 1, O = 16.)
3 marks
Answer
From the equation: 2 mol H₂ → 2 mol H₂O [1].
In grams: 2 × 2 = 4 g of H₂ produces 2 × 18 = 36 g of H₂O.
Scaling down: 1 g of H₂ is ¼ of 4 g, so water produced = 36 ÷ 4 = 9 g [1].
(Check: 1 g H₂ + 8 g O₂ = 9 g H₂O — mass is conserved.) [1]
Three steps: mole ratio from equation [1], convert to grams [1], scale to actual mass [1]. Always check by adding reactant masses — they should equal product mass.

6
What volume of carbon dioxide gas (at rtp) is produced when 6 g of carbon burns in plenty of air? Equation: C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g). (Aᵣ: C = 12.)
3 marks
Answer
Moles of carbon = 6 ÷ 12 = 0.5 mol [1].
From equation: 1 mol C gives 1 mol CO₂, so 0.5 mol C gives 0.5 mol CO₂ [1].
Volume = 0.5 × 24 = 12 dm³ (or 12,000 cm³) [1].
This is the classic three-step gas calculation: mass → moles → use equation ratio → volume. Practise this pattern until it's automatic.

7
A solution is made by dissolving 4 g of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) in water to make 500 cm³ of solution. Calculate the concentration in mol/dm³. (Aᵣ: Na = 23, O = 16, H = 1.)
3 marks
Answer
Mᵣ of NaOH = 23 + 16 + 1 = 40 [1].
Moles = 4 ÷ 40 = 0.1 mol [1].
Volume = 500 cm³ = 500 ÷ 1000 = 0.5 dm³.
Concentration = 0.1 ÷ 0.5 = 0.2 mol/dm³ [1].
The most common error is forgetting to convert 500 cm³ to 0.5 dm³. If you used 500 in the formula, you would get 0.0002 — a tiny number that should alert you something is wrong. Always convert to dm³ first.

Advanced — Can You Combine Skills and Apply Them?

Worth 4-6 marks each. These combine multiple calculation skills in one question — exactly like the real exam.
8
A compound contains 40% carbon, 6.7% hydrogen, and 53.3% oxygen by mass. (a) Find its empirical formula. (b) If its Mᵣ is 180, find its molecular formula. (Aᵣ: C = 12, H = 1, O = 16.)
5 marks
Answer
(a) Assume 100 g: C = 40 g, H = 6.7 g, O = 53.3 g.
Moles: C = 40÷12 = 3.33, H = 6.7÷1 = 6.7, O = 53.3÷16 = 3.33 [1].
Divide by smallest (3.33): C = 1, H = 2, O = 1 [1].
Empirical formula = CH₂O [1].

(b) Empirical formula mass = 12 + 2 + 16 = 30 [1].
n = 180 ÷ 30 = 6.
Molecular formula = 6 × CH₂O = C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) [1].
Part (a): set up the table — mass, Aᵣ, moles, ratio. Part (b): just divide Mᵣ by empirical mass. This is a very common 5-mark question and always follows the same method.

9
Mercury(II) oxide decomposes on heating: 2HgO(s) → 2Hg(l) + O₂(g). 21.7 g of mercury(II) oxide is heated and only 19.0 g of mercury is collected. Calculate the percentage yield of mercury. (Aᵣ: Hg = 201, O = 16.)
5 marks
Answer
Mᵣ of HgO = 201 + 16 = 217 [1].
Moles of HgO = 21.7 ÷ 217 = 0.1 mol [1].
From equation: 2 mol HgO → 2 mol Hg, so 0.1 mol HgO gives 0.1 mol Hg.
Theoretical mass of Hg = 0.1 × 201 = 20.1 g [1].
Actual mass = 19.0 g [1].
% yield = (19.0 ÷ 20.1) × 100 = 94.5% [1].
The chain: mass → moles → equation ratio → theoretical mass → % yield formula. Each step earns a mark. If you get the arithmetic wrong, you still earn method marks for showing every step.

10
A 5.0 g sample of impure magnesium carbonate is reacted with excess hydrochloric acid. 1200 cm³ of carbon dioxide gas is collected at rtp. Calculate the percentage purity of the magnesium carbonate. Equation: MgCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g). (Aᵣ: Mg = 24, C = 12, O = 16.)
5 marks
Answer
Moles of CO₂ = 1200 ÷ 24000 = 0.05 mol [1].
From equation: 1 mol MgCO₃ gives 1 mol CO₂, so 0.05 mol MgCO₃ reacted [1].
Mᵣ of MgCO₃ = 24 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 84 [1].
Mass of pure MgCO₃ = 0.05 × 84 = 4.2 g [1].
% purity = (4.2 ÷ 5.0) × 100 = 84% [1].
This question tests everything: gas volumes → moles → equation ratio → mass conversion → purity formula. It is exactly the type of 5-mark question that appears on IGCSE papers. If you can do this, you can handle any moles question they throw at you.
