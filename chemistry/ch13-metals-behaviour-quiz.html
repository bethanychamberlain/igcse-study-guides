Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Give the meanings of the terms malleable, ductile, and sonorous.
3 marks
Answer
Malleable: can be hammered into shape without breaking [1].
Ductile: can be drawn out into wires [1].
Sonorous: makes a ringing sound when struck [1].
These are classic definition marks. The examiner wants the exact phrases: "hammered into shape," "drawn into wires," "ringing sound." One mark each.

2
State the two substances that must be present for iron to rust. Give the word equation for rusting.
3 marks
Answer
Oxygen (from the air) [1] and water [1] must both be present.

iron + oxygen + water → hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust) [1]
Both substances must be named for full marks. "Air" alone is not enough — say "oxygen." The word equation must include "hydrated iron(III) oxide" not just "rust" for the full mark.

List the metals of the reactivity series in order from most reactive to least reactive. Include where carbon and hydrogen sit.
2 marks
Answer
Potassium, Sodium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, (Carbon), Zinc, Iron, (Hydrogen), Copper, Silver, Gold [1 for correct order, 1 for correct placement of carbon and hydrogen].
Mnemonic: Please Stop Calling Me A Cute Zebra, I Hate Copying Silly Games. Carbon sits between Al and Zn; hydrogen sits between Fe and Cu.

4
Give two differences between the chemical properties of metal oxides and non-metal oxides.
2 marks
Answer
Metal oxides are bases (they neutralise acids to form salts and water) [1].
Non-metal oxides are acidic (they dissolve in water to form acidic solutions) [1].
The key contrast: metal oxides = basic, non-metal oxides = acidic. Some students also mention that metal oxides are often solid while non-metal oxides are often gases, but the chemical difference is what the examiner wants here.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY using the reactivity series, not just name things.
5
An iron nail is placed in a blue copper(II) sulfate solution. After 30 minutes the nail is coated with a brown-orange solid and the solution has turned pale green. Explain these observations and write a word equation for the reaction.
4 marks
Answer
Iron is more reactive than copper [1], so iron displaces copper from the copper(II) sulfate solution [1]. The brown-orange coating is copper metal deposited on the nail. The solution turns green because iron(II) sulfate has formed in solution [1].

Word equation: iron + copper(II) sulfate → iron(II) sulfate + copper [1]
Four ideas: (1) iron is more reactive, (2) displacement happens, (3) identify the brown solid as copper, (4) identify the green solution as iron(II) sulfate. The word equation ties it all together.

6
Describe the experiment using three test tubes that proves both oxygen and water are needed for rusting. State what is in each test tube and what happens.
4 marks
Answer
Tube 1: Iron nail in dry air (calcium chloride dries the air, no water present) → no rust [1].
Tube 2: Iron nail in boiled water with a layer of oil on top (water present but no air/oxygen) → no rust [1].
Tube 3: Iron nail in ordinary water exposed to air (both oxygen and water present) → rust forms [1].

Conclusion: Since rust only formed when both oxygen and water were present, both are required for rusting [1].
The boiled water removes dissolved oxygen, and the oil layer stops air getting back in. The calcium chloride absorbs moisture from the air. Mention these details if asked "how" the conditions are controlled.

7
Carbon powder is heated with copper(II) oxide, and a reaction occurs. But when carbon powder is heated with magnesium oxide, nothing happens. Explain both results using the reactivity series, and write a word equation for the reaction that does occur.
4 marks
Answer
Carbon is more reactive than copper (carbon is above copper in the reactivity series) [1], so carbon reduces copper(II) oxide by removing its oxygen. Copper metal and carbon dioxide are produced [1].

Word equation: carbon + copper(II) oxide → copper + carbon dioxide [1]

Magnesium is more reactive than carbon (magnesium is above carbon in the reactivity series) [1], so carbon cannot remove the oxygen from magnesium oxide. No reaction occurs.
The principle: a more reactive element reduces the oxide of a less reactive element. Carbon sits between aluminium and zinc in the series, so it can reduce ZnO, FeO, and CuO, but NOT MgO or Al₂O₃.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Aluminium is higher than iron in the reactivity series, yet aluminium does not need to be protected from corrosion while iron does. Explain why this is the case, and describe two different methods used to protect iron from rusting, explaining how each one works.
6 marks
Answer
Aluminium: When aluminium is exposed to air, it reacts instantly with oxygen to form a thin layer of aluminium oxide on the surface [1]. This oxide layer is tough and sticks tightly to the metal, forming a barrier that prevents further oxygen and water from reaching the aluminium underneath [1]. So aluminium effectively protects itself.

Method 1 — Painting / greasing (barrier method): Covering iron with paint, grease, or plastic creates a physical barrier that keeps oxygen and water away from the iron surface, preventing the rusting reaction [1]. If the barrier is damaged, the exposed iron will rust [1].

Method 2 — Galvanising (barrier + sacrificial): Coating iron with a layer of zinc provides a barrier to keep out oxygen and water [1]. But even if the zinc coating is scratched, the zinc still protects the iron because zinc is more reactive than iron, so the zinc corrodes preferentially (sacrificial protection), giving up its electrons instead of the iron [1].
Six marks, six points. The aluminium explanation needs TWO ideas: it reacts with oxygen AND the oxide layer is a barrier. For rust prevention, name the method AND explain the mechanism. Galvanising is the strongest answer because it covers both barrier and sacrificial.

9
Three metals X, Y, and Z are tested. Metal X reacts violently with cold water. Metal Y reacts slowly with steam but not with cold water. Metal Z does not react with water or steam but reacts slowly with dilute hydrochloric acid. (a) Place X, Y, and Z in order of reactivity. (b) Suggest an identity for each metal. (c) State the products when metal Y reacts with steam. (d) Write a word equation for the reaction of metal Z with hydrochloric acid.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Order of reactivity: X (most reactive), Y, Z (least reactive) [1].

(b) X = sodium or potassium (reacts violently with cold water) [1]. Y = iron or zinc (reacts with steam, not cold water) [1]. Z = copper is wrong — copper doesn't react with acid. Z could be iron (slow with HCl). Accept: Y = magnesium, Z = iron or zinc [1].

(c) Metal Y + steam → metal oxide + hydrogen [1]. For example: iron + steam → iron oxide + hydrogen.

(d) Metal Z + hydrochloric acid → metal chloride + hydrogen [1]. For example: iron + hydrochloric acid → iron(II) chloride + hydrogen.
The key skill is matching descriptions to positions in the reactivity series. "Violent with cold water" = K, Na, Ca. "Reacts with steam" = Mg, Zn, Fe. "Reacts with acid only" = metals just above hydrogen. "No reaction at all" = Cu, Ag, Au.

10
Magnesium powder is added to copper(II) sulfate solution. (a) Describe what you would see. (b) Write a word equation for the reaction. (c) Explain why this reaction occurs, in terms of electron transfer. (d) Explain why copper cannot displace magnesium from magnesium sulfate solution.
6 marks
Answer
(a) The blue copper(II) sulfate solution would become colourless (or lighter) [1] and a brown/orange solid (copper) would form on or around the magnesium [1].

(b) magnesium + copper(II) sulfate → magnesium sulfate + copper [1]

(c) Magnesium is more reactive than copper, meaning it has a stronger drive to lose electrons and form positive ions [1]. Magnesium atoms lose electrons (are oxidised: Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻) and the copper ions gain those electrons (are reduced: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu). This is a redox reaction [1].

(d) Copper is less reactive than magnesium, so copper has a weaker drive to form ions. Copper cannot push magnesium out of its compound because magnesium holds its place in the compound more strongly [1].
This question tests observations (what you SEE), the equation, and the explanation (electron transfer / redox). "Colourless" for the solution is important — magnesium sulfate solution is colourless, not green. For part (c), mention electron loss (oxidation) and electron gain (reduction).
