Section 19.1 The Scientific Method
All science follows a structured process: observe, hypothesise, test, analyse, conclude, share. Understanding the scientific method and variables is essential for Paper 6 (practical/alternative to practical).
W
Write This Down — The Scientific Method (7 Steps)

1. Make an observation (notice something interesting).
2. Form a hypothesis — a testable explanation or prediction.
3. Plan an experiment to test the hypothesis.
4. Carry out the experiment and record results.
5. Analyse the results (tables, graphs, calculations).
6. Conclude — do the results support the hypothesis?
7. Share conclusions for peer review (other scientists check your work).

W
Write This Down — Variables

• Independent variable = the thing you change on purpose (e.g. temperature).
• Dependent variable = the thing you measure (e.g. rate of reaction).
• Control variables = everything you keep the same to make the test fair.

The golden rule: Change only one thing at a time. If you change two things, you won't know which one caused the result.

?
Why This Matters

Paper 6 questions almost always ask: "What is the independent variable?" and "What is the dependent variable?" and "Name one control variable." These are free marks — but only if you know the definitions cold. Also expect: "How would you improve reliability?" Answer: repeat the experiment and calculate an average.

!
Don't Confuse — Hypothesis vs Prediction

A hypothesis is a testable statement that explains something (e.g. "X contains more sodium hydroxide than Y"). "I am late for class again" is not a hypothesis because it cannot be tested by an experiment. "December follows November" is a fact, not a hypothesis. Make sure the statement can actually be tested in a lab.

T
Study Tip — Lab Skills Checklist

The five key lab skills: (1) Thinking — plan carefully. (2) Observing — watch closely. (3) Using apparatus — measure accurately. (4) Doing maths — calculate and graph. (5) Writing up — report and evaluate. The exam expects you to know what makes a good experiment, not just how to follow instructions.

  • I can list the seven steps of the scientific method
  • I can define independent, dependent, and control variables
  • I know what a hypothesis is (and what it is not)
  • I can explain how to improve reliability of results
Section 19.2 Writing Up an Experiment
Being able to write up an experiment properly — including the use of titrations and indicators — is a key exam skill. This section uses a titration experiment as an example.
W
Write This Down — Structure of a Lab Report

• Introduction — what you observed and your hypothesis.
• Method — what you did, step by step (enough detail for someone to repeat it).
• Results — data in a clear table (initial reading, final reading, volume used).
• Analysis — what the results show.
• Conclusion — did the results support the hypothesis?
• Evaluation — how could reliability be improved? (Repeat trials, calculate averages, use more accurate apparatus.)

W
Write This Down — Titration Key Points

• A burette delivers acid drop by drop. A volumetric pipette measures the alkali accurately.
• Use a conical flask (not a beaker) so you can swirl without spilling.
• An indicator shows when neutralisation is complete (the end-point).
• Use methyl orange or thymolphthalein for titrations — NOT universal indicator (its colour change is too gradual to spot the exact end-point).
• Methyl orange: yellow (alkali) → red (acid) at end-point.

!
Don't Confuse — Accuracy of Apparatus

For measuring small precise volumes: volumetric pipette > burette > measuring cylinder > beaker. If the exam asks "Which is more accurate, a burette or a volumetric pipette?" the answer is the volumetric pipette for a fixed volume, and the burette for variable volumes delivered drop by drop.

T
Study Tip

If the exam gives you a lab report to evaluate, look for: (1) Were control variables kept the same? (2) Were measurements repeated? (3) Was the apparatus appropriate? (4) Does the conclusion match the data? These are the standard evaluation marks.

  • I can describe the structure of a lab report
  • I know how a titration works and what apparatus is used
  • I can explain why universal indicator is not used in titrations
  • I can rank apparatus by accuracy
Section 19.3 Preparing and Testing Gases
You need to know how to make common gases in the lab, how to collect them, and how to test for them. This is one of the most heavily tested topics on the exam — especially gas tests.
W
Write This Down — How to Prepare Three Common Gases
Gas Reactants Reaction
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Calcium carbonate (marble chips) + dilute hydrochloric acid CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
Hydrogen (H₂) Zinc pieces + dilute hydrochloric acid Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
Oxygen (O₂) Manganese(IV) oxide (catalyst) + hydrogen peroxide 2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g)

Ammonia (NH₃): Heat any ammonium compound with a base (e.g. ammonium chloride + sodium hydroxide). Both reactants must be solid.

W
Write This Down — Four Ways to Collect a Gas
Collection Method Use When Examples
Upward displacement of air Gas is heavier than air (denser) CO₂, SO₂, HCl
Downward displacement of air Gas is lighter than air (less dense) NH₃, H₂
Over water Gas is only sparingly soluble in water CO₂, H₂, O₂
Gas syringe You need to measure the volume accurately Any gas
W
Write This Down — Gas Tests (MEMORISE THIS TABLE)
Gas Test Positive Result
Hydrogen (H₂) Hold a lighted splint to the gas Burns with a squeaky pop
Oxygen (O₂) Hold a glowing splint to the gas The splint relights (bursts into flame)
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Bubble the gas through limewater The limewater turns cloudy/milky
Ammonia (NH₃) Hold damp red litmus paper in the gas The litmus paper turns blue (+ sharp smell)
Chlorine (Cl₂) Hold damp indicator paper in the gas The indicator paper is bleached white
Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) Hold filter paper soaked in acidified potassium manganate(VII) in the gas The purple colour changes to colourless
!
Don't Confuse — Lighted vs Glowing Splint

Lighted splint (with a flame) = test for hydrogen (squeaky pop). Glowing splint (ember, no flame) = test for oxygen (relights). If you mix these up, you lose the mark. Remember: hydrogen is flammable (it pops when the flame ignites it), oxygen supports burning (it makes the ember relight).

!
Don't Confuse — Limewater

Limewater is a solution of calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂. It is NOT lime juice. The reaction with CO₂ is: CO₂(g) + Ca(OH)₂(aq) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l). The white precipitate of calcium carbonate makes it go cloudy. This equation comes up in exam questions.

?
Why This Matters

Gas tests appear on almost every IGCSE Chemistry paper. You might be asked: "How would you confirm the gas produced is hydrogen?" or "A gas turns limewater milky. Name the gas." These are often worth 1–2 marks each, and there can be several of them across the paper. Memorise the table above — it's worth easy marks every single year.

T
Study Tip — Mnemonics for Gas Tests

Make flashcards with the gas on one side and the test + result on the other. Test yourself until you can recall all six instantly. A useful memory trick: Hydrogen = Hear it (squeaky pop). Oxygen = Oh! It relights. CO₂ = Cloudy limewater. Ammonia = turns litmus Alkaline (blue). Cl₂ = bleaches. SO₂ = decolourises (potassium manganate goes See-through).

  • I can state how to prepare CO₂, H₂, and O₂ in the lab
  • I can name four methods of gas collection and when to use each
  • I can describe the test and positive result for ALL six gases from memory
  • I know the difference between a lighted and a glowing splint
  • I know that limewater is calcium hydroxide solution
Section 19.4 Testing for Cations (Positive Ions)
Salts are made of positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions). To identify the cation, you can use flame tests or chemical tests with sodium hydroxide / ammonia. Remember: CAP — Cations Are Positive.
W
Write This Down — Flame Tests (MEMORISE THIS TABLE)
Cation Flame Colour
Lithium, Li⁺ Red
Sodium, Na⁺ Yellow
Potassium, K⁺ Lilac
Calcium, Ca²⁺ Orange-red
Barium, Ba²⁺ Light green
Copper(II), Cu²⁺ Blue-green

How to do a flame test: (1) Clean a platinum/nichrome wire by dipping in concentrated HCl and holding in a hot Bunsen flame. (2) Dip the clean wire in acid, then into the solid salt. (3) Hold in the clear part of a blue Bunsen flame and observe the colour.

W
Write This Down — Chemical Tests for Cations Using NaOH and Ammonia
Cation With Dilute NaOH With Aqueous Ammonia
Ammonium, NH₄⁺ Add NaOH, heat gently → ammonia gas given off (turns damp red litmus blue) —
Iron(II), Fe²⁺ Pale green precipitate. Excess NaOH: no change. Pale green precipitate. Excess: no change.
Iron(III), Fe³⁺ Red-brown precipitate. Excess NaOH: no change. Red-brown precipitate. Excess: no change.
Zinc, Zn²⁺ White precipitate. Dissolves in excess NaOH (amphoteric). White precipitate. Dissolves in excess ammonia.
Aluminium, Al³⁺ White precipitate. Dissolves in excess NaOH (amphoteric). White precipitate. Does NOT dissolve in excess ammonia.
Calcium, Ca²⁺ White precipitate. Excess NaOH: no change. Very slight white precipitate or none.
Copper(II), Cu²⁺ Pale blue precipitate. Excess NaOH: no change. Pale blue precipitate. Dissolves in excess ammonia → deep blue solution.
Chromium(III), Cr³⁺ Green precipitate. Dissolves in excess NaOH. Grey-green precipitate. Excess: no change.
!
Don't Confuse — Al³⁺ vs Zn²⁺

Both give a white precipitate with NaOH that dissolves in excess NaOH. So NaOH alone cannot tell them apart! To distinguish them: test with aqueous ammonia. Zinc's white precipitate dissolves in excess ammonia. Aluminium's does NOT. This is the most common "distinguish between two cations" question on the exam.

!
Don't Confuse — Iron(II) vs Iron(III)

Fe²⁺ gives a pale green precipitate (think: FeII = green). Fe³⁺ gives a red-brown precipitate (think: rusty iron = brown). Colour is the only difference — neither dissolves in excess.

?
Why This Matters

Cation tests appear every year on Paper 3 and Paper 6. The exam gives you observations like "A white precipitate forms which dissolves in excess sodium hydroxide" and you must identify the cation. If you know the table, it's quick marks. If you don't, you're guessing. Potassium is the tricky one: it does NOT form a precipitate with NaOH or ammonia, so you must use a flame test (lilac) to identify it.

T
Study Tip — Colour-Code Your Notes

Make a set of flashcards, one per cation. On each card write the cation, its precipitate colour with NaOH, whether it dissolves in excess, and what happens with ammonia. Use coloured pens: green for Fe²⁺, brown for Fe³⁺, blue for Cu²⁺. The colours are the key to getting these right. Test yourself until you can do all eight from memory.

  • I can list the six flame test colours from memory
  • I can describe the procedure for a flame test
  • I know the precipitate colour for each cation with NaOH
  • I can distinguish Al³⁺ from Zn²⁺ using ammonia
  • I can test for ammonium ions (heat with NaOH, test gas with damp red litmus)
  • I know that potassium must be identified by flame test (no precipitate)
Section 19.5 Testing for Anions (Negative Ions)
Anions are negative ions — they'd go to the anode (+) during electrolysis. Remember: AN = Anions are Negative. Tests for anions work by either producing a gas or forming a precipitate.
W
Write This Down — Anion Tests (MEMORISE THIS TABLE)
Anion Test Positive Result
Chloride, Cl⁻ Add dilute nitric acid, then silver nitrate solution White precipitate (AgCl)
Bromide, Br⁻ Add dilute nitric acid, then silver nitrate solution Cream precipitate (AgBr)
Iodide, I⁻ Add dilute nitric acid, then silver nitrate solution Yellow precipitate (AgI)
Sulfate, SO₄²⁻ Add dilute nitric acid, then barium nitrate solution White precipitate (BaSO₄)
Sulfite, SO₃²⁻ Add acidified potassium manganate(VII) solution Purple colour changes to colourless
Nitrate, NO₃⁻ Add dilute NaOH + aluminium foil, heat gently Ammonia gas given off (turns damp red litmus blue)
Carbonate, CO₃²⁻ Add dilute hydrochloric acid Fizzes; gas turns limewater milky (CO₂)
!
Don't Confuse — Halide Precipitate Colours

All three halide tests use silver nitrate, but the precipitate colours are different: Cl⁻ = white, Br⁻ = cream, I⁻ = yellow. Think: they get darker as you go down Group VII (white → cream → yellow). Also, you must add nitric acid first to remove any carbonate or sulfate ions that would also form precipitates with silver and confuse the result.

!
Don't Confuse — Sulfate vs Sulfite

Sulfate (SO₄²⁻) has 4 oxygen atoms; test with barium nitrate → white precipitate. Sulfite (SO₃²⁻) has 3 oxygen atoms; test with acidified potassium manganate(VII) → purple turns colourless. Different tests, different anions. Sulfates are salts of sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄); sulfites are salts of sulfurous acid (H₂SO₃).

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves questions like: "A student adds dilute nitric acid and silver nitrate to a solution. A cream precipitate forms. Identify the anion." (Answer: bromide.) Or: "A solid fizzes when acid is added and the gas turns limewater milky. Name the anion." (Answer: carbonate.) These are 1–2 marks each, and they appear regularly. The carbonate test is especially common because it combines an anion test with a gas test.

T
Study Tip — Group by Method

There are really only three "methods" to learn:
1. Silver nitrate → tests for halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) — remember the colours.
2. Barium nitrate → tests for sulfate — white precipitate.
3. Acid → tests for carbonate (fizzes + CO₂).
Then the special ones: sulfite (KMnO₄ decolourises) and nitrate (NaOH + Al → ammonia). Organising them into groups makes the table much easier to memorise.

  • I can describe the test for all three halide ions and know their precipitate colours
  • I can describe the test for sulfate ions
  • I can describe the test for carbonate ions (including the gas test)
  • I know the test for sulfite and nitrate ions
  • I can explain why nitric acid is added before testing for halides
  • I understand the difference between sulfate and sulfite
