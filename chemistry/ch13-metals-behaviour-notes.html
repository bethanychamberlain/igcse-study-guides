Section 13.1 Comparing Metals and Non-Metals
Over 80% of the elements in the Periodic Table are metals. They sit to the left of the zig-zag line; non-metals sit to the right. Their physical and chemical properties are very different — and the exam loves asking you to compare them.

Malleability Malleable — can be hammered into shape Not malleable

W
Write This Down — Chemical Properties

Metals:
• React with dilute acids to form salts + hydrogen gas
• React with oxygen to form oxides, which are bases (they neutralise acids)
• Form positive ions when they react → their compounds are ionic

Non-metals:
• React with oxygen to form acidic oxides (dissolve in water to give acidic solutions)
• React with metals to form ionic compounds (the non-metal gains electrons, forming negative ions)
• React with each other to form covalent compounds (e.g. CO₂)

!
Don't Confuse — Carbon Is a Weirdo

Carbon is a non-metal but its two forms — diamond and graphite — have giant structures, so they don't behave like typical non-metals. Diamond is extremely hard with a very high melting point. Graphite conducts electricity. The exam loves asking "Give an exception" — carbon is the answer.

?
Why This Matters

The comparison table is a favourite source of easy exam marks. "Give three differences between metals and non-metals" is worth 3 quick marks if you know the table. Mercury (liquid metal) and graphite (conducting non-metal) are the two classic exceptions they test.

T
Study Tip — Learn the Key Words

The four vocabulary words they always test: malleable (hammered into shape), ductile (drawn into wires), sonorous (rings when struck), high density (heavy for their size). Say them out loud with the definitions until they're automatic.

  • I can define malleable, ductile, and sonorous
  • I can list at least four physical differences between metals and non-metals
  • I can describe how metal oxides differ from non-metal oxides
  • I can name exceptions: mercury (liquid metal), graphite (conducts electricity)
Section 13.2 Comparing Metals for Reactivity
You can rank metals by how vigorously they react. The three key tests: reaction with water, reaction with dilute acid, and reaction with oxygen. The more reactive the metal, the faster and more violent the reaction.
W
Write This Down — Metals + Water
Metal Reaction with Water Products
Potassium Very violent with cold water; catches fire Hydrogen + potassium hydroxide, KOH
Sodium Violent with cold water; whizzes on surface Hydrogen + sodium hydroxide, NaOH
Calcium Less violent with cold water; bubbles Hydrogen + calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂
Magnesium Very slow with cold water; vigorous with steam Hydrogen + magnesium oxide, MgO
Zinc Quite slow with steam Hydrogen + zinc oxide, ZnO
Iron Slow with steam Hydrogen + iron oxide, Fe₂O₃
Copper No reaction —

Key pattern: The top three (K, Na, Ca) react with cold water and make hydroxides. The others need steam and make oxides. Copper does nothing.

W
Write This Down — Metals + Dilute Hydrochloric Acid

• Magnesium: vigorous → MgCl₂ + H₂
• Zinc: quite slow → ZnCl₂ + H₂
• Iron: slow → FeCl₂ + H₂
• Copper, silver, gold: no reaction, even with concentrated acid

General equation: metal + acid → salt + hydrogen

W
Write This Down — Displacement of Hydrogen

When a metal reacts with water or acid, it displaces hydrogen — it pushes hydrogen out and takes its place in the compound. This means the metal is more reactive than hydrogen. Copper, silver, and gold do NOT displace hydrogen, so they are less reactive than hydrogen.

!
Don't Confuse — Hydroxides vs Oxides

When metals react with cold water, they produce metal hydroxides (e.g. NaOH). When metals react with steam, they produce metal oxides (e.g. MgO). The exam will check whether you know which product goes with which reaction. Only the most reactive metals (K, Na, Ca) make hydroxides with water.

?
Why This Matters

Reactivity is one of the biggest topics in IGCSE Chemistry. The exam will describe a reaction (e.g. "Metal X reacts slowly with steam but not with cold water") and ask you to identify or rank the metal. If you know the tables above, you can answer these instantly.

T
Study Tip — Practise the Equations

Write out the equations for sodium + water, magnesium + steam, and magnesium + HCl from memory. These three are the most commonly tested. If you can write them with correct formulae and balancing, you're ahead of most students.

  • I can rank K, Na, Ca, Mg, Zn, Fe, Cu by how they react with water
  • I know which metals make hydroxides (cold water) vs oxides (steam)
  • I can write the word equation: metal + acid → salt + hydrogen
  • I can explain what "displacement of hydrogen" means
  • I can write balanced equations for Na + water and Mg + HCl
Section 13.3 Metals in Competition (Displacement Reactions)
A more reactive metal will push a less reactive metal out of its compound. This is called a displacement reaction. It works with metal oxides (heated) and with solutions of metal salts.
W
Write This Down — Competing with Carbon

Carbon is a non-metal, but it sits between aluminium and zinc in the reactivity series. This means:

• Carbon can reduce (remove oxygen from) the oxides of zinc, iron, and copper
• Carbon cannot reduce the oxides of magnesium or aluminium (they are above it)

Example: 2CuO + C → 2Cu + CO₂
Carbon reduces copper(II) oxide to copper. The carbon is oxidised (gains oxygen), and the copper oxide is reduced (loses oxygen).

W
Write This Down — Competing for Oxygen

A more reactive metal will grab oxygen from the oxide of a less reactive metal:

Example: Fe + CuO → FeO + Cu
Iron takes the oxygen from copper(II) oxide. Iron is acting as a reducing agent (it removes oxygen from the copper oxide). The reaction is exothermic — it gives out heat.

Rule: A metal will reduce the oxide of any metal below it in the reactivity series.

W
Write This Down — Competing in Solution

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from a solution of its salt:

Example: Fe + CuSO₄ (aq) → FeSO₄ (aq) + Cu
An iron nail placed in blue copper(II) sulfate solution. The iron pushes copper out. You see: copper coats the nail, the blue solution turns pale green (iron(II) ions).

Rule: A metal displaces a less reactive metal from solutions of its compounds.

!
Don't Confuse — Which Way Does Displacement Go?

Displacement only works downhill in the reactivity series. Iron displaces copper (iron is above copper). But copper will NOT displace iron — nothing happens. If the exam asks "Will copper react with iron(II) sulfate solution?" the answer is no, because copper is less reactive than iron.

?
Why This Matters

Displacement reactions are one of your weakest areas from past exams. The exam gives you two substances and asks "Will they react?" You must compare their positions in the reactivity series. Higher beats lower = reaction. Lower cannot beat higher = no reaction. Simple once you know the series.

T
Study Tip — Think of It Like a Fight

The more reactive metal always wins the "fight" for the compound. Iron vs copper? Iron wins — it kicks copper out. Zinc vs iron? Zinc wins. If someone asks "Will X displace Y?" just check who is higher in the reactivity series. Higher = stronger = wins.

  • I can explain why carbon reduces CuO but not MgO
  • I can write the equation for iron + copper(II) oxide
  • I can write the equation for iron + copper(II) sulfate solution
  • I can predict whether two substances will react using the reactivity series
  • I can describe what you would SEE when iron is placed in CuSO₄ solution
Section 13.4 The Reactivity Series
The reactivity series is a list of metals in order of how easily they give up electrons to form positive ions. You need to memorise it. All the displacement, extraction, and rusting questions depend on it.

W
Write This Down — Key Points About the Series

• The reactivity series is a list of metals ordered by their drive to give up electrons and form positive ions. The easier this is, the more reactive the metal.
• More reactive metals have more stable compounds that are harder to break down.
• More reactive metals are harder to extract from their ores (need electrolysis).
• Less reactive metals (Cu, Ag, Au) are found as elements in nature, not compounds.
• Thermal decomposition: Carbonates of less reactive metals break down on heating (e.g. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂), but sodium and potassium carbonates do NOT decompose on heating.

W
Write This Down — The Aluminium Puzzle

Aluminium is high in the reactivity series (above zinc and iron), yet it doesn't corrode like iron does. Why? Because aluminium reacts instantly with oxygen in the air to form a very thin layer of aluminium oxide on its surface. This layer is tough, sticks tightly, and acts as a barrier that prevents further corrosion. That's why we use aluminium for ladders, window frames, and aerials without painting it.

!
Don't Confuse — "Aluminium Doesn't React" Is WRONG

Students often think aluminium is unreactive because it doesn't seem to corrode. But it IS reactive — it has already reacted with oxygen. The oxide layer just protects it from further reaction. If you scratch through the oxide layer, aluminium reacts vigorously. The thermit process (aluminium + iron(III) oxide) is so exothermic it produces molten iron!

Section 13.5 The Rusting of Iron
Iron is the most widely used metal, but it has a big problem: it rusts. Rusting costs billions of dollars a year worldwide. You need to know what causes it, the equation, the experiment that proves it, and how to prevent it.
W
Write This Down — What Causes Rusting

Rusting requires both oxygen AND water. If either one is missing, iron will not rust.

The equation:
iron + oxygen + water → hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust)
4Fe + 3O₂ + 4H₂O → 2Fe₂O₃.2H₂O

Salt water speeds up rusting because the salt water conducts electricity, helping electrons move from the iron more easily.

W
Write This Down — The Three Test-Tube Experiment

This is the classic experiment that proves both oxygen and water are needed:

• Tube 1: Iron nail in dry air (calcium chloride dries the air) → no rust
• Tube 2: Iron nail in boiled water with oil on top (no air) → no rust
• Tube 3: Iron nail in air and water (both present) → rust forms

Conclusion: Both oxygen and water must be present for rusting to occur.

W
Write This Down — Preventing Rust (Two Approaches)

Approach 1 — Barrier methods (keep out oxygen and water):
• Painting (bridges, railings)
• Greasing / oiling (tools, machine parts)
• Plastic coating (outdoor chairs)
• Galvanising — coating with zinc (roofing, car bodies)
• Tin plating (food tins)

Approach 2 — Sacrificial protection:
Attach a block of a more reactive metal (usually magnesium or zinc) to the iron. The more reactive metal has a stronger drive to lose electrons, so it corrodes instead of the iron. The magnesium or zinc is "sacrificed" to protect the iron. It must be replaced when it dissolves.

Galvanising is special — it provides both barrier protection (the zinc layer keeps out air and water) AND sacrificial protection (if the zinc coat is scratched, the zinc still protects the iron because zinc is more reactive).

!
Don't Confuse — Barrier vs Sacrificial

Painting is only a barrier. If the paint chips, the iron underneath rusts immediately because paint is not a reactive metal. Galvanising (zinc) still works even if scratched, because the zinc corrodes sacrificially. The exam will ask "Why does galvanised iron not rust even when scratched?" — you must explain the sacrificial protection part.

?
Why This Matters

Rusting is a major real-world application question. Ships have magnesium blocks welded to their hulls. Oil rigs have zinc bars attached to their legs. Cars are galvanised. The exam tests whether you can explain why these work, not just name them. Always link back to the reactivity series: the sacrificial metal must be more reactive than iron.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Three Tubes

Sketch the three test-tube experiment from memory. Label each tube: what's in it, what's excluded, and the result. If you can draw and explain all three, you'll handle any rusting question. Also, write out the word equation for rusting and the two prevention approaches as a mini revision card.

  • I can write the word equation for rusting
  • I can describe the three test-tube experiment and explain the results
  • I can name four barrier methods of rust prevention
  • I can explain sacrificial protection using the reactivity series
