Section 9.1 Introducing Reaction Rates
Some reactions are fast (like a precipitation or an explosion) and some are slow (like rusting or concrete setting). In chemistry, we need to know exactly how fast a reaction is going — that means measuring its rate.
W
Write This Down — Definition of Rate

Rate is a measure of the change that happens in a single unit of time. For a chemical reaction, the rate tells you how fast reactants are used up or how fast products are formed.

W
Write This Down — Two Ways to Measure Rate

To find the rate of a reaction, you measure either:
• the amount of a reactant used up per unit of time, or
• the amount of a product formed per unit of time.

Example: Zinc reacts with sulfuric acid:
Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂
You could measure the rate by tracking the volume of hydrogen gas produced per minute, or the mass of zinc used up per minute.

?
Why This Matters

In factories, knowing the rate of a reaction tells you how long it will take to make your product and how much it will cost. On the exam, they often ask "suggest how you could measure the rate of this reaction" — you need to identify a reactant or product whose amount you can easily track (usually a gas you can collect, or a mass you can weigh).

T
Study Tip — Units Matter

Rate always has "something per time" as its unit. Common examples: cm³ per minute, grams per second, grams per minute. If the exam says "state the units for rate," think "amount ÷ time."

  • I can define rate as "the change that happens in a single unit of time"
  • I know the two ways to measure the rate of a chemical reaction
  • I can give examples of fast and slow reactions
  • I can suggest suitable units for rate of reaction
Section 9.2 Measuring the Rate of a Reaction
The key experiment: magnesium reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to produce hydrogen gas. You collect the gas in a syringe and record the volume at regular intervals, then plot a graph of the results.
W
Write This Down — The Gas Syringe Method

Reaction: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂

Method: (1) Put dilute HCl in a flask. (2) Drop in the magnesium. (3) Insert the stopper and gas syringe immediately. (4) Start the clock. (5) Record the volume of hydrogen in the syringe at regular intervals (e.g. every 30 seconds).

W
Write This Down — Reading the Rate Graph

When you plot volume of gas (y-axis) against time (x-axis), the graph is a curve that levels off. Three key things to remember:

• Steep curve = fast reaction. The steeper the line, the faster the rate at that moment.
• Rate decreases over time. The curve gets less steep as reactants are used up.
• Flat line = reaction over. When the curve goes flat, no more gas is being produced — the reaction is complete.

W
Write This Down — Average Rate Calculation

Average rate = total amount of product ÷ total time for the reaction

Worked example: 40 cm³ of hydrogen is produced in 5 minutes.
Average rate = 40 ÷ 5 = 8 cm³ of hydrogen per minute.

!
Don't Confuse — Rate at a Moment vs Average Rate

The average rate is total product ÷ total time for the whole reaction. The rate at any given moment is found by looking at how steep the curve is at that point. In the first minute the rate is highest (steepest). By the end, the rate is zero (flat). The exam may ask for either, so read the question carefully.

?
Why This Matters

Graph interpretation is one of the most common exam questions for this chapter. You will be given a graph and asked: "How can you tell the reaction is over?" (the curve goes flat), "When is the reaction fastest?" (at the start, where the curve is steepest), or "Calculate the rate during the second minute" (read off the volumes and subtract).

T
Study Tip — Practice Reading Graphs

Take the data from p.104 of your textbook and try these: (1) How much gas was produced in the first minute? (14 cm³). (2) How much gas in the second minute? (25 − 14 = 11 cm³). (3) When did the reaction finish? (about 5 minutes, when volume stops increasing). Being able to read values off a curve quickly is a skill that takes practice.

  • I can describe the gas syringe experiment step by step
  • I know what a steep curve means vs a flat line on a rate graph
  • I can calculate the average rate of a reaction
  • I can calculate the rate during a specific time interval from a graph
Section 9.3 Changing the Rate (Part I) — Concentration and Temperature
There are four main factors that affect the rate of a reaction: concentration, temperature, surface area, and catalysts. This section covers the first two.
W
Write This Down — Factor 1: Concentration

A reaction goes faster when the concentration of a reactant is increased.

Experiment: React magnesium with two different concentrations of HCl (same mass of Mg, same volume of acid, but acid B is twice as concentrated as acid A). Collect hydrogen gas and plot both on the same graph.

Results:
• Curve B is steeper → reaction is faster
• Both produce the same total volume of H₂ (same amount of Mg)
• B finishes sooner (60 s vs 120 s)
• Doubling the concentration doubled the average rate

W
Write This Down — Factor 2: Temperature

A reaction goes faster when the temperature is raised. The rate generally doubles for every 10°C increase.

Experiment (disappearing cross): Mix sodium thiosulfate solution with HCl on top of a paper cross. A yellow precipitate of sulfur forms, making the cross disappear. Repeat at different temperatures.

Temperature / °C 20 30 40 50 60
Time for cross to disappear / s 200 125 50 33 24

At 40°C the reaction was four times faster than at 20°C. Higher temperature = faster reaction.

!
Don't Confuse — Fair Tests and Variables

When testing the effect of concentration, you must keep everything else the same (temperature, mass of solid, volume of solution). The thing you change is the independent variable. The thing you measure is the dependent variable. Everything else must be controlled. The exam loves asking "What must be kept the same for a fair test?"

?
Why This Matters

Real-life examples: food cooks faster in a pressure cooker (higher temperature). Putting food in the fridge slows the reactions that make it rot (lower temperature). These are the examples the textbook gives, and they're the kind the exam likes too.

T
Study Tip — Sketch Two Curves

Practice drawing two curves on the same axes where one reaction is faster: the faster one is steeper but both level off at the same final volume (because the same amount of reactant was used). This "same height, different steepness" pattern shows up constantly on exams. Be able to draw it from memory.

  • I can state the effect of increasing concentration on rate
  • I can describe the disappearing cross experiment for temperature
  • I know the "rate doubles for every 10°C" rule of thumb
  • I can identify independent, dependent, and controlled variables in a rate experiment
Section 9.4 Changing the Rate (Part II) — Surface Area and Pressure
Two more factors: increasing the surface area of a solid reactant speeds things up, and increasing the pressure of gaseous reactants does the same.
W
Write This Down — Factor 3: Surface Area

A reaction goes faster when the surface area of a solid reactant is increased.

Experiment: React marble chips (CaCO₃) with HCl. Measure the loss of mass as CO₂ escapes (using a balance).
CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

Repeat with the same mass of marble, but as small chips instead of large chips (greater surface area). Results:
• Curve for small chips is steeper → reaction is faster
• Both give the same final loss of mass (2.0 g of CO₂)
• Small chips finish about 2 minutes sooner

W
Write This Down — Factor 4: Pressure (Gases Only)

A reaction involving gases goes faster when the pressure is increased.

Increasing the pressure squeezes the gas into a smaller volume. This increases the concentration of gas particles (more particles per unit volume), which has the same effect as increasing concentration in a solution — the reaction speeds up.

Important: Pressure only affects reactions involving gases. It has no effect on reactions between two solids or between liquids.

!
Don't Confuse — Surface Area vs Mass

Smaller pieces have a greater surface area, but the total mass stays the same. That's why both experiments produce the same total amount of product. The small chips just get there faster because more surface is exposed. The exam might ask: "Why is the final mass loss the same?" Answer: same mass of CaCO₃ was used in both experiments.

?
Why This Matters — Explosions!

Fine powders (flour dust, coal dust, wood dust) have a huge surface area. If they catch fire from a spark, the reaction is so fast it causes an explosion. This is why flour mills and coal mines have strict safety rules. Fireworks use powdered metals for the same reason — maximum surface area means maximum speed of reaction.

T
Study Tip — The Four Factors

Memorise the four factors that increase rate: (1) increase concentration, (2) increase temperature, (3) increase surface area of solids, (4) increase pressure of gases. Plus there's a fifth: add a catalyst (next sections). A useful mnemonic: Cats That Scratch People + Catalyst (Concentration, Temperature, Surface area, Pressure, Catalyst).

  • I can describe the marble chips experiment for surface area
  • I can explain why small chips react faster but produce the same total product
  • I can explain how increasing pressure affects gas reactions
  • I know that pressure has no effect on reactions between solids or liquids
Section 9.5 Explaining Rate Changes — Collision Theory
So far you know what affects the rate. Now you need to explain why. The answer is collision theory — and this is the part that earns the big marks on the exam.
W
Write This Down — Collision Theory (The Big Idea)

For a reaction to occur, two things must happen:
• The particles must collide with each other
• The collision must have enough energy to be successful

The minimum energy a collision needs for reaction to occur is called the activation energy (Eₐ). If the collision doesn't have enough energy, the particles just bounce apart — no reaction.

The rate of a reaction depends on how many successful collisions there are in a given unit of time.

W
Write This Down — Copy This Table Into Your Notes
Factor Why It Increases the Rate
Concentration More particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → more successful collisions per unit of time
Temperature Two reasons: (1) particles move faster → collide more frequently, and (2) more particles have energy ≥ activation energy → a greater proportion of collisions are successful
Surface area More particles of the solid are exposed to the other reactant → more frequent successful collisions
Gas pressure Gas is squeezed into smaller volume → more particles per unit volume (higher concentration) → more frequent successful collisions
!
Don't Confuse — Temperature Has TWO Reasons

Most students only give one reason for why temperature increases rate ("particles move faster"). But there are two reasons: (1) particles collide more frequently and (2) more collisions have enough energy to be successful (i.e. more particles exceed the activation energy). The exam often asks for both — if you only give one, you lose a mark.

?
Why This Matters

Collision theory is the most heavily tested part of this chapter on the extended paper. The exam will describe a scenario (like "the acid was heated" or "the metal was ground to a powder") and ask you to explain the effect on rate "in terms of particles" or "using collision theory." You must mention collisions, frequency, and successful/activation energy to get full marks.

T
Study Tip — The Magic Sentence

For any "explain using collision theory" question, use this template: "Increasing [factor] causes [what happens to particles]. This means the frequency of collisions increases, and so the frequency of successful collisions increases, so the rate increases." Practice writing this sentence for each of the four factors until it's automatic.

  • I can state the two conditions needed for a successful collision
  • I can define activation energy
  • I can explain using collision theory why concentration increases rate
  • I can give BOTH reasons why temperature increases rate
  • I can explain using collision theory why surface area increases rate
  • I can explain using collision theory why gas pressure increases rate
Section 9.6 Catalysts
A catalyst is the fifth factor that affects rate — and it works differently from the other four. Instead of making particles collide more often, it gives them an easier route to react.
W
Write This Down — Definition of a Catalyst

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction and is unchanged at the end of the reaction.

Because it is unchanged, it can be reused. You only need a small amount. If you remove the catalyst before the reaction is complete, the reaction slows down again.

W
Write This Down — How Catalysts Work (Activation Energy)

A catalyst provides an alternative pathway for the reaction that has a lower activation energy.

Because the activation energy is lower, more particles now have enough energy for a successful collision. So more collisions are successful, and the rate increases.

On a reaction pathway diagram: the curve with a catalyst has a lower "hump" (lower Eₐ) than the curve without. The reactants and products are at the same energy levels — only the hump changes.

W
Write This Down — Key Example

Decomposition of hydrogen peroxide:
2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂

Without a catalyst, this reaction is very slow. Adding manganese(IV) oxide (MnO₂) makes it thousands of times faster. The oxygen produced relights a glowing splint.

Enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins made by living cells. Example: catalase (found in raw liver) also speeds up the decomposition of H₂O₂. Enzymes are essential for life — without them, body reactions would be far too slow at 37°C.

!
Don't Confuse — What a Catalyst Does NOT Do

A catalyst does not change the total amount of product formed. It does not get used up. It does not change what products are made. It only makes the reaction faster. Also: a catalyst is specific — MnO₂ catalyses the decomposition of H₂O₂ but might not work for other reactions.

?
Why This Matters

Catalysts are hugely important in industry. They save time and energy (reactions can run at lower temperatures, which means lower fuel bills). Many industrial catalysts are transition metals or their oxides. The exam may ask you to explain why catalysts are important in industry — mention speed, lower temperature, reusability, and cost savings.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Reaction Pathway Diagram

Sketch the energy profile from memory: x-axis = progress of reaction, y-axis = energy. Draw the reactant level, then a big hump (activation energy without catalyst), then the product level. Now draw a second, smaller hump on the same diagram (activation energy with catalyst). Label both humps. This diagram is a classic exam question and easy marks if you practice it.

  • I can define a catalyst in one sentence
  • I can explain how a catalyst works in terms of activation energy
  • I can describe the hydrogen peroxide + MnO₂ experiment
  • I can define enzyme and give an example (catalase)
  • I can draw and label a reaction pathway diagram with and without a catalyst
  • I can explain why catalysts are important in industry
