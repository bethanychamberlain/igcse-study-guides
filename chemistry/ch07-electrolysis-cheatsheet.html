Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Electrolysis Decomposition of an ionic compound (molten or dissolved) by passing an electric current through it. Electrolyte (the liquid being decomposed)
Electrolyte The molten or dissolved ionic compound that is decomposed during electrolysis. Electrolysis (that's the process, not the substance)
Anode The positive electrode. Negative ions (anions) go here. Oxidation happens here. Cathode (negative electrode)
Cathode The negative electrode. Positive ions (cations) go here. Reduction happens here. Anode (positive electrode)
Electrode A rod (usually carbon/graphite or platinum) that carries current into/out of the electrolyte. Usually inert. —
Electroplating Coating an object with a layer of metal using electrolysis. Object = cathode, plating metal = anode. —
Inert electrode An electrode that carries current but does not react (e.g. graphite, platinum). Active electrode (e.g. copper anode that dissolves)

What Conducts Electricity — and How?

Substance Conducts? What Carries the Current?
Metals (solid or liquid) Yes, always Free electrons
Graphite Yes Delocalised electrons
Ionic compound (solid) No Ions locked in lattice — cannot move
Ionic compound (molten) Yes Free ions
Ionic compound (dissolved) Yes Free ions
Molecular substances No (never) No free electrons or ions

Electrolysis Products — The Table You MUST Know

Electrolyte At Cathode (−) At Anode (+)
Molten PbBr₂ Lead (metal) Bromine (non-metal)
Molten NaCl Sodium (metal) Chlorine (non-metal)
Concentrated NaCl solution Hydrogen Chlorine
Dilute NaCl solution Hydrogen Oxygen
Dilute H₂SO₄ Hydrogen Oxygen
Concentrated HCl Hydrogen Chlorine
Dilute CuSO₄ (carbon electrodes) Copper Oxygen
CuSO₄ with copper electrodes Copper deposited Copper dissolves
The Two Rules for Aqueous Solutions
Cathode (−): Metal more reactive than H → hydrogen forms. Metal less reactive than H (e.g. copper) → metal deposits.
Anode (+): Concentrated halide present (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) → halogen forms. No halide or dilute → oxygen forms.

CuSO₄ Electrolysis — Carbon vs Copper Electrodes

Carbon (Inert) Electrodes Copper Electrodes
At cathode Copper deposited Copper deposited (grows larger)
At anode Oxygen gas bubbles off Copper dissolves (anode shrinks)
Blue colour Fades (Cu²⁺ ions removed) Stays same (Cu²⁺ replaced as fast as removed)
Net effect CuSO₄ decomposes Copper moves from anode to cathode

Key Half-Equations to Memorise

Electrolyte Cathode (Reduction) Anode (Oxidation)
Molten PbBr₂ Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻
Concentrated NaCl(aq) 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
Dilute H₂SO₄ 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂ 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻
CuSO₄(aq) with carbon Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻
CuSO₄(aq) with copper Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻

Say It in One Sentence

Electrolysis Using electricity to break down an ionic compound into its elements.
Why it must be liquid The ions must be free to move to carry the current — in a solid they're locked in the lattice.
Anode = Positive PA: Positive = Anode. Negative ions (anions) travel to the anode. Oxidation happens here.
Cathode = Negative Positive ions (cations) travel to the cathode. Reduction happens here. Remember: RAC = Reduction At Cathode.
Electroplating Object to coat = cathode, plating metal = anode, solution of plating metal compound = electrolyte.
Copper purification Impure copper anode dissolves → pure copper deposits on cathode → impurities fall as sludge.

Memory Tricks

PA = Positive Anode
Positive = Anode. Write "PA" at the top of every electrolysis question. If you get anode and cathode backwards, every half-equation will be wrong.
RAC = Reduction At Cathode
Reduction At Cathode. The cathode is negative so it has extra electrons to give away. Ions gain electrons at the cathode = reduction. Ions lose electrons at the anode = oxidation.
Opposites Attract
Positive ions (Na⁺, Cu²⁺) go to the negative electrode (cathode). Negative ions (Cl⁻, OH⁻) go to the positive electrode (anode). Just like magnets — opposite charges attract.
Aqueous Cathode: Reactivity Check
Is the metal above or below hydrogen in the reactivity series? Above H = hydrogen gas (the metal stays dissolved). Below H = metal deposits (copper is the common one). Most metals are above H, so hydrogen is the most common answer.
Aqueous Anode: Halide Check
Is there a concentrated halide (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻)? Yes = halogen gas. No (or dilute) = oxygen. Two questions, two answers. That's all you need.
Electroplating Setup
The thing you want to coat goes at the cathode (metal goes TO the cathode). The plating metal is the anode (it dissolves and goes into solution). The electrolyte is a solution of the plating metal.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Define electrolysis" The decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current
"Why can't solid NaCl be electrolysed?" The ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move to carry the current. They must be free to move
"What is produced at the cathode when you electrolyse concentrated NaCl solution?" Hydrogen gas (Na is more reactive than H, so H⁺ ions are reduced instead). Half-equation: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂
"What is produced at the anode when you electrolyse concentrated NaCl solution?" Chlorine gas (concentrated halide). Half-equation: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
"What happens at the anode when dilute NaCl solution is electrolysed?" Oxygen forms (not enough Cl⁻ when dilute). Half-equation: 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻
"Write the half-equations for molten PbBr₂" Cathode: Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb. Anode: 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻
"Why does the blue colour fade when CuSO₄ is electrolysed with carbon electrodes?" Cu²⁺ ions are being removed from solution (deposited at cathode), so the blue colour fades
"Why does the blue colour stay the same with copper electrodes?" Cu²⁺ ions are replaced by the copper anode dissolving at the same rate as they are removed at the cathode
"Describe the set-up for electroplating a spoon with silver" Cathode = spoon (object to be coated). Anode = pure silver (plating metal). Electrolyte = silver nitrate solution (soluble compound of silver)
"How is copper purified by electrolysis?" Anode = impure copper. Cathode = pure copper. Electrolyte = dilute CuSO₄. Impure copper dissolves from anode, pure copper deposits on cathode. Impurities fall as sludge
"What carries the current in the wires vs in the electrolyte?" In the wires and electrodes: electrons. In the electrolyte: ions
