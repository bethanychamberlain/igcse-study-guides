Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State the test and positive result for each gas: (a) hydrogen, (b) oxygen, (c) carbon dioxide.
3 marks
Answer
(a) Hydrogen: Hold a lighted splint to the gas — it burns with a squeaky pop [1].
(b) Oxygen: Hold a glowing splint to the gas — the splint relights [1].
(c) Carbon dioxide: Bubble the gas through limewater — the limewater turns cloudy/milky [1].
Lighted = has a flame (hydrogen). Glowing = ember only (oxygen). These three are tested the most — know them perfectly.

2
What flame colour would you see for each of these cations? (a) sodium, (b) potassium, (c) copper(II).
3 marks
Answer
(a) Sodium: yellow [1].
(b) Potassium: lilac [1].
(c) Copper(II): blue-green [1].
If you wrote "purple" instead of "lilac" for potassium, you'd likely still get the mark — but "lilac" is the textbook term. "Green" alone for copper is not enough — it's "blue-green."

3
In an experiment, a student investigates the effect of temperature on the rate of a reaction. (a) What is the independent variable? (b) What is the dependent variable?
2 marks
Answer
(a) The independent variable is the temperature (what the student changes) [1].
(b) The dependent variable is the rate of reaction (what is measured) [1].
Independent = what you change. Dependent = what you measure. If the question says "the effect of A on B," then A is independent and B is dependent.

4
A solution is tested with dilute nitric acid and silver nitrate solution. A cream-coloured precipitate forms. Name the anion present.
1 mark
Answer
Bromide ions, Br⁻ [1].
White = chloride. Cream = bromide. Yellow = iodide. The colour tells you which halide.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Describe how you would prepare and collect a sample of carbon dioxide gas in the lab. Name the reactants, state the equation, and explain your choice of collection method.
4 marks
Answer
Reactants: Calcium carbonate (marble chips) and dilute hydrochloric acid [1].
Equation: CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) [1].
Collection: Collect by upward displacement of air (or over water) [1], because CO₂ is denser/heavier than air (so it sinks and pushes air up and out of the gas jar) [1].
You could also collect over water because CO2 is only sparingly soluble. Either collection method with a correct reason gets the mark.

6
A student adds aqueous sodium hydroxide to a solution and observes a pale blue precipitate. (a) Identify the cation. (b) Describe what would happen if aqueous ammonia were added to the same solution in excess.
3 marks
Answer
(a) The cation is copper(II), Cu²⁺ [1]. (A pale blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide, Cu(OH)₂, forms.)
(b) With aqueous ammonia, a pale blue precipitate would first form [1], but in excess ammonia it would dissolve to give a deep blue solution [1].
This deep blue solution (a copper-ammonia complex ion) is unique to copper(II). It's a distinguishing feature the exam exploits.

7
A white solid fizzes when dilute hydrochloric acid is added. The gas produced turns limewater milky. (a) Name the gas. (b) Name the anion in the solid. (c) A flame test on the solid gives a lilac flame. Identify the solid.
3 marks
Answer
(a) The gas is carbon dioxide [1].
(b) The anion is carbonate, CO₃²⁻ [1].
(c) Lilac flame = potassium. The solid is potassium carbonate, K₂CO₃ [1].
This is a classic "identify the unknown" question that combines three tests: acid test (gas), gas test (limewater), and flame test. You use all three to narrow it down. Practice working through these step by step.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A student is given two solutions. One contains aluminium ions (Al³⁺) and the other contains zinc ions (Zn²⁺). Both give a white precipitate when dilute aqueous sodium hydroxide is added, and both dissolve in excess sodium hydroxide. Describe what further test the student should carry out to tell them apart, and state the expected results for each solution.
5 marks
Answer
Add aqueous ammonia to each solution [1].

Zinc ions: A white precipitate forms [1], which dissolves in excess ammonia to give a colourless solution [1].

Aluminium ions: A white precipitate forms [1], which does NOT dissolve in excess ammonia [1].
This is the single most important "distinguish between two cations" question. Both dissolve in excess NaOH (both are amphoteric), so NaOH cannot tell them apart. Ammonia is the deciding test. Zinc dissolves in excess ammonia; aluminium does not.

9
An unknown white solid food preservative may be potassium sulfite. Describe the tests you would carry out to confirm: (a) the cation is potassium, and (b) the anion is sulfite. Give the expected results for each test.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Test for potassium: Carry out a flame test [1]. Dip a clean wire in concentrated HCl, then into the solid, and hold in a blue Bunsen flame [1]. If potassium is present, the flame will be lilac [1].

(b) Test for sulfite: Add a small amount of acidified potassium manganate(VII) solution to a solution of the solid [1]. If sulfite ions are present, the purple colour will change to colourless [1].
Potassium cannot be tested by precipitation (NaOH and ammonia don't form precipitates with K+), so flame test is the only option. For sulfite, acidified KMnO4 is the specific reagent — don't confuse with the sulfate test (barium nitrate).

10
A solution of a metal chloride is tested. Aqueous sodium hydroxide is added to one portion: a white precipitate forms that dissolves in excess sodium hydroxide. Aqueous ammonia is added to another portion: a white precipitate forms that dissolves in excess ammonia. (a) Identify the cation. Explain your reasoning. (b) Describe the test and result you would expect to confirm the anion is chloride. (c) Give the ionic equation for the halide test.
6 marks
Answer
(a) The cation is zinc, Zn²⁺ [1]. Both aluminium and zinc give a white precipitate that dissolves in excess NaOH (both are amphoteric) [1]. However, aluminium's precipitate does NOT dissolve in excess ammonia, while zinc's does. Since the precipitate dissolves in excess ammonia, the cation must be zinc [1].

(b) Add dilute nitric acid followed by silver nitrate solution to a portion of the solution [1]. A white precipitate (of silver chloride, AgCl) confirms the presence of chloride ions [1].

(c) Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) [1].
This question tests both cation and anion identification in one go. The reasoning for zinc vs aluminium (excess ammonia) is the critical step. The ionic equation is a bonus mark — make sure you include state symbols.
