Section 8.1 Probability of an Event
Probability measures how likely something is to happen, using a number between 0 and 1. A probability of 0 means impossible. A probability of 1 means certain. Everything else is somewhere in between.
W
Write This Down — The Probability Scale

Probability is measured on a scale from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

0 — impossible   |   0.25 — unlikely   |   0.5 — even chance   |   0.75 — likely   |   1 — certain

Probability can be written as a fraction, a decimal, or a percentage.

W
Write This Down — The Key Formula

Probability of an event = (number of favourable outcomes) ÷ (total number of possible outcomes)

This only works when all outcomes are equally likely (like a fair dice or a fair coin).

Example: A bag contains 3 white discs and 5 black discs. One disc is taken out at random. What is the probability it is white?

1.Favourable outcomes (white) = 3
2.Total outcomes = 3 + 5 = 8
3.P(white) = 3/8
P(white) = 3/8 = 0.375
!
Don't Confuse — Probability Is NEVER Greater Than 1

If you calculate a probability and get a number bigger than 1 (or negative), you've made a mistake. Go back and check. Probability is always between 0 and 1 (inclusive).

T
Study Tip — Always Count Carefully

The most common mistake in probability is miscounting the total. Before you divide, write out: "Favourable = ___. Total = ___." Double-check the total by adding all the individual groups.

  • I understand the probability scale from 0 to 1
  • I can calculate the probability of a single event using the formula
  • I know that probability can never be greater than 1 or less than 0
  • I always count the total number of outcomes carefully
Section 8.2 Exclusive Events and P(not A)
Events are mutually exclusive if they cannot happen at the same time. For example, a coin can't land on heads AND tails at the same time. This is the foundation for the most important rule in probability.
W
Write This Down — The Golden Rule

P(something NOT happening) = 1 − P(it happening)

This is the single most useful formula in probability. If you know the chance of something happening, you instantly know the chance of it NOT happening.

Example: The probability of rain tomorrow is 0.3. What is the probability it does NOT rain?

1.P(not rain) = 1 − P(rain) = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7
P(not rain) = 0.7
W
Write This Down — Mutually Exclusive Events Add Up to 1

If events are mutually exclusive and cover ALL possibilities, their probabilities add up to 1.

Example: Anna can travel by bus (P = 0.5), train (P = 0.3), or taxi. Find P(taxi).

1.These three options cover everything, so they add up to 1.
2.P(taxi) = 1 − 0.5 − 0.3 = 0.2
P(taxi) = 0.2
?
Why This Matters

The "1 minus" trick appears in almost every probability exam question. Sometimes it's much easier to work out the probability of what you DON'T want, then subtract from 1. If the question asks "at least one," it's almost always easier to calculate "none" and subtract from 1.

!
Don't Confuse — "Not Red" Isn't the Same as "Blue"

If a bag has red, blue, and green balls, P(not red) includes BOTH blue AND green. Don't assume "not red" means just one other colour.

  • I know what mutually exclusive events are
  • I can use P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
  • I know that all mutually exclusive probabilities add up to 1
  • I understand that "not red" can include multiple other colours
Section 8.3 Relative Frequency and Expected Frequency
Sometimes you can't calculate probability from theory (like with a biased dice). Instead, you run an experiment many times and use the results to estimate the probability. This is called relative frequency.
W
Write This Down — Relative Frequency Formula

Relative frequency = (number of times the event happened) ÷ (total number of trials)

The more trials you do, the more reliable the estimate becomes. A dice rolled 10 times might give misleading results. A dice rolled 1000 times gives a very good estimate.

Example: A dice is rolled 300 times. A six comes up 63 times. Estimate the probability of rolling a six.

1.Relative frequency = 63 ÷ 300 = 0.21
2.For a fair dice, we'd expect 1/6 ≈ 0.167. Since 0.21 is higher, the dice might be biased.
Estimated P(six) = 0.21
W
Write This Down — Expected Frequency

Expected number of successes = probability × number of trials

Example: A fair dice is rolled 240 times. How many times would you expect to roll a number greater than 4?

1.Numbers greater than 4 are: 5 and 6. That's 2 out of 6.
2.P(greater than 4) = 2/6 = 1/3
3.Expected frequency = (1/3) × 240 = 80
Expected: 80 times
!
Don't Confuse — "Expected" Doesn't Mean "Guaranteed"

If you expect 80 sixes in 480 rolls, you probably won't get exactly 80. You might get 76 or 84. "Expected frequency" is a prediction based on probability, not a certainty. The exam might ask "How many would you expect?" — use the formula, but know it's an estimate.

  • I can calculate relative frequency from experimental data
  • I know that more trials give a better estimate
  • I can calculate expected frequency using probability × trials
  • I understand that expected frequency is an estimate, not exact
Section 8.4 Venn Diagrams
A Venn diagram uses overlapping circles to show how items belong to different groups. The overlap shows items in BOTH groups. Items outside both circles (but inside the rectangle) belong to neither group.
W
Write This Down — Reading a Venn Diagram

The rectangle (marked with a curly E) represents the universal set — everything.

Each circle represents one set/group.

The overlap (intersection) = items in BOTH sets.

Items outside both circles but inside the rectangle = items in NEITHER set.

Example: In a class of 25, some study French, some German, some both, some neither. F circle shows: 9 (only French), 7 (both), G circle shows: 6 (only German), and 3 are outside both.

•Study French = 9 + 7 = 16 (French only + both)
•Study French AND German = 7 (the overlap)
•Study neither = 3
•Total check: 9 + 7 + 6 + 3 = 25. Correct!
W
Write This Down — Using Venn Diagrams for Probability

Once you've filled in the Venn diagram, finding probability is simple: divide the relevant number by the total.

Example (continued): A student is chosen at random from the class of 25.

•P(studies French) = 16/25
•P(studies both) = 7/25
•P(studies neither) = 3/25
•P(German but NOT French) = 6/25
T
Study Tip — Always Fill In the Middle First

When drawing a Venn diagram, always start with the overlap (the "both" number). Then work outwards. If 20 study maths and 7 study both maths and science, then 20 − 7 = 13 study ONLY maths. This order prevents double-counting errors.

!
Don't Confuse — "Likes Food A" vs. "Likes Only Food A"

"Likes food A" includes everyone in circle A — both the "only A" part AND the overlap. "Likes ONLY food A" means just the part of circle A that doesn't overlap. Read the question carefully.

  • I can read numbers from a Venn diagram
  • I know to start with the overlap when filling in a Venn diagram
  • I can calculate probabilities from a Venn diagram
  • I understand the difference between "A" and "only A"
Section 8.5 Tree Diagrams and Combined Events
When two (or more) events happen one after the other, we use a tree diagram to show all possible outcomes. This is probably the most important section in the chapter — tree diagrams appear on almost every IGCSE exam paper.
W
Write This Down — The AND/OR Rules

AND rule: When you want two things to happen together, MULTIPLY the probabilities along the branches.

OR rule: When either outcome would satisfy you, ADD the probabilities of the different paths.

Memory trick: AND = × (multiply).   OR = + (add).

W
Write This Down — How to Draw a Tree Diagram (Step by Step)

Step 1: Draw the first set of branches for Event 1. Label each branch with the outcome AND its probability. The probabilities on each set of branches must add up to 1.

Step 2: From the end of each branch, draw a new set of branches for Event 2. Again, label outcomes and probabilities.

Step 3: To find the probability of any particular combination, multiply along the branches (the AND rule).

Step 4: If the question asks for "this OR that," add up the relevant branch products (the OR rule).

Step 5: CHECK — all the final probabilities should add up to 1.

W
Write This Down — Full Worked Example

Example: A coin is tossed and a dice is rolled. Find the probability of getting a head AND a six.

Draw the tree:

Coin:   Head (1/2)   or   Tail (1/2)
Dice (from each coin branch):   Six (1/6)   or   Not six (5/6)

P(Head AND Six) = multiply along the "Head then Six" branch:

=1/2 × 1/6 = 1/12
P(Head and Six) = 1/12
W
Write This Down — A Harder Example with OR

Example: A bag has 3 red and 5 blue balls. Two balls are taken out one at a time WITHOUT replacement. Find the probability of getting one of each colour.

First pick:

•P(Red 1st) = 3/8    P(Blue 1st) = 5/8

Second pick (after Red first): Now 2 red, 5 blue left (7 total)

•P(Blue 2nd | Red 1st) = 5/7

Second pick (after Blue first): Now 3 red, 4 blue left (7 total)

•P(Red 2nd | Blue 1st) = 3/7

"One of each" means Red-then-Blue OR Blue-then-Red:

=(3/8 × 5/7) + (5/8 × 3/7)
=15/56 + 15/56 = 30/56 = 15/28
P(one of each) = 15/28
!
Don't Confuse — With Replacement vs. Without Replacement

With replacement: You put the item back before picking again. The probabilities STAY THE SAME for the second pick.

Without replacement: You DON'T put it back. The total goes down by 1, and the group you picked from goes down by 1. The probabilities CHANGE for the second pick.

Read the question carefully to see which one it is. "Without replacement" is more common on exams.

T
Study Tip — The Check That Saves You

After drawing your tree diagram, add up ALL the end probabilities. They must equal 1. If they don't, you've made an error somewhere. This 10-second check catches most mistakes.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Tree Neatly

On the exam, draw a BIG, clear tree diagram. Write the probability on each branch. Write the outcome at the end of each branch. Then write the multiplication next to each final branch. This makes it easy for you to read and for the examiner to give you method marks.

  • I know the AND rule: multiply along branches
  • I know the OR rule: add the results of different paths
  • I can draw a clear tree diagram with labelled branches
  • I understand the difference between with and without replacement
  • I always check that all end probabilities add up to 1
  • I can solve "one of each" problems using tree diagrams
Putting It All Together Key Study Strategies for Probability
T
The Three Questions to Ask Yourself

When you see a probability question, ask:

• 1. What are ALL the possible outcomes? (Count them carefully.)

• 2. Which outcomes do I WANT? (Count those too.)

• 3. Is there more than one event? (If yes, you probably need a tree diagram.)

T
Practise Tree Diagrams Until They Feel Natural

Tree diagrams are worth the most marks and are the area most students struggle with. Do at least 5 full tree diagram questions from the textbook exercises. Draw them out fully every time — don't take shortcuts.

T
Check Your Answer Makes Sense

• Probability is always between 0 and 1. If you get 1.3 or −0.2, redo it.

• "At least one" should be a relatively high probability in most cases.

• "Both" should usually be lower than "at least one."

• If the question says the dice is fair, each face has probability 1/6. If it says biased, you need the given probabilities.
