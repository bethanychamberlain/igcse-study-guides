Section 9.1 Similar Shapes
Two shapes are similar if one is an enlargement of the other. They have the same shape but different sizes. All the angles are equal, and the sides are in the same ratio (the scale factor).
W
Write This Down — What Makes Shapes Similar

Two shapes are mathematically similar if:

1. All corresponding angles are equal

2. All corresponding sides are in the same ratio

For triangles, you only need to check the angles — if the angles match, the sides will automatically be in ratio.

For other shapes (rectangles, quadrilaterals), you must check both angles AND side ratios. Two rectangles can have the same angles (all 90°) but NOT be similar if the side ratios differ.

W
Write This Down — Finding Missing Sides Using Scale Factor

Step 1: Find two corresponding sides (one from each shape) where you know both lengths.

Step 2: Work out the scale factor: scale factor = bigger side ÷ smaller side

Step 3: Use the scale factor to find the missing side.

Example: Triangles A and B are similar. Triangle A has sides 2, 3, x. Triangle B has sides 3, 4.5, 5. Find x.

1.Corresponding sides: 2 and 3, or 3 and 4.5.
2.Scale factor = 3 ÷ 2 = 1.5 (or 4.5 ÷ 3 = 1.5 — same, which confirms they're similar)
3.x corresponds to 5, so x = 5 ÷ 1.5 = 3⅓
x = 3⅓ cm
!
Don't Confuse — Similar vs. Congruent

Similar = same shape, different size (one is an enlargement of the other).

Congruent = same shape AND same size (exact copies).

All congruent shapes are similar (scale factor = 1), but similar shapes are NOT necessarily congruent.

T
Study Tip — Match the Right Sides

The most common mistake is matching the wrong sides. Look at which sides are opposite the same angles. The longest side of one shape corresponds to the longest side of the other. The shortest to the shortest. If the shapes are drawn at different angles, rotate them mentally until they line up.

  • I can explain the difference between similar and congruent shapes
  • I can find the scale factor from two corresponding sides
  • I can use the scale factor to find a missing length
  • I know that similar triangles need matching angles; other shapes need matching angles AND side ratios
Section 9.2 Congruent Shapes
Two shapes are congruent if one fits exactly on top of the other. They are the same size and same shape. The second shape might be rotated, reflected, or translated, but it has the exact same measurements.
W
Write This Down — Recognising Congruence

To check if two shapes are congruent:

1. All corresponding sides must be the same length

2. All corresponding angles must be the same size

A shape and its mirror image are congruent. A shape rotated 180° is congruent to the original. Sliding (translating) a shape doesn't change congruence.

?
Why This Matters

Congruence is a quick-marks topic on the exam — usually 1–2 marks for identifying congruent pairs from a diagram. It's free marks if you can visually match shapes that are just rotated or reflected versions of each other.

T
Study Tip — Trace and Turn

If you're not sure whether two shapes are congruent, imagine tracing one on paper and then rotating, flipping, or sliding it. If it lands perfectly on the other shape, they're congruent. On the exam, you can use a ruler to measure sides to check.

  • I can identify congruent shapes in a diagram
  • I know that rotation, reflection, and translation don't change congruence
Section 9.3 Trigonometry — SOH CAH TOA
Trigonometry lets you find missing sides and angles in right-angled triangles. It uses three ratios: sine, cosine, and tangent. This is one of the most important topics in IGCSE maths.
W
Write This Down — Labelling the Triangle

In a right-angled triangle, relative to a chosen angle x:

Hypotenuse (H) = the side opposite the right angle. Always the longest side.

Opposite (O) = the side opposite to angle x (directly across from it).

Adjacent (A) = the side next to angle x (that isn't the hypotenuse).

The labels O and A change depending on which angle you're looking at. H stays the same.

W
Write This Down — The Three Ratios

sin x = O / H   (Opposite ÷ Hypotenuse)

cos x = A / H   (Adjacent ÷ Hypotenuse)

tan x = O / A   (Opposite ÷ Adjacent)

Remember: SOH CAH TOA

Or the sentence: "Silly Old Harry Caught A Herring Trawling Off Afghanistan"

?
Why This Matters

Trigonometry questions are worth 3–6 marks on every IGCSE paper. They appear in real-world contexts: ladders against walls, angles of elevation, bearings, building heights. Learn SOHCAHTOA and you unlock a whole category of exam questions.

  • I can label H, O, and A on a right-angled triangle
  • I've memorised SOHCAHTOA
Section 9.3 (continued) Finding a Missing Side
When you know an angle and one side, you can find another side using trig.
W
Write This Down — Step-by-Step Method

Step 1: Label the sides H, O, A relative to the given angle.

Step 2: Identify what you HAVE and what you WANT.

Step 3: Pick the right ratio — the one that uses both the side you have and the side you want.

Step 4: Set up the equation and solve.

You have & wantUseFormula
O and Hsinsin x = O / H
A and Hcoscos x = A / H
O and Atantan x = O / A

Example: Find length l. Angle = 32°, hypotenuse = 10 cm, l is adjacent.

1.Have: angle (32°) and H (10). Want: A (l).
2.A and H → use cos.
3.cos 32° = l / 10
4.l = 10 × cos 32° = 10 × 0.848 = 8.5 cm (1 d.p.)
l = 8.5 cm
W
Write This Down — When the Unknown Is on the Bottom

Sometimes you set up the ratio and the unknown ends up on the bottom of the fraction. You need to rearrange.

Example: Find x. Angle = 36°, opposite = 11, x is the hypotenuse.

1.Have: O (11) and angle. Want: H (x). Use sin.
2.sin 36° = 11 / x
3.Multiply both sides by x:   x × sin 36° = 11
4.Divide both sides by sin 36°:   x = 11 / sin 36° = 18.7 cm (1 d.p.)
x = 18.7 cm
!
Don't Confuse — Multiplying vs. Dividing

If the unknown is on the top of the fraction: multiply. (e.g. x/10 = cos 32° → x = 10 × cos 32°)

If the unknown is on the bottom: divide. (e.g. 11/x = sin 36° → x = 11 / sin 36°)

Many students always multiply. This only works when the unknown is the numerator.

  • I can choose the right ratio (sin, cos, or tan) based on what I have and what I need
  • I can find a side when the unknown is on top (multiply)
  • I can find a side when the unknown is on the bottom (divide)
Section 9.3 (continued) Finding a Missing Angle
When you know two sides and need the angle, you use the inverse trig functions on your calculator: sin−1, cos−1, or tan−1.
W
Write This Down — Step-by-Step Method

Step 1: Label sides H, O, A relative to the angle you want.

Step 2: Identify which two sides you know.

Step 3: Pick the ratio and calculate the fraction.

Step 4: Use the inverse button (shift/2nd function + sin/cos/tan) on your calculator.

Example: Find angle x. Opposite = 3, Adjacent = 8.

1.Have O (3) and A (8). Use tan.
2.tan x = 3 / 8 = 0.375
3.x = tan−1(0.375)
4.On calculator: 3 ÷ 8 = INV tan
x = 20.6° (1 d.p.)
!
Don't Confuse — Degrees Mode

Make sure your calculator is in DEGREES mode, not radians. If you get tiny weird answers like 0.36 instead of 20.6°, your calculator is in the wrong mode. Look for a "DEG" or "D" indicator on the screen.

T
Study Tip — Sense-Check Your Answer

Angles in a right-angled triangle are between 0° and 90° (the two non-right angles). If you get an answer like 150° or −20°, something went wrong. Also, if the opposite side is much shorter than the adjacent, the angle should be small (well under 45°).

  • I can use inverse sin, cos, and tan on my calculator
  • I know to check my calculator is in degrees mode
  • I can sense-check whether my angle answer is reasonable
Section 9.3 (continued) Real-World Trigonometry Problems
Exam questions love putting trigonometry into real-life contexts: ladders, towers, ramps, kites, bearings. The maths is the same — you just need to draw the right triangle first.
W
Write This Down — Key Vocabulary

Angle of elevation = the angle you look UP from the horizontal to see something above you.

Angle of depression = the angle you look DOWN from the horizontal to see something below you.

Bearing = a direction measured clockwise from North. Always written as 3 digits (e.g. 070°, 215°, 005°).

W
Write This Down — Approach for Word Problems

Step 1: Draw a large, clear diagram. Label all the information you're given.

Step 2: Identify the right-angled triangle in the diagram.

Step 3: Label H, O, A relative to the angle you're using.

Step 4: Use SOHCAHTOA as normal.

Example: From a point 20 m away, the angle of elevation to the top of a tower is 35°. Find the tower's height.

1.Draw: the tower is vertical, the ground is horizontal, 20 m = adjacent, height = opposite, 35° is the angle at the ground.
2.Have: A (20) and angle (35°). Want: O (height). Use tan.
3.tan 35° = height / 20
4.height = 20 × tan 35° = 20 × 0.700 = 14.0 m
Height = 14.0 m
T
Study Tip — Always Draw the Diagram

The exam question might not give you a diagram for word problems. Always draw one yourself. Make it big. Label everything. A clear diagram is half the solution.

  • I understand angle of elevation and angle of depression
  • I can draw a diagram from a word problem
  • I know bearings are measured clockwise from North in 3 digits
  • I can solve ladder, tower, and ramp problems using trig

The Big Three

This chapter boils down to three skills:

• Similar shapes: Find the scale factor, use it to find missing sides.

• Trig — find a side: Label H/O/A, pick the ratio, set up the equation, solve.

• Trig — find an angle: Same as above, but use the inverse button.

If you can do these three things confidently, you can handle any question in this chapter.

T
Check, Check, Check

• Similar shapes: Check that your scale factor is consistent across all pairs of sides.

• Trig sides: Your answer should be shorter than the hypotenuse (O and A are always shorter than H).

• Trig angles: The two non-right angles must add up to 90°.
