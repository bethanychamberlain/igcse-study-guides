Section 5.1 Transforming Shapes
A transformation changes the position, size, or orientation of a shape. There are four types you need to know. Each one has specific information you must give when describing it.
W
Write This Down — The Four Transformations
TransformationWhat It DoesWhat You Must State
ReflectionFlips the shape over a mirror lineThe equation of the mirror line (e.g. y = 1, x = −2, y = x)
RotationTurns the shape around a fixed pointThe centre, the angle, and the direction (clockwise or anticlockwise)
TranslationSlides the shape without turning or flippingThe vector (how far across and up/down)
EnlargementMakes the shape bigger or smallerThe scale factor and the centre of enlargement
?
Why This Matters

Transformation questions are worth 3–5 marks on the IGCSE exam. The marks come from describing the transformation fully. If you say "rotation" but forget to give the centre, angle, AND direction, you lose marks. Every piece of information in the table above is required.

W
Write This Down — Reflection

Each point of the shape is the same distance from the mirror line, but on the other side.

Common mirror lines:

• x-axis (the horizontal line, y = 0)

• y-axis (the vertical line, x = 0)

• y = x (the diagonal going up-right)

• y = −x (the diagonal going down-right)

• Lines like x = 3 or y = −1

The image is the same size and shape but reversed (like a mirror).

W
Write This Down — Rotation

The shape turns around a fixed point called the centre of rotation.

You need three things: (1) centre, (2) angle (90°, 180°, 270°), (3) direction (clockwise or anticlockwise).

Tip: for 180° rotations, you don't need to say clockwise or anticlockwise — it's the same either way.

W
Write This Down — Translation

A translation slides every point the same distance in the same direction. Described using a column vector.

The vector:

Top number = move right (+) or left (−)
Bottom number = move up (+) or down (−)

Example: Vector (4, −3) means move 4 right and 3 down.

Adding vectors: (4, 1) + (3, 5) = (7, 6). Just add the top numbers and add the bottom numbers.

Multiplying by a scalar: 5 × (3, 2) = (15, 10). Multiply both numbers.

W
Write This Down — Enlargement

An enlargement changes the size of the shape. All lengths are multiplied by the scale factor. All lengths are measured from the centre of enlargement.

Scale factor > 1: shape gets bigger.

Scale factor between 0 and 1 (e.g. 1/2): shape gets smaller (but it's still called an "enlargement").

Example: Enlarge triangle ABC with scale factor 3, centre O.

1.Measure the distance from O to each vertex.
2.Multiply each distance by 3.
3.Plot the new vertices at these new distances from O.
OA' = 3 × OA, OB' = 3 × OB, OC' = 3 × OC
!
Don't Confuse — "Describe Fully" Means Give ALL Details

If the exam says "describe fully the single transformation," you MUST give:

• Reflection: "Reflection in the line y = ..." (name the line)

• Rotation: "Rotation of ...° clockwise/anticlockwise about (x, y)" (angle + direction + centre)

• Translation: "Translation by vector (...)" (give the vector)

• Enlargement: "Enlargement with scale factor ... and centre (...)" (scale factor + centre)

Missing any piece loses marks. This is the most common way students throw away marks in this topic.

T
Study Tip — Use Tracing Paper

In the exam, you're allowed to ask for tracing paper. For rotations, trace the shape, put your pencil on the centre, and turn. This makes rotation questions much easier to visualise.

  • I can reflect a shape in horizontal, vertical, and diagonal mirror lines
  • I can rotate a shape and give the centre, angle, and direction
  • I can translate a shape using a column vector
  • I can enlarge a shape with a given scale factor and centre
  • I can describe a transformation fully with all required information
Section 5.2 Quadrilaterals and Other Polygons
A quadrilateral is any shape with four sides. You need to know the properties of each special quadrilateral — their sides, angles, diagonals, and symmetry.
W
Write This Down — Properties of Special Quadrilaterals
ShapeSidesAnglesSymmetry
Square4 equal sidesAll 90°4 lines, rotational order 4
Rectangle2 pairs of equal sidesAll 90°2 lines, rotational order 2
Rhombus4 equal sidesOpposite angles equal2 lines, rotational order 2
ParallelogramOpposite sides equal & parallelOpposite angles equalNo lines, rotational order 2
Trapezium1 pair of parallel sidesVariesNo symmetry (in general)
Kite2 pairs of adjacent equal sides1 pair of opposite angles equal1 line, no rotational symmetry

Key fact: The angles in any quadrilateral add up to 360°.

W
Write This Down — Diagonals

Square: Diagonals are equal, bisect each other at right angles, and bisect the corner angles.

Rectangle: Diagonals are equal and bisect each other (but NOT at right angles).

Rhombus: Diagonals bisect each other at right angles and bisect the corner angles.

Parallelogram: Diagonals bisect each other (not at right angles, not equal).

Kite: One diagonal bisects the other at right angles. The main diagonal bisects the angles it passes through.

W
Write This Down — Polygons

A polygon is any flat shape with straight sides. Common names:

• 3 sides: triangle   • 4 sides: quadrilateral   • 5 sides: pentagon

• 6 sides: hexagon   • 8 sides: octagon   • 10 sides: decagon

A regular polygon has all sides equal AND all interior angles equal.

!
Don't Confuse — Rhombus vs. Parallelogram vs. Rectangle

A square is a special rectangle AND a special rhombus. A rhombus has equal sides but angles are NOT 90° (unless it's a square). A rectangle has 90° angles but sides are NOT all equal (unless it's a square). A parallelogram is the most general — opposite sides equal and parallel, but no right angles and not all sides equal.

  • I can name and describe the properties of all six special quadrilaterals
  • I know that angles in a quadrilateral sum to 360°
  • I can describe the diagonal properties of each quadrilateral
  • I know the names of common polygons and what "regular" means
Section 5.3 Bearings
A bearing is a way of describing direction. It's used in navigation for ships, aircraft, and hiking. Bearings are always measured from North, turning clockwise, and written as three figures.
W
Write This Down — Three Rules of Bearings

1. Measured from North. Always start at the North line.

2. Measured clockwise. Turn clockwise from North to the direction you're going.

3. Written as three figures. Use leading zeros: 045° (not 45°), 008° (not 8°).

Example: Due East = 090°. Due South = 180°. Due West = 270°. North-East = 045°.

W
Write This Down — Back Bearings

The back bearing (or reverse bearing) is the bearing to get back from B to A (the opposite direction).

Rule: Add or subtract 180°.

• If the bearing is less than 180°: back bearing = bearing + 180°

• If the bearing is 180° or more: back bearing = bearing − 180°

Example: Bearing of Sketty from Ashby = 110°. Back bearing (Ashby from Sketty) = 110 + 180 = 290°.

!
Don't Confuse — "From" Matters

"The bearing of B from A" means: stand at A, face North, turn clockwise until you're pointing at B. That angle is the bearing.

Students often get confused about which point they're standing at. Always put your finger on the "from" point and draw the North line there.

T
Study Tip — Always Draw the North Line

For every bearing question, draw a North arrow at the point you're measuring from. Then measure clockwise. This prevents the most common mistake (measuring from the wrong point or in the wrong direction).

  • I know the three rules: from North, clockwise, three figures
  • I can measure and state a bearing from a diagram
  • I can calculate a back bearing by adding or subtracting 180°
  • I always draw the North line at the correct point
Section 5.4 Pythagoras' Theorem
Pythagoras' theorem is one of the most important results in mathematics. It works for right-angled triangles only and lets you find a missing side when you know the other two.
W
Write This Down — The Formula

a2 + b2 = c2

where c is the hypotenuse (the longest side, opposite the right angle) and a and b are the other two sides.

In words: "The square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares on the other two sides."

W
Write This Down — Finding the Hypotenuse

When you need to find the longest side (hypotenuse):

Example: Two shorter sides are 6 cm and 8 cm. Find the hypotenuse.

1.c2 = a2 + b2
2.c2 = 62 + 82 = 36 + 64 = 100
3.c = √100 = 10 cm
Hypotenuse = 10 cm
W
Write This Down — Finding a Shorter Side

When you need to find one of the shorter sides, rearrange the formula:

a2 = c2 − b2

Example: Hypotenuse = 7 cm, one side = 4 cm. Find the other side d.

1.d2 + 42 = 72
2.d2 = 49 − 16 = 33
3.d = √33 = 5.74 cm (3 s.f.)
d = 5.74 cm (3 s.f.)
!
Don't Confuse — Adding vs. Subtracting

Finding the hypotenuse? You ADD the squares, then square root.

Finding a shorter side? You SUBTRACT the squares, then square root.

The most common mistake is adding when you should subtract (or vice versa). Ask yourself: "Am I finding the longest side or a shorter side?" That tells you which operation to use.

?
Why This Matters

Pythagoras appears in many unexpected places: finding distances between two coordinates on a grid, working out the diagonal of a rectangle, calculating how high a ladder reaches, finding distances in bearing problems. Master this formula and you can solve all of these.

T
Study Tip — The 3-4-5 Triangle

The most famous right-angled triangle has sides 3, 4, and 5 (because 9 + 16 = 25). Multiples also work: 6-8-10, 5-12-13, 8-15-17. Recognising these can save time and help you check answers.

W
Write This Down — Distance Between Two Points

To find the distance between points (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) on a coordinate grid:

Distance = √[(x2 − x1)2 + (y2 − y1)2]

Example: A = (1, 2) and B = (6, 4).

1.Horizontal distance: 6 − 1 = 5
2.Vertical distance: 4 − 2 = 2
3.AB = √(52 + 22) = √(25 + 4) = √29 = 5.39 (3 s.f.)
AB = 5.39 units (3 s.f.)
  • I can state Pythagoras' theorem: a2 + b2 = c2
  • I can find the hypotenuse (add then square root)
  • I can find a shorter side (subtract then square root)
  • I know to check: am I adding or subtracting?
  • I can use Pythagoras to find the distance between two coordinate points
Putting It All Together Key Study Strategies for Shape and Space
T
The Number One Rule

Draw a diagram. If the question doesn't give you one, draw your own. Bearings, Pythagoras, transformations — they all become easier when you can see what's happening. Use a ruler, label everything, and mark right angles with a small square.

T
"Describe Fully" Is the Most Important Phrase

When the exam says "describe fully the single transformation," you need to:

• Name the type (reflection / rotation / translation / enlargement)

• Give ALL the required details (see the table in Section 5.1)

Practise writing full descriptions until it becomes automatic.

T
Check Your Pythagoras

After finding a side, check: is the hypotenuse the longest side? If you find a "shorter side" that's longer than the hypotenuse, you made an error. Also check: does a2 + b2 actually equal c2 with your answer? Plug the numbers back in to verify.
