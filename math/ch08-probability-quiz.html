Foundation — Can You Calculate It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Straightforward probability calculations.
1
A bag contains 8 orange balls, 5 green balls, and 4 silver balls. One ball is picked at random. Find the probability that it is: (a) orange, (b) green.
2 marks
Answer
Total balls = 8 + 5 + 4 = 17
(a) P(orange) = 8/17 [1]
(b) P(green) = 5/17 [1]
Always write the total first. 8 + 5 + 4 = 17. Then the probability is "what I want" over 17.

2
The probability of a bus arriving on time is 0.72. What is the probability that the bus does NOT arrive on time?
1 mark
Answer
P(not on time) = 1 − 0.72 = 0.28 [1]
The golden rule: P(not A) = 1 − P(A). This is the most commonly tested formula in probability.

3
A fair dice is rolled 300 times. How many times would you expect to roll a six?
2 marks
Answer
P(six) = 1/6 [1]
Expected number = (1/6) × 300 = 50 [1]
Expected frequency = probability × number of trials. Show the probability first, then multiply.

4
A spinner has 8 equal sectors labelled: 0, 5, 1, 2, 2, 4, 5, 3. Find the probability of: (a) spinning a 5, (b) NOT spinning a 2.
2 marks
Answer
(a) There are two 5s out of 8 sectors. P(5) = 2/8 = 1/4 [1]
(b) There are two 2s. P(2) = 2/8 = 1/4. So P(not 2) = 1 − 1/4 = 3/4 [1]
Count carefully! The sectors are 0, 5, 1, 2, 2, 4, 5, 3 — that is two 5s and two 2s. For part (b), use the "1 minus" rule.

Intermediate — Can You Apply the Rules?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to use Venn diagrams, relative frequency, and explain your reasoning.
5
In a class of 25 students, some choose French, some German, some both, some neither. The Venn diagram shows: French only = 9, Both = 7, German only = 6, Neither = 3. A student is chosen at random. Find: (a) P(studies French), (b) P(studies French and German), (c) P(studies German but not French).
3 marks
Answer
Total = 9 + 7 + 6 + 3 = 25 (check!)
(a) Studies French = French only + Both = 9 + 7 = 16. P(French) = 16/25 [1]
(b) P(French AND German) = 7/25 [1]
(c) P(German but NOT French) = 6/25 [1]
"Studies French" means everyone in the French circle, including the overlap. "German but not French" is ONLY the German-only section (6), not the overlap (7).

6
A biased dice is rolled 200 times. A three comes up 52 times. (a) Calculate the relative frequency of rolling a three. (b) If the dice were fair, what relative frequency would you expect for a three? (c) Does this evidence suggest the dice is biased? Explain your answer.
4 marks
Answer
(a) Relative frequency = 52/200 = 0.26 [1]
(b) For a fair dice: 1/6 = 0.167 (to 3 d.p.) [1]
(c) Yes, the dice appears biased [1] because the relative frequency (0.26) is significantly higher than the expected 0.167 for a fair dice. After 200 rolls, this is a large enough sample to be meaningful [1].
For part (c), you need TWO things: say yes/no AND give a reason comparing the relative frequency to the expected value. Also mention that 200 rolls is a reasonable sample size.

7
A bag contains a large number of balls coloured red, white, black, or green. The probabilities of selecting each colour are: Red = 0.3, White = 0.1, Black = ?, Green = 0.3. Find: (a) the probability of selecting a black ball, (b) the probability of selecting a ball which is NOT white.
3 marks
Answer
(a) All probabilities must add to 1: 0.3 + 0.1 + ? + 0.3 = 1 [1]
0.7 + ? = 1, so P(black) = 0.3 [1]
(b) P(not white) = 1 − 0.1 = 0.9 [1]
The key idea: mutually exclusive probabilities covering all outcomes must add to 1. For part (b), "not white" includes red + black + green, but it is faster to use 1 − P(white).

Advanced — Can You Use Tree Diagrams?

Worth 5-6 marks each. Multi-step problems with tree diagrams. Draw the tree, show ALL working.
8
A bag contains 4 red balls and 6 blue balls. A ball is taken out at random and NOT replaced. A second ball is then taken out. Draw a tree diagram and find: (a) P(both red), (b) P(both the same colour), (c) P(one of each colour).
6 marks
Answer
Tree diagram branches:
1st pick: P(R) = 4/10 = 2/5,   P(B) = 6/10 = 3/5

2nd pick (after R first): 3 red + 6 blue = 9 left.
   P(R) = 3/9 = 1/3,   P(B) = 6/9 = 2/3
2nd pick (after B first): 4 red + 5 blue = 9 left.
   P(R) = 4/9,   P(B) = 5/9

(a) P(both red) = P(R, R) = 2/5 × 1/3 = 2/15 [2]

(b) P(both same colour) = P(R, R) + P(B, B) [1]
P(B, B) = 3/5 × 5/9 = 15/45 = 1/3
P(same) = 2/15 + 1/3 = 2/15 + 5/15 = 7/15 [1]

(c) P(one of each) = 1 − P(same) = 1 − 7/15 = 8/15 [2]
Or: P(R,B) + P(B,R) = (2/5 × 2/3) + (3/5 × 4/9) = 4/15 + 12/45 = 4/15 + 4/15 = 8/15
This is WITHOUT replacement, so the probabilities change for the second pick. For (c), you can either calculate both mixed paths and add, or use 1 minus the answer to (b). The second method is quicker. Always draw the tree diagram neatly to collect your method marks.

9
The probability that it rains on any given day is 0.4. On two consecutive days, find: (a) P(it rains on both days), (b) P(it rains on exactly one of the two days), (c) P(it does not rain on either day). Assume the days are independent.
5 marks
Answer
Let R = rain, N = no rain. P(R) = 0.4, P(N) = 0.6.

Tree diagram:
Day 1: R (0.4) or N (0.6)
Day 2 from each: R (0.4) or N (0.6)

(a) P(both rain) = P(R, R) = 0.4 × 0.4 = 0.16 [1]

(b) P(exactly one day of rain) = P(R, N) + P(N, R) [1]
= (0.4 × 0.6) + (0.6 × 0.4) = 0.24 + 0.24 = 0.48 [1]

(c) P(no rain on either day) = P(N, N) = 0.6 × 0.6 = 0.36 [1]

Check: 0.16 + 0.48 + 0.36 = 1.00 [1]
This is WITH replacement (independent days), so the probabilities stay the same on Day 2. The check at the end (adding to 1) is worth doing — if it doesn't add to 1, you've made an error. Note that "exactly one" means rain-then-dry OR dry-then-rain, so you need BOTH paths.

10
A club of 50 members are asked about ghosts and aliens. 20 believe in both ghosts and aliens. 18 believe in ghosts but not aliens. 3 believe in neither. (a) How many believe in aliens but not ghosts? (b) A member is chosen at random. Find the probability that this member believes in aliens. (c) Find the probability that this member believes in ghosts OR aliens (or both).
5 marks
Answer
Venn diagram: Both = 20, Ghosts only = 18, Neither = 3.
Total accounted for so far = 20 + 18 + 3 = 41.
(a) Aliens only = 50 − 41 = 9 [1]

(b) Believe in aliens = aliens only + both = 9 + 20 = 29.
P(aliens) = 29/50 [2]

(c) "Ghosts OR aliens" includes everyone EXCEPT "neither".
P(ghosts or aliens) = 1 − P(neither) = 1 − 3/50 = 47/50 [2]
For (c), the quickest method is 1 minus P(neither). This avoids having to add up three separate groups. Always check: ghosts only (18) + both (20) + aliens only (9) + neither (3) = 50. Correct!
