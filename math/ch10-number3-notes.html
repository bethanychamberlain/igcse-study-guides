Section 10.1 Powers and Roots
Indices (powers) are a shorthand for repeated multiplication. Instead of writing 3 × 3 × 3 × 3, we write 34. The small number tells you how many times to multiply.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Square number: a number multiplied by itself. E.g. 52 = 25.

Cube number: a number multiplied by itself three times. E.g. 23 = 8.

Square root (√): the reverse of squaring. √25 = 5 because 52 = 25.

Cube root (∛): the reverse of cubing. ∛8 = 2 because 23 = 8.

!
Don't Confuse — Negative Power vs. Negative Number

2−3 does NOT mean −8. It means 1/23 = 1/8.

(−3)2 = 9 (negative times negative = positive).

−32 = −9 (only the 3 is squared, then the minus is applied).

The brackets make all the difference!

T
Study Tip — The Rules Only Work with the Same Base

You can only add/subtract indices when the base number is the same. 32 × 34 = 36 works. But 32 × 54 CANNOT be simplified using these rules. The bases (3 and 5) are different.

  • I know all five rules of indices
  • I understand what negative and zero indices mean
  • I can find square roots and cube roots
Section 10.2 Standard Form (Scientific Notation)
Standard form is a way to write very large or very small numbers neatly. It's written as a × 10n, where 1 ≤ a < 10 and n is an integer.
W
Write This Down — How to Convert to Standard Form

Step 1: Move the decimal point so the number is between 1 and 10.

Step 2: Count how many places you moved it. That's your power of 10.

Step 3: If the original number was big (you moved left), the power is positive. If it was small (you moved right), the power is negative.

Large number: 2000 = 2 × 103 (moved decimal 3 places left)

Small number: 0.0004 = 4 × 10−4 (moved decimal 4 places right)

W
Write This Down — Calculating in Standard Form

When multiplying: multiply the numbers, add the powers of 10.

When dividing: divide the numbers, subtract the powers of 10.

Example: (1.5 × 103) × (8 × 106)

1.1.5 × 8 = 12
2.103 × 106 = 109
3.12 × 109 — but 12 is not between 1 and 10!
4.Adjust: 12 = 1.2 × 10, so answer = 1.2 × 1010
Answer: 1.2 × 1010
!
Don't Confuse — The Number Must Be Between 1 and 10

32 × 105 is NOT standard form because 32 is not between 1 and 10.

Correct: 3.2 × 106.

0.5 × 103 is NOT standard form either. Correct: 5 × 102.

  • I can convert large numbers to standard form
  • I can convert small numbers to standard form
  • I can multiply and divide numbers in standard form
  • I know to adjust the answer so a is between 1 and 10
Section 10.3 Fractions
This section covers adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions — including mixed numbers and improper fractions. These are core skills that come up everywhere.
W
Write This Down — Key Vocabulary

Proper fraction: top < bottom. E.g. 3/5, 2/11.

Improper fraction: top ≥ bottom. E.g. 9/4, 11/3.

Mixed number: whole number + fraction. E.g. 2¼ = 9/4.

Common denominator: the shared bottom number needed for adding/subtracting.

W
Write This Down — The Four Operations
OperationMethod
Adding / SubtractingFind common denominator, add/subtract tops, keep bottom
MultiplyingMultiply tops together, multiply bottoms together
DividingFlip the second fraction, then multiply (KFC: Keep, Flip, Change)

Example: 3/4 + 2/5

1.Common denominator: 4 × 5 = 20
2.3/4 = 15/20.   2/5 = 8/20.
3.15/20 + 8/20 = 23/20 = 1 3/20
!
Don't Confuse — You Only Need a Common Denominator for +/−

For adding and subtracting: you MUST find a common denominator first.

For multiplying: just go straight across. No common denominator needed.

For dividing: flip and multiply. No common denominator needed.

  • I can add and subtract fractions with different denominators
  • I can multiply fractions
  • I can divide fractions (keep, flip, change)
  • I can convert between mixed numbers and improper fractions
Section 10.4 Negative Numbers
Negative numbers appear everywhere: temperatures, bank balances, coordinates, and especially in algebra. The rules for adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing with negatives are essential.
W
Write This Down — Adding and Subtracting Rules

Think of a number line. Adding moves right, subtracting moves left.

Subtracting a negative = adding.   3 − (−6) = 3 + 6 = 9

Adding a negative = subtracting.   −4 + (−5) = −4 − 5 = −9

W
Write This Down — Multiplying and Dividing Rules
SignsResultExample
positive × positivepositive+7 × +3 = +21
negative × negativepositive−6 × −4 = +24
positive × negativenegative+7 × −5 = −35
negative × positivenegative−8 × +4 = −32

Same signs = positive. Different signs = negative. This rule works for both multiplication and division.

T
Study Tip — Two Signs Next to Each Other

When you see two signs next to each other (like + − or − −), replace them:

• + + = +    − − = +

• + − = −    − + = −

Same = positive. Different = negative. Same rule as multiplication.

  • I can add and subtract negative numbers using the number line
  • I know the sign rules for multiplying and dividing
  • I can handle two signs next to each other
Section 10.5 Substituting into Formulae
Substitution means replacing the letters in a formula with actual numbers, then calculating the result. This is a fundamental skill used in science, engineering, and every maths exam.
W
Write This Down — Step-by-Step Method

Step 1: Write down the formula.

Step 2: Replace each letter with its given value. Use brackets around negative numbers.

Step 3: Calculate carefully, following BIDMAS/BODMAS.

Example: If v = u + at, find v when u = 3, a = 4, t = 6.

1.v = u + at
2.v = 3 + (4 × 6)
3.v = 3 + 24 = 27
v = 27
W
Write This Down — Example with Negative Values

Example: If x = 3 and y = −4, find 2x + y and xy − y.

a)2x + y = 2(3) + (−4) = 6 − 4 = 2
b)xy − y = (3)(−4) − (−4) = −12 + 4 = −8
!
Don't Confuse — BIDMAS Order Matters

In v = u + at, you must do a × t first (multiplication before addition).

v = 3 + 4 × 6 = 3 + 24 = 27. NOT (3 + 4) × 6 = 42.

Always use brackets when substituting negative numbers to avoid sign errors.

?
Why This Matters

Substitution appears in every IGCSE science and maths exam. Physics formulae (F = ma, v = u + at, E = mc2), chemistry calculations, distance/speed/time — they all require substitution. Get this right and you can handle them all.

  • I can substitute positive values into a formula
  • I can substitute negative values and use brackets correctly
  • I follow BIDMAS when calculating after substitution
  • I can use formulae involving squares, square roots, and fractions
