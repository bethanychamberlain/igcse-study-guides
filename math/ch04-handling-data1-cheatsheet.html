Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Mean Add all values, divide by how many there are. Median (middle value) or Mode (most common)
Median The middle value when data is in order. Mean (the calculated average). Must order first!
Mode The value that appears most often. Median (middle) or Mean (total ÷ count)
Range Largest value − smallest value. Measures spread. It's NOT an average — it's a measure of how spread out the data is
Frequency How many times a value occurs. —
Correlation The relationship between two sets of data on a scatter graph. Cause — correlation does NOT prove one thing causes the other
Line of best fit A straight line through the middle of the points on a scatter graph. It does NOT have to go through the origin (0, 0)
Discrete data Data that can only take certain values (shoe size, number of children). Continuous data (can take any value: height, weight, time)
Stem-and-leaf A way of displaying data where each number is split into a stem (tens) and leaf (units). Must be in order and include a key
Frequency polygon Formed by joining the midpoints of bar chart tops with straight lines. Bar chart (uses bars, not connected points)

Three Averages + Range — Side by Side

Average How to Find It Best When...
Mean Total ÷ count No extreme values (outliers)
Median Order the data, pick the middle There ARE outliers (extreme values)
Mode Most frequent value Category data (e.g. favourite colour)

Exam Trap — Median Requires Ordering
You must put the data in order before finding the median. Picking the "middle" of an unordered list gives the wrong answer every time. For an even count, average the two middle values.

Types of Correlation

Type Points Look Like Example
Strong positive Tight cluster going up-right More revision → higher marks
Weak positive Loosely trending upward Height vs. arm span
Strong negative Tight cluster going down-right Higher price → fewer sales
No correlation Scattered randomly Maths score vs. 800m run time

When describing correlation, always give two words: the strength (strong/weak) and the direction (positive/negative).

Key Formulae

What Formula Check
Mean Sum of all values ÷ number of values Answer should be between smallest and largest
Pie chart angle (value ÷ total) × 360° All angles must sum to 360°
Read a pie chart (angle ÷ 360) × total All values must sum to the total
Midpoint (freq. polygon) (lower + upper) ÷ 2 Plot midpoint vs. frequency, then join

Say It in One Sentence

Mean Add them all up, divide by how many — the "fair share" everyone would get if the total were split equally.
Median Put the numbers in order, find the one in the middle — not affected by extreme values.
Mode The most popular value — the one that shows up the most.
Range Biggest minus smallest — tells you how spread out the data is.
Pie chart Angle = fraction of the total × 360° — use a protractor, and check all angles add to 360°.
Scatter graph Plot two variables against each other to see if there's a relationship — describe the correlation with two words.
Line of best fit A straight line through the trend, with roughly equal points on each side — use it to estimate unknown values.
Stem-and-leaf Split numbers into stems and leaves, put leaves in order, always include a key.
Discrete vs. continuous Discrete = counted (gaps between bars). Continuous = measured (bars touch).

Memory Tricks

MeMoMeR
Mean = add and divide. Mode = Most common. Median = Medium (middle). Range = the Reach (biggest minus smallest).
Median — "Middle of the Alphabet"
MeDIan has a D-I in the middle, just like it's the value in the miDdle. But only after you put the data In order!
Pie Charts — "One Formula, Every Time"
Angle = (value ÷ total) × 360. That's it. Drawing a pie chart? Use it for each slice. Reading a pie chart? Flip it: value = (angle ÷ 360) × total.
Correlation is NOT Causation
Ice cream sales and drowning rates both go up in summer. But ice cream doesn't cause drowning! If the exam asks "does this prove...?", the answer is almost always no — it only shows a correlation, not a cause.
Bar Chart Gaps = Discrete, No Gaps = Continuous
If you can count it (shoes, goals, children), bars have gaps. If you can measure it (height, weight, time), bars touch. Think: measured data flows smoothly, so the bars flow together.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Calculate the mean" Add all values, divide by the total count. Show the full sum.
"Find the median" Order the data first, then find the middle value. Even count? Average the two middle values.
"Which average best represents the data?" If there are outliers (extreme values), the median is better. The mean is dragged towards outliers.
"Draw a pie chart" Calculate angle = (value ÷ total) × 360 for each category. Use a protractor. Check angles sum to 360°.
"Describe the correlation" Use two words: strength + direction. E.g. "strong positive correlation" or "no correlation."
"Draw a line of best fit" A straight line through the trend with roughly equal points on each side. It does NOT need to pass through (0, 0).
"Draw a stem-and-leaf diagram" Leaves must be in order (smallest to largest in each row). Include a key (e.g. 1|7 means 17).
"Draw a frequency polygon" Plot the midpoint of each class against the frequency, then join with straight lines.
"Is the data discrete or continuous?" Can you count it? Discrete. Can you measure it? Continuous.
"Label your axes" They mean it. Every chart needs: title, labelled axes, units, and a key where relevant. Missing labels = lost marks.
Exam Trap — Mean vs. Median with Outliers
Marks: 20, 21, 21, 22, 23, 25, 27, 27, 27, 29, 98, 98. Mean = 35.5 (dragged up by 98s). Median = 25 (fair picture). If the exam gives data with extreme values and asks "which average is most appropriate?", say median and explain that it is not affected by outliers.
