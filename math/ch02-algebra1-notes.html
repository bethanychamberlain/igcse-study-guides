Section 2.1 Sequences
A sequence is a list of numbers that follow a rule. Your job is to spot the rule, continue the pattern, and write a formula (the nth term) that gives you any term without listing them all out.
W
Write This Down — What "nth Term" Means

n = the position number (1st, 2nd, 3rd, ...).

nth term = a formula that gives you the value at position n.

Example: If the nth term is 3n + 1, then:

• 1st term (n=1): 3(1) + 1 = 4

• 2nd term (n=2): 3(2) + 1 = 7

• 3rd term (n=3): 3(3) + 1 = 10

So the sequence is: 4, 7, 10, 13, ... — you add 3 each time.

W
Write This Down — How to Find the nth Term (Step by Step)

This method works for any sequence where you add the same amount each time (an arithmetic or linear sequence):

Step 1: Find the common difference (what you add each time). Call it d.

Step 2: The formula starts with dn.

Step 3: Work out the adjustment: plug in n = 1. Compare d × 1 to the actual first term. The difference is your adjustment.

Step 4: Write: nth term = dn + adjustment

Step 5: CHECK — put n = 1, 2, 3 into your formula and make sure you get the original sequence.

Example: Find the nth term of: 5, 9, 13, 17, ...

1.Common difference (d): 9 − 5 = 4
2.Formula starts with 4n
3.When n=1: 4 × 1 = 4. But the first term is 5. Adjustment = 5 − 4 = +1
4.nth term = 4n + 1
Check:n=1: 4+1=5. n=2: 8+1=9. n=3: 12+1=13. n=4: 16+1=17. All correct!
nth term = 4n + 1
W
Write This Down — Another Example (Negative Adjustment)

Example: Find the nth term of: 3, 7, 11, 15, ...

1.d = 7 − 3 = 4
2.Starts with 4n
3.n=1: 4 × 1 = 4. First term is 3. Adjustment = 3 − 4 = −1
4.nth term = 4n − 1
Check:n=1: 4−1=3. n=2: 8−1=7. n=3: 12−1=11. Correct!
nth term = 4n − 1
!
Don't Confuse — Position vs. Value

n is the position (1st, 2nd, 3rd...). The term is the value at that position.

In the sequence 5, 9, 13, 17: the term at position 3 is 13. So n = 3, and the term = 13.

If they ask "What is the 100th term?", put n = 100 into your formula: 4(100) + 1 = 401.

T
Study Tip — Two Numbers, That's All

The nth term of a linear sequence is always dn + something. You just need to find two numbers: the common difference (d) and the adjustment. That's it. Practice five sequences and this will feel automatic.

?
Why This Matters

Nth term questions appear on almost every IGCSE paper. They're worth 3–4 marks and follow the exact same method every time. Once you've got it, it's guaranteed marks.

  • I can find the common difference of a sequence
  • I can write the nth term formula step by step
  • I can use the nth term to find any term (e.g., the 50th)
  • I always check my formula by plugging in n = 1, 2, 3
Section 2.2 Solving Equations
Solving an equation means finding the value of the letter (usually x) that makes the equation true. Think of it like a puzzle: what number goes in the gap to make both sides balance?
W
Write This Down — The Balance Rule

Whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other side.

An equation is like a set of scales. Both sides are currently balanced. To keep them balanced, every operation you do must happen on BOTH sides.

To "undo" an operation, use its inverse (opposite):

OperationInverse
+ (add)− (subtract)
− (subtract)+ (add)
× (multiply)÷ (divide)
÷ (divide)× (multiply)
W
Write This Down — One-Step Equations

Example 1: Solve x + 7 = 12

1.x is being added to 7. Inverse: subtract 7 from both sides.
2.x + 7 − 7 = 12 − 7
x = 5

Example 2: Solve 3x = 18

1.x is being multiplied by 3. Inverse: divide both sides by 3.
2.3x ÷ 3 = 18 ÷ 3
x = 6
W
Write This Down — Two-Step Equations

When the letter has two things happening to it, undo them in reverse order. Undo the addition/subtraction first, then the multiplication/division.

Example: Solve 4x − 3 = 17

1.x is multiplied by 4, then 3 is subtracted. Undo in reverse: add 3 first.
2.Add 3 to both sides: 4x − 3 + 3 = 17 + 3 → 4x = 20
3.Divide both sides by 4: 4x ÷ 4 = 20 ÷ 4
x = 5
W
Write This Down — Equations with x on Both Sides

If x appears on both sides, collect the x terms on one side first.

Example: Solve 5x + 2 = 3x + 10

1.Get x terms together: subtract 3x from both sides. → 2x + 2 = 10
2.Subtract 2 from both sides: 2x = 8
3.Divide both sides by 2: x = 4
Check:Left: 5(4)+2 = 22. Right: 3(4)+10 = 22. Both sides equal. Correct!
x = 4
W
Write This Down — Equations with Brackets

Expand the brackets first, then solve as normal.

Example: Solve 3(2x − 1) = 15

1.Expand: 3 × 2x = 6x.   3 × (−1) = −3.   So: 6x − 3 = 15
2.Add 3: 6x = 18
3.Divide by 6: x = 3
Check:3(2×3 − 1) = 3(6−1) = 3(5) = 15. Correct!
x = 3
!
Don't Confuse — Expanding Brackets with Negatives

When you expand a bracket with a negative sign, the sign of every term inside flips:

−2(x − 3) = −2x + 6   (NOT −2x − 6)

The minus times the minus gives a plus. This is the most common algebra mistake. Always be extra careful with negative signs.

T
Study Tip — Always Check Your Answer

After solving, substitute your answer back into the original equation. Both sides should be equal. If they're not, you made an error somewhere — go back and find it. This takes 15 seconds and catches mistakes before the examiner does.

  • I can solve one-step equations
  • I can solve two-step equations
  • I can solve equations with x on both sides
  • I can expand brackets and then solve
  • I always check by substituting back in
Section 2.3 Drawing Graphs
Graphs are pictures of equations. Every equation like y = 2x + 1 draws a line on a grid. You need to be able to make a table of values, plot the points, and draw the line.
W
Write This Down — How to Draw a Graph (Step by Step)

Step 1: Make a table. Write the x values you're given (usually −3 to 3 or similar).

Step 2: Substitute each x value into the equation to find y.

Step 3: Plot each (x, y) point on the grid. Use a sharp pencil and make a small cross at each point.

Step 4: Join the points. For a straight line, use a ruler. For a curve, draw a smooth freehand curve through all points.

Example: Draw the graph of y = 2x + 1 for x = −2 to 3.

x2x + 1y
−22(−2) + 1 = −4 + 1−3
−12(−1) + 1 = −2 + 1−1
02(0) + 1 = 0 + 11
12(1) + 1 = 2 + 13
22(2) + 1 = 4 + 15
32(3) + 1 = 6 + 17

Plot the points: (−2, −3), (−1, −1), (0, 1), (1, 3), (2, 5), (3, 7). Join with a ruler.

!
Don't Confuse — Negative Numbers in Tables

When x is negative, be very careful with signs:

2 × (−3) = −6   (positive times negative = negative)

−3 + 1 = −2   (not −4)

If your graph has a point that clearly doesn't fit the line, go back and recheck that calculation. It's almost always a sign error.

T
Study Tip — At Least 3 Points

Always plot at least 3 points. If all 3 lie on a straight line, your calculations are probably right. If one doesn't line up, one of your calculations has an error — recheck it.

  • I can make a table of values from an equation
  • I can plot points accurately on a grid
  • I use a ruler for straight lines
  • I check my graph by making sure all points lie on the line
Section 2.4 Gradient and y = mx + c
Every straight line can be described by the equation y = mx + c. The two letters m and c tell you everything about the line: how steep it is and where it crosses the y-axis.
W
Write This Down — What m and c Mean

m = the gradient (slope/steepness). It tells you how much y increases when x increases by 1.

• If m is positive, the line goes uphill (left to right).

• If m is negative, the line goes downhill.

• If m is zero, the line is horizontal (flat).

c = the y-intercept. This is where the line crosses the y-axis. It's the value of y when x = 0.

W
Write This Down — Reading m and c from an Equation

Examples:

y = 3x + 2:   gradient = 3, y-intercept = 2
y = −x + 5:   gradient = −1, y-intercept = 5
y = ½x:   gradient = ½, y-intercept = 0 (line goes through the origin)
y = 4:   gradient = 0, y-intercept = 4 (horizontal line)
W
Write This Down — How to Calculate the Gradient

Gradient = rise ÷ run = (change in y) ÷ (change in x)

Pick any two points on the line: (x1, y1) and (x2, y2).

Gradient = (y2 − y1) ÷ (x2 − x1)

Example: A line passes through (2, 5) and (6, 13). Find the gradient.

1.Rise: 13 − 5 = 8
2.Run: 6 − 2 = 4
3.Gradient = 8 ÷ 4 = 2
Gradient = 2
W
Write This Down — Finding the Equation of a Line

If you know the gradient and y-intercept, just plug them into y = mx + c.

Example: A line has gradient 3 and crosses the y-axis at (0, −2). Find its equation.

1.m = 3, c = −2
2.y = mx + c = 3x + (−2)
y = 3x − 2
!
Don't Confuse — Gradient vs. y-Intercept

In y = 5 − 2x, the gradient is −2 (the number attached to x) and the y-intercept is 5. The equation is the same as y = −2x + 5, just written in a different order. Always look for the number attached to x to find the gradient.

!
Don't Confuse — Gradient Sign

When calculating gradient, the order matters. If you subtract in one direction for y, you must subtract in the same direction for x.

Using (2, 5) and (6, 13): either do (13−5)/(6−2) = 8/4 = 2, or (5−13)/(2−6) = −8/−4 = 2. Both give the same answer. But don't mix them up: (13−5)/(2−6) = 8/−4 = −2 is WRONG.

T
Study Tip — Quick Sketch

To quickly sketch a line from y = mx + c: mark the y-intercept (c) on the y-axis, then use the gradient to find one more point. If m = 2, go 1 right and 2 up. If m = −3, go 1 right and 3 down. Join with a ruler. Done.

  • I can identify m (gradient) and c (y-intercept) from y = mx + c
  • I can calculate the gradient from two points
  • I know positive gradient = uphill, negative = downhill
  • I can write the equation of a line given gradient and y-intercept
Putting It All Together Key Study Strategies for Algebra
T
Letters Are Just Numbers in Disguise

If algebra feels abstract, replace the letters with numbers first to understand what's happening. For example, if 4x − 3 = 17 feels confusing, think: "What number times 4, minus 3, gives 17?" You might guess 5. Then the algebra method just does that guessing systematically.

T
One Line Per Step

Write every step on a new line. This is the single most important habit in algebra. If you try to do two steps at once, you'll make mistakes and lose marks. One step per line = maximum marks even if you get the final answer wrong.

T
Check Everything

Every topic here has a built-in check:

• Sequences: Put n=1, n=2, n=3 into your nth term formula.

• Equations: Substitute your answer back into the original equation.

• Graphs: Check that all your plotted points lie on the line.

• Gradient: Does the sign match the direction of the line? (Uphill = positive)
