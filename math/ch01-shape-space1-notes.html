Section 1.1 Angle Facts
Angles are the foundation of geometry. Before you can tackle polygons, circles, or anything else in this chapter, you need these basic angle facts memorised.
W
Write This Down — The Five Key Angle Facts

1. Angles on a straight line add up to 180°.

2. Angles at a point add up to 360°.

3. Vertically opposite angles are equal.

4. Angles in a triangle add up to 180°.

5. Angles in a quadrilateral add up to 360°.

Write This Down — Parallel Line Angle Facts

When a line crosses two parallel lines, it creates special angle pairs:

Alternate angles (Z-angles) are equal. They make a Z shape.

Corresponding angles (F-angles) are equal. They make an F shape.

Co-interior angles (C-angles or allied angles) add up to 180°. They make a C or U shape.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Letters

When you see parallel lines on the exam, draw the Z, F, or C on the diagram to identify the angle type. This makes it obvious which rule to use and shows the examiner your reasoning.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

Example: Two parallel lines are crossed by a transversal. One angle is 65°. Find the co-interior angle.

1.Co-interior angles add up to 180°.
2.Missing angle = 180° − 65° = 115°.
Answer: 115°
!
Don't Confuse — Alternate vs. Corresponding

Alternate angles are on opposite sides of the transversal (Z shape). Corresponding angles are on the same side (F shape). Both are equal, but you must name the correct one on the exam to get the mark.

  • I can list the five basic angle facts from memory
  • I know the three parallel line angle rules (alternate, corresponding, co-interior)
  • I can find missing angles using these rules and show my working
Section 1.2 Angles in Polygons
A polygon is any flat shape with straight sides. Triangles (3 sides), quadrilaterals (4), pentagons (5), hexagons (6), etc. There are two key formulae you need.
W
Write This Down — Interior Angle Sum

Sum of interior angles = (n − 2) × 180°

where n = the number of sides.

Example: Find the sum of interior angles in a hexagon (6 sides).

1.Sum = (6 − 2) × 180° = 4 × 180°
Answer: 720°
W
Write This Down — Regular Polygons

A regular polygon has all sides equal and all angles equal.

Each interior angle = (n − 2) × 180° ÷ n

Each exterior angle = 360° ÷ n

And remember: interior + exterior = 180° (they always add up to a straight line).

▶ Interactive: Interior & Exterior Angles of Polygons
Try it: Change the number of sides with the slider and see how interior & exterior angles change. Notice the exterior angles always sum to 360°!
W
Write This Down — Worked Example

Example: Find each interior angle of a regular octagon (8 sides).

1.Each interior angle = (8 − 2) × 180° ÷ 8 = 6 × 180° ÷ 8
2.= 1080° ÷ 8
Answer: 135°
?
Why This Matters

The polygon angle question appears on nearly every IGCSE paper, usually worth 3–4 marks. If you memorise the two formulae, it's free marks every time.

!
Don't Confuse — Interior vs. Exterior

The interior angle is the one INSIDE the polygon. The exterior angle is the one you'd turn through if you walked along the edge. They are NOT the same thing. Always check which one the question asks for.

  • I can calculate the sum of interior angles using (n − 2) × 180°
  • I can find each interior and exterior angle of a regular polygon
  • I know that interior + exterior = 180°
Section 1.3 Symmetry
There are two types of symmetry you need to know: line symmetry (reflective) and rotational symmetry.
W
Write This Down — Two Types of Symmetry

Line symmetry: A shape has a line of symmetry if you can fold it along that line and both halves match exactly. The number of lines of symmetry is how many different ways you can fold it.

Rotational symmetry: A shape has rotational symmetry if you can rotate it less than 360° and it looks the same. The order is how many times it looks the same during a full turn.

ShapeLines of symmetryOrder of rotational symmetry
Equilateral triangle33
Square44
Rectangle22
Regular pentagon55
Regular hexagon66

On the exam, you can use tracing paper to check rotational symmetry. Trace the shape, put your pencil on the centre, and rotate. Count how many times it matches before you get back to the start.

Section 1.4 Circle Calculations
Two formulae to learn for circles. Once you know these, you can also find arc lengths and sector areas. Learn the vocab first: radius (centre to edge), diameter (edge to edge through centre), circumference (distance around the outside).
W
Write This Down — The Two Circle Formulae

Circumference = π × d   (or 2πr)

Area = π × r²

where d = diameter, r = radius. Remember: d = 2r (the diameter is always twice the radius).

Example: Find the circumference and area of a circle with radius 5 cm.

1.Circumference = 2 × π × 5 = 10π = 31.4 cm (1 d.p.)
2.Area = π × 5² = 25π = 78.5 cm² (1 d.p.)
C = 31.4 cm, A = 78.5 cm²
▶ Interactive: Visualise the Area of a Circle
Try it: Increase the number of wedges and watch them rearrange into a rectangle — proving that A = πr².
!
Don't Confuse — Radius vs. Diameter

If the question gives you the diameter, you must halve it to get the radius before using the area formula. This is the single most common mistake on circle questions. Always check: did they give me r or d?

W
Write This Down — Arc Length and Sector Area

A sector is a "slice" of a circle (like a pizza slice). An arc is the curved edge of that slice.

Arc length = (θ / 360) × πd

Sector area = (θ / 360) × πr²

where θ is the angle of the sector in degrees.

Example: Find the arc length of a sector with radius 8 cm and angle 90°.

1.Arc length = (90 / 360) × π × 16
2.= (1/4) × 16π = 4π
Answer: 4π = 12.6 cm (1 d.p.)
▶ Interactive: Arc Length & Sector Area
Try it: Adjust the radius and sector angle to see how arc length and sector area change. Notice they’re always a fraction (θ/360) of the full circle.
T
Study Tip — The Fraction Trick

Arc length and sector area are just a fraction of the full circle. The fraction is always θ/360. So find the full circumference or area first, then multiply by the fraction. Easy.

  • I can calculate circumference using C = πd
  • I can calculate area using A = πr²
  • I always check whether the question gives radius or diameter
  • I can find arc length and sector area using the fraction θ/360
Section 1.5 Area of 2D Shapes
You need to know the area formulae for the key shapes. The exam won't give you these on the formula sheet for Core level — you must memorise them.
W
Write This Down — Area Formulae
ShapeFormula
RectangleA = length × width
TriangleA = ½ × base × height
ParallelogramA = base × perpendicular height
TrapeziumA = ½ × (a + b) × h
CircleA = πr²
▶ Interactive: Parallelogram Area = Rectangle Area
Try it: Drag the slider to cut and rearrange the parallelogram into a rectangle — proving that Area = base × height.
!
Don't Confuse — Perpendicular Height

The height in a triangle or parallelogram must be the perpendicular (vertical) height, NOT the slant height. The height is always at a right angle to the base. If you use the slant side by accident, you'll get the wrong answer.

W
Write This Down — Compound Shapes

For compound (composite) shapes, split them into simple shapes, work out each area separately, then add (or subtract) them.

Example: An L-shaped room is 8 m long and 5 m wide, with a 3 m × 2 m rectangle cut from one corner.

1.Full rectangle area: 8 × 5 = 40 m²
2.Cut-out area: 3 × 2 = 6 m²
3.L-shape area: 40 − 6 = 34 m²
Answer: 34 m²
T
Study Tip — Units Matter

Area is always in squared units (cm², m²). If lengths are in cm, area is in cm². If you mix up units (e.g., one side in cm and one in m), your answer will be completely wrong. Convert first, then calculate.

  • I know the area formulae for rectangle, triangle, parallelogram, trapezium, and circle
  • I always use perpendicular height, not slant height
  • I can find the area of compound shapes by splitting and adding/subtracting
Section 1.6 Volume and Surface Area
Volume measures how much space a 3D shape takes up. Surface area is the total area of all its faces. These questions are worth good marks on the exam.
W
Write This Down — Volume Formulae
ShapeVolume
CuboidV = length × width × height
Prism (any)V = cross-section area × length
CylinderV = πr² × h

Key idea: A prism is any 3D shape with the same cross-section all the way through. A cylinder is a circular prism. A cuboid is a rectangular prism. So really, you just need one formula: V = area of cross-section × length.

W
Write This Down — Surface Area

Surface area = sum of the areas of all faces.

For a cuboid: SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)

For a cylinder: SA = 2πr² + 2πrh   (two circles + the curved part)

Example: Find the volume and surface area of a cylinder with radius 3 cm and height 10 cm.

1.Volume = π × 3² × 10 = π × 9 × 10 = 90π = 283 cm³ (3 s.f.)
2.Surface area = 2π(3)² + 2π(3)(10) = 18π + 60π = 78π = 245 cm² (3 s.f.)
V = 283 cm³, SA = 245 cm²

Try it: Change the dimensions and shape of the prism to see how volume and surface area are calculated. Rotate the 3D view to see all faces.
!
Don't Confuse — Volume Units vs. Area Units

Volume is in cubed units (cm³, m³). Surface area is in squared units (cm², m²). If you write cm² for a volume answer, you lose a mark even if the number is right.

T
Study Tip — Cylinder Curved Surface

If you unroll the curved surface of a cylinder, it becomes a rectangle. The width is the circumference (2πr) and the height is h. So the curved surface area = 2πrh. Visualising this helps you remember the formula.

  • I know V = cross-section area × length for any prism
  • I can calculate the volume of a cylinder
  • I can find the surface area of a cuboid and a cylinder
  • I always use the correct units (cm³ for volume, cm² for area)
Section 1.7 Scale Drawing and Nets
Scale drawings use a ratio to represent real-life objects at a smaller (or larger) size. Nets are flat patterns that fold up to make 3D shapes.
W
Write This Down — Working with Scales

A scale like 1 : 500 means 1 cm on the drawing represents 500 cm (= 5 m) in real life.

Drawing → Real: multiply by the scale factor.

Real → Drawing: divide by the scale factor.

Example: A map has a scale of 1 : 20 000. Two towns are 8 cm apart on the map. What is the real distance?

1.Real distance = 8 × 20 000 = 160 000 cm
2.Convert: 160 000 cm ÷ 100 = 1 600 m ÷ 1 000 = 1.6 km
Answer: 1.6 km
!
Don't Confuse — Unit Conversions with Scales

Scale ratios are in the same unit. 1 : 500 means 1 cm to 500 cm, NOT 1 cm to 500 m. Always convert to useful units (m or km) after you've done the multiplication.

W
Write This Down — Nets

A net is the flat shape that folds up into a 3D shape. Common nets to recognise:

• Cube: 6 connected squares (there are 11 possible arrangements)

• Cuboid: 6 rectangles (3 pairs of matching rectangles)

• Triangular prism: 2 triangles + 3 rectangles

• Cylinder: 2 circles + 1 rectangle (the height = cylinder height, the width = circumference)

Study Tip — Bearings in Scale Drawings

Bearings are measured clockwise from North and always written as 3 digits (e.g., 045°, not 45°). If a scale drawing question involves bearings, use a protractor from the North line and measure clockwise.

Putting It All Together Key Study Strategies for Shape & Space
T
Draw Diagrams

If the question doesn't give you a diagram, draw one yourself. Label everything — sides, angles, measurements. A good diagram is worth 2 minutes of thinking time saved.

T
Show Your Formulae

Before you start calculating, write down the formula you're using. This gets you a method mark even if you make a calculation error. Example: write "A = πr²" before plugging in numbers.

T
Check Your Units

At the end of every answer, ask yourself: "Are my units correct?" Length = cm or m. Area = cm² or m². Volume = cm³ or m³. Angles = degrees (°). This is the easiest mark to lose and the easiest to keep.
