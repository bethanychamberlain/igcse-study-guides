Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Percentage change How big an increase or decrease is relative to the original value. Formula: (change ÷ original) × 100. —
Percentage profit How much profit you make as a % of the cost price: (profit ÷ cost price) × 100. Profit as a % of selling price (wrong — always divide by cost price)
Equivalent fractions Fractions that represent the same value: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6. —
Ratio A way of comparing amounts. 3:5 means 3 parts to 5 parts (8 parts total). Fraction — ratio 3:5 = fraction 3/8, NOT 3/5
Estimate A rough answer found by rounding numbers to 1 significant figure before calculating. Guess — an estimate uses rounding, not guessing
Lower bound The smallest possible value a rounded measurement could be. Measurement minus half the accuracy. Upper bound (that's measurement PLUS half)
Upper bound The largest possible value a rounded measurement could be. Measurement plus half the accuracy. The upper bound uses < (strict), not ≤
BIDMAS Order of operations: Brackets, Indices, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction. BODMAS (same thing — O for "Orders" = I for "Indices")

Fraction → Decimal: divide top by bottom.   Decimal → %: multiply by 100.   % → Fraction: write over 100, simplify.

Exam Trap — Always Divide by the ORIGINAL
If a price goes from $50 to $54, the increase is $4. Divide $4 by $50 (the original), NOT by $54. For profit/loss questions, always divide by the cost price.

Exam Trap — Inequality Notation
If d = 42 m to the nearest metre: 41.5 ≤ d < 42.5. The upper bound uses < (strictly less than), because 42.5 would round UP to 43.

Say It in One Sentence

% change Find the actual change, divide by the original, multiply by 100 — three steps every time.
Converting Fraction → decimal: divide top by bottom. Decimal → %: multiply by 100. % → fraction: write over 100 and simplify.
Estimating Round everything to 1 significant figure, then calculate — use the ≈ symbol for your answer.
Bounds The maximum error is always half the level of accuracy — add and subtract it from the measurement.
Mental % 10% = divide by 10, 5% = half of 10%, 1% = divide by 100, then combine for any percentage.
BIDMAS Brackets first, then Indices, then Division/Multiplication (left to right), then Addition/Subtraction (left to right).
Ratio to fraction Add all parts to get the total — ratio 3:5 means 3 out of 8, not 3 out of 5.

Memory Tricks

Percentage Change — Three Steps Every Time
1. Find the change. 2. Divide by the original. 3. Multiply by 100. Write these three steps at the top of every % change answer and you'll never go wrong.
Ratio to Fraction — "Add for the Total"
See a ratio like 5:7:3? Add them up: 5 + 7 + 3 = 15 total parts. Now each part is over 15. The "3" part = 3/15 = 1/5 = 20%.
Multiplying by 5 — The Lazy Way
Multiply by 10 and halve. E.g. 36 × 5 = 360 ÷ 2 = 180. Works every time and is faster than long multiplication.
Building Percentages from 10% and 1%
15% = 10% + 5% (which is half of 10%).   17.5% = 10% + 5% + 2.5% (which is half of 5%). Break any percentage into chunks of 10%, 5%, and 1%.
Bounds — "Halve the Accuracy"
"To the nearest cm" → error = ±0.5 cm. "To the nearest 0.1 cm" → error = ±0.05. "To the nearest 10 m" → error = ±5 m. Always just halve whatever unit they tell you.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Find the percentage increase from A to B" ((B − A) ÷ A) × 100. Always divide by the original.
"Which is greater: 3/8, 0.4, or 35%?" Convert all to decimals: 0.375, 0.4, 0.35. 0.4 is greatest.
"Estimate the value of 49.3 × 0.52" Round to 1 s.f.: 50 × 0.5 = 25. Use the ≈ symbol.
"A length is 35 cm to the nearest cm. Write down the bounds." 34.5 ≤ length < 35.5. Half a cm either side. Upper bound is strict (<).
"Find 15% of $240" 10% = $24, 5% = $12, so 15% = $24 + $12 = $36.
"Express the ratio 5:3 as fractions" Total parts = 8. Fractions are 5/8 and 3/8 (not 5/3).
"Work out 8 + 3 × 4" Multiply first (BIDMAS): 3 × 4 = 12, then 8 + 12 = 20. NOT 44.
"The fraction bar acts as..." Brackets around the top and bottom. Always work out top and bottom separately first.
