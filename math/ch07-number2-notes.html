Section 7.1 Percentage Change
Percentage change tells you how big an increase or decrease is relative to the original. A $70 increase on a $100 jacket is huge (70%), but a $70 increase on a $7000 car is tiny (1%).
W
Write This Down — The Formulas

Percentage increase = (actual increase ÷ original value) × 100

Percentage decrease = (actual decrease ÷ original value) × 100

For profit/loss questions, the "original value" is always the cost price (what you paid), not the selling price.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

Example: A car's price increases from $6400 to $6800. Find the percentage increase.

1.Actual increase = $6800 − $6400 = $400
2.Percentage increase = (400 ÷ 6400) × 100 = 6.25%
Answer: 6.25% increase
!
Don't Confuse — Always Divide by the ORIGINAL

A common mistake: dividing by the new value instead of the original. If a price goes from $50 to $54, the increase is $4. You divide $4 by $50 (the original), NOT by $54.

For percentage profit, divide by the cost price. For percentage loss, divide by the cost price. Always the starting point.

T
Study Tip — The Three-Step Method

Every percentage change question follows the same three steps: (1) find the actual change, (2) divide by the original, (3) multiply by 100. Write these three steps every time and you won't go wrong.

  • I can calculate a percentage increase
  • I can calculate a percentage decrease
  • I know to always divide by the original value, not the new value
  • I can calculate percentage profit and percentage loss
Section 7.2 Fractions, Ratio, Decimals and Percentage
Fractions, decimals, and percentages are just three different ways of writing the same number. Being able to switch between them quickly is essential — exams test this constantly.
W
Write This Down — How to Convert
FromToMethod
Fraction → DecimalDivide the top by the bottom. E.g. 7/8 = 7 ÷ 8 = 0.875
Decimal → FractionWrite as "over 10, 100, 1000..." then simplify. E.g. 0.35 = 35/100 = 7/20
Fraction → PercentageMultiply by 100. E.g. 3/8 = (3/8) × 100 = 37.5%
Percentage → FractionWrite over 100, then simplify. E.g. 45% = 45/100 = 9/20
Decimal → PercentageMultiply by 100 (move decimal point 2 places right). E.g. 0.72 = 72%
Percentage → DecimalDivide by 100 (move decimal point 2 places left). E.g. 15% = 0.15
?
Why This Matters

Exam questions often ask you to compare values given in different forms — for example, "Which is greater: 1/2, 45%, or 0.6?" You have to convert them all to the same form (usually decimals) to compare. If you can't convert quickly, you lose easy marks.

W
Write This Down — Key Equivalents to Memorise
FractionDecimalPercentage

1/30.333...33.3%

!
Don't Confuse — Ratio vs. Fraction

The ratio 5:7:3 means three parts adding up to 15 total parts. To find a percentage of one part: divide that part by the total. E.g., the percentage represented by the "3" part = (3/15) × 100 = 20%.

Don't confuse "5:7" with "5/7". A ratio of 5:7 means 5 parts out of 12 total, so as a fraction it's 5/12, not 5/7.

  • I can convert between fractions, decimals, and percentages
  • I know the key equivalents (1/4 = 0.25 = 25%, etc.)
  • I can compare values given in different forms
  • I understand how ratios relate to fractions and percentages
Section 7.3 Estimating
Estimating means getting a rough answer quickly by rounding numbers to simpler values. It's used to check calculator answers and in real-life situations where an exact answer isn't needed.
W
Write This Down — How to Estimate

Step 1: Round each number to one significant figure (or the nearest easy number).

Step 2: Do the calculation with the rounded numbers.

Step 3: Write your answer with the ≈ symbol (meaning "approximately equal to").

Example: Estimate 9.7 × 3.1

1.Round: 9.7 ≈ 10,   3.1 ≈ 3
2.10 × 3 = 30
Estimate: about 30   (actual = 30.07, so the estimate is very close)
W
Write This Down — Estimating with Division

Example: Estimate 102.7 ÷ 19.6

1.Round: 102.7 ≈ 100,   19.6 ≈ 20
2.100 ÷ 20 = 5
Estimate: about 5   (actual = 5.24)
?
Why This Matters

If you use a calculator and get 5956.8 for 58.4 × 102, you should spot the mistake. A quick estimate: 60 × 100 = 6000. The answer should be near 6000, and 5956.8 is indeed close. But if you'd got 595.68, the estimate would tell you something went wrong.

T
Study Tip — Multiplying and Dividing by Numbers Near 1

Multiplying by a number greater than 1 makes the result bigger. Multiplying by a number less than 1 makes it smaller. Dividing by a number greater than 1 makes it smaller. Use this to sense-check your answers.

  • I can round numbers to one significant figure
  • I can estimate calculations by rounding first
  • I use estimation to check my calculator answers
  • I understand the ≈ symbol
Section 7.4 Errors in Measurement (Bounds of Accuracy)
Every measurement has a degree of error. When someone says a book is "22 cm long," the true length could be anything from 21.5 cm up to (but not quite) 22.5 cm. The lower bound and upper bound are the smallest and largest values the measurement could actually be.
W
Write This Down — The Rule for Bounds

The maximum possible error is always half the level of accuracy.

Lower bound = measurement − half a unit

Upper bound = measurement + half a unit

MeasurementAccuracyLower BoundUpper Bound
22 cmnearest cm21.5 cm22.5 cm
3.8 cmnearest 0.1 cm3.75 cm3.85 cm
4200 mnearest 100 m4150 m4250 m
43 gnearest gram42.5 g43.5 g
W
Write This Down — Writing It Mathematically

If a length d is 42 m to the nearest metre:

41.5 ≤ d < 42.5

Note: the lower bound uses ≤ (can equal), but the upper bound uses < (strictly less than). This is because a value of exactly 42.5 would round UP to 43, not down to 42.

!
Don't Confuse — The Upper Bound Is Strict

The upper bound is never actually reached. If a mass is 72 kg to the nearest kg, the upper bound is 72.5 kg — but a mass of exactly 72.5 kg would round to 73, not 72. So we write m < 72.5, not m ≤ 72.5.

T
Study Tip — "To the Nearest" Tells You Everything

"To the nearest cm" means the error is ±0.5 cm. "To the nearest 0.1 cm" means the error is ±0.05 cm. "To the nearest 10 m" means ±5 m. Just halve whatever the level of accuracy is.

  • I can find upper and lower bounds for a measurement
  • I know the maximum error is half the level of accuracy
  • I can write bounds using inequality notation (e.g. 41.5 ≤ d < 42.5)
  • I understand why the upper bound uses < not ≤
Section 7.5 Mental Arithmetic
Mental arithmetic is about doing calculations in your head without a calculator. The key is knowing quick tricks and having number facts at your fingertips. The textbook includes several timed tests — practice them!
W
Write This Down — Key Mental Strategies

Multiplying by 10, 100, 1000: Move the digits left (or add zeros). 3.5 × 100 = 350.

Dividing by 10, 100, 1000: Move the digits right. 450 ÷ 100 = 4.5.

Finding percentages mentally:

• 10% = divide by 10

• 5% = half of 10%

• 1% = divide by 100

• 15% = 10% + 5%

Multiplying by 5: Multiply by 10 and halve. E.g. 36 × 5 = 360 ÷ 2 = 180.

T
Study Tip — Practice the Timed Tests

The textbook has 7 mental arithmetic tests with 30 questions each. Time yourself (30 minutes per test) and track your score. These are the best way to build speed and confidence. Do one a day for a week.

  • I can multiply and divide by 10, 100, and 1000 in my head
  • I can find 10%, 5%, and 1% of any number mentally
  • I have practised at least two of the timed tests
  • I can quickly convert between simple fractions and decimals in my head
Section 7.6 Using a Calculator
Calculators can do the maths, but you have to decide the correct order of button presses. The exam expects you to know BIDMAS/BODMAS and to use brackets properly. Always check your answer with an estimate.
W
Write This Down — Order of Operations (BIDMAS)

Brackets first, then Indices (powers), then Division and Multiplication (left to right), then Addition and Subtraction (left to right).

Example: 6 × 4 − 8 ÷ 2

1.Multiply: 6 × 4 = 24
2.Divide: 8 ÷ 2 = 4
3.Subtract: 24 − 4 = 20
Answer: 20 (NOT 8)
W
Write This Down — The Division Line IS a Bracket

When you see a fraction bar like (4.2) / (1.2 − 0.7), the division line acts as brackets around the top AND the bottom. On a calculator, you MUST use brackets or the memory button.

Example: Work out 4.2 / (1.2 − 0.7)

1.Work out the bottom first: 1.2 − 0.7 = 0.5
2.Then divide: 4.2 ÷ 0.5 = 8.4
Answer: 8.4
!
Don't Confuse — Negative Numbers on a Calculator

To enter a negative number, use the (−) or (+/−) button, NOT the subtract button. If you press subtract first, you're doing a subtraction, not entering a negative number.

T
Study Tip — Always Check with an Estimate

After every calculator question, do a quick estimate by rounding to 1 significant figure. If your calculator says 10.3 and your estimate is about 9, that's fine. If your calculator says 103 and your estimate is about 9, you pressed the wrong buttons.

  • I know BIDMAS and can apply it correctly
  • I know that a fraction bar acts as brackets
  • I can enter negative numbers correctly on my calculator
  • I always check calculator answers with an estimate
