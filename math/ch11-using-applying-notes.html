Section 11.1 The Problem-Solving Toolkit
The key to solving unfamiliar maths problems isn't knowing more formulas — it's having a systematic approach. These strategies work on investigation tasks, exam questions, and real-world problems alike.
W
Write This Down — The 5-Step Problem-Solving Process

Step 1: Understand the problem. Read it twice. What are you being asked? What information do you have?

Step 2: Try a simpler case. If the problem is complicated, try it with smaller numbers first.

Step 3: Be systematic. Organise your results in a table. Don't just try random things.

Step 4: Look for a pattern. Can you see a rule? Does the pattern hold for more cases?

Step 5: Generalise. Can you write a formula or rule that works for ANY case, not just the ones you tried?

?
Why This Matters

IGCSE Paper 3 often includes questions where you haven't seen the exact problem before. They test whether you can think mathematically, not just repeat a method. These skills also directly prepare you for 11th-grade math in the US, where problem-solving and reasoning are heavily emphasised.

T
Study Tip — "If It's Too Complicated, Try an Easier Case"

This is the single most powerful problem-solving strategy. If a question asks about a 10 × 10 grid, try a 2 × 2 grid first, then 3 × 3, then 4 × 4. The pattern will usually become obvious from the smaller cases.

  • I can list the 5 steps of problem-solving
  • I understand why starting with a simpler case helps
  • I know to organise results in a table
Section 11.1 (continued) Finding Patterns and Rules
Many investigation tasks ask you to find a pattern in numbers or diagrams, describe the pattern, and then write a general formula.
W
Write This Down — How to Find a Pattern

Step 1: Calculate several cases and record the results in a table.

Step 2: Look at the differences between consecutive results. Are they constant? Increasing?

Step 3: If the differences are constant, the rule is linear (something like dn + c).

Step 4: Test your rule on the cases you already have.

Step 5: Use your rule to predict a case you haven't tried. Then check it.

Example: "Opposite corners" in a number grid (9 columns).

In a 2×2 square: products of opposite corners differ by 9.

In a 3×3 square: products of opposite corners differ by 36.

In a 4×4 square: the difference is 81.

Square sizeDifferencePattern
2 × 299 × 12
3 × 3369 × 22
4 × 4819 × 32
Rule: For an n × n square in a 9-column grid, the difference = 9(n − 1)2
!
Don't Confuse — Spotting a Pattern vs. Proving It

Finding that a pattern works for 3 or 4 cases does NOT prove it always works. On the exam, they'll often ask you to "test" your rule on additional cases. Always do this — it's easy marks.

If they ask you to "prove" the rule, you need to use algebra (e.g. show it works for general n, not just specific numbers).

  • I can organise results in a table
  • I can look at differences to identify a pattern
  • I can write a formula to describe the pattern
  • I know to test my formula on extra cases to verify it
Section 11.1 (continued) Real-World Problem Solving
Some problems require you to compare options, calculate costs, or make decisions using mathematics. These are practical skills you'll use throughout your life.
W
Write This Down — Comparison Problems

When comparing options (e.g. which car hire company is cheapest):

Step 1: Identify the variables (how many days, how many km, etc.).

Step 2: Calculate the total cost for each option.

Step 3: Consider different scenarios (what if you drive 200 km vs. 800 km?).

Step 4: State which is best AND for what situation.

Example: Hiring a car for 7 days.

CompanyCost for 500 kmCost for 1000 km
Gibson (\$170/week)\$170\$170
Snowdon (\$10/day + 6.5c/km)\$70 + \$32.50 = \$102.50\$70 + \$65 = \$135
Hav-a-car (\$60/week + 22c/km over 500)\$60\$60 + \$110 = \$170
Hav-a-car is cheapest for low mileage; Gibson is best for very high mileage.
T
Study Tip — Show Your Working, Even on "Explain" Questions

When a question says "explain your answer" or "give reasons," the examiner wants to see calculations, not just words. "Company A is cheaper" gets 0 marks. "Company A costs \$102.50 while Company B costs \$170, so A is cheaper by \$67.50" gets full marks.

  • I can set up a comparison table for different options
  • I consider multiple scenarios, not just one
  • I show calculations in my explanations, not just words
Section 11.2 Puzzles, Games, and Mental Maths
Crossnumber puzzles, number messages, and estimation games sharpen your arithmetic skills and build fluency with the operations you need throughout the course.
W
Write This Down — Key Arithmetic Skills Tested

These puzzles practice skills from across the whole course:

• BIDMAS/BODMAS — correct order of operations

• Powers and roots — squares, cubes, and their roots

• Negative numbers — all four operations with negatives

• Fractions and decimals — converting between them, mixed operations

• Estimation — rounding and approximating to check answers quickly

?
Why This Matters

Paper 1 of the IGCSE (the non-calculator paper) requires you to do all of these without a calculator. Strong mental maths saves time on both papers — even on Paper 3 (calculator), you can estimate to check your answer is sensible before writing it down.

T
Study Tip — Estimation as a Check

Before writing your final answer, always estimate: "Is this answer roughly right?"

If you calculate 37 × 5 × 3 and get 555, estimate: 40 × 5 × 3 = 600. Is 555 close to 600? Yes — so it's probably right.

If you get 55.5, something went wrong — it should be near 600, not 55.

  • I can apply BIDMAS to multi-step calculations
  • I can do basic calculations without a calculator
  • I use estimation to check my answers
Pulling It Together Investigation Checklist
When you're working on an investigation or extended problem, use this checklist to make sure you've covered everything the examiner is looking for.
W
Write This Down — The Investigation Checklist

For any investigation, make sure you:

a) Try simpler cases if the problem is too complicated.

b) Draw your own diagrams where helpful.

c) Make tables of your results — be systematic.

d) Look for patterns in the numbers.

e) Find a rule or formula to describe the results.

f) Predict further results using your rule.

g) Explain any rules you find — say WHY they work, not just WHAT they are.

h) Extend the task — ask "what happens if...?" questions.

T
The Most Important Exam Skill

Show your working. In problem-solving questions, the method marks are worth more than the final answer. Even if your final answer is wrong, correct working can still earn most of the marks. Write every step clearly, one line at a time.
