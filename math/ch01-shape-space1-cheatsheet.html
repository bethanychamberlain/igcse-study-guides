Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Acute angle An angle less than 90°. Obtuse (90°–180°) or Reflex (>180°)
Vertically opposite Two angles formed when lines cross — they are always equal. Adjacent angles (which are NOT equal)
Alternate angles Z-shaped angles between parallel lines — they are equal. Corresponding (F-shape) or Co-interior (C-shape, sum to 180°)
Polygon Any flat shape with straight sides (triangle, quad, pentagon, etc.). Circle (not a polygon — no straight sides)
Regular polygon All sides equal AND all angles equal. Irregular polygon (sides or angles differ)
Circumference The distance around the outside of a circle. Area (the space inside), Diameter (a straight line through the centre)
Radius Distance from the centre of a circle to the edge. Diameter (which is twice the radius)
Sector A "pizza slice" of a circle (two radii + an arc). Segment (the region between a chord and the arc)
Prism A 3D shape with the same cross-section all the way through. Pyramid (comes to a point)
Net A flat pattern that folds up into a 3D shape. —

Key Formulae — Memorise These

What Formula Watch Out
Interior angle sum (n − 2) × 180° n = number of sides
Each exterior angle (regular) 360° ÷ n Interior + Exterior = 180°
Circumference π × d  (or 2πr) Check: given r or d?
Circle area π × r² Halve d first if given diameter
Arc length (θ/360) × πd θ = sector angle in degrees
Sector area (θ/360) × πr² It's just a fraction of the full circle
Triangle area ½ × base × height Height must be perpendicular
Parallelogram area base × perpendicular height NOT the slant side
Trapezium area ½ × (a + b) × h a & b = the two parallel sides
Prism volume cross-section area × length Works for cuboids, cylinders, any prism
Cylinder volume πr² × h A cylinder is a circular prism
Cuboid surface area 2(lw + lh + wh) Three different pairs of faces
Cylinder surface area 2πr² + 2πrh Two circles + the curved rectangle
The Fraction Trick for Sectors
Arc length and sector area are just a fraction of the full circle. The fraction is always θ/360. Find the full circumference or full area first, then multiply by that fraction.

Say It in One Sentence

Angle sum (polygon) Split any polygon into triangles from one vertex: number of triangles = (n − 2), total degrees = that × 180°.
Exterior angles The exterior angles of any polygon always add up to 360°, no matter how many sides it has.
Line symmetry If you can fold a shape along a line and both halves match exactly, that line is a line of symmetry.
Rotational symmetry The order is how many times the shape looks the same during one full turn — a square has order 4, a regular hexagon has order 6.
Compound shapes Split into simple shapes, find each area separately, then add (or subtract if there's a hole).
Prism volume Find the area of the end face (the cross-section), then multiply by the length — this one formula covers cuboids, cylinders, and triangular prisms.
Scale drawings Scale 1 : 500 means 1 cm on paper = 500 cm in real life. Drawing to real = multiply; real to drawing = divide.
Bearings Always from North, always clockwise, always three digits (045°, not 45°).

Memory Tricks

Z, F, C for Parallel Lines
When you see parallel lines on the exam, physically draw the letter Z (alternate/equal), F (corresponding/equal), or C (co-interior/add to 180°) right on the diagram. This makes the correct rule obvious and shows the examiner your reasoning.
Radius vs. Diameter — "r Is Half"
The area formula uses r (the little one). If the question gives you a diameter, halve it first. Every time you see a circle question, ask: "Did they give me r or d?" This one check prevents the most common circle mistake.
Perpendicular Height — "Right Angle to the Base"
For triangles and parallelograms, the height must be at a right angle to the base — picture a dotted line dropping straight down, not the slant edge. If you use the slant side by mistake, the answer is always too big.
Cylinder Curved Surface — "Unroll the Label"
Imagine peeling the label off a tin can. It becomes a rectangle. Width = circumference (2πr), height = h. So curved surface area = 2πrh. Visualising the unrolled label makes the formula stick.
Units Checklist
Length = cm, m. Area = cm², m². Volume = cm³, m³. Writing the wrong unit loses a mark even if the number is perfect. Check your units last thing before moving on.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Find the sum of interior angles of a polygon" Use (n − 2) × 180°. Count the sides first.
"Find each exterior angle of a regular polygon" 360° ÷ n. Then interior = 180° − exterior.
"State the order of rotational symmetry" How many times the shape maps onto itself in one full turn. Regular n-gon = order n.
"Find the circumference" π × d (or 2πr). Check whether they gave radius or diameter.
"Find the area of the shaded region" Usually: big area − small area. Find each part separately, then subtract.
"Find the arc length / sector area" It's a fraction of the full circle: multiply by θ/360.
"Find the volume of this prism" Cross-section area × length. Find the end-face area first.
"Find the surface area" Add up the areas of ALL faces. For a cuboid: 2(lw + lh + wh). For a cylinder: 2πr² + 2πrh.
"A map has scale 1 : 25 000. Find the real distance." Multiply the map measurement by 25 000, then convert units (cm → m → km).
"Give your answer in terms of π" Leave π in the answer (e.g. 25π cm²). Do NOT multiply out to a decimal.
Exam Trap — "Not Drawn Accurately"
If the diagram says "not drawn accurately," do NOT measure with a ruler or protractor. Use the angle/length rules and formulae instead. The diagram is just there to help you visualise.
