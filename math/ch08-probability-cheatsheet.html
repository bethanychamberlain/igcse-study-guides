Probability

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Probability A number from 0 to 1 that measures how likely something is. 0 = impossible, 1 = certain. A number greater than 1 (impossible for probability)
Outcome A single possible result. E.g. rolling a 4 on a dice. Event (an event can include multiple outcomes)
Mutually exclusive Events that cannot happen at the same time. E.g. rolling a 3 AND a 5 on one dice. Independent events (those CAN both happen, just without affecting each other)
Relative frequency An estimate of probability from experiments: (times it happened) ÷ (total trials). Theoretical probability (that uses equally likely outcomes, not experiments)
Expected frequency The predicted number of times an event will happen: probability × number of trials. Guaranteed frequency (expected is a prediction, not a certainty)
Venn diagram Overlapping circles showing how items belong to different groups. Rectangle = universal set. —
Tree diagram A branching diagram showing all possible outcomes of two or more events in sequence. —
With replacement You put the item back before picking again. Probabilities stay the same. Without replacement (total goes down by 1 each time, probabilities change)

All the Probability Formulas You Need

What You Need Formula
Basic probability P(event) = favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes
P(not A) 1 − P(A)
AND rule (both happen) Multiply: P(A) × P(B)
OR rule (either happens) Add: P(A) + P(B)   [if mutually exclusive]
Relative frequency times event happened ÷ total trials
Expected frequency probability × number of trials
The Golden Rule
AND = multiply (×).   OR = add (+). Get these two rules right and you can solve almost any probability question.

Step-by-Step: Basic Probability Question

Method — Finding P(event)
Step 1:Count the favourable outcomes (the ones you want).
Step 2:Count the total outcomes (everything possible).
Step 3:Divide: P = favourable ÷ total.
Check:Is your answer between 0 and 1? If not, recount.

Step-by-Step: Tree Diagram Question

Method — Two-Event Problems
Step 1:Draw branches for Event 1. Label each with outcome AND probability.
Step 2:From each branch end, draw branches for Event 2. Label again.
Step 3:Check: probabilities on each set of branches add up to 1.
Step 4:AND = multiply along branches to the end of each path.
Step 5:OR = add up the paths that match what the question asks.
Check:All end probabilities added together must equal 1.
Exam Trap — Without Replacement
If the question says "without replacement," the second pick probabilities change. Total goes down by 1, and the group you picked from goes down by 1. E.g. 3 red out of 8 total → after picking red: 2 red out of 7 total.

Step-by-Step: Venn Diagram Question

Method — Filling In a Venn Diagram
Step 1:Always fill in the overlap (both) first.
Step 2:Subtract the overlap from each circle total to get "only A" and "only B."
Step 3:Add up all regions. Subtract from the grand total to get "neither."
Step 4:For probability: divide the relevant number by the total.
Check:All four regions (only A, both, only B, neither) add up to the grand total.

With Replacement vs Without Replacement

With Replacement Without Replacement
What happens Put item back before next pick Don't put item back

Say It in One Sentence

Probability scale 0 = impossible, 0.5 = even chance, 1 = certain — probability is always between 0 and 1.
P(not A) The chance of something NOT happening = 1 minus the chance of it happening.
Mutually exclusive Events that can't happen at the same time — their probabilities add up to 1 if they cover everything.
Relative frequency Estimate probability from experiments: divide the number of successes by the total number of trials.
Expected frequency Probability × number of trials gives you the predicted count — but reality may differ slightly.
Venn overlap Always fill in the "both" section first, then subtract to find the "only" sections.
Tree diagram Multiply along branches (AND), add between paths (OR), and all end probabilities must total 1.

Memory Tricks

AND = Multiply, OR = Add
AND means Across (multiply along the branches). OR means Open up (add different paths together).
"At Least One" = Use 1 Minus
If the question asks "at least one," it's almost always easiest to calculate P(none) and subtract from 1. P(at least one) = 1 − P(none).
Venn Diagrams — Middle First
Think: "The middle is the most important part." Start there, subtract outwards, then whatever's left goes outside the circles.
Check Your Tree — "All Ends = 1"
After you finish a tree diagram, add up ALL the final path probabilities. If they don't equal 1, you've got an error. This 10-second check catches most mistakes.
"Not Red" Isn't Just "Blue"
If a bag has red, blue, AND green balls, P(not red) includes both blue and green. Don't assume "not X" means only one other option.
More Trials = Better Estimate
A dice rolled 10 times gives a rough estimate. A dice rolled 1000 times gives a much more reliable one. If asked "How could you improve the estimate?" the answer is always "Do more trials."

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"A bag has 4 red and 6 blue balls. Find P(red)." Favourable = 4, Total = 10. P(red) = 4/10 = 2/5
"P(rain) = 0.3. Find P(no rain)." 1 − 0.3 = 0.7
"Find P(A AND B)" Multiply: P(A) × P(B). Go along the tree branches.
"Find P(A OR B)" Add the probabilities of each valid path.
"Two balls are drawn without replacement..." Second pick probabilities change. Total drops by 1.
"How many times would you expect a six in 300 rolls?" P(six) × 300 = (1/6) × 300 = 50
"The dice was rolled 200 times and 3 appeared 42 times. Estimate P(3)." Relative frequency = 42 ÷ 200 = 0.21
"Is the dice fair?" Compare relative frequency to 1/6 ≈ 0.167. If very different, probably biased.
"Find P(at least one red)" Use 1 − P(no red). Calculate P(no red) using the tree, then subtract from 1.
"From the Venn diagram, find P(only A)" Use the number in circle A that is NOT in the overlap, divided by the total.
