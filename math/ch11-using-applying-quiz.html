Foundation — Arithmetic Fluency

Worth 1-2 marks each. Test your mental maths and BIDMAS skills.
1
Work out without a calculator:   (a) 327 + 198   (b) 1000 − 913   (c) 37 × 5 × 3
3 marks
Answer
(a) 327 + 198 = 525 [1] — (tip: 327 + 200 − 2 = 525)
(b) 1000 − 913 = 87 [1]
(c) 37 × 5 × 3 = 185 × 3 = 555 [1]
For (a), adding 200 then subtracting 2 is faster than column addition. Use tricks like this to speed up mental maths.

Work out, showing your order of operations:   (a) (11 × 5) ÷ 8   (b) 102 + 12   (c) 32 × 11
3 marks
Answer

BIDMAS: powers first, then multiplication/division, then addition. In (b), square both numbers first, then add.

Intermediate — Can You Find the Pattern?

Worth 3-4 marks each. Find patterns, make predictions, test your rules.
4
Look at this sequence of diagrams. Diagram 1 has 1 square. Diagram 2 has 5 squares. Diagram 3 has 13 squares. (a) How many squares are in Diagram 4? [2] (b) Describe the pattern in words. [1]
3 marks
Answer
Differences: 5 − 1 = 4,   13 − 5 = 8.   The differences are 4, 8, so the next difference = 12 [1].
Diagram 4: 13 + 12 = 25 squares [1].

(b) The differences between consecutive terms increase by 4 each time (4, 8, 12, 16, ...) [1]. Each new "ring" of squares adds 4 more than the previous ring.
When the differences aren't constant, look at the "second differences" (differences of the differences). If those are constant, the pattern is quadratic.

5
The final score in a football match was 3–2. How many different scores were possible at half-time? List them all systematically.
3 marks
Answer
Team A could have scored 0, 1, 2, or 3 goals by half-time. Team B could have scored 0, 1, or 2 goals by half-time [1].
Possible half-time scores: 0–0, 0–1, 0–2, 1–0, 1–1, 1–2, 2–0, 2–1, 2–2, 3–0, 3–1, 3–2 [1].
Total: 12 different half-time scores [1].
Be systematic! Fix Team A's score (0, then 1, then 2, then 3) and list all possible Team B scores for each. That way you won't miss any. The formula is (a+1)(b+1) where a-b is the final score.

6
Work out the following using correct order of operations (no calculator):   (a) (−3)2 + 42   (b) (−8) ÷ (−16)   (c) (21 − 18)3
3 marks
Answer
(a) (−3)2 + 42 = 9 + 16 = 25 [1]
(b) (−8) ÷ (−16) = 0.5 (or 1/2) [1] — negative ÷ negative = positive
(c) (21 − 18)3 = 33 = 27 [1] — brackets first, then power
Remember: (−3)2 = 9 (positive), not −9. The brackets mean the whole thing is squared.

7
Estimate the answer to 49.3 × 2.1 ÷ 9.8 by rounding each number to 1 significant figure. Then explain whether your estimate is likely to be higher or lower than the exact answer.
3 marks
Answer
Round: 49.3 ≈ 50,   2.1 ≈ 2,   9.8 ≈ 10 [1]
Estimate: 50 × 2 ÷ 10 = 100 ÷ 10 = 10 [1]
The estimate is likely to be slightly lower than the exact answer because we rounded 9.8 up to 10 (dividing by a larger number gives a smaller result), and the effect of rounding 2.1 down to 2 partially offsets rounding 49.3 up [1].
(Exact answer: 49.3 × 2.1 ÷ 9.8 = 10.56...)
Estimation questions want you to show the rounding, the calculation, and ideally a comment on accuracy. Always round to 1 significant figure unless told otherwise.

Advanced — Can You Reason and Investigate?

Worth 4-6 marks each. Multi-step problems requiring systematic investigation.
8
A large cube is made of 27 small unit cubes (3 × 3 × 3). All six faces of the large cube are painted green. (a) How many unit cubes have 3 green faces? [1] (b) How many have 2 green faces? [1] (c) How many have 1 green face? [1] (d) How many have 0 green faces? [1] (e) Check your answers add up to 27. [1]
5 marks
Answer
(a) 3 green faces: corner cubes = 8 [1] (a cube always has 8 corners)
(b) 2 green faces: edge cubes (not corners) = 12 [1] (each of the 12 edges has 1 middle cube)
(c) 1 green face: face-centre cubes = 6 [1] (each of the 6 faces has 1 centre cube)
(d) 0 green faces: the hidden cube in the very centre = 1 [1]
(e) Check: 8 + 12 + 6 + 1 = 27 [1]
This is a classic investigation question. For an n × n × n cube: corners = 8, edges = 12(n−2), faces = 6(n−2)2, hidden = (n−2)3. Try n = 4 to test your understanding.

9
You have a square sheet of card, 24 cm × 24 cm. You cut equal squares from each corner and fold up the sides to make an open box. (a) If you cut 2 cm squares from each corner, what are the dimensions and volume of the box? [3] (b) If you cut 4 cm squares, what is the volume? [1] (c) Which gives the larger volume? [1]
5 marks
Answer
(a) Cut 2 cm from each corner:
Base: 24 − 2 − 2 = 20 cm each side [1]. Height = 2 cm.
Volume = 20 × 20 × 2 = 800 cm3 [2]

(b) Cut 4 cm from each corner:
Base: 24 − 4 − 4 = 16 cm each side. Height = 4 cm.
Volume = 16 × 16 × 4 = 1024 cm3 [1]

(c) Cutting 4 cm corners gives a larger volume (1024 > 800) [1].
The investigation extends to: what size corners give the maximum volume? (Answer: 4 cm for a 24 cm card, or in general 1/6 of the card's side length.) You'd find this by trying many values and spotting where the volume peaks.

10
In a 4 × 7 rectangle drawn on squared paper, a diagonal line is drawn from one corner to the opposite corner. The diagonal passes through some of the small squares. (a) By drawing the rectangle, count how many squares the diagonal passes through. [2] (b) A rectangle is 640 × 250. Without drawing, predict how many squares the diagonal passes through. Explain your reasoning. [4]
6 marks
Answer
(a) Drawing the 4 × 7 rectangle and counting: the diagonal passes through 10 squares [2].

(b) The formula for an a × b rectangle is: a + b − HCF(a, b) [2]
where HCF is the highest common factor.
HCF(640, 250) = 10 [1]
Squares = 640 + 250 − 10 = 880 [1]
This is a famous investigation. To find the formula, try several rectangles, record results in a table, and notice that it's always width + height − HCF. Testing: 4 + 7 − HCF(4,7) = 4 + 7 − 1 = 10, which matches part (a).
