Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State the rule for the forces between magnetic poles. Name three magnetic materials.
2 marks
Answer
Like poles repel; unlike poles attract [1]. Three magnetic (ferromagnetic) materials: iron, steel, nickel (also accept cobalt) [1].
Copper, aluminium, brass, and zinc are NOT magnetic. This is a common mistake.

2
State two ways to increase the strength of an electromagnet.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of:
• Increase the current [1]
• Increase the number of turns on the coil [1]
• Add a soft iron core (if not already present) [1]
Do not say "use a bigger battery" — say "increase the current." The examiner wants precise scientific language.

3
What is the difference between a step-up transformer and a step-down transformer?
2 marks
Answer
A step-up transformer has more turns on the secondary coil than the primary, so it increases the voltage [1]. A step-down transformer has fewer turns on the secondary coil than the primary, so it decreases the voltage [1].
Mention BOTH the turns and the voltage effect to get full marks.

4
In Fleming's left-hand rule, what does each finger represent? (Thumb, first finger, second finger)
2 marks
Answer
Thumb = Force (or thrust/motion of the wire) [1]
First finger = Field direction (N to S)
Second finger = Current direction (conventional, + to −) [1]
All three must be correct for full marks. Remember: Thumb = Thrust, First = Field, seCond = Current.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain HOW things work and apply rules, not just name them.
5
Explain why the core of an electromagnet is made of iron rather than steel.
3 marks
Answer
Iron is a soft magnetic material [1]. It is easily magnetised when the current flows [1], and it loses its magnetism quickly when the current is switched off [1]. Steel is a hard magnetic material and would stay magnetised, so the electromagnet could not be switched off.
Three marks, three ideas: (1) iron is magnetically soft, (2) it magnetises easily, (3) it demagnetises when current stops. Mentioning what would go wrong with steel is a bonus.

6
Describe the function of the commutator (split ring) in a simple d.c. motor. Explain what would happen without it.
3 marks
Answer
The commutator reverses the direction of the current in the coil every half-turn [1]. This ensures the forces on each side of the coil always push in the same rotational direction, so the coil keeps spinning [1]. Without the commutator, the coil would rotate to the vertical position and then stop (or oscillate back and forth), because the forces would reverse and push it back the other way [1].
Key phrase: "reverses the current every half-turn." Then explain the consequence: "keeps the coil spinning in the same direction."

7
A transformer has 500 turns on its primary coil and 2000 turns on its secondary coil. The input voltage is 12 V. (a) Calculate the output voltage. [2] (b) State whether this is a step-up or step-down transformer. [1]
3 marks
Answer
(a) Vs/Vp = Ns/Np
Vs/12 = 2000/500 = 4
Vs = 12 x 4 = 48 V [1 for method, 1 for answer].

(b) Step-up transformer [1] (because the output voltage is higher than the input, and there are more turns on the secondary).
Show the formula, the substitution, and the answer. For part (b), just naming it is enough, but mentioning why (more turns on secondary / higher output voltage) shows understanding.

Advanced — Can You Apply and Analyse?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need multi-step reasoning, hand rules, and extended explanations.
8
A magnet is pushed into a coil of wire connected to a sensitive ammeter. (a) What is observed on the ammeter? [1] (b) State three ways to increase the size of the reading. [3] (c) The magnet is then held stationary inside the coil. What happens to the ammeter reading? Explain why. [2]
6 marks
Answer
(a) The ammeter needle deflects (a current is induced in the coil) [1].

(b) Any three of:
• Move the magnet faster [1]
• Use a stronger magnet [1]
• Use a coil with more turns [1]

(c) The ammeter reading returns to zero [1]. When the magnet is stationary, no field lines are being cut, so no e.m.f. is induced and no current flows [1].
Part (c) is where most students lose marks. They think the magnet "still has a field" so there should be a current. But it is the CHANGE in field (cutting field lines) that matters, not just the presence of a field. No movement = no induction.

9
Explain why electricity is transmitted across the country at very high voltages (e.g. 400,000 V) rather than at the voltage produced by the generator (e.g. 25,000 V).
5 marks
Answer
Transmission cables have resistance, which causes energy to be wasted as heat [1]. The power lost in the cables is given by P = I2R [1]. A step-up transformer is used to increase the voltage before transmission [1]. Since P = IV, for the same power being transmitted, a higher voltage means a lower current [1]. A lower current means much less power is wasted as heat in the cables (because power loss depends on current squared) [1].
This is a classic 5-mark question. The chain of reasoning must be complete: resistance causes heat loss → P = I2R → step-up transformer increases V → P = IV so lower I → less I2R loss. Each link in the chain is worth a mark.

10
A power station produces electricity at 25,000 V. A step-up transformer increases this to 400,000 V for transmission. The transformer has 2000 turns on its primary coil. (a) Calculate the number of turns on the secondary coil. [2] (b) The power output is 500 MW. Calculate the current in the transmission cables. [2] (c) Explain why the transformer will not work with a d.c. supply. [2]
6 marks
Answer
(a) Vs/Vp = Ns/Np
400,000/25,000 = Ns/2000
16 = Ns/2000
Ns = 16 x 2000 = 32,000 turns [1 for method, 1 for answer].

(b) P = IV, so I = P/V
I = 500,000,000 / 400,000 = 1250 A [1 for method, 1 for answer].
(Note: 500 MW = 500,000,000 W)

(c) A transformer works by electromagnetic induction [1]. The a.c. in the primary coil creates a changing magnetic field in the iron core, which induces a voltage in the secondary coil. D.c. would create a steady (constant) magnetic field which would not induce any voltage in the secondary coil [1].
For part (c), the key word is "changing." A.c. creates a changing field; d.c. creates a steady field. No change = no induction.
