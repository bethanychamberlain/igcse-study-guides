Section 11.1 Sun, Earth, and Moon
This section covers the basic facts about the Sun, Earth, and Moon, and explains day/night, the seasons, and Moon phases.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts

• The Sun is a star — a huge, hot ball of gas. Surface temperature: ~6000 °C. Core: ~15 million °C. Diameter: 1,390,000 km.
• The Earth orbits the Sun. Diameter: 12,800 km. Distance from Sun: ~150 million km.
• The Moon orbits the Earth. Diameter: 3,500 km. Distance from Earth: ~384,000 km. One orbit takes 27.3 days.

W
Write This Down — Day and Night

Day and night are caused by the Earth rotating on its axis once every 24 hours. Half the Earth faces the Sun (daytime) and half faces away (night-time). As the Earth rotates, any location passes from sunlight into shadow.

W
Write This Down — Seasons

Seasons are caused by the tilt of the Earth's axis (23.5°). As the Earth orbits the Sun over a year:

• When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted towards the Sun → summer (longer days, Sun higher in sky, more concentrated heating)
• When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun → winter (shorter days, Sun lower in sky, less concentrated heating)

There are two reasons it is warmer in summer: (1) the Sun's radiation hits the surface more directly (less spread out), and (2) there are more hours of sunlight.

!
Don't Confuse — Seasons Are NOT About Distance

A very common misconception: students say "it is summer because the Earth is closer to the Sun." This is WRONG. The Earth's distance from the Sun barely changes during the year. Seasons are caused by the tilt of the axis, not by distance. If it were about distance, both hemispheres would have summer at the same time, but they don't.

W
Write This Down — Moon Phases

The Moon does not produce its own light — it reflects sunlight. Half the Moon is always lit by the Sun, but we see different amounts of the lit side as the Moon orbits the Earth. This creates the phases:

• New Moon → Waxing Crescent → First Quarter → Waxing Gibbous → Full Moon → Waning Gibbous → Third Quarter → Waning Crescent → back to New Moon

The full cycle takes about 29.5 days (one lunar month).

T
Study Tip — The Same Face

The Moon always shows the same face to the Earth because it takes the same time to rotate on its axis as it does to orbit the Earth (27.3 days). This comes up as a one-mark question.

  • I know that day/night is caused by Earth's rotation (not its orbit)
  • I know seasons are caused by the tilt of Earth's axis (NOT distance from Sun)
  • I can name the phases of the Moon in order
  • I know why we always see the same face of the Moon
Sections 11.2 – 11.3 The Solar System
The Solar System consists of the Sun, eight planets, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and moons.
W
Write This Down — Planet Order

Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune

Memory trick: My Very Easy Method Just Speeds Up Naming

W
Write This Down — Inner vs Outer Planets
Feature Inner Planets (Mercury-Mars) Outer Planets (Jupiter-Neptune)
Size Small Large
Made of Rock and iron (terrestrial) Mainly gas (gas giants)
Density High Low
Surface Solid surface No solid surface
W
Write This Down — Other Objects

• Asteroids: thousands of rocky objects, mostly between Mars and Jupiter. Ranging from a few km to ~1000 km across.
• Comets: icy objects with highly elliptical orbits. When near the Sun, they develop a visible tail of gas and dust.
• Dwarf planets: includes Pluto (reclassified in 2006) and Ceres (in the asteroid belt).
• Meteors: small pieces of space debris that burn up in Earth's atmosphere (shooting stars).
• Meteorites: larger chunks that survive and reach the ground.

W
Write This Down — Key Patterns

• The further a planet is from the Sun, the slower it orbits and the longer its orbital period.
• The further from the Sun, the colder the average surface temperature (the Sun's radiation weakens with distance).
• Planets do not produce their own light — we see them because they reflect sunlight.

!
Don't Confuse — Meteor vs Meteorite vs Comet

Meteor = burns up in the atmosphere (a streak of light). Meteorite = reaches the ground. Comet = an icy body in a long elliptical orbit around the Sun, with a visible tail. These are three completely different things.

  • I can name the eight planets in order from the Sun
  • I know two differences between inner and outer planets
  • I can distinguish between a meteor, meteorite, and comet
Section 11.4 Orbits and Satellites
Anything that orbits another object does so because of gravity. This section covers how gravity controls orbits and the types of artificial satellites.
W
Write This Down — Gravity and Orbits

Planets orbit the Sun because the Sun's gravitational pull provides the centripetal force that keeps them moving in a curved path. Without gravity, they would travel in a straight line.

Gravitational force follows an inverse square law: doubling the distance reduces the force to a quarter.

W
Write This Down — Orbital Speed Formula
orbital speed = 2πr / T

r = radius of orbit
T = orbital period (time for one complete orbit)
2πr = circumference (distance around the orbit)

W
Write This Down — Comets in Orbit

Comets have highly elliptical orbits. They move fastest when closest to the Sun (gravitational pull is strongest, and GPE converts to KE) and slowest when furthest away.

W
Write This Down — Types of Satellite

Geostationary orbit: 35,900 km above the equator. Period = 24 hours (matches Earth's rotation). Stays above the same point. Used for communications satellites (TV, phone).

Low orbit: A few hundred km up. Faster speed, shorter period. Used for monitoring/weather satellites and navigation (GPS).

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions often ask why communications satellites use geostationary orbits. The answer: the satellite stays above the same point on Earth, so ground-based dish aerials can point at it in a fixed direction without needing to track its movement.

  • I know that gravity provides the force that keeps planets in orbit
  • I can write the orbital speed formula: speed = 2πr / T
  • I know comets move faster when closer to the Sun
  • I can explain the difference between geostationary and low orbits
Section 11.5 Stars, Galaxies, and the Light Year
Beyond our Solar System, there are billions of other stars. Stars are grouped into galaxies, and there are billions of galaxies in the Universe.
W
Write This Down — Light Year

A light year (ly) is the distance that light travels in one year. It is a unit of distance, not time.

1 light year = 9.5 × 1012 km

The nearest star to the Sun is Proxima Centauri, at 4.2 light years away. Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes to reach Earth.

W
Write This Down — Galaxies

A galaxy is a huge system of billions of stars held together by gravity. Our galaxy is called the Milky Way and contains at least 100 billion (1011) stars.

The Universe is made up of billions of galaxies. Neighbouring galaxies are typically a few million light years apart.

W
Write This Down — Birth of a Star

Stars form inside a nebula (a huge cloud of gas and dust):

• Gravity pulls gas and dust together into a clump
• As material collects, the clump gets hotter and denser — this is called a protostar
• When the core is hot enough (millions of degrees), nuclear fusion begins — hydrogen fuses into helium
• The protostar becomes a star

!
Don't Confuse — Light Year Is Distance

A light year is a measure of distance, not time. "The star is 4.2 light years away" means its light takes 4.2 years to reach us — it tells us how far away it is, not how old it is.

  • I can define a light year as a unit of distance
  • I know what a galaxy is and that our galaxy is the Milky Way
  • I can describe how a star forms from a nebula
Section 11.6 The Life Cycle of Stars
All stars eventually run out of fuel and "die." What happens at the end depends on the star's mass.
W
Write This Down — Stars Like the Sun (Medium Mass)

Nebula → Protostar → Main sequence star (like our Sun now) → Red giant (swells up, cools) → Outer layers drift away → White dwarf (hot, dense core) → Eventually cools and fades

W
Write This Down — Massive Stars

Nebula → Protostar → Main sequence star → Red supergiant (swells even larger) → Supernova (huge explosion) → Either a neutron star or a black hole (depending on how massive the remaining core is)

W
Write This Down — Key Terms

• Red giant/supergiant: a star that has expanded and cooled — it is bigger but has a lower surface temperature (hence red)
• White dwarf: the hot, dense remnant core of a medium-mass star
• Supernova: a massive explosion when a large star collapses and then rebounds
• Neutron star: an extremely dense collapsed core left after a supernova
• Black hole: if the core is massive enough, gravity is so strong that not even light can escape

?
Why This Matters — Made from Stardust

Heavy elements like gold, iron, and uranium are created inside stars and spread through space by supernovae. The Sun and Earth contain these heavy elements, which means our Solar System formed from the remains of an earlier star. We are literally made of stardust.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Diagram

Draw the two life cycle paths as a flow chart. One path for Sun-sized stars (ending in white dwarf) and one for massive stars (ending in neutron star or black hole). This diagram is worth 4-5 marks on the exam.

  • I can describe the life cycle of a Sun-sized star
  • I can describe the life cycle of a massive star
  • I know the difference between a white dwarf, neutron star, and black hole
Section 11.7 The Expanding Universe and the Big Bang
Scientists have strong evidence that the Universe is expanding. This supports the theory that it all began from a single point — the Big Bang.
W
Write This Down — Red Shift

When a galaxy is moving away from us, the light waves from it are "stretched out," shifting towards the red end of the spectrum. This is called red shift.

• The more distant a galaxy is, the greater the red shift
• This means more distant galaxies are moving away from us faster
• This is evidence that the Universe is expanding

W
Write This Down — The Big Bang Theory

The Big Bang theory states that the Universe began billions of years ago when all matter and energy were concentrated in a single, extremely hot, dense point. It then expanded rapidly, and is still expanding today.

Two pieces of evidence for the Big Bang:

• Red shift: galaxies are moving apart — if we "rewind," they were once all together [1]
• Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR): faint microwave radiation detected from every direction in space. This is thought to be the remnant radiation from the Big Bang itself, now cooled and stretched [1]

W
Write This Down — Hubble Constant and Age of Universe

The Hubble constant (H0) relates the speed at which a galaxy is receding to its distance from us:

H0 = v / d

The age of the Universe can be estimated as approximately 1 / H0, giving a value of about 13.8 billion years.

!
Don't Confuse — The Big Bang Was NOT an Explosion Into Space

The Big Bang was not an explosion that happened somewhere in space. It was the expansion of space itself. The galaxies are not flying through space — the space between them is stretching. Think of dots on an inflating balloon: as the balloon expands, all dots move apart from each other, but no dot is at the "centre."

?
Why This Matters — Exam Favourite

"State two pieces of evidence that the Universe is expanding and may have started with a Big Bang" is a classic 4-mark question. You must name BOTH red shift AND CMBR, and briefly explain each. Just naming them gets 2 marks; explaining them gets all 4.

  • I can explain what red shift is and what it tells us
  • I can state two pieces of evidence for the Big Bang (red shift + CMBR)
  • I know that the Big Bang was the expansion of space, not an explosion into space
  • I know what the Hubble constant is
Putting It All Together The Big Picture — From Smallest to Largest
This chapter covers an enormous range of scales. Make sure you can place everything in order.
W
Write This Down — Scale of the Universe

Moon orbits Earth → Earth orbits the Sun → The Sun is one star in the Milky Way galaxy → The Milky Way is one of billions of galaxies in the Universe

Distances:
• Earth to Moon: 384,000 km (~1.3 light seconds)
• Earth to Sun: 150 million km (~8 light minutes)
• Sun to nearest star: 4.2 light years
• Across the Milky Way: ~100,000 light years
• To the most distant galaxies: ~13 billion light years
