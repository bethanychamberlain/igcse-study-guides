Foundation — Do You Know the Basics?

Worth 1-2 marks each. These test whether you can recall formulas and definitions.
1
Write down the formula for work done. State what each letter represents and give its unit.
3 marks
Answer
W = F x d [1]
W = work done in joules (J), F = force in newtons (N) [1], d = distance in metres (m) [1]
Show your working: One mark for the formula itself, one mark per correct letter-and-unit pair. The examiner wants to see you know what each letter stands for.
Rate your answer:
2
State the law of conservation of energy.
2 marks
Answer
Energy cannot be created or destroyed [1]. It can only be transferred from one form to another [1].
Show your working: Both parts are needed for full marks. "Energy is conserved" on its own is not enough — you must say it cannot be created or destroyed AND that it can be transferred.
Rate your answer:
3
Write down the formula for kinetic energy. What does the "v2" mean?
2 marks
Answer
KE = ½ x m x v² [1]
v² means velocity squared — you multiply the speed by itself (e.g. if v = 5, then v² = 25) [1]
Show your working: The examiner wants to see that you understand v² means v x v, not 2v. This is the most common mistake in energy calculations.
Rate your answer:
4
Define power and state its unit. Write the formula.
3 marks
Answer
Power is the rate of doing work / rate of energy transfer [1]. The unit is the watt (W) [1]. Formula: P = W / t [1].
Show your working: "Rate" means "per second." 1 watt = 1 joule per second. The examiner will accept "energy transferred per second" as a definition.

Intermediate — Can You Calculate It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You MUST show all working: formula, substitution, answer with units.
5
A person pushes a trolley with a force of 45 N over a distance of 8 m. Calculate the work done.
3 marks
Full Working
1. Write the formula: W = F x d [1]
2. Substitute: W = 45 x 8 [1]
3. Calculate: W = 360 J [1]
Show your working: Even though 45 x 8 is easy, writing all three lines guarantees you get full marks. If you just write "360 J" and make a typo, you get 0.
Rate your answer:
6
A 5 kg rock is sitting on a cliff 12 m above the ground. Calculate its gravitational potential energy. (g = 10 N/kg)
3 marks
Full Working
1. Write the formula: GPE = m x g x h [1]
2. Substitute: GPE = 5 x 10 x 12 [1]
3. Calculate: GPE = 600 J [1]
Show your working: Three numbers multiplied together. Write them in the order m, g, h so the examiner can see you used the right formula.
Rate your answer:
7
A 2 kg ball is thrown at a speed of 6 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.
3 marks
Full Working
1. Write the formula: KE = ½ x m x v² [1]
2. Substitute: KE = ½ x 2 x 6² [1]
3a. Square the speed first: 6² = 36
3b. Calculate: KE = ½ x 2 x 36 = 36 J [1]
Show your working: Notice the answer is NOT 12 (that is ½ x 2 x 6 without squaring). The most common mistake is forgetting to square the speed. Write v² as a separate step to avoid this.

Advanced — Multi-Step Problems

Worth 4-6 marks each. These combine multiple formulas or require rearranging. Full working is essential.
8
A machine receives 4000 J of electrical energy. It converts 1200 J into useful kinetic energy. The rest is wasted as heat.
(a) Calculate the efficiency of the machine.
(b) How much energy is wasted?
4 marks
Full Working

(a) Efficiency:

1. Write the formula: Efficiency = (useful output / total input) x 100% [1]
2. Substitute: Efficiency = (1200 / 4000) x 100% [1]
3. Calculate: Efficiency = 0.3 x 100% = 30% [1]

(b) Wasted energy:

1. Wasted = Total input - Useful output = 4000 - 1200 = 2800 J [1]
Show your working: For part (a), the useful energy (1200) goes on top because it is the SMALLER number. If you get more than 100%, you put them the wrong way around. For part (b), subtraction is one easy mark — don't forget it.
Rate your answer:
9
A crane lifts a 200 kg steel beam to a height of 15 m in 10 seconds. (g = 10 N/kg)
(a) Calculate the GPE gained by the beam.
(b) Calculate the power of the crane.
5 marks
Full Working

(a) GPE gained:

1. Write the formula: GPE = m x g x h [1]
2. Substitute: GPE = 200 x 10 x 15 [1]
3. Calculate: GPE = 30,000 J [1]

(b) Power of the crane:

1. Write the formula: P = W / t (work done = GPE gained = 30,000 J) [1]
2. Substitute: P = 30,000 / 10
3. Calculate: P = 3000 W [1]
Show your working: This is a TWO-FORMULA question. You need the answer from part (a) to do part (b). The work done by the crane equals the GPE gained by the beam. The examiner loves this type of question — it tests whether you can link formulas together.
Rate your answer:
10
A 0.4 kg ball is dropped from a height of 20 m. (g = 10 N/kg). Ignore air resistance.
(a) Calculate its GPE at the top.
(b) Using conservation of energy, state its KE just before it hits the ground.
(c) Calculate its speed just before hitting the ground.
6 marks
Full Working

(a) GPE at the top:

1. Write the formula: GPE = m x g x h [1]
2. Substitute: GPE = 0.4 x 10 x 20 [1]
3. Calculate: GPE = 80 J

(b) KE at the bottom:

By conservation of energy, all GPE is converted to KE (no air resistance).
KE = 80 J [1]

(c) Speed at the bottom:

1. Write the formula: KE = ½ x m x v² [1]
2. Substitute: 80 = ½ x 0.4 x v²
3. Simplify: 80 = 0.2 x v²
4. Rearrange: v² = 80 / 0.2 = 400 [1]
5. Square root: v = √400 = 20 m/s [1]
Show your working: This is the hardest question type — it uses THREE ideas: GPE formula, conservation of energy, and KE formula rearranged. The key step is part (c): divide both sides by 0.2, then take the square root. If you can do this, you can handle any energy question on the exam.
