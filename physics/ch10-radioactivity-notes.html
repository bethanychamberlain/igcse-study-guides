Section 10.1 Inside Atoms
Everything is made of atoms. To understand radioactivity, you first need to know what is inside an atom.
W
Write This Down — Structure of the Atom

An atom has a central nucleus made up of protons and neutrons. Around the nucleus, electrons orbit at high speed.

• Protons have a positive (+) charge
• Electrons have a negative (-) charge
• Neutrons have no charge (neutral)
• Protons and neutrons are called nucleons
• An atom normally has equal numbers of protons and electrons, so the total charge is zero

W
Write This Down — Key Numbers

Atomic number (proton number) = the number of protons in the nucleus. This tells you what element it is.

Mass number (nucleon number) = the total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.

So: Number of neutrons = mass number - atomic number

W
Write This Down — Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. They have the same atomic number but different mass numbers.

Example: Lithium has two isotopes — lithium-6 (3 protons, 3 neutrons) and lithium-7 (3 protons, 4 neutrons).

!
Don't Confuse — Atomic Number vs Mass Number

Atomic number = protons only (the small number, written at the bottom).
Mass number = protons + neutrons (the big number, written at the top).
In the notation 73Li, the 7 is mass number and the 3 is atomic number.

T
Study Tip

Practise working out the number of neutrons: neutrons = mass number - atomic number. Try: carbon-14 has atomic number 6, so neutrons = 14 - 6 = 8. Do several until it becomes automatic.

  • I can name the three particles in an atom and their charges
  • I know the difference between atomic number and mass number
  • I can define "isotope" in one sentence
Section 10.2 Nuclear Radiation — Alpha, Beta, and Gamma
Some isotopes have unstable nuclei. Over time, these nuclei break apart (decay), releasing particles and energy. This is called nuclear radiation. There are three main types.
W
Write This Down — The Big Comparison Table (Copy This!)
Property Alpha (α) Beta (β) Gamma (γ)
What is it? 2 protons + 2 neutrons (a helium nucleus) A high-speed electron (from the nucleus) Electromagnetic wave (like X-rays)
Charge +2 -1 0 (no charge)
Mass Heavy (4 nucleons) Very light No mass
Speed Up to 0.1 × speed of light Up to 0.9 × speed of light Speed of light
Ionising power Strong (most ionising) Weak Very weak (least ionising)
Penetrating power Stopped by paper or skin Stopped by a few mm of aluminium Never fully stopped; reduced by thick lead or concrete
Deflected by fields? Yes (by magnetic and electric fields) Yes (opposite direction to alpha) No
W
Write This Down — Ionisation

Ionisation means knocking electrons off atoms, turning them into ions (charged particles). Nuclear radiation causes ionisation, which is why it can damage living cells.

Alpha is the most ionising because it is big, slow, and heavily charged — it has more time to knock electrons off atoms it passes.

?
Why This Matters — Exam Favourite

The comparison table above is one of the most commonly tested topics in IGCSE Physics. You will be asked to compare the three types of radiation in terms of penetration, ionisation, charge, and mass. Learn this table cold.

T
Study Tip — Memory Trick

Think of it as a trade-off: more ionising = less penetrating. Alpha is a big heavy bullet — it smashes into everything nearby (very ionising) but gets stopped quickly (not penetrating). Gamma is like a tiny fast needle — it slips through almost everything (very penetrating) but rarely hits anything (not ionising).

  • I can draw the comparison table from memory
  • I know what ionisation means
  • I understand the trade-off: more ionising = less penetrating
Section 10.3 Radiation Dangers and Detection
Radiation can be dangerous to living things, but there is also radiation around us all the time from natural sources.
W
Write This Down — Dangers

Nuclear radiation can damage or destroy living cells and cause mutations (changes to DNA), which may lead to cancer.

• Radiation inside the body: alpha is most dangerous (most ionising, damages cells most)
• Radiation outside the body: beta and gamma are most dangerous (they can penetrate skin to reach organs; alpha is stopped by skin)

W
Write This Down — Background Radiation

Background radiation is a low level of radiation that is around us all the time. Sources include:

• Radon gas from rocks (the biggest single source)
• Rocks and soil
• Food and drink
• Cosmic rays from space
• Medical X-rays
• Nuclear fallout (very small amount)

W
Write This Down — Detecting Radiation

A Geiger-Müller (GM) tube detects radiation. It connects to a ratemeter (shows counts per second) or a scaler (shows total count). When radiation enters the tube, it ionises the gas inside, producing a pulse of current.

!
Don't Confuse — Inside vs Outside the Body

Students often say "alpha is the most dangerous" without saying where. Alpha is most dangerous INSIDE the body (e.g. if you breathe in radioactive dust). Outside the body, gamma is the biggest concern because it penetrates skin. The exam will ask you to distinguish between these two scenarios.

T
Study Tip — Safety Rules

Remember the three safety rules for handling radioactive sources: (1) store in a lead container, (2) pick up with tongs (never hands), (3) keep away from the body and point away from people. These come up in practical questions.

  • I know why alpha is most dangerous inside the body but least dangerous outside
  • I can name at least 3 sources of background radiation
  • I know what a GM tube is and what it does
Section 10.4 Radioactive Decay — Nuclear Equations
When an unstable nucleus decays, it emits an alpha or beta particle (sometimes with gamma rays). The nucleus changes into a different element. You need to be able to write nuclear equations for this.
W
Write This Down — Alpha Decay

In alpha decay, the nucleus loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons (an alpha particle = helium-4 nucleus).

What changes:
• Mass number goes down by 4
• Atomic number goes down by 2
• A new element is formed

Example: Radium-226 → Radon-222 + alpha particle

22688Ra → 22286Rn + 42He
W
Write This Down — Beta Decay

In beta decay, a neutron changes into a proton, and an electron (beta particle) is emitted at high speed.

What changes:
• Mass number stays the same
• Atomic number goes up by 1
• A new element is formed

Example: Iodine-131 → Xenon-131 + beta particle

13153I → 13154Xe + 0-1β
W
Write This Down — Gamma Emission

Gamma emission often accompanies alpha or beta decay. It does not change the mass number or atomic number — it just releases energy from the nucleus.

W
Write This Down — Balancing Nuclear Equations

In every nuclear equation:
• The top numbers (mass numbers) must add up to the same total on both sides
• The bottom numbers (atomic numbers) must add up to the same total on both sides

1. Write the parent nucleus on the left
2. Write the emitted particle on the right: alpha = 42He, beta = 0-1e
3. Work out the daughter nucleus by balancing top and bottom numbers
!
Don't Confuse — Alpha vs Beta Changes

Alpha: mass number drops by 4, atomic number drops by 2.
Beta: mass number stays the SAME, atomic number goes UP by 1.
The most common mistake is thinking beta decay reduces the atomic number. It doesn't — a neutron turns into a proton, so the atomic number increases.

T
Study Tip — Practice Equations

Try this: Thorium-232 (atomic number 90) undergoes alpha decay. What is the daughter nucleus?
Top: 232 - 4 = 228. Bottom: 90 - 2 = 88. The daughter is 22888Ra (radium). Check by adding both sides.

  • I can write a nuclear equation for alpha decay
  • I can write a nuclear equation for beta decay
  • I know how to check equations by balancing top and bottom numbers
Section 10.5 Half-Life
Radioactive decay is random and spontaneous — you cannot predict when any individual nucleus will decay. But with large numbers of nuclei, you can predict the overall rate using half-life.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Half-life is the time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. It is also the time for the activity (count rate) to fall to half its original value.

Activity = the number of disintegrations per second, measured in becquerels (Bq). 1 Bq = 1 decay per second.

W
Write This Down — How to Do Half-Life Calculations

To find how much remains after a number of half-lives, keep halving:

Ex: A sample has an activity of 800 Bq. Its half-life is 2 hours. What is the activity after 6 hours?
1. Number of half-lives: 6 / 2 = 3 half-lives
2. After 1 half-life: 800 / 2 = 400 Bq
3. After 2 half-lives: 400 / 2 = 200 Bq
4. After 3 half-lives: 200 / 2 = 100 Bq
W
Write This Down — Reading Half-Life from a Graph

On a decay curve (activity vs time), pick any value on the y-axis, then find how long it takes to halve. That time interval is the half-life. You can check by picking a different starting point — you should get the same answer.

?
Why This Matters

Half-life calculations are worth 3-4 marks on the exam and come up nearly every year. The key is to count the number of half-lives, then keep halving. Never try to divide by a fraction — just halve repeatedly.

!
Don't Confuse

Radioactive decay is random and spontaneous. It is NOT affected by temperature, pressure, or chemical changes. The half-life of an isotope is a fixed property — nothing can speed it up or slow it down.

  • I can define half-life in one sentence
  • I solved the worked example (800 Bq after 3 half-lives = 100 Bq)
  • I know how to read half-life from a graph
  • I know decay is random and not affected by external conditions
Section 10.6 Nuclear Fission
Nuclear fission is the process used in nuclear power stations and nuclear weapons. It releases a huge amount of energy by splitting heavy nuclei.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a large, unstable nucleus into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy and two or three neutrons.

It is triggered when a neutron hits a uranium-235 nucleus. The nucleus becomes highly unstable and splits.

W
Write This Down — Chain Reaction

The neutrons released from one fission can hit other uranium-235 nuclei, causing them to split too. This is called a chain reaction.

• Controlled chain reaction = nuclear power station (steady energy release)
• Uncontrolled chain reaction = nuclear bomb (huge, rapid energy release)

W
Write This Down — Nuclear Reactor Parts

• Fuel: uranium dioxide (enriched with U-235)
• Moderator: slows down neutrons (graphite or water) so they can be captured by other U-235 nuclei
• Control rods: contain boron or cadmium, which absorb neutrons. Lowering them slows the reaction; raising them speeds it up.

?
Why This Matters

The energy released per atom in nuclear fission is about a million times greater than in a chemical reaction like burning. This is why a small amount of nuclear fuel can produce enormous amounts of energy.

!
Don't Confuse — Fission vs Fusion

Fission = splitting a big nucleus into smaller ones. Think "fission = fissure = split."
Fusion = joining small nuclei together. Think "fusion = fuse = join."
Both release energy, but by opposite methods.

  • I can define nuclear fission
  • I know what a chain reaction is
  • I can name the three key parts of a reactor (fuel, moderator, control rods)
Section 10.7 Nuclear Fusion
Fusion is the opposite of fission. It is the process that powers the Sun and all stars.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Nuclear fusion is the joining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy.

In the Sun, hydrogen nuclei fuse together to form helium. This releases the energy that keeps the Sun shining.

W
Write This Down — Why Fusion Is Hard

Nuclei are positively charged, so they repel each other. To overcome this repulsion and fuse, they must be travelling extremely fast, which requires temperatures of at least 40 million degrees Celsius. No ordinary container can hold gas this hot.

W
Write This Down — Fission vs Fusion Summary
Feature Fission Fusion
Process Splitting heavy nuclei Joining light nuclei
Fuel Uranium-235 or plutonium-239 Hydrogen isotopes
Where it happens Nuclear power stations Stars (Sun)
Waste Radioactive waste (long-lived) Helium (not radioactive)
Energy per kg Very high Even higher
?
Why This Matters

Fusion is the "holy grail" of energy. It would produce more energy per kilogram than fission, use fuel from seawater (hydrogen), and produce no long-lived radioactive waste. Scientists are still trying to build a working fusion reactor.

  • I can define nuclear fusion
  • I know why fusion needs extremely high temperatures
  • I can compare fission and fusion in a table
Section 10.8 Uses of Radioactivity
Despite its dangers, radioactivity has many useful applications in medicine, industry, and everyday life. The key is choosing the right type of radiation for each job.
W
Write This Down — Medical Uses

Tracers: A patient swallows or is injected with a small amount of a radioactive isotope (e.g. iodine-123). A detector outside the body tracks where it goes, checking how organs are working. Tracers must have a short half-life and emit gamma rays (so they can escape the body).

Radiotherapy: Gamma rays are used to kill cancer cells. Several beams from different angles are aimed at the tumour to concentrate the dose on the cancer and reduce damage to healthy tissue.

W
Write This Down — Industrial Uses

Thickness monitoring: A beta source is placed on one side of a material (e.g. paper or metal sheet) and a detector on the other. If the material gets too thick, less radiation reaches the detector, and the machine adjusts.

Gamma irradiation: Gamma rays are used to sterilise medical equipment and kill bacteria in food, making it last longer.

Smoke detectors: Contain a weak alpha source (americium-241). Alpha particles ionise the air, creating a small current. Smoke particles absorb the ions, reducing the current and triggering the alarm.

T
Study Tip — Why THAT Type of Radiation?

Exam questions often ask "why is a beta source used?" or "why not alpha?" The answer always links to the properties table:
• Thickness monitoring uses beta because it passes through thin materials but is absorbed by thicker ones. Alpha would be stopped by anything; gamma would pass through everything.
• Tracers use gamma because it can escape the body. Alpha or beta would be absorbed.

  • I can name and explain at least 3 uses of radioactivity
  • I can explain why a specific type of radiation is used for each application
Sections 10.9 – 10.10 Atoms and Particles — How We Know
Our understanding of the atom has changed over time as new evidence was discovered. You need to know the key experiments that shaped the model.
W
Write This Down — Two Models of the Atom

Thomson's "plum pudding" model (1897): The atom is a ball of positive charge with negative electrons dotted throughout it, like raisins in a pudding.

Rutherford's nuclear model (1911): Geiger and Marsden fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. Most went straight through, but a few bounced back at large angles. This proved the atom is mostly empty space with a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the centre.

W
Write This Down — Evidence from the Gold Foil Experiment

• Most alpha particles passed straight through → the atom is mostly empty space
• Some were deflected slightly → the nucleus has a positive charge (repelling the positive alphas)
• A very few bounced straight back → the nucleus is very small, dense, and heavy

?
Why This Matters

The gold foil experiment is a classic "describe and explain" exam question worth 4-6 marks. You need to state what happened to the alpha particles AND explain what each observation tells us about the atom's structure.

  • I can describe the plum pudding model
  • I can describe the gold foil experiment and what was observed
  • I can explain what the results tell us about the atom
Key Formula Energy and Mass — E = mc²
Einstein showed that mass and energy are linked. In nuclear reactions, a tiny amount of mass is converted into a huge amount of energy.
W
Write This Down — Einstein's Equation
E = mc²

E = energy released (joules, J)
m = mass lost (kilograms, kg)
c = speed of light (3 × 108 m/s)

Because c² is so enormous (9 × 1016), even a tiny loss of mass produces a huge amount of energy. This is why nuclear reactions release so much more energy than chemical reactions.

  • I can write E = mc² and explain what each letter means
  • I understand why nuclear reactions release so much energy
