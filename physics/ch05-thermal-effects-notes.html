Section 5.1 Moving Particles
Everything is made of tiny moving particles. The kinetic theory explains how solids, liquids, and gases behave.
W
Write This Down — Particle Behaviour in Three States
Property Solid Liquid Gas
Arrangement Regular pattern, close together Irregular, close together Very far apart, random
Movement Vibrate in fixed positions Slide past each other Move fast in all directions
Forces Strong bonds Weaker bonds Almost no bonds
Shape/volume Fixed shape, fixed volume No fixed shape, fixed volume No fixed shape or volume
W
Write This Down — Internal Energy

Internal energy is the total kinetic energy and potential energy of all the particles in a material. The hotter the material, the more internal energy it has.

Heat is the transfer of thermal energy from a hotter object to a cooler one. Energy flows until both objects reach the same temperature.

W
Write This Down — Brownian Motion

Brownian motion is the random, zig-zag movement of tiny visible particles (like smoke) caused by collisions with invisible gas molecules. It provides evidence that gas molecules are in constant, random motion.

?
Why This Matters

The particle table above is one of the most commonly tested topics in IGCSE physics. You need to know the arrangement, movement, and forces for all three states. Learn it until you can fill in the table from memory.

  • I can describe particle arrangement and movement in solids, liquids, and gases
  • I can define internal energy
  • I can explain Brownian motion
Section 5.2 Temperature
Temperature measures how hot something is. It is linked to how fast the particles are moving.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Temperature is related to the average kinetic energy of the particles. Higher temperature = faster particles on average.
• The Celsius scale has two fixed points: 0 °C (melting ice) and 100 °C (boiling water at standard pressure).
• Absolute zero = -273 °C = 0 K. This is the lowest possible temperature — particles have minimum energy and cannot move any slower.

W
Write This Down — Converting Celsius to Kelvin
K = °C + 273

Examples: 0 °C = 273 K. 100 °C = 373 K. -273 °C = 0 K.

!
Don't Confuse — Temperature vs Heat

Temperature tells you how hot something is (average KE per particle).
Heat is the transfer of thermal energy from hot to cold.
A cup of boiling water (100 °C) has the same temperature as a bathtub of boiling water, but the bathtub contains far more thermal energy because it has more particles.

  • I can convert between Celsius and Kelvin
  • I know what absolute zero is (-273 °C = 0 K)
  • I know the difference between temperature and heat
Section 5.3 Expansion of Solids and Liquids
Most materials expand when heated. This is usually tiny, but if you don't allow for it, bridges can buckle and roads can crack.
W
Write This Down — Thermal Expansion

Thermal expansion happens because heated particles vibrate faster and take up more space. Most solids expand when heated. Liquids expand more than solids. Gases expand the most.

W
Write This Down — Applications and Problems

• Bridges have expansion gaps at the ends, and one end rests on rollers.
• Overhead cables are hung with slack so they don't snap when they contract in cold weather.
• Steel rods are used to reinforce concrete because they expand by the same amount.
• A bimetal strip (two metals bonded together) bends when heated because one metal expands more. Used in thermostats.

W
Write This Down — Water Is Unusual

Water expands when it freezes (unlike most substances). This is why ice floats and why frozen water can burst pipes. Water has its maximum density at 4 °C.

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions often ask you to "explain why" bridges have gaps or cables have slack. The answer is always: thermal expansion. Materials expand when heated and contract when cooled. If there is no room to expand, the forces can crack concrete or buckle rails.

  • I can explain thermal expansion using particle theory
  • I can give examples of where expansion is allowed for (bridges, cables)
  • I know that water is unusual — it expands when it freezes
Section 5.4 Heating Gases
When a gas is heated, either its pressure or its volume (or both) will change. This links back to the kinetic theory and to Boyle's law from Chapter 3.
W
Write This Down — Two Situations

Constant volume (e.g. sealed container): Heating makes particles move faster. They hit the walls harder and more often. Pressure increases.

Constant pressure (e.g. balloon open to atmosphere): Heating makes particles move faster and push the walls outward. Volume increases.

W
Write This Down — Comparing Expansions

At constant pressure, for the same temperature rise:
• Gases expand the most (attractions between particles are very weak).
• Liquids expand moderately.
• Solids expand the least (strong attractions hold particles tightly).

T
Study Tip

When explaining gas behaviour, always use the kinetic theory chain: heating → more kinetic energy → faster particles → harder/more frequent collisions → more pressure (or more volume). Each step in the chain is a mark.

  • I can explain what happens when a gas is heated at constant volume
  • I can explain what happens when a gas is heated at constant pressure
  • I know the order: gases expand most, then liquids, then solids
Section 5.5 Thermal Conduction
Conduction is how thermal energy travels through solids. It is why a metal spoon in hot soup gets hot, but a wooden spoon does not.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material without the material itself moving. Energy is passed from particle to particle by vibrations.

W
Write This Down — Conductors and Insulators

• Good conductors: metals (especially copper, aluminium, iron). Also silicon, graphite.
• Good insulators (poor conductors): glass, water, plastics, rubber, wood, wool.
• Materials with trapped air (wool, fibreglass, foam) are excellent insulators because air is a very poor conductor.

W
Write This Down — Why Metals Conduct Best

Metals have free electrons that can move through the metal. When heated, these electrons speed up, collide with atoms further along, and transfer energy very quickly. This is much faster than particle-to-particle vibration alone.

!
Don't Confuse — "Metal Feels Cold" Trap

A metal door handle feels cold because it conducts heat away from your hand quickly. A wooden door feels warm because wood is an insulator — it conducts heat away slowly. They are actually the same temperature (room temperature). The metal just transfers your body heat faster.

  • I can define conduction
  • I can name good conductors and insulators
  • I can explain why metals are the best conductors (free electrons)
Section 5.6 Convection
Convection is how thermal energy travels through fluids (liquids and gases) by the movement of the material itself. It creates circulating currents.
W
Write This Down — Definition and Process

Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a heated fluid (liquid or gas).

The process: Fluid is heated → it expands → becomes less dense → rises → cooler, denser fluid sinks to replace it → this creates a convection current (a circulating loop).

W
Write This Down — Examples

• Room heating: A radiator heats air, which rises. Cooler air sinks and is drawn towards the radiator, creating a convection current that heats the whole room.
• Refrigerator: The freezer is at the top. Cold air sinks and circulates around the food.
• Sea breezes: During the day, land heats up faster than sea. Warm air rises over the land, and cooler air moves in from the sea. At night, the reverse happens.

!
Don't Confuse — Convection Does NOT Happen in Solids

Convection requires the material to move. In a solid, particles are locked in place, so convection is impossible. If an exam question asks about heat transfer in a solid, the answer is conduction, not convection.

  • I can define convection and describe the convection current process
  • I can explain examples: room heating, fridge, sea breezes
  • I know convection cannot happen in solids
Section 5.7 Thermal Radiation
Radiation is the only way thermal energy can travel through a vacuum (empty space). It is how the Sun's energy reaches Earth.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Thermal radiation is energy transferred by infrared waves (a type of electromagnetic wave). It does not need a medium — it can travel through a vacuum. All objects emit thermal radiation. The hotter the object, the more radiation it emits.

W
Write This Down — Surfaces and Radiation
Surface Emitter Absorber Reflector
Matt black Best Best Worst
White Poor Poor Good
Silver / shiny Worst Worst Best

Good emitters are also good absorbers. Good reflectors are poor absorbers.

?
Why This Matters

This explains real-world design: solar panels are black (absorb more radiation), houses in hot countries are painted white (reflect radiation), emergency blankets are shiny (reflect body heat back). The surface comparison table is heavily tested on the exam.

  • I can define thermal radiation
  • I know that radiation travels through a vacuum (no medium needed)
  • I know which surfaces are the best/worst emitters, absorbers, and reflectors

Study Tip — Multi-Process Questions

Many exam questions describe a situation (like a car engine cooling system) and ask you to identify which heat transfer process is involved at each stage. Often multiple processes are happening at once. For each stage, ask: "Are the particles moving to a new place?" (convection) "Is it passing through the material?" Section 5.8 Liquids and Vapours
This section covers evaporation, boiling, and condensation — and the crucial differences between evaporation and boiling that come up on nearly every exam.
W
Write This Down — Evaporation vs Boiling
Feature Evaporation Boiling
Temperature Any temperature below boiling point Only at the boiling point
Where it happens Surface only Throughout the liquid
Bubbles? No bubbles Yes, bubbles form inside
Speed Slow, gradual Rapid
W
Write This Down — Factors That Speed Up Evaporation

1. Higher temperature — more particles have enough energy to escape.
2. Larger surface area — more particles are near the surface.
3. Lower humidity — fewer vapour particles return to the liquid.
4. Air blowing across the surface — carries away escaping particles.

W
Write This Down — Cooling Effect of Evaporation

When a liquid evaporates, the fastest (highest energy) particles escape from the surface. This leaves behind the slower (lower energy) particles, so the average kinetic energy decreases and the temperature drops. This is why sweating cools you down.

!
Don't Confuse — This Connects to Chemistry

Evaporation vs boiling comes up in both physics and chemistry exams. The physics version focuses on the kinetic theory explanation (fast particles escape). The chemistry version focuses on state changes and heating curves. You need the physics explanation for this exam.

  • I can state at least three differences between evaporation and boiling
  • I can list four factors that speed up evaporation
  • I can explain the cooling effect of evaporation using kinetic theory
Section 5.9 Specific Heat Capacity
Different materials take different amounts of energy to heat up. Specific heat capacity tells you how much energy is needed per kilogram per degree.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Specific heat capacity (c) is the energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a material by 1 °C (or 1 K). Unit: J/(kg °C) or J/(kg K).

Water has a very high specific heat capacity: 4200 J/(kg °C). That is why water is so good at storing thermal energy.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
E = m x c x Δθ

E = energy transferred (joules, J)
m = mass (kilograms, kg)
c = specific heat capacity (J/(kg °C))
Δθ = temperature change (°C or K)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

2 kg of water cools from 70 °C to 20 °C. How much thermal energy does it lose? (c = 4200 J/(kg °C))

1. Write the formula: E = m x c x Δθ
2. Temperature change: Δθ = 70 - 20 = 50 °C
3. Substitute: E = 2 x 4200 x 50
4. Calculate: E = 420 000 J
!
Don't Confuse — Temperature CHANGE, Not Temperature

Δθ is the change in temperature, not the final temperature. If water heats from 20 °C to 70 °C, Δθ = 50 °C (not 70 °C). Always subtract: final - initial (or the other way for cooling).

T
Study Tip — Common SHC Values

You don't need to memorise specific heat capacities — they will always be given in the question. But know that water (4200) is much higher than metals like aluminium (900) or copper (400). This is why water is used in heating systems and car cooling systems.

  • I can define specific heat capacity
  • I can write E = mcΔθ from memory and know what each letter means
  • I solved the worked example (420 000 J)
  • I know to use temperature CHANGE, not final temperature
Section 5.10 Latent Heat
When a substance changes state (melts, boils, freezes, condenses), energy is transferred but the temperature does NOT change. This energy is called latent heat.
W
Write This Down — Definitions

• Latent heat of fusion: Energy needed to change 1 kg of a substance from solid to liquid at the same temperature. For ice: 330 000 J/kg.
• Latent heat of vaporization: Energy needed to change 1 kg of a substance from liquid to gas at the same temperature. For water: 2 300 000 J/kg.

W
Write This Down — Formula
E = m x L

E = energy transferred (joules, J)
m = mass (kilograms, kg)
L = specific latent heat (J/kg)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

How much energy is needed to melt 2 kg of ice at 0 °C? (L = 330 000 J/kg)

1. Write the formula: E = m x L
2. Substitute: E = 2 x 330 000
3. Calculate: E = 660 000 J
W
Write This Down — Why Temperature Stays Constant

During a change of state, the energy supplied is used to break the bonds (forces of attraction) between particles, NOT to increase their kinetic energy. Since temperature depends on kinetic energy, the temperature stays the same until the change of state is complete.

!
Don't Confuse — When to Use Which Formula

• Changing temperature (no state change)? Use E = mcΔθ.
• Changing state (melting, boiling, freezing, condensing — no temperature change)? Use E = mL.
• If a question asks you to heat ice from -10 °C to steam at 100 °C, you need BOTH formulas for different stages of the process.

?
Why This Matters

The flat sections of heating curves (temperature constant while state changes) are explained by latent heat. This is one of the most tested concepts. Know the explanation by heart: "Energy is used to break bonds between particles, not to increase kinetic energy, so the temperature does not change."

  • I can define latent heat of fusion and latent heat of vaporization
  • I can write E = mL from memory
  • I solved the worked example (660 000 J)
  • I know why temperature stays constant during a change of state
  • I know when to use E = mcΔθ vs E = mL
