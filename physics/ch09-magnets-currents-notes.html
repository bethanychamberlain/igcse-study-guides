Sections 9.1 – 9.2 Magnets and Magnetic Fields
Before you can understand motors and generators, you need to know the basics of magnets and the fields they create.
W
Write This Down — Magnetic Poles

Every magnet has a north-seeking (N) pole and a south-seeking (S) pole.

Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.
The closer the poles, the greater the force between them.

W
Write This Down — Magnetic vs Non-Magnetic Materials
Magnetic (Ferromagnetic) Non-Magnetic
Iron, steel, nickel, cobalt Copper, brass, aluminium, zinc, wood, plastic

Hard magnetic materials (steel): hard to magnetise, keep magnetism → permanent magnets.
Soft magnetic materials (iron): easy to magnetise, lose magnetism quickly → electromagnet cores.

W
Write This Down — Induced Magnetism

Materials such as iron and steel are attracted to magnets because the magnet induces magnetism in them. The induced pole nearest the magnet is always the opposite pole — this is why there is attraction.

Steel keeps some of its induced magnetism when pulled away → becomes a permanent magnet.
Iron loses virtually all of its induced magnetism → it was only a temporary magnet.

A magnet can be demagnetised by hammering it or heating it to a high temperature.

W
Write This Down — Magnetic Field Lines

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where a magnetic force acts. Field lines:

• Run from N pole to S pole (outside the magnet)
• Never cross each other
• Are closest together where the field is strongest
• Show the direction a N pole of a compass would point

!
Don't Confuse — The Earth's Poles

A compass N pole points towards geographic north. Since unlike poles attract, the Earth's magnetic pole near the geographic North Pole is actually a magnetic S pole. The exam sometimes tests this — don't get tripped up.

T
Study Tip

Draw the field around a bar magnet from memory: curved lines from N to S, closest at the poles. Then draw the field between two N poles facing each other (lines repel, there is a neutral point in between where the field is zero).

  • I know: like poles repel, unlike poles attract
  • I can name magnetic materials (iron, steel, nickel, cobalt)
  • I know the difference between hard and soft magnetic materials
  • I can explain induced magnetism and why magnets attract iron
  • I can draw field lines around a bar magnet (N to S)
Sections 9.3 – 9.4 Electromagnetism
A current creates a magnetic field. Wrap the wire into a coil and you get an electromagnet — a magnet you can switch on and off.
W
Write This Down — Magnetic Field Around a Wire

When a current flows through a wire, it produces a magnetic field. The field lines are concentric circles around the wire. The field is strongest close to the wire.

Right-hand grip rule (for a wire): Grip the wire with your right hand, thumb pointing in the conventional current direction. Your fingers curl in the direction of the field lines.

W
Write This Down — Solenoids and Electromagnets

A solenoid is a long coil of wire. When current flows through it, the field pattern looks like a bar magnet (field lines run through the centre and loop around outside).

An electromagnet = solenoid + soft iron core. Steel would not be suitable as a core because it would become permanently magnetised — you could not switch it off.

The field strength of a solenoid can be increased by:
• Increasing the current
• Increasing the number of turns on the coil
• Adding a soft iron core (this makes it an electromagnet, about 1000 times stronger)

W
Write This Down — Right-Hand Grip Rule (for a Coil)

Grip the coil with your right hand so your fingers point in the conventional current direction. Your thumb points towards the N pole of the electromagnet.

W
Write This Down — Uses of Electromagnets

• Magnetic relay: Small current in one circuit operates a switch in a more powerful circuit
• Circuit breaker: If current gets too high, the electromagnet becomes strong enough to open the contacts and break the circuit
• Scrapyard crane: Pick up and drop iron/steel objects on demand

?
Why This Matters

The core must be soft iron (not steel) because iron loses its magnetism when the current is turned off. If you used steel, the electromagnet would stay magnetised permanently — you could not switch it off.

  • I know a current creates a magnetic field (circular around a wire)
  • I can use the right-hand grip rule for a wire AND a coil
  • I know how to increase the strength of an electromagnet (more current, more turns)
  • I know why the core must be soft iron, not steel
Sections 9.5 – 9.6 The Motor Effect and Electric Motors
When a current-carrying wire is in a magnetic field, there is a force on it. This is the motor effect — and it is how every electric motor works.
W
Write This Down — The Motor Effect

When a wire carrying a current is placed in a magnetic field, there is a force on the wire (provided the wire is not parallel to the field).

The force is increased by:
• Increasing the current
• Using a stronger magnet
• Increasing the length of wire in the field

W
Write This Down — Fleming's Left-Hand Rule

Hold your left hand with the thumb and first two fingers at right angles:

• Thumb = direction of Force (thrust/motion)
• First finger = direction of Field (N to S)
• Second finger = direction of Current (conventional, + to −)

Memory trick: TFC — Thumb = Thrust, First = Field, seCond = Current.

W
Write This Down — The Simple D.C. Motor

A d.c. motor has a coil of wire between the poles of a magnet. When current flows:

• One side of the coil is pushed up, the other is pushed down (by Fleming's left-hand rule)
• This creates a turning effect (the coil rotates)
• The commutator (split ring) reverses the current direction every half-turn so the coil keeps spinning the same way
• The brushes are carbon contacts that connect the rotating commutator to the battery

The turning effect on the coil can be increased by:
• Increasing the current
• Using a stronger magnet
• Increasing the number of turns on the coil
• Increasing the area of the coil

!
Don't Confuse — Left Hand vs Right Hand

Left-hand rule = motor effect (current causes motion).
Right-hand rule = generator effect (motion causes current).
Easy way to remember: Left = eLectric motor. The left-hand rule is for when a current is causing a force.

T
Study Tip — Practice With Your Actual Hand

Get a diagram of a motor from the textbook. Point your left-hand fingers in the field direction (N to S), curl your second finger in the current direction, and check that your thumb points the right way for the force. Do this physically, not just in your head. The exam often asks you to work out a force direction.

  • I can state the motor effect (current + field = force)
  • I can use Fleming's LEFT-hand rule (thumb=force, first=field, second=current)
  • I can describe how a simple d.c. motor works
  • I know the purpose of the commutator (reverses current each half-turn)
  • I know 4 ways to increase the turning effect of a motor
Sections 9.7 – 9.8 Electromagnetic Induction
The motor effect works in reverse: just as a current in a magnetic field creates motion, motion in a magnetic field creates a current. This is how generators and power stations work.
W
Write This Down — Electromagnetic Induction

When a conductor (wire) moves through a magnetic field so that it cuts field lines, an e.m.f. (voltage) is induced in the wire. If the wire is part of a complete circuit, a current flows.

The induced e.m.f. can be increased by:
• Moving the wire/magnet faster
• Using a stronger magnet
• Increasing the number of turns on the coil (more wire cutting field lines)

The direction of the induced current is reversed by:
• Moving the wire/magnet in the opposite direction
• Reversing the direction of the field

W
Write This Down — Faraday's Law

Faraday's law: The e.m.f. induced in a conductor is proportional to the rate at which magnetic field lines are cut by the conductor.

In simple terms: cut field lines faster = bigger voltage.

W
Write This Down — Lenz's Law

Lenz's law: An induced current always flows in a direction such that it opposes the change that produced it.

Example: If you push a N pole into a coil, the induced current makes the coil act like a magnet with its N pole facing the approaching magnet — it repels the magnet (opposes the motion).

W
Write This Down — Fleming's Right-Hand Rule

For a wire moving through a magnetic field (generator effect), use your right hand:

• Thumb = direction of Motion
• First finger = direction of Field (N to S)
• Second finger = direction of induced Current

?
Why This Matters — No Movement = No Current

If the wire is stationary, or moving parallel to the field lines (not cutting them), there is no induced e.m.f. and no current. The exam loves testing this: "the magnet is held still inside the coil — what happens?" Answer: nothing.

!
Don't Confuse — When to Use Which Rule

• A current causes motion → use the LEFT-hand rule (motor)
• Motion causes a current → use the RIGHT-hand rule (generator)
The exam will test whether you know which situation calls for which hand.

  • I can state what electromagnetic induction is (moving wire in field = induced e.m.f.)
  • I know 3 ways to increase the induced e.m.f.
  • I can state Lenz's law (induced current opposes the change)
  • I can use Fleming's RIGHT-hand rule (thumb=motion, first=field, second=current)
  • I know: no movement = no induced current
Section 9.9 Generators
A generator is essentially a motor running backwards. Instead of putting current in and getting motion out, you put motion in and get current out.
W
Write This Down — The A.C. Generator

An a.c. generator (alternator) produces alternating current. It has:

• A coil that rotates between the poles of a magnet
• Slip rings fixed to the coil (rotate with it)
• Brushes that maintain contact with the slip rings and connect to the external circuit

As the coil rotates, it cuts magnetic field lines, inducing an e.m.f. The current reverses direction every half-turn → alternating current (a.c.).

W
Write This Down — The A.C. Output Graph

The output of an a.c. generator is a sine wave:

• Current is maximum when the coil is horizontal (cutting field lines at the fastest rate)
• Current is zero when the coil is vertical (moving parallel to field lines, cutting none)

W
Write This Down — Increasing the Output

The maximum e.m.f. from a generator can be increased by:
• Increasing the number of turns on the coil
• Increasing the area of the coil
• Using a stronger magnet
• Rotating the coil faster (this also increases the frequency)

!
Don't Confuse — Motor vs Generator Parts
D.C. Motor A.C. Generator
Has a commutator (split ring) Has slip rings (complete rings)
Electricity in → motion out Motion in → electricity out
Produces d.c. motion Produces a.c. output
  • I can describe how an a.c. generator works
  • I know it uses slip rings (not a commutator)
  • I know when the current is maximum (coil horizontal) and zero (coil vertical)
  • I know 4 ways to increase the generator's output
Sections 9.10 – 9.12 Transformers and Power Transmission
Transformers change the voltage of an a.c. supply. They are essential for transmitting electricity across the country efficiently. This section has a key formula.
W
Write This Down — How a Transformer Works

A transformer has two coils of wire wound on a shared soft iron core:

• Primary coil (input) — connected to the a.c. supply
• Secondary coil (output) — connected to the load

The alternating current in the primary creates a changing magnetic field in the iron core. This changing field induces a voltage in the secondary coil (mutual induction).

Transformers only work with a.c. A steady d.c. would not create a changing field, so no voltage would be induced.

W
Write This Down — The Transformer Equation
Vs / Vp = Ns / Np

Vs = voltage on secondary coil (V)
Vp = voltage on primary coil (V)
Ns = number of turns on secondary coil
Np = number of turns on primary coil

W
Write This Down — Step-Up vs Step-Down
Step-Up Transformer Step-Down Transformer
More turns on secondary than primary Fewer turns on secondary than primary
Output voltage is HIGHER Output voltage is LOWER
Used at power stations (to increase voltage for transmission) Used at substations (to reduce voltage for homes)
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Transformer)

A transformer has 2000 turns on its primary coil and 100 turns on its secondary coil. If the input voltage is 200 V, what is the output voltage?

1. Write the formula: Vs / Vp = Ns / Np
2. Substitute: Vs / 200 = 100 / 2000
3. Simplify: Vs / 200 = 0.05
4. Rearrange: Vs = 200 x 0.05 = 10 V

This is a step-down transformer (fewer turns on secondary = lower voltage out).

W
Write This Down — Power Through a Transformer

If a transformer is 100% efficient (no energy lost):

Vp x Ip = Vs x Is

Input power = output power. If a transformer increases voltage, it decreases current by the same factor (and vice versa).

W
Write This Down — Why Transmit at High Voltage?

Transmission cables have resistance, which wastes energy as heat. Power loss in the cables = I2R.

By using a step-up transformer to increase the voltage, the current is reduced (since P = IV, same power at higher V means lower I). Lower current means much less power lost as heat in the cables (because power loss depends on I2).

At the other end, step-down transformers reduce the voltage to safe levels for homes (110–230 V).

?
Why This Matters — The Whole System

Power station → step-up transformer (to ~400,000 V) → transmission cables → step-down transformers (in stages) → homes at 230 V. This is a very common extended-answer exam question.

T
Study Tip — Explaining High-Voltage Transmission

The exam answer needs these four steps in order:
1. Step-up transformer increases voltage.
2. Since P = IV, higher V means lower I (for same power).
3. Power loss = I2R, so lower I means much less heat loss.
4. Step-down transformers reduce voltage for consumers.
Practise writing this out as a four-sentence paragraph.

  • I can describe how a transformer works (a.c. in primary, changing field, induces voltage in secondary)
  • I can write Vs/Vp = Ns/Np from memory
  • I know step-up = more turns on secondary = higher voltage
  • I can explain why electricity is transmitted at high voltage (reduce I, reduce I2R losses)
  • I know transformers only work on a.c.
  • I solved the worked example correctly (output = 10 V)
