Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State the difference between a conductor and an insulator. Give one example of each.
2 marks
Answer
A conductor allows electrons/current to flow through it (e.g. copper) [1]. An insulator does not allow electrons/current to flow through it (e.g. plastic/rubber) [1].
Key words: "electrons" or "current" + "flow through." Don't just say "conducts electricity" — say what that means.

2
What is the name and unit of the instrument used to measure: (a) current, (b) potential difference?
2 marks
Answer
(a) Ammeter, measured in amperes (A) [1].
(b) Voltmeter, measured in volts (V) [1].
Also know: ammeter goes in series, voltmeter goes in parallel.

3
State two differences between series and parallel circuits.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of:
• In series the current is the same everywhere; in parallel the current splits at junctions [1].
• In series the voltage is shared between components; in parallel each component gets the full battery voltage [1].
• In series, if one component breaks the whole circuit stops; in parallel the other branches keep working [1].
One mark per correct difference. Make sure you clearly compare series AND parallel in each point.

4
Name the three wires in a mains plug and state the purpose of the earth wire.
2 marks
Answer
The three wires are live, neutral, and earth [1]. The earth wire is a safety wire that connects the metal casing of an appliance to the ground, so that if a fault occurs (live wire touches the case), current flows safely to earth and the fuse blows [1].
The earth wire on its own is not enough — it works WITH the fuse. Mentioning both gets full marks on longer questions.

Intermediate — Can You Calculate and Explain?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to use V = IR, P = IV, and show your working clearly.
5
A resistor has a resistance of 20 Ω. The p.d. across it is 10 V. Calculate: (a) the current through the resistor [2], (b) the power dissipated by the resistor [2].
4 marks
Answer
(a) I = V / R = 10 / 20 = 0.5 A [1 for formula/substitution, 1 for correct answer with unit].

(b) P = I x V = 0.5 x 10 = 5 W [1 for formula/substitution, 1 for correct answer with unit].
Always show three steps: formula, substitution, answer + unit. Even if you get the number slightly wrong, you still pick up marks for method.

6
A current of 5 A flows through a heater for 2 minutes. The p.d. across the heater is 230 V. Calculate the energy transferred to the heater.
3 marks
Answer
First convert time: 2 minutes = 120 seconds [1].
E = V x I x t = 230 x 5 x 120 = 138,000 J (or 138 kJ) [1 for formula/substitution, 1 for correct answer with unit].
The sneaky part is converting minutes to seconds. If you forget, you lose a mark AND get the wrong number. Always check the time unit.

7
A hairdryer is rated at 1000 W, 230 V. It is made of plastic (double insulated). (a) What current does it draw? [2] (b) Should it have a 3 A or 13 A fuse? Explain. [2]
4 marks
Answer
(a) I = P / V = 1000 / 230 = 4.3 A (accept 4.35 A) [1 for method, 1 for answer].

(b) A 13 A fuse [1]. The normal current is 4.3 A, so a 3 A fuse would blow during normal use. The fuse must be rated slightly above the normal current but as close as possible, so 13 A is the correct choice from the available options [1].
The explanation must say WHY 3 A is too small (it would blow) and WHY 13 A is appropriate (above normal current). Just naming the fuse gets only 1 of the 2 marks.

Advanced — Can You Solve Multi-Step Problems?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need multiple formulas, clear working, and careful reasoning.
8
Two resistors, 4 Ω and 12 Ω, are connected in series to a 24 V battery. Calculate: (a) the total resistance [1], (b) the current in the circuit [2], (c) the p.d. across each resistor [2], (d) the power dissipated by the 12 Ω resistor [1].
6 marks
Answer
(a) Rtotal = 4 + 12 = 16 Ω [1].

(b) I = V / R = 24 / 16 = 1.5 A [1 for substitution, 1 for answer].

(c) P.d. across 4 Ω: V = IR = 1.5 x 4 = 6 V [1].
P.d. across 12 Ω: V = IR = 1.5 x 12 = 18 V [1].
Check: 6 + 18 = 24 V (matches battery).

(d) P = IV = 1.5 x 18 = 27 W [1].
This is a classic multi-step series circuit question. Each part feeds into the next. If you get (b) wrong, use your wrong answer consistently in (c) and (d) — you can still get "error carried forward" marks.

9
Two resistors, 6 Ω and 3 Ω, are connected in parallel to a 12 V battery. Calculate: (a) the combined resistance [2], (b) the current through each resistor [2], (c) the total current from the battery [1].
5 marks
Answer
(a) R = (R1 x R2) / (R1 + R2) = (6 x 3) / (6 + 3) = 18 / 9 = 2 Ω [1 for method, 1 for answer].

(b) In parallel, each resistor gets the full 12 V.
Current through 6 Ω: I = V / R = 12 / 6 = 2 A [1].
Current through 3 Ω: I = V / R = 12 / 3 = 4 A [1].

(c) Total current = 2 + 4 = 6 A [1].
Check: I = V / Rtotal = 12 / 2 = 6 A. Matches.
Key parallel rule: each branch gets the FULL battery voltage. The combined resistance (2 Ω) is less than either individual resistor — this is always true in parallel.

10
A small electric heater takes a power of 60 W from a 12 V supply. Calculate: (a) the current in the heater [2], (b) the resistance of the heater [2], (c) how much charge passes through the heater in 20 seconds [2].
6 marks
Answer
(a) P = IV, so I = P / V = 60 / 12 = 5 A [1 for rearranging, 1 for answer].

(b) R = V / I = 12 / 5 = 2.4 Ω [1 for formula, 1 for answer].

(c) Q = I x t = 5 x 20 = 100 C [1 for formula, 1 for answer].
This question tests THREE different formulas: P = IV, V = IR, and Q = It. You must be comfortable rearranging all of them. Each answer feeds into the next — get (a) right and the rest follows.
