Foundation — Can You Name It and Calculate It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
A car travels 600 m in 30 s. (a) Calculate its average speed. (b) Why is the actual speed at any moment usually different from the average speed?
3 marks
Answer
(a) speed = distance / time [1] = 600 / 30 = 20 m/s [1].
(b) Because the car's speed varies during the journey — it may speed up, slow down, or stop at different points [1].
Always write the formula first, then substitute. Even for an easy question like this, writing v = s/t gets you a mark.

2
A car increases its velocity from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 8 s. Calculate its average acceleration.
2 marks
Answer
acceleration = change in velocity / time [1]
a = (30 - 10) / 8 = 20 / 8 = 2.5 m/s2 [1].
Remember to subtract initial from final velocity first. Don't forget the unit m/s2.

3
State Newton's first law of motion and give one example of it in everyday life.
2 marks
Answer
If no resultant/external force acts on an object, it will remain stationary if at rest, or continue to move at a constant/steady speed in a straight line if moving [1].
Example: a passenger lurches forward when a bus brakes suddenly — the passenger's body continues moving at the original speed because no force has acted on it yet [1].
Any valid example accepted. Other good ones: a puck sliding on ice, an object floating in space. The key phrase examiners want is "constant speed in a straight line."

4
An astronaut has a mass of 80 kg. Calculate her weight (a) on Earth (g = 10 N/kg) and (b) on Mars (g = 3.7 N/kg). (c) What is her mass on Mars?
3 marks
Answer
(a) W = mg = 80 x 10 = 800 N [1].
(b) W = mg = 80 x 3.7 = 296 N [1].
(c) Her mass is 80 kg — mass does not change with location [1].
The key concept: weight changes with gravity, mass stays the same everywhere. If you wrote "80 kg" for (c) you understand the difference.

Intermediate — Can You Apply and Interpret?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to interpret graphs, apply formulas to multi-step problems, and explain physical situations.
5
A speed-time graph shows a motor cycle that: accelerates steadily from 0 to 30 m/s in the first 10 s, then travels at 30 m/s for the next 10 s, then decelerates to 0 m/s in the final 5 s.

(a) Calculate the acceleration in the first 10 s. (b) Calculate the total distance travelled during the entire 25 s journey.
4 marks
Answer
(a) acceleration = change in velocity / time = (30 - 0) / 10 = 3 m/s2 [1].

(b) Split the journey into three sections and find the area under the graph:
Section 1 (triangle): 1/2 x 10 x 30 = 150 m [1]
Section 2 (rectangle): 10 x 30 = 300 m [1]
Section 3 (triangle): 1/2 x 5 x 30 = 75 m
Total distance = 150 + 300 + 75 = 525 m [1].
Area under a speed-time graph = distance. Break it into simple shapes (triangles and rectangles). This exact type of question appears on almost every paper.

6
A car of mass 800 kg is driving along a straight road. The engine produces a driving force of 2000 N. The total drag (friction + air resistance) is 2000 N.

(a) State the size of the resultant force. (b) Describe the motion of the car. (c) The driving force is increased to 3200 N. Calculate the car's acceleration. (d) Explain why the car will eventually reach a new, higher constant speed.
4 marks
Answer
(a) Resultant force = 2000 - 2000 = 0 N [1].
(b) The car is travelling at a constant/steady speed (Newton's first law — balanced forces, no acceleration) [1].
(c) Resultant force = 3200 - 2000 = 1200 N. Using F = ma: a = F/m = 1200 / 800 = 1.5 m/s2 [1].
(d) As the car speeds up, the air resistance/drag increases [1]. Eventually, the drag force increases until it equals 3200 N, so the resultant force becomes zero again and the car reaches a new constant speed.
Part (d) tests the concept behind terminal velocity. The key mark: "as speed increases, drag/air resistance increases." This same logic applies to skydivers too.

7
A stone is dropped from rest near the Earth's surface. Assume g = 10 m/s2 and there is no air resistance.

(a) What is the stone's speed after 3 seconds? (b) A second stone is thrown downwards at 5 m/s. What is its speed after 3 seconds? (c) A third stone is thrown upwards at 20 m/s. What is its speed after 3 seconds, and in which direction is it moving?
4 marks
Answer
(a) The stone gains 10 m/s every second. After 3 s: speed = 0 + (10 x 3) = 30 m/s downwards [1].
(b) Already moving at 5 m/s. After 3 s: speed = 5 + (10 x 3) = 35 m/s downwards [1].
(c) Thrown upwards at 20 m/s. It decelerates at 10 m/s2.
After 1 s: 20 - 10 = 10 m/s (upwards)
After 2 s: 10 - 10 = 0 m/s (momentarily stationary at top)
After 3 s: 0 + 10 = 10 m/s downwards [2].
For (c), the acceleration due to gravity is always downwards, even when the stone is moving up. The stone slows down, stops, then speeds up downwards. After 2 s it has zero velocity at its highest point. 1 mark for 10 m/s, 1 mark for stating it's now moving downwards.

Advanced — Can You Explain and Solve Multi-Step Problems?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple steps, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
A girl wearing a parachute jumps from a helicopter. She does not open the parachute straight away. Her speed increases for several seconds, then becomes constant at about 60 m/s. She then opens the parachute and her speed decreases rapidly before becoming constant at about 5 m/s.

Explain, in terms of the forces acting on her, why her speed:
(a) increases at first,
(b) reaches a constant value of 60 m/s,
(c) decreases after the parachute opens,
(d) reaches a new, lower constant value.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Her weight acts downwards and is greater than the air resistance [1]. The resultant force is downwards, so she accelerates downwards [1].

(b) As her speed increases, the air resistance increases [1]. Eventually air resistance equals her weight. The forces are balanced / resultant force is zero, so she stops accelerating and moves at a constant (terminal) velocity [1].

(c) Opening the parachute greatly increases the air resistance [1]. Air resistance is now much greater than weight. The resultant force is upwards, so she decelerates.

(d) As she slows down, the air resistance decreases. Eventually air resistance equals weight again. Forces are balanced once more, so she reaches a new, lower terminal velocity [1].
This is THE classic IGCSE Physics question. The chain of logic must be: compare forces (which is bigger?) → state direction of resultant force → state the effect on motion (accelerate, decelerate, or constant speed). Don't just say "air resistance increases" — always say what happens to the RESULTANT force as a result.

9
A stone of mass 3 kg is sliding across a frozen pond at 10 m/s. It collides head-on with a lump of clay of mass 7 kg that is stationary on the ice. The stone sticks to the clay and they slide together in the same direction.

(a) Calculate the momentum of the stone before the collision. [2]
(b) State the total momentum after the collision. [1]
(c) Calculate the speed of the stone and clay after the collision. [2]
5 marks
Answer
(a) momentum = mass x velocity [1] = 3 x 10 = 30 kg m/s [1].

(b) By the law of conservation of momentum, total momentum after = total momentum before = 30 kg m/s [1].

(c) Total mass after collision = 3 + 7 = 10 kg.
momentum = mass x velocity, so velocity = momentum / mass [1]
v = 30 / 10 = 3 m/s [1].
Step-by-step: find momentum before, state it's conserved, use the combined mass to find the new velocity. The clay is stationary so its momentum = 0. Always show the formula first for the working mark.

10
A car of mass 900 kg accelerates from rest at a rate of 1.2 m/s2.

(a) Calculate the time taken to reach a velocity of 30 m/s. [3]
(b) Calculate the resultant force needed to produce this acceleration. [3]
(c) Even with the engine working at full power, the car's acceleration decreases as it goes faster. Explain why. [2]
8 marks
Answer
(a) acceleration = change in velocity / time [1]
Rearrange: time = change in velocity / acceleration [1]
t = 30 / 1.2 = 25 s [1].

(b) F = ma [1]
F = 900 x 1.2 [1]
F = 1080 N [1].

(c) As the car goes faster, the air resistance / drag force increases [1]. This means the resultant force (driving force minus drag) becomes smaller, so by F = ma, the acceleration decreases [1].
Part (c) is a 2-mark explanation question. The two marks are: (1) air resistance increases with speed, and (2) this reduces the resultant force so acceleration decreases. This is the same principle as terminal velocity. Always link back to the formula: smaller resultant F with the same mass means smaller a.
