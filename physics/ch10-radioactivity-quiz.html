Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the three particles found in an atom and state the charge of each one.
2 marks
Answer
Proton — positive (+) charge [1]
Neutron — no charge (neutral) [1]
Electron — negative (-) charge [1]
All three particles and their charges for full marks. 2 marks for at least two correct pairs. Protons and neutrons are in the nucleus; electrons orbit around it.

2
An atom of carbon has an atomic number of 6 and a mass number of 14. How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does it have?
2 marks
Answer
Protons = 6 (same as atomic number) [1]
Electrons = 6 (same as protons in a neutral atom)
Neutrons = 14 - 6 = 8 [1]
Neutrons = mass number - atomic number. This is the most common calculation in this topic.

3
State one thing that is the same and one thing that is different about the atoms of two isotopes of the same element.
2 marks
Answer
Same: number of protons (atomic number) [1]
Different: number of neutrons (and therefore mass number) [1]
Do NOT say "same number of electrons" for the first mark — the defining feature is the number of protons. The examiner wants "protons" specifically.

4
Name the three types of nuclear radiation and state which one is the most penetrating.
2 marks
Answer
Alpha, beta, and gamma [1]. Gamma is the most penetrating [1].
Remember the order: alpha is least penetrating (stopped by paper), beta is in the middle (stopped by aluminium), gamma is most penetrating (only reduced by thick lead/concrete).

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Define half-life. A radioactive sample has an activity of 1200 Bq and a half-life of 3 hours. What will the activity be after 9 hours?
4 marks
Answer
Definition: Half-life is the time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay, OR the time for the activity to fall to half its original value [1].

Calculation:
Number of half-lives = 9 / 3 = 3 half-lives [1]
After 1 half-life: 1200 / 2 = 600 Bq
After 2 half-lives: 600 / 2 = 300 Bq
After 3 half-lives: 300 / 2 = 150 Bq [1] [1]
Show each halving step — this is your working and earns marks even if you make an arithmetic slip at the end.

6
Radium-226 (atomic number 88) undergoes alpha decay to form radon. Write the nuclear equation for this decay and state the mass number and atomic number of the radon formed.
3 marks
Answer
22688Ra → 22286Rn + 42He [1]

Mass number of radon = 226 - 4 = 222 [1]
Atomic number of radon = 88 - 2 = 86 [1]
Alpha decay: mass number drops by 4, atomic number drops by 2. Always check: top numbers must balance (226 = 222 + 4) and bottom numbers must balance (88 = 86 + 2).

7
Explain why a beta source is used (rather than alpha or gamma) for monitoring the thickness of metal sheets in a factory.
3 marks
Answer
Alpha would be stopped by even a thin sheet of metal, so no radiation would reach the detector regardless of thickness — it could not detect changes [1].
Gamma would pass through metal of any thickness without being significantly absorbed, so the reading would not change with thickness [1].
Beta is partially absorbed by thin metal — the amount reaching the detector varies with thickness, so changes can be detected [1].
This is a classic "why this type and not the others" question. Explain why both alternatives would NOT work, then why the chosen one DOES work.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
In the Rutherford-Geiger-Marsden experiment, alpha particles were fired at a thin sheet of gold foil. Describe the three observations and explain what each tells us about the structure of the atom.
6 marks
Answer
Observation 1: Most alpha particles passed straight through the foil [1].
Explanation: The atom is mostly empty space [1].

Observation 2: Some alpha particles were deflected at small angles [1].
Explanation: There is a positive charge in the atom that repels the positive alpha particles [1].

Observation 3: A very few alpha particles bounced straight back [1].
Explanation: The nucleus is very small, dense, and massive — concentrated enough to repel the alpha particle completely [1].
6-mark question = 3 observations + 3 explanations. Pair each observation with its explanation. This is one of the most commonly examined extended-answer questions in IGCSE Physics.

9
Describe what happens during nuclear fission and explain how a chain reaction occurs. Include the roles of the moderator and control rods in a nuclear reactor.
6 marks
Answer
A neutron hits a uranium-235 nucleus [1]. The nucleus becomes unstable and splits into two smaller (daughter) nuclei, releasing energy and two or three more neutrons [1]. These released neutrons can go on to hit other U-235 nuclei, causing them to split as well — this is a chain reaction [1].

In a nuclear reactor, the chain reaction must be controlled:
• The moderator (e.g. graphite or water) slows down the neutrons so they are more likely to be captured by U-235 nuclei [1].
• The control rods (containing boron or cadmium) absorb excess neutrons. They are raised to speed up the reaction and lowered to slow it down [1] [1].
Cover: what fission is, what a chain reaction is, what the moderator does, what control rods do. Each idea is roughly one mark.

10
Compare alpha, beta, and gamma radiation in terms of: (a) what each one is, (b) penetrating power, (c) ionising ability. Then explain why alpha radiation is the most dangerous type if a radioactive source is inside the body, but the least dangerous if the source is outside the body.
6 marks
Answer
(a) What each is: Alpha = 2 protons + 2 neutrons (helium nucleus). Beta = high-speed electron. Gamma = electromagnetic wave [1].

(b) Penetrating power: Alpha is stopped by paper/skin. Beta is stopped by a few mm of aluminium. Gamma is only reduced by thick lead or concrete [1].

(c) Ionising ability: Alpha is the most ionising, beta is weakly ionising, gamma is the least ionising [1].

Inside the body: Alpha is most dangerous because it is the most ionising — it causes the most damage to cells and DNA in a small area [1]. It cannot escape the body, so all its energy is absorbed by nearby tissue [1].

Outside the body: Alpha is least dangerous because it is stopped by the skin and cannot reach internal organs [1]. Beta and gamma can penetrate the skin and reach organs.
This is the "if you can answer this, you own the chapter" question. It combines the comparison table with application to a real scenario. Examiners love "inside vs outside" questions.
