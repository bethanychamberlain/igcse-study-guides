Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Current (I) Flow of charge (electrons in a metal). Measured in amperes (A) by an ammeter in series. Voltage (current is the flow; voltage is what pushes it)
Potential difference (V) Work done per unit charge. Measured in volts (V) by a voltmeter in parallel. E.m.f. (e.m.f. is the p.d. across the battery; p.d. is across a component)
Resistance (R) How much a component opposes current flow. Measured in ohms (Ω). —
Ohm's law Current is proportional to p.d. at constant temperature. Gives a straight-line graph through the origin. V = IR (V = IR is just a formula and always works; Ohm's law is the specific condition of constant R)
Thermistor Resistance decreases when temperature increases. LDR (that responds to light, not temperature)
LDR Light-dependent resistor. Resistance decreases in bright light. Thermistor (that responds to heat, not light)
Diode Lets current flow in one direction only (low R forward, very high R reverse). —
Power (P) Rate of energy transfer. Measured in watts (W). 1 W = 1 J/s. Energy (power is energy per second, not total energy)
Kilowatt-hour (kWh) The energy used by a 1 kW appliance in 1 hour. 1 kWh = 3,600,000 J. This is what electricity companies charge for. Joules (same thing, different scale — kWh is the practical unit)
Fuse Thin wire in the live wire that melts if current is too high, breaking the circuit. Circuit breaker (same job, but can be reset)
Earth wire Safety wire connecting the metal case to ground. If a fault occurs, current flows to earth and blows the fuse. Neutral wire (neutral completes the circuit; earth is only for safety)

Series vs Parallel Circuits — THE Rules

Series Parallel
Current Same through all components Splits at junctions; total = sum of branches
Voltage (p.d.) Shared; p.d.s add up to battery p.d. Same across each branch
Resistance Rtotal = R1 + R2 (adds up) R = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2) (gets smaller)
If one breaks Everything stops Other branches keep working
Exam Trap — #1 Mistake Students Make
Which quantity is the SAME?
Series = same I (current).
Parallel = same V (voltage).
Memorise it: S = same I. P = same V.

A.C. vs D.C.

Direct Current (d.c.) Alternating Current (a.c.)
Direction Flows one way only Reverses direction repeatedly
Source Batteries, solar cells Mains supply, generators
Frequency n/a 50 Hz (UK/EU) or 60 Hz (US)

Special Components — How Resistance Changes

Component Resistance When... Used For
Thermistor Hot → LOW resistance Temperature sensors
LDR Bright → LOW resistance Light sensors, street lights
Diode Forward → LOW; Reverse → VERY HIGH One-way valves for current
Filament lamp Hotter → HIGHER resistance Does NOT obey Ohm's law

The Big Seven Formulas (Learn These Cold)

Formula What It Calculates Units
Q = I × t Charge C (coulombs), A, s
V = W / Q Potential difference V (volts), J, C
V = I × R Resistance (or current, or voltage) V, A, Ω (ohms)
P = I × V Power W (watts), A, V
E = V × I × t Energy transferred J (joules), V, A, s
R = R1 + R2 Total resistance in series Ω
R = (R1×R2) / (R1+R2) Total resistance in parallel (2 resistors) Ω
Exam Trap — Milliamps
1000 mA = 1 A. If the exam gives milliamps, divide by 1000 before substituting into any formula. Example: 250 mA = 0.25 A.

Say It in One Sentence

Current The rate of flow of charge — how many coulombs pass a point each second.
Voltage The energy given to (or taken from) each coulomb of charge — 1 V means 1 J per coulomb.
Resistance How hard it is for current to flow — more resistance means less current for the same voltage.
Ohm's law Current is proportional to p.d. at constant temperature — gives a straight-line graph through the origin.
Power How fast energy is transferred — a 60 W bulb uses 60 joules every second.
Series circuit One single loop — same current everywhere, voltages add up, resistances add up.
Parallel circuit Branches — same voltage across each branch, currents add up, total resistance is smaller than the smallest.
Fuse + earth wire If the live wire touches the metal case, current goes through earth wire, fuse melts, circuit breaks — you don't get electrocuted.

Memory Tricks

The VIR Triangle
Draw a triangle: V on top, I and R on the bottom. Cover what you want to find: Cover V → V = I × R. Cover I → I = V / R. Cover R → R = V / I.
Ammeter vs Voltmeter
Ammeter = in series (think: the current has to go through it, so it sits IN the line). Voltmeter = in parallel (think: it sits across the component, measuring the difference between two points).
Parallel Resistance Shortcut
"Product over sum" for two resistors: R = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2). The answer is always less than the smallest individual resistor.
Choosing a Fuse
Use P = IV to find the normal current, then pick the fuse slightly above that value. Example: 500 W at 230 V → I = 500/230 = 2.2 A → use a 3 A fuse (not 13 A).
Switch and Fuse Location
Both go in the LIVE wire. Why? So when they operate, the dangerous live supply is disconnected from the appliance.
Electron Flow vs Conventional Current
Electrons flow from − to +. Conventional current is defined as + to −. The exam uses conventional current unless it specifically says "electron flow."

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Calculate the current through a 6 Ω resistor with 12 V across it" I = V/R = 12/6 = 2 A
"Two resistors (4 Ω and 6 Ω) in series. Total resistance?" R = 4 + 6 = 10 Ω
"Two resistors (4 Ω and 12 Ω) in parallel. Total resistance?" R = (4 × 12)/(4 + 12) = 48/16 = 3 Ω
"A lamp takes 3 A at 12 V. What is the power?" P = IV = 3 × 12 = 36 W
"A 2 kW heater runs for 3 hours. Energy used in kWh?" E = P × t = 2 × 3 = 6 kWh
"What happens to the current when resistance increases?" Current decreases (V = IR, same V, bigger R means smaller I)
"Why does a thermistor's resistance decrease when heated?" Higher temperature releases more charge carriers, making it easier for current to flow
"Why must the fuse be in the live wire?" So when it blows, the appliance is disconnected from the dangerous live supply
"How do fuse and earth wire protect the user?" Fault → live wire touches case → current flows through earth wire → large current blows fuse → circuit breaks
"State the difference between a.c. and d.c." D.C. flows in one direction; A.C. reverses direction repeatedly
