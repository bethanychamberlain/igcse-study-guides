Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Atomic number Number of protons in the nucleus. Tells you what element it is. (The small bottom number.) Mass number (that counts protons + neutrons)
Mass number Total number of protons + neutrons (nucleons). (The big top number.) Atomic number (protons only)
Isotopes Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Ions (they have different numbers of electrons, not neutrons)
Ionisation Knocking electrons off atoms, turning them into charged ions. This is how radiation damages cells. —
Half-life The time for half the radioactive nuclei to decay (or for the activity to halve). It is NOT the time for ALL nuclei to decay
Activity Number of decays per second. Measured in becquerels (Bq). 1 Bq = 1 decay/s. —
Background radiation Low-level radiation always present from natural sources (radon gas, rocks, cosmic rays, food). —
Nuclear fission Splitting a heavy nucleus (e.g. U-235) into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy and 2–3 neutrons. Fusion (that is JOINING light nuclei)
Nuclear fusion Joining two light nuclei (e.g. hydrogen) to form a heavier one, releasing energy. Powers the Sun. Fission (that is SPLITTING heavy nuclei)
Chain reaction Neutrons from one fission trigger more fissions, which release more neutrons, and so on. —

Alpha vs Beta vs Gamma — THE Table to Learn

Alpha (α) Beta (β) Gamma (γ)
What is it? 2 protons + 2 neutrons (helium nucleus) High-speed electron from the nucleus Electromagnetic wave
Charge +2 −1 0
Mass Heavy (4 nucleons) Very light No mass
Ionising power Strongest Weak Weakest
Penetrating power Stopped by paper or skin Stopped by a few mm of aluminium Reduced (never fully stopped) by thick lead or concrete

Memory Trick — The Trade-Off
More ionising = less penetrating. Alpha is a big heavy bullet — smashes everything nearby but gets stopped fast. Gamma is a tiny fast needle — slips through almost everything but rarely hits anything.

What Changes in Alpha vs Beta Decay

Alpha Decay Beta Decay
Mass number Goes down by 4 Stays the same
Atomic number Goes down by 2 Goes up by 1
What happens Nucleus loses 2p + 2n (a He-4 nucleus) A neutron turns into a proton; electron is ejected
Particle emitted 42He 0−1e
Exam Trap — Beta Decay Direction
Students often think beta decay reduces the atomic number. It does the opposite — a neutron turns into a proton, so the atomic number goes UP by 1 and a new element is formed.

Fission vs Fusion

Fission Fusion
Process Splitting heavy nuclei Joining light nuclei
Fuel Uranium-235 or plutonium-239 Hydrogen isotopes
Where Nuclear power stations Stars (Sun)
Waste Radioactive (long-lived) Helium (not radioactive)
Temperature needed Moderate (neutron-triggered) Extremely high (~40 million °C)

Memory Trick
Fission = fissure = split apart. Fusion = fuse = join together.

Say It in One Sentence

Isotopes Same element, same protons, different neutrons — they behave the same chemically but may be radioactive.
Radioactive decay Unstable nuclei randomly and spontaneously break apart, emitting alpha, beta, or gamma radiation.
Half-life The time it takes for half the radioactive atoms to decay — keep halving to solve problems.
Alpha danger Most dangerous inside the body (most ionising); least dangerous outside (stopped by skin).
Gamma danger Most dangerous outside the body (penetrates skin to reach organs); least ionising overall.
Chain reaction Each fission releases neutrons that trigger more fissions — controlled in a reactor, uncontrolled in a bomb.
Gold foil experiment Most alphas went straight through (atom is mostly empty space); a few bounced back (nucleus is tiny, dense, and positive).

Memory Tricks

Balancing Nuclear Equations
Top numbers (mass) must add up the same on both sides. Bottom numbers (atomic) must add up the same on both sides. If you know the parent and the particle, you can always find the daughter nucleus.
Half-Life Calculations — Just Keep Halving
800 Bq, half-life 2 hours. After 6 hours = 3 half-lives.
800 → 400 → 200 → 100 Bq. Never divide by a fraction. Just halve, halve, halve.
Which Radiation for Which Job?
Smoke detector = alpha (stopped by a tiny bit of smoke). Thickness gauge = beta (passes through thin material, absorbed by thick). Medical tracer = gamma (escapes the body so detector can see it). Always ask: "Does it need to get through, or does it need to be stopped?"
Decay Is Random and Spontaneous
Nothing speeds it up or slows it down — not temperature, pressure, or chemical changes. Half-life is a fixed property of each isotope.
Reactor Parts — FMC
Fuel (uranium). Moderator (slows neutrons — graphite or water). Control rods (absorb neutrons — boron or cadmium). Lower rods = slower reaction.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What is an isotope?" Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
"Which radiation is most ionising?" Alpha — big, slow, heavily charged, smashes into everything nearby
"Which radiation is most penetrating?" Gamma — only reduced by thick lead or concrete, never fully stopped
"Why is alpha most dangerous inside the body?" It is the most ionising, so it causes the most damage to cells and DNA when inside
"Define half-life" The time for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay (or activity to halve)
"Activity of 6400 Bq, half-life 3 days, activity after 12 days?" 12/3 = 4 half-lives. 6400 → 3200 → 1600 → 800 → 400 Bq
"What is nuclear fission?" Splitting a heavy nucleus into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy and 2–3 neutrons
"What is nuclear fusion?" Joining two light nuclei to form a heavier one, releasing energy. Needs ~40 million °C.
"Describe the gold foil experiment results" Most α passed through (atom mostly empty); some deflected (nucleus is positive); a few bounced back (nucleus is tiny, dense, heavy)
