Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State the difference between a transverse wave and a longitudinal wave. Give one example of each.
2 marks
Answer
In a transverse wave, the oscillations are at right angles to the direction of wave travel [1]. Example: light waves / water waves. In a longitudinal wave, the oscillations are parallel to (in the same direction as) the direction of wave travel [1]. Example: sound waves.
Must mention direction of oscillations relative to wave travel. Just saying "up and down" vs "back and forth" without linking to wave direction loses marks.

2
State the equation linking wave speed, frequency, and wavelength.
1 mark
Answer
speed = frequency × wavelength (or v = f × λ) [1].
Either the word equation or the symbol equation gets the mark. Learn both.

3
Why can sound not travel through a vacuum?
2 marks
Answer
Sound waves need a medium (material) to travel through [1]. In a vacuum there are no particles to vibrate / pass on the compressions and rarefactions [1].
Two marks, two ideas: (1) sound needs a medium, (2) no particles in a vacuum. Saying just "there's no air" only gets one mark — explain WHY air is needed.

4
What is ultrasound? State the approximate frequency range of human hearing.
2 marks
Answer
Ultrasound is sound with a frequency above 20 000 Hz (above the upper limit of human hearing) [1]. The human hearing range is approximately 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz [1].
Must state "above 20 000 Hz" — just saying "above what humans can hear" is too vague for full marks.

Intermediate — Can You Explain and Calculate?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to show working in calculations and explain effects using correct terminology.
5
A sound wave has a frequency of 660 Hz and travels at 330 m/s in air. Calculate its wavelength. Show your working.
3 marks
Answer
Step 1: Write the formula: v = f × λ [1]
Step 2: Rearrange: λ = v / f [1]
Step 3: Substitute and calculate: λ = 330 / 660 = 0.5 m [1]
Three-step method: formula [1], rearrange/substitute [1], correct answer with unit [1]. If you skip straight to the answer and get it wrong, you get 0. Show your working!

6
A person stands 220 m from a large cliff and claps. They hear the echo 1.33 seconds later. Calculate the speed of sound.
3 marks
Answer
Step 1: Total distance = 2 × 220 = 440 m (sound goes there and back) [1]
Step 2: speed = distance / time [1]
Step 3: speed = 440 / 1.33 = 331 m/s (accept 330 m/s) [1]
The key step most students miss: the sound travels to the cliff AND back, so total distance = 2 × distance to cliff. If you use 220 instead of 440, you get half the real speed.

7
Two sounds are displayed on an oscilloscope. Sound A has waves that are taller and closer together than sound B. Describe how the two sounds would differ to a listener.
3 marks
Answer
Sound A has a greater amplitude than sound B, so it would be louder [1]. Sound A has waves closer together, meaning it has a higher frequency [1], so it would have a higher pitch [1].
Always link the oscilloscope feature to the correct physical property AND the listening experience. Taller = bigger amplitude = louder. Closer together = higher frequency = higher pitch.

Advanced — Can You Discuss and Apply?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Waves in a ripple tank travel from deep water into shallow water. (a) What happens to the speed of the waves? (b) What happens to the wavelength? (c) What happens to the frequency? (d) Explain why the waves change direction at the boundary.
5 marks
Answer
(a) The speed decreases (waves slow down in shallow water) [1].
(b) The wavelength decreases [1].
(c) The frequency stays the same [1].
(d) Because v = fλ and the frequency is unchanged, the decrease in speed causes a decrease in wavelength [1]. When wavefronts on one side of the beam slow down before the other side, the direction of travel changes — this is called refraction [1].
The key fact is that frequency NEVER changes during refraction. If speed decreases and frequency is constant, then wavelength must decrease (from v = fλ). Part (d) needs you to link wavefront behaviour to the direction change.

9
A ship's echo-sounder uses ultrasound with a frequency of 40 kHz. The speed of sound in seawater is 1400 m/s. A pulse takes 0.03 seconds to travel to the sea-bed and return. (a) Calculate the depth of the water. (b) Calculate the wavelength of the ultrasound. (c) Give two reasons why ultrasound is used instead of X-rays for scanning unborn babies.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Total distance = 1400 × 0.03 = 42 m [1]. Depth = 42 / 2 = 21 m [1].

(b) Frequency = 40 kHz = 40 000 Hz. λ = v / f = 1400 / 40 000 = 0.035 m [1].

(c) Ultrasound does not damage/harm living cells, while X-rays can cause cell damage [1]. Ultrasound can distinguish between different layers of soft tissue, which X-rays cannot [1]. Also accept: ultrasound is non-ionising [1].
Part (a): remember to divide by 2. Part (b): convert kHz to Hz first (40 kHz = 40 000 Hz). Part (c): two DIFFERENT reasons needed — don't repeat the same point in different words.

10
Waves pass through a gap in a harbour wall. (a) What is the name of the effect where waves spread out after passing through the gap? (b) How would the amount of spreading change if the gap were made much wider? Explain your answer. (c) Explain why you can hear someone talking around a corner of a building, but you cannot see them around the same corner.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Diffraction [1].

(b) The amount of spreading would decrease [1]. Diffraction is most significant when the gap size is about the same as the wavelength; a much wider gap produces less spreading [1].

(c) Sound waves have wavelengths of a few centimetres to a few metres, which is similar to the size of everyday obstacles like buildings, so they diffract (spread) significantly around corners [1]. Light waves have very short wavelengths (less than a thousandth of a millimetre), much smaller than the building, so they hardly diffract and travel in straight lines — you cannot see around the corner [1].
Part (c) is a classic exam question. The answer hinges on comparing wavelengths to obstacle size. Sound wavelength is similar to obstacle size = lots of diffraction. Light wavelength is much smaller = very little diffraction.
