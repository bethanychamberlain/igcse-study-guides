Rays and Waves

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Normal An imaginary line drawn at 90° to a surface at the point where a ray hits it. All angles are measured from this line. The surface itself (angles are NEVER measured from the surface)
Angle of incidence The angle between the incoming ray and the normal. Angle of reflection (they are equal, but they are on opposite sides of the normal)
Refraction The bending of light when it passes from one medium to another because its speed changes. Reflection (bouncing off) or diffraction (spreading around edges)
Refractive index (n) A number that tells you how much a material slows down light. n = speed in vacuum / speed in medium. —
Critical angle The angle of incidence at which the refracted ray travels along the surface (angle of refraction = 90°). Total internal reflection (TIR happens when you go ABOVE the critical angle)
Total internal reflection When light hits a boundary at more than the critical angle, ALL of it reflects back inside. No refracted ray. Ordinary reflection (TIR only works from dense → less dense, e.g. glass → air)
Dispersion White light splitting into a spectrum of colours when passing through a prism, because each colour refracts differently. Diffraction (that is waves spreading through a gap)
Convex lens Thickest in the middle. Converges (brings together) parallel rays to a focal point. Concave lens (thinnest in the middle, diverges rays)
Virtual image An image formed where rays only appear to come from. Cannot be projected onto a screen. Real image (formed where rays actually meet; CAN be projected)
Electromagnetic spectrum The family of transverse waves: radio, microwave, infrared, visible, UV, X-ray, gamma. All travel at 3 × 108 m/s in a vacuum. —
Digital signal A signal with only two values (on/off, 1/0). Can be cleaned up perfectly, unlike analogue. Analogue signal (varies continuously, degrades with noise)

Reflection vs Refraction — Side by Side

Reflection Refraction
What happens Light bounces off a surface Light bends as it enters a new medium
Why Surface acts like a mirror Light changes speed in a different material
Key rule angle i = angle r n = sin i / sin r (Snell's law)
Angles from The normal (not the surface!) The normal (not the surface!)
Direction change Same medium Into denser medium → bends towards normal

Convex vs Concave Lens

Convex (Converging) Concave (Diverging)
Shape Fat in middle, thin at edges Thin in middle, fat at edges
What it does Brings parallel rays together at F Spreads parallel rays apart
Image types Real or virtual (depends on object distance) Always virtual, upright, diminished
Corrects Long sight (hyperopia) Short sight (myopia)
Memory Trick
Short sight = concave (both dip inwards like a valley). Long sight = convex (bulges outwards like a hill).

Say It in One Sentence

Reflection Light bounces off a surface; angle in = angle out, always measured from the normal.
Refraction Light bends when it enters a new medium because it changes speed — towards the normal if it slows down, away if it speeds up.
Snell's law n = sin i / sin r — links the angles to the refractive index (no units).
Critical angle sin c = 1/n — the angle where the refracted ray runs along the boundary; above this angle, you get TIR.
TIR All light reflects back inside when going from dense to less dense at an angle greater than the critical angle — used in optical fibres.
Plane mirror image Same size, same distance behind, laterally inverted, virtual — cannot be put on a screen.
EM waves All transverse, all travel at 3 × 108 m/s in a vacuum, all obey v = fλ.
Digital vs analogue Digital signals (1s and 0s) can be cleaned up perfectly; analogue signals (continuous) amplify noise along with the signal.

Memory Tricks

Bending Direction
Light going into a denser medium (e.g. air → glass) slows down and bends towards the normal. Think of a car driving from road onto sand: the first wheel to hit sand slows down, pulling the car towards that side.
TIR = Two Conditions
Travelling from dense to less dense, and the angle must be Greater than the critical angle. If either condition fails, no TIR.
Wavelength and Frequency Are Opposite
All EM waves travel at the same speed (v = fλ). So if wavelength goes up, frequency must come down. Radio = long λ, low f. Gamma = short λ, high f.
ROYGBIV for Visible Spectrum
The colours of the visible spectrum in order: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet. Red is deviated least by a prism; violet is deviated most.
Optical Fibres Beat Copper
Remember: M-I-S-S — More data, no Interference, less Signal loss, Smaller and lighter.

Key Formulas (with Units)

Formula What It Tells You Units
angle i = angle r Law of reflection degrees (°)
n = sin i / sin r Snell's law (refraction) n has no unit (ratio)
n = speed in vacuum / speed in medium Refractive index definition n has no unit
sin c = 1 / n Critical angle for TIR c in degrees (°)
v = f × λ Wave equation (all EM waves) m/s, Hz, m

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"State the laws of reflection" (1) Angle of incidence = angle of reflection. (2) Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal are all in the same plane.
"Describe the image in a plane mirror" Same size, same distance behind, laterally inverted, virtual
"Light enters glass from air. Describe what happens." It slows down and bends towards the normal (into the denser medium)
"State two conditions for total internal reflection" (1) Light going from denser to less dense medium. (2) Angle of incidence greater than the critical angle.
"Why are optical fibres better than copper cables?" Carry more data, less signal loss, no electrical interference, thinner and lighter
"Which lens corrects short sight?" Concave (diverging) lens — spreads rays out so they focus on the retina
"List the EM spectrum in order of increasing frequency" Radio, microwave, infrared, visible, UV, X-ray, gamma
"What properties do all EM waves share?" All transverse, all travel at 3 × 108 m/s in vacuum, all transfer energy, all obey v = fλ
"Advantage of digital over analogue signals" Digital signals can be regenerated (noise removed); analogue signals amplify the noise
