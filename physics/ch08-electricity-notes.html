Sections 8.1 – 8.3 Charge, Conductors, and Electric Fields
Everything electrical starts here: what charge is, how it moves, and the fields it creates. This is the foundation for the whole chapter.
W
Write This Down — Two Types of Charge

There are two types of electric charge: positive (+) and negative (−).

Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.
The closer the charges, the greater the force between them.

W
Write This Down — Where Charges Come From

Atoms contain protons (+), neutrons (no charge), and electrons (−). Normally atoms are neutral (equal + and −). When materials are rubbed together, electrons transfer from one to the other. One gains electrons (becomes negative), the other loses electrons (becomes positive).

Rubbing does not create charge — it just separates charges that are already there.

W
Write This Down — Conductors vs Insulators
Conductors Insulators
Let electrons pass through them Electrons are tightly held, cannot move
Metals (copper, silver, aluminium) Plastics, glass, rubber, dry air
Have free electrons Easy to charge by rubbing (charge stays put)

Semiconductors (silicon, germanium) are in between — poor conductors when cold, better when warm.

W
Write This Down — Electric Fields

An electric field is the region around a charged object where another charge would feel a force. Field lines point away from positive and towards negative. The closer the field lines, the stronger the field.

?
Why This Matters

Electrostatic charge has real uses: electrostatic precipitators clean smoke from factory chimneys, and photocopiers/laser printers use static charge to place ink. The exam loves asking about practical applications of static charge.

!
Don't Confuse — Electrons vs Current Direction

Electrons are negative. They flow from − to +. But conventional current direction is defined as + to −. The exam uses conventional current unless it specifically says "electron flow." Don't mix them up.

  • I know: like charges repel, unlike attract
  • I can explain charging by rubbing (electrons transfer)
  • I know the difference between conductors and insulators
  • I know electric field lines point away from + and towards −
Sections 8.4 – 8.5 Current, Voltage, and Charge
Now we get to circuits. Current, voltage, and charge are the three big ideas you need before tackling any circuit problem.
W
Write This Down — Current

Current is a flow of charge (electrons in a metal). It is measured in amperes (A) using an ammeter connected in series.

The current is the same at all points in a simple (series) circuit.

W
Write This Down — Charge and Current Formula
Q = I x t

Q = charge (coulombs, C)
I = current (amperes, A)
t = time (seconds, s)

1 ampere = 1 coulomb per second. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C).

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Charge)

A current of 3 A flows for 20 seconds. How much charge passes?

1. Write the formula: Q = I x t
2. Substitute: Q = 3 x 20
3. Calculate: Q = 60 C
W
Write This Down — Potential Difference (Voltage)

Potential difference (p.d.) is the work done per unit charge. It is measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter connected in parallel across a component.

V = W / Q

V = potential difference (volts, V)
W = work done / energy transferred (joules, J)
Q = charge (coulombs, C)

If the p.d. across a lamp is 6 V, then 6 joules of energy are transferred for every coulomb of charge that passes through it.

W
Write This Down — E.M.F. and P.D. Rule

E.m.f. (electromotive force) is the p.d. across a cell/battery when no current flows. In a circuit:

The sum of the p.d.s across the components equals the p.d. across the battery.

!
Don't Confuse — Ammeter vs Voltmeter

Ammeter: connected in series (in line with the component). Measures current (A).
Voltmeter: connected in parallel (across the component). Measures p.d. (V).
If you swap them round, you get wrong readings and might damage the ammeter.

T
Study Tip — Milliamps

1000 mA = 1 A. If the exam gives you milliamps, divide by 1000 before using it in any formula. Example: 500 mA = 0.5 A.

  • I can write Q = It from memory and know all units
  • I know V = W/Q and what "1 volt" means (1 joule per coulomb)
  • I know ammeter = series, voltmeter = parallel
  • I know p.d.s across components add up to the battery p.d.
Sections 8.6 – 8.8 Resistance and Ohm's Law
Resistance is what opposes current flow. V = IR is probably the single most important equation in this chapter — you will use it constantly.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Resistance is a measure of how much a component opposes the flow of current. It is measured in ohms (Ω).

W
Write This Down — The Big Formula
V = I x R

V = potential difference (volts, V)
I = current (amperes, A)
R = resistance (ohms, Ω)

This can be rearranged:
I = V / R   and   R = V / I

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (V = IR)

A 12 Ω resistor has a p.d. of 6 V across it. What is the current?

1. Write the formula: I = V / R
2. Substitute: I = 6 / 12
3. Calculate: I = 0.5 A
W
Write This Down — Ohm's Law

Ohm's law: The current through a metal conductor is proportional to the p.d. across it, provided the temperature stays constant.

On a graph, this gives a straight line through the origin. The resistance stays constant.

W
Write This Down — Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance of a wire depends on:

• Length — longer = more resistance (double length = double resistance)
• Cross-sectional area — thinner = more resistance (half area = double resistance)
• Material — nichrome has more resistance than copper
• Temperature — for metals, higher temp = more resistance

W
Write This Down — Special Components
Component How Its Resistance Changes
Thermistor High resistance when cold, low resistance when hot
LDR (light-dependent resistor) High resistance in the dark, low resistance in bright light
Diode Very low resistance in one direction (forward), very high in the other (reverse)
T
Study Tip — The VIR Triangle

Draw a triangle with V on top, I and R on the bottom. Cover the letter you want to find:
• Cover V → V = I x R
• Cover I → I = V / R
• Cover R → R = V / I
Practise using this until you can rearrange without thinking.

!
Don't Confuse — Ohm's Law is NOT V = IR

V = IR is just a formula — it always works. Ohm's law is the specific statement that current is proportional to p.d. at constant temperature. A filament lamp does NOT obey Ohm's law (its resistance changes as it heats up), but you can still use V = IR for it at any given moment.

  • I can write V = IR and rearrange it three ways
  • I can state Ohm's law (current proportional to p.d. at constant temp)
  • I know the 4 factors affecting resistance of a wire
  • I know how thermistors, LDRs, and diodes work
Sections 8.9 – 8.10 Series and Parallel Circuits
This is where your exam results told us you struggle. Series and parallel circuits have different rules. You MUST know both sets of rules cold.
W
Write This Down — Series vs Parallel Rules
Series Parallel
Current Same through all components Splits at junctions; total = sum of branches
Voltage (p.d.) Shared between components; sum = battery p.d. Same across each branch
Resistance Rtotal = R1 + R2 (adds up, gets bigger) Rtotal = (R1 x R2) / (R1 + R2) (gets smaller)
If one breaks Everything stops (circuit broken) Other branches keep working
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Series)

Two resistors (3 Ω and 6 Ω) in series with an 18 V battery. Find the current and p.d. across each.

1. Total resistance: R = 3 + 6 = 9 Ω
2. Current: I = V / R = 18 / 9 = 2 A
3. P.d. across 3 Ω: V = IR = 2 x 3 = 6 V
4. P.d. across 6 Ω: V = IR = 2 x 6 = 12 V
5. Check: 6 + 12 = 18 V (matches battery) ✓
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Parallel)

Two resistors (3 Ω and 6 Ω) in parallel with an 18 V battery. Find the currents.

1. Each resistor gets the full 18 V
2. Current in 3 Ω: I = 18 / 3 = 6 A
3. Current in 6 Ω: I = 18 / 6 = 3 A
4. Total current from battery: I = 6 + 3 = 9 A
!
Don't Confuse — Which Quantity is the SAME?

This is the number one mistake students make:
• Series: same CURRENT, different voltages
• Parallel: same VOLTAGE, different currents
Memorise it: S = same I (series, same I). P = same V (parallel, same V).

T
Study Tip — Parallel Resistance Shortcut

For two resistors in parallel: product over sum.
R = (R1 x R2) / (R1 + R2)
Example: 3 Ω and 6 Ω in parallel = (3 x 6) / (3 + 6) = 18 / 9 = 2 Ω.
The combined resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistor.

  • I can state all the rules for series circuits (current same, voltages add, resistances add)
  • I can state all the rules for parallel circuits (voltage same, currents add, resistance decreases)
  • I solved the series worked example and got the right answers
  • I solved the parallel worked example and got the right answers
  • I know: series = same current, parallel = same voltage
Section 8.12 Electrical Power and Energy
Power tells you how fast energy is transferred. These are calculation-heavy sections — use the three-step method every time.
W
Write This Down — Power Formula
P = I x V

P = power (watts, W)
I = current (amperes, A)
V = potential difference (volts, V)

Power is the rate of energy transfer. 1 watt = 1 joule per second.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Power)

A lamp has 12 V across it and 2 A flowing through it. What is the power?

1. Write the formula: P = I x V
2. Substitute: P = 2 x 12
3. Calculate: P = 24 W
W
Write This Down — Energy Formula
E = V x I x t

E = energy transferred (joules, J)
V = potential difference (volts, V)
I = current (amperes, A)
t = time (seconds, s)

Since P = IV, this is the same as E = P x t (energy = power x time).

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Energy)

A 12 V heater draws 2 A for 60 seconds. How much energy is transferred?

1. Write the formula: E = V x I x t
2. Substitute: E = 12 x 2 x 60
3. Calculate: E = 1440 J
W
Write This Down — The Kilowatt-Hour

Electricity companies measure energy in kilowatt-hours (kWh), not joules.
1 kWh = the energy used by a 1 kW appliance running for 1 hour.
1 kWh = 3,600,000 J

T
Study Tip — Which Formula to Use?

If the question asks for power → use P = IV
If the question asks for energy → use E = IVt or E = Pt
If they give you power and time but not current → use E = Pt
Always check: is the time in seconds or hours? Convert if needed.

  • I can write P = IV from memory
  • I can write E = VIt from memory
  • I know what a kilowatt-hour is
  • I solved both worked examples correctly
Section 8.13 Mains Electricity and Safety
This section is less about calculations and more about knowing safety features. It comes up on almost every exam paper as a short-answer question.
W
Write This Down — A.C. vs D.C.

Direct current (d.c.) flows in one direction only (e.g. from a battery).
Alternating current (a.c.) flows backwards and forwards repeatedly (e.g. mains supply).
Mains frequency: 50 Hz (UK/Europe) or 60 Hz (US).

W
Write This Down — The Three Wires
Wire What It Does
Live (line) Carries the alternating current to the appliance. Goes between + and − voltage.
Neutral Completes the circuit. Kept at zero voltage.
Earth (ground) Safety wire. Connects metal case to ground. Carries current away if a fault occurs.
W
Write This Down — Safety Features

• Fuse: Thin wire in the live wire that melts and breaks the circuit if the current is too high. Protects against overheating/fire.
• Circuit breaker (trip switch): Does the same as a fuse but can be reset. Uses an electromagnet.
• Earth wire: If the live wire touches the metal case, current flows to earth, which blows the fuse.
• Double insulation: Plastic casing means no exposed metal, so no earth wire needed.
• RCD (residual current device): Detects difference between live and neutral currents; cuts off very quickly.

!
Don't Confuse — Where the Switch and Fuse Go

Both the switch and the fuse are in the live wire, NOT the neutral. This is so that when either operates, the appliance is disconnected from the dangerous live supply. If they were in the neutral, the appliance would still be connected to live voltage even when switched off.

?
Why This Matters — Fuse Size

The exam often asks you to choose the correct fuse (3 A or 13 A). Use P = IV to find the current, then pick the fuse that is slightly above the normal current. Example: a 500 W, 230 V hairdryer takes I = P/V = 500/230 = 2.2 A, so use a 3 A fuse (not 13 A, which would not blow in a fault).

W
Write This Down — Electrical Hazards

• Frayed or damaged wiring → overheating, fire
• Water near sockets/plugs → electrocution risk (water conducts)
• Overloaded sockets / long extension leads coiled up → overheating
• Accidentally cutting cables → electrocution risk, use RCD

  • I know the difference between a.c. and d.c.
  • I can name and describe the three wires (live, neutral, earth)
  • I can explain how a fuse and earth wire work together
  • I know the switch and fuse go in the live wire
  • I can calculate which fuse to use (P = IV, then pick the right size)
