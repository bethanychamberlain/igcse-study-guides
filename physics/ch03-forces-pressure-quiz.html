Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the moment of a force. State the formula and its unit.
2 marks
Answer
The moment of a force is its turning effect about a point [1]. It is calculated by: moment = force x perpendicular distance from the pivot. The unit is newton metre (N m) [1].
Must include "turning effect" and "perpendicular distance." Just saying "force x distance" without "perpendicular" can lose you the mark.

2
State Hooke's law.
2 marks
Answer
Below the elastic limit [1], the extension of a spring is directly proportional to the force applied [1].
"Below the elastic limit" is essential for full marks. Without it you only get 1 mark. "Proportional" means double the force = double the extension.

3
Write down the equation linking pressure, force, and area. State the unit of pressure.
2 marks
Answer
pressure = force / area, or p = F / A [1]. The unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), which is equivalent to N/m2 [1].
Both the formula and the unit are needed for full marks.

4
Give two features that make an object more stable.
2 marks
Answer
1. A wider base [1].
2. A lower centre of gravity [1].
Both features are needed. "Heavier" is NOT the answer — a heavier object is not necessarily more stable.

Intermediate — Can You Calculate It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to use formulas, show your working, and include units.
5
A force of 200 N acts on an area of 4 m2. (a) Calculate the pressure. (b) What would the pressure be if the same force acted on half the area?
4 marks
Answer
(a) p = F / A [1] = 200 / 4 = 50 Pa [1].
(b) Half the area = 2 m2. p = 200 / 2 = 100 Pa [1]. The pressure doubles when the area is halved [1].
Show the formula first, then substitute, then calculate with units. The pattern: halving the area doubles the pressure. This is a common exam question.

6
A uniform metre rule is pivoted at the 75 cm mark and balanced by a force of 2 N acting downward at the 95 cm mark. The rule has weight W acting at its centre. Calculate W.
4 marks
Answer
The centre of gravity of a uniform rule is at the 50 cm mark [1].
Moment of 2 N force about pivot: 2 x (95 - 75) = 2 x 20 = 40 N cm (clockwise) [1].
Moment of weight about pivot: W x (75 - 50) = W x 25 (anticlockwise) [1].
By the principle of moments: W x 25 = 40, so W = 1.6 N [1].
Key step: the weight of the rule acts at its midpoint (50 cm). The distances are measured from the pivot (75 cm), not from the ends. Always draw a quick diagram first.

7
Calculate the pressure at the bottom of a tank filled with water to a depth of 3 m. (Density of water = 1000 kg/m3, g = 10 N/kg)
3 marks
Answer
p = ρgh [1]
p = 1000 x 10 x 3 [1]
p = 30 000 Pa (or 30 kPa) [1].
Three steps = three marks. Don't skip the formula or the substitution line. If you wrote 30 kPa instead of 30 000 Pa, that's fine — both are accepted.

Advanced — Can You Explain and Apply It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, calculations, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
A balloon contains 6 m3 of helium at a pressure of 100 kPa at ground level. As it rises, the pressure falls to 50 kPa. Assuming the temperature does not change, calculate the new volume. State the law you used and explain why the volume changes using kinetic theory.
5 marks
Answer
Calculation:
Using Boyle's law: p1V1 = p2V2 [1].
100 x 6 = 50 x V2 [1].
V2 = 600 / 50 = 12 m3 [1].

Explanation: At lower pressure, the gas molecules collide with the walls of the balloon with the same force (temperature unchanged), but the external pressure pushing inwards is reduced [1]. The gas expands until the rate of collisions per unit area on the inside matches the reduced external pressure [1].
5-mark question = 3 for calculation + 2 for explanation. The kinetic theory part must mention particle collisions. Just saying "the gas expands" is not enough — say WHY.

9
A spring has an unstretched length of 12.0 cm. When a load of 4.5 N is hung from it, its length becomes 19.0 cm. (a) Calculate the extension. (b) Calculate the spring constant. (c) Predict the length with a 7.0 N load. (d) State one assumption you made in part (c).
5 marks
Answer
(a) Extension = 19.0 - 12.0 = 7.0 cm [1].
(b) F = kx, so k = F / x = 4.5 / 7.0 = 0.643 N/cm (or approximately 0.64 N/cm) [1].
(c) x = F / k = 7.0 / 0.643 = 10.9 cm. New length = 12.0 + 10.9 = 22.9 cm [1+1].
(d) The elastic limit has not been exceeded / the spring still obeys Hooke's law at this load [1].
In part (c), you must add the extension back to the original length. The assumption in (d) is always about the elastic limit — if the spring has gone past it, Hooke's law no longer applies.

10
A diving bell holds 6 m3 of air at the surface where pressure is 100 kPa. It is lowered to a depth where the pressure is 300 kPa. (a) Calculate the volume of air in the bell at this depth. (b) Explain why a tractor's big tyres stop it sinking too far into soft soil, using the concept of pressure. (c) A glass window has an area of 0.6 m2. If atmospheric pressure exerts a force of 60 000 N on it, calculate the pressure. Explain why the window does not break.
6 marks
Answer
(a) p1V1 = p2V2: 100 x 6 = 300 x V2, so V2 = 600 / 300 = 2 m3 [2].

(b) The big tyres have a large area of contact with the ground [1]. Since pressure = force / area, a larger area means a lower pressure on the soil, so the tractor does not sink as far [1].

(c) p = F / A = 60 000 / 0.6 = 100 000 Pa (100 kPa) [1]. The window does not break because the same pressure acts on both sides (inside and outside), so the forces are balanced [1].
Part (c) is the tricky one. Students calculate the pressure correctly but forget to explain WHY the window survives. The answer: the atmosphere pushes equally on both sides, so the net force is zero.
