Before You Start The Golden Rule for Every Calculation
This chapter introduces the units and measurements you will use in every other topic. Getting units right is how you avoid throwing away easy marks throughout the whole course.
T
Study Tip — The Three-Step Method (Use This EVERY Time)

Step 1: Write the formula.
Step 2: Substitute the numbers in (with units).
Step 3: Calculate and write the units in your answer.

Even if you can do it in your head, write all three steps. The examiner gives marks for each step separately. If you skip to the answer and get the number wrong, you get 0. If you show your working and make a small mistake, you still get 2 out of 3.

?
Why This Matters — Real Talk

Your exam results show you lose marks on calculation questions. This chapter is where you build the habit of always showing working and always including units. Every formula in every chapter works the same way — practise it here first.

  • I understand the three-step method: formula, substitute, calculate + units
Section 1.1 Numbers and Units
Every measurement in physics has two parts: a number and a unit. Together, they are called a physical quantity. You can treat units like algebraic symbols.
W
Write This Down — Physical Quantities

A physical quantity = a number + a unit. For example, 10 m means 10 times metres. You can treat the unit letter just like a symbol in algebra (10 m = 10 x m).

W
Write This Down — Combining Units

When you combine measurements, the units combine too. For example:
speed = distance / time = 10 m / 2 s = 5 m/s
The unit m/s (metres per second) is a combined unit.

W
Write This Down — Prefixes (Bigger and Smaller)

Prefixes make units bigger or smaller:

Prefix Symbol Meaning Power of 10
kilok1000103
centic1/10010-2
millim1/100010-3
microμ1/1 000 00010-6

So: 1 km = 1000 m,   1 cm = 0.01 m,   1 mm = 0.001 m,   1 mg = 0.001 g

W
Write This Down — Scientific Notation

Scientific notation (or standard form) writes numbers using powers of 10.
320 000 = 3.2 x 105  (move the decimal 5 places left)
0.005 = 5 x 10-3  (move the decimal 3 places right)
The number of significant figures tells you how accurate the measurement is. 3.2 x 105 has 2 significant figures.

!
Don't Confuse — "Milli" vs "Micro"

Milli (m) means thousandth (1/1000). Micro (μ) means millionth (1/1 000 000). Students often think milli means millionth — it does not. There are 1000 millimetres in a metre, and 1 000 000 micrometres in a metre.

T
Study Tip — Converting Units

To convert a bigger unit to a smaller unit, multiply (e.g. 2.5 km = 2.5 x 1000 = 2500 m).
To convert a smaller unit to a bigger unit, divide (e.g. 4500 g = 4500 / 1000 = 4.5 kg).
Write this rule on a sticky note and keep it visible until it becomes automatic.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Unit Conversion)

Convert 3500 mm to metres.

1. Know the conversion: 1 m = 1000 mm
2. Going from smaller to bigger, so divide: 3500 / 1000
3. Answer: 3500 mm = 3.5 m
  • I know what a physical quantity is (number + unit)
  • I can list the prefixes kilo, centi, milli, micro and their meanings
  • I can convert between units using multiply or divide
  • I can write a number in scientific notation
Section 1.2 A System of Units
Scientists worldwide use the same system so that everyone's measurements mean the same thing. This is the SI system.
W
Write This Down — The SI System

SI units (Système International d'Unités) are the standard units used by scientists worldwide. The three base SI units you need are:

Quantity SI Base Unit Symbol
Masskilogramkg
Lengthmetrem
Timeseconds

Other SI base units include the ampere (A) for electric current and the kelvin (K) for temperature.

W
Write This Down — Mass

Mass is the quantity of matter in an object, measured in kilograms (kg).
1 tonne (t) = 1000 kg  |  1 kg = 1000 g  |  1 g = 1000 mg

The SI base unit of mass is the kilogram, not the gram. This catches people out.

W
Write This Down — Time

The SI base unit of time is the second (s).
1 s = 1000 ms (milliseconds)  |  1 ms = 10-3 s
1 s = 1 000 000 μs (microseconds)  |  1 μs = 10-6 s

W
Write This Down — Length

The SI base unit of length is the metre (m).
1 km = 1000 m  |  1 m = 100 cm  |  1 cm = 10 mm
1 m = 1000 mm  |  1 μm = 10-6 m  |  1 nm = 10-9 m

!
Don't Confuse — The Base Unit of Mass

The SI base unit of mass is the kilogram, NOT the gram. This is the only base unit that already has a prefix (kilo). If an exam question asks "What is the SI unit of mass?" the answer is kg, not g.

?
Why This Matters

In every formula in physics, you must use SI units for the calculation to work. If a question gives you mass in grams, you must convert to kg first. If distance is in cm, convert to m first. Getting this wrong is the most common source of wrong answers in calculation questions.

  • I know the three base SI units: kg, m, s
  • I know that kg (not g) is the SI unit of mass
  • I can convert between g/kg, mm/cm/m/km, ms/s
Section 1.3 Measuring Length and Time
Knowing how to measure accurately and how to improve accuracy are key practical skills tested on the exam.
W
Write This Down — Measuring Length

Ruler: Measures lengths from a few mm up to about 1 m. Place the scale right next to the object being measured.
Calipers: Used when a ruler cannot be placed next to the object. Set the calipers to match the ends of the object, then move them to the ruler to take the reading.
Tape measure: For lengths of several metres.

W
Write This Down — Measuring Small Thicknesses

To find the thickness of something very thin (like a sheet of paper):
1. Measure the thickness of a large stack (e.g. 500 sheets)
2. Divide by the number of sheets
Example: 500 sheets = 49 mm, so 1 sheet = 49 / 500 = 0.098 mm

W
Write This Down — Measuring Time

Time intervals are measured using a stopwatch or stopclock.
• Analogue: a needle moves round a circular scale
• Digital: shows a number on a display

W
Write This Down — Pendulum Timing (Improving Accuracy)

The time for one complete swing of a pendulum is called its period.
To measure the period accurately: time many swings (e.g. 25) and divide by the number of swings.

1. Time 25 swings: 55 seconds
2. Divide: 55 / 25 = 2.2 seconds
3. Period of one swing = 2.2 s

This reduces the error caused by your reaction time when pressing start/stop.

W
Write This Down — Zero Error

A zero error is when a measuring instrument does not read exactly zero before you start. You must subtract the zero error from your reading to get the correct value.
Example: Scales show 0.1 kg with nothing on them. You weigh an object and get 46.2 kg. Corrected mass = 46.2 - 0.1 = 46.1 kg.

T
Study Tip — Exam Favourite

The pendulum timing question appears almost every year. The exam will give you "20 swings take 46 seconds" and ask for the period of one swing. Divide: 46 / 20 = 2.3 s. They may also ask "How could the student improve accuracy?" — the answer is always: time more swings and divide.

!
Don't Confuse — Period vs Frequency

Period = time for one complete swing (measured in seconds).
A short period means fast swings. Don't confuse the time for one swing with the time for all the swings — always divide.

  • I know when to use a ruler, calipers, or tape measure
  • I know the trick for measuring thin objects (measure many, divide)
  • I can calculate the period of a pendulum from multiple-swing data
  • I know what a zero error is and how to correct for it
Section 1.4 Volume and Density
Volume is the space an object takes up. Density tells you how much mass is packed into that space. This section has the most important formula in this chapter.
W
Write This Down — Volume Units

The SI unit of volume is the cubic metre (m3). But for everyday use:
• 1 m3 = 1000 litres (L)
• 1 litre = 1000 cm3 = 1000 ml
• 1 cm3 = 1 ml

W
Write This Down — Density Definition + Formula

Density is the mass per unit volume of a material — how much mass is packed into each cubic metre (or cubic centimetre).

ρ = m / V

ρ (Greek letter "rho") = density
m = mass
V = volume

Units: kg/m3 or g/cm3
Handy conversion: 1 g/cm3 = 1000 kg/m3

W
Write This Down — Rearranging the Density Formula

The density triangle helps you rearrange. Cover the quantity you want:

• To find density: ρ = m / V
• To find mass: m = ρ x V
• To find volume: V = m / ρ

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Finding Density)

A block has a mass of 240 g and a volume of 80 cm3. What is its density?

1. Write the formula: ρ = m / V
2. Substitute: ρ = 240 / 80
3. Calculate: ρ = 3 g/cm³
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Finding Mass)

Steel has a density of 7800 kg/m3. What is the mass of 2 m3 of steel?

1. Write the formula: m = ρ x V
2. Substitute: m = 7800 x 2
3. Calculate: m = 15 600 kg
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Finding Volume)

Aluminium has a density of 2700 kg/m3. What volume does 5400 kg of aluminium occupy?

1. Write the formula: V = m / ρ
2. Substitute: V = 5400 / 2700
3. Calculate: V = 2 m³

!
Don't Confuse — Units Must Match!

If mass is in grams, volume must be in cm3 → density in g/cm3.
If mass is in kg, volume must be in m3 → density in kg/m3.
Never mix them. If you put grams and m3 into the formula, your answer will be wrong by a factor of a million.

T
Study Tip — Practise All Three Rearrangements

Don't just practise finding density. The exam is equally likely to ask you to find mass or volume. Write out all three versions of the formula right now, then do one worked example of each. If you can do all three without looking at your notes, you are ready.

  • I can write ρ = m / V from memory
  • I can rearrange it to find m or V
  • I solved a practice problem for each rearrangement using three steps
  • I know the density of water is 1000 kg/m3 (or 1 g/cm3)
  • I know that 1 g/cm3 = 1000 kg/m3
Section 1.5 Measuring Volume and Density
This section is about the practical methods for measuring volume. The exam loves asking how you would measure the density of a regularly-shaped object, an irregular solid, or a liquid.
W
Write This Down — Measuring the Volume of a Liquid

Use a measuring cylinder. Pour the liquid in and read the scale at the bottom of the meniscus (the curved surface). Measuring cylinders are usually marked in cm3 or ml.

W
Write This Down — Volume of a Regular Solid

If the object has a simple shape, calculate its volume using a formula:
• Rectangular block: V = length x width x height
• Cylinder: V = π x radius2 x height

W
Write This Down — Volume of an Irregular Solid

If the shape is too awkward to calculate, use water displacement:
1. Fill a measuring cylinder partway with water. Record the level.
2. Lower the solid into the water. Record the new level.
3. Volume of solid = new level - original level.

For objects too big for a measuring cylinder, use a displacement can: fill the can to the spout, lower the object in, and collect the overflowing water in a measuring cylinder.

W
Write This Down — Measuring the Density of a Liquid

Step-by-step method:

1. Weigh the empty measuring cylinder: mass = 240 g
2. Pour in liquid and read volume: V = 400 cm³
3. Weigh the cylinder with liquid: mass = 560 g
4. Mass of liquid = 560 - 240 = 320 g
5. Density = 320 / 400 = 0.8 g/cm³
?
Why This Matters

The practical exam (Paper 6) and the theory paper both ask about these methods. You need to describe the steps clearly. The liquid density experiment in particular requires you to remember to subtract the mass of the container — students often forget this and lose marks.

T
Study Tip — Write the Method as a List

Practise writing out "How would you find the density of an irregular solid?" as a numbered list without looking at your notes. You need: (1) measure mass on a balance, (2) measure volume by displacement, (3) calculate density = mass / volume. Three clear steps gets full marks.

  • I know how to measure the volume of a liquid (measuring cylinder)
  • I know how to find the volume of a regular solid (formula) and irregular solid (displacement)
  • I can describe how to measure the density of a liquid, including subtracting the container mass
  • I know when to use a displacement can
Section 1.6 More About Mass and Density
This section covers how to compare masses using a beam balance, and how density determines whether things float or sink.
W
Write This Down — Beam Balance

A beam balance compares the mass of an unknown object with known standard masses. Place the object on one pan and add standard masses to the other until it balances. The balance really compares weights (gravitational forces), but because gravity is the same on both sides, the masses must be equal.

!
Don't Confuse — Mass vs Weight

Mass is the amount of matter in an object, measured in kg. It stays the same everywhere.
Weight is the gravitational force on an object, measured in newtons (N). It changes if gravity changes (e.g. on the Moon).
When you "weigh" something on a balance, you are actually finding its mass.

W
Write This Down — Floating and Sinking Rule

An object will float in a liquid if it is less dense than the liquid.
An object will sink if it is more dense than the liquid.

Examples:
• Wood (750 kg/m3) floats on water (1000 kg/m3) because wood is less dense.
• Steel (7800 kg/m3) sinks in water because steel is more dense.
• Ice (920 kg/m3) floats on water because ice is less dense.
• Oil floats on water because oil is less dense than water.

?
Why This Matters

Floating and sinking questions are a common way the exam tests whether you truly understand density. The question will give you a table of densities and ask which materials float in water (or in another liquid). Remember: compare the density of the object with the density of the liquid. Less dense = floats. More dense = sinks.

T
Study Tip — Quick Mental Check

Water has a density of 1.0 g/cm3 (or 1000 kg/m3). Anything with a density below 1.0 g/cm3 floats on water. Anything above 1.0 g/cm3 sinks. This one number is worth memorising.

  • I know how a beam balance works
  • I know the difference between mass (kg) and weight (N)
  • I can predict whether an object floats or sinks by comparing densities
  • I know the density of water (1 g/cm3 or 1000 kg/m3)
