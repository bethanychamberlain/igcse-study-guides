Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Light year The distance light travels in one year (9.5 × 1012 km). A unit of distance. It is NOT a unit of time
Galaxy A huge system of billions of stars held together by gravity. Ours is the Milky Way. Solar System (that is just one star and its planets)
Nebula A cloud of gas and dust in space where stars are born. —
Red giant A star that has expanded and cooled after running low on hydrogen fuel. Larger but cooler surface. Red supergiant (much more massive stars — end in supernovae)
Supernova A massive explosion when a large star collapses and rebounds. Spreads heavy elements into space. —
White dwarf The hot, dense remnant core of a medium-mass star (like the Sun) after it sheds its outer layers. Neutron star (that is the remnant of a much bigger star, after a supernova)
Black hole An object so dense that nothing, not even light, can escape its gravity. Formed from the most massive stars. —
Red shift Light from a galaxy moving away from us is stretched towards the red end of the spectrum. Greater red shift = moving away faster. Blue shift (galaxy moving towards us — rarely tested)
CMBR Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation — faint microwave radiation from every direction, the afterglow of the Big Bang. —
Comet An icy body in a highly elliptical orbit around the Sun. Develops a visible tail when near the Sun. Meteor (burns up in atmosphere) or meteorite (reaches the ground)

Star Life Cycles — Two Paths

Sun-Sized Star (Medium Mass):

Nebula → Protostar (gravity pulls gas together)
Protostar → Main sequence (fusion begins: H → He)
Main sequence → Red giant (swells up, cools)
Red giant → White dwarf (outer layers drift off; hot dense core remains)

Massive Star:

Nebula → Protostar (same start as above)
Protostar → Main sequence (burns hotter and faster)
Main sequence → Red supergiant (swells even larger)
Red supergiant → Supernova (huge explosion)
Supernova → Neutron star or Black hole (depends on remaining mass)

Say It in One Sentence

Day and night The Earth rotates on its axis once every 24 hours — the side facing the Sun gets day, the other gets night.
Seasons The Earth's axis is tilted 23.5°, so each hemisphere takes turns being tilted towards the Sun as Earth orbits.
Moon phases The Moon reflects sunlight; we see different amounts of the lit half as it orbits Earth every 29.5 days.
Gravity and orbits The Sun's gravitational pull provides the centripetal force that keeps planets moving in curved paths.
Red shift Light from distant galaxies is shifted to longer (redder) wavelengths because they are moving away — the Universe is expanding.
Big Bang All matter started from a single hot dense point and has been expanding ever since — evidence: red shift + CMBR.
Orbital speed Speed = 2πr / T — further from the Sun means slower speed and longer orbital period.

Memory Tricks

Seasons Are About Tilt, NOT Distance
If seasons were caused by distance, both hemispheres would have summer at the same time. They don't — when it is summer in the Northern Hemisphere, it is winter in the Southern. The cause is the 23.5° tilt of Earth's axis.
Two Reasons Summer Is Warmer
1. Sun's rays hit the surface more directly (less spread out). 2. More hours of daylight. Both of these are caused by the axial tilt, not by distance.
Scale of the Universe (Smallest to Largest)
Moon → Earth → Sun → Solar System → Milky Way Galaxy → Universe. "The Sun is at the centre of the Solar System, which is part of a galaxy called the Milky Way, which is part of the Universe."
Big Bang Evidence = Red Shift + CMBR
You need BOTH for full marks. (1) Red shift: galaxies moving apart → Universe is expanding → must have started from one point. (2) CMBR: faint microwave radiation from every direction → the afterglow of the Big Bang.
Comets Speed Up Near the Sun
Comets in elliptical orbits go fastest when closest to the Sun (gravitational pull is strongest, GPE converts to KE) and slowest when furthest away.

Key Formulas (with Units)

Formula What It Tells You Units
orbital speed = 2πr / T Speed of an object in orbit m/s, m, s
H0 = v / d Hubble constant (recession speed / distance) km/s per Mpc
Age of Universe ≈ 1 / H0 Approximate age of the Universe (~13.8 billion years) seconds (or years)

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Explain why we have day and night" The Earth rotates on its axis every 24 hours; half faces the Sun (day), half faces away (night)
"Explain why we have seasons" The Earth's axis is tilted at 23.5°; the tilted hemisphere gets more direct sunlight and longer days = summer
"Are seasons caused by distance from the Sun?" NO. If they were, both hemispheres would have summer at the same time. Seasons are caused by the tilt.
"What is a light year?" The distance light travels in one year (9.5 × 1012 km). It is a unit of distance, not time.
"Describe the life cycle of a Sun-like star" Nebula → protostar → main sequence → red giant → white dwarf
"Describe the life cycle of a massive star" Nebula → protostar → main sequence → red supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole
"What is red shift?" Light from galaxies moving away is stretched to longer wavelengths (towards red). Greater distance = greater red shift = faster recession.
"State two pieces of evidence for the Big Bang" (1) Red shift shows galaxies are moving apart. (2) CMBR is the remnant radiation from the Big Bang.
"Why do communications satellites use geostationary orbits?" The satellite stays above the same point on Earth, so dish aerials can point at it without moving
