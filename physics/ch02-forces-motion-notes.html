Sections 2.1 – 2.1 Speed, Velocity, and Acceleration
These are the three most fundamental quantities in motion. You must know how to define each one, how they differ, and how to calculate them.
W
Write This Down — Speed

Speed is the distance an object moves per unit of time.

v = s / t

v = speed (metres per second, m/s)
s = distance (metres, m)
t = time (seconds, s)

Average speed is calculated over the whole journey. Actual speed at any moment may be different.

W
Write This Down — Velocity vs Speed

Velocity is speed in a particular direction. A cyclist travelling at 10 m/s due east has a velocity of +10 m/s. If they turn around and travel at the same speed going west, their velocity is -10 m/s.

Velocity is a vector (has magnitude AND direction). Speed is a scalar (magnitude only).

W
Write This Down — Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. It tells you how quickly something is speeding up or slowing down.

a = Δv / Δt

a = acceleration (m/s2)
Δv = change in velocity (m/s) = final velocity - initial velocity
Δt = time taken (s)

A negative acceleration means slowing down. This is called deceleration or retardation.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A car passes post A at 12 m/s. It has a steady acceleration of 3 m/s2. What is its velocity 5 s later?

1. Write the relationship: final velocity = original velocity + (acceleration x time)
2. Substitute: v = 12 + (3 x 5)
3. Calculate: v = 12 + 15 = 27 m/s

Now try: A truck at 25 m/s brakes with a retardation of 2 m/s2 for 4 s. What does its velocity drop to? (Hint: use -2 for the acceleration.) You should get 17 m/s.

!
Don't Confuse — Speed vs Velocity

Speed = how fast (just a number, e.g. 10 m/s).
Velocity = how fast AND which direction (e.g. 10 m/s east).
Two cars travelling at the same speed in opposite directions have the same speed but different velocities.

T
Study Tip — Units Tell You the Formula

m/s means "metres per second" = distance / time. m/s2 means "metres per second per second" = change in velocity / time. If you forget the formula, read the units.

  • I can write v = s / t from memory and know the units
  • I know velocity = speed + direction
  • I can write a = Δv / Δt and explain what each symbol means
  • I solved the braking truck problem (answer: 17 m/s)
Sections 2.2 & 2.5 Motion Graphs
Graphs are one of the most common exam topics. You must be able to read, interpret, and calculate from both distance-time and speed-time graphs.
W
Write This Down — Distance-Time Graphs

On a distance-time graph:

• The gradient (slope) = speed
• A straight line = steady (constant) speed
• A steeper line = faster speed
• A horizontal line (flat) = stationary (not moving)
• A curve getting steeper = accelerating

W
Write This Down — Speed-Time Graphs (THE BIG ONE)

On a speed-time graph:

• The gradient (slope) = acceleration
• The area under the line = distance travelled
• A straight line going up = steady (uniform) acceleration
• A horizontal line = constant speed (zero acceleration)
• A straight line going down = steady deceleration
• A curve = non-uniform (changing) acceleration

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Area Under a Graph)

A car accelerates uniformly from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 s. What distance does it travel?

1. The speed-time graph is a triangle (starts at 0, rises to 20).
2. Area of triangle: 1/2 x base x height
3. Substitute: 1/2 x 5 x 20 = 50 m
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Gradient = Acceleration)

A speed-time graph shows a car going from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 10 seconds. What is the acceleration?

1. Acceleration = gradient = rise / run
2. Rise = 30 - 10 = 20 m/s. Run = 10 s.
3. Acceleration: 20 / 10 = 2 m/s²
!
Don't Confuse — The Two Types of Graph

Students constantly mix these up:
• Distance-time: gradient = speed, flat line = stopped
• Speed-time: gradient = acceleration, flat line = constant speed (still moving!)
A flat line on a speed-time graph does NOT mean stopped. It means the speed is constant.

T
Study Tip — Area Under the Line

For the area under a speed-time graph, break it into rectangles and triangles. Rectangle area = base x height. Triangle area = 1/2 x base x height. Use the scale numbers on the axes, not the physical length on paper.

  • I know what the gradient means on each type of graph
  • I know the area under a speed-time graph = distance travelled
  • I can calculate acceleration from a speed-time graph gradient
  • I won't confuse a flat line on distance-time vs speed-time
Sections 2.3 – 2.4 & 2.6 Free Fall, Forces in Balance, and Terminal Velocity
When objects fall, they accelerate due to gravity. But air resistance complicates things. This section covers free fall, balanced forces, Newton's first law, and terminal velocity.
W
Write This Down — Free Fall

Free fall is when an object falls with only gravity acting on it (no air resistance). All objects in free fall near Earth's surface have the same acceleration:

g = 10 m/s2

This means the object's speed increases by 10 m/s every second. A ball dropped from rest has a speed of 10 m/s after 1 s, 20 m/s after 2 s, 30 m/s after 3 s, and so on.

The symbol g has two meanings: it is both the acceleration of free fall (10 m/s2) and the gravitational field strength (10 N/kg).

W
Write This Down — Newton's First Law

Newton's first law of motion: If no external force is acting on it, an object will:
• if stationary, remain stationary
• if moving, keep moving at a steady speed in a straight line

In other words: objects don't need a force to keep moving. They need a force to change their motion (speed up, slow down, or change direction).

W
Write This Down — Balanced Forces

When forces on an object are balanced (equal and opposite), the resultant force is zero. The object behaves as if there is no force at all — it is either at rest OR moving at a steady velocity. This follows directly from Newton's first law.

W
Write This Down — Terminal Velocity

When a skydiver falls:

1. At first, weight > air resistance, so she accelerates.
2. As speed increases, air resistance increases.
3. Eventually, air resistance = weight. Forces are balanced.
4. She falls at a constant speed called terminal velocity (~60 m/s).

When the parachute opens, air resistance suddenly increases, becomes greater than weight, so she decelerates. A new, much lower terminal velocity is reached.

?
Why This Matters

Terminal velocity is a favourite exam question. They will show you a speed-time graph of a falling object and ask you to explain each section. The key is explaining why the acceleration changes by comparing the sizes of weight and air resistance at each stage.

!
Don't Confuse — Balanced Forces Does NOT Mean Stopped

If forces are balanced, the object could be stationary OR moving at a constant velocity. A skydiver at terminal velocity has balanced forces but is definitely moving! Balanced forces = no change in motion.

T
Study Tip — Drawing Force Diagrams

When answering terminal velocity questions, always draw arrows for weight (down) and air resistance (up). Label them clearly. If accelerating: make the weight arrow longer. If at terminal velocity: make them the same length. The diagram gets you marks.

  • I know g = 10 m/s2 and what it means for a falling object
  • I can state Newton's first law from memory
  • I know balanced forces means no change in motion (not necessarily stopped)
  • I can explain each stage of terminal velocity in order
  • I know what happens when a parachute opens (air resistance increases, deceleration, new lower terminal velocity)
Sections 2.7 – 2.8 Force, Mass, Acceleration, and Friction
This is Newton's second law: the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration. It is the most important equation in this chapter, and probably in all of IGCSE Physics.
W
Write This Down — Resultant Force

The resultant force is the overall force on an object when all forces are combined. If a car has a 1500 N driving force and a 500 N friction force opposing it, the resultant force is 1500 - 500 = 1000 N forwards.

If forces are balanced, the resultant force is zero and there is no acceleration.

W
Write This Down — Newton's Second Law (F = ma)
F = m x a

F = resultant force (newtons, N)
m = mass (kilograms, kg)
a = acceleration (m/s2)

The definition of a newton: 1 newton is the force required to give a mass of 1 kg an acceleration of 1 m/s2.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A model car has a mass of 2 kg. A force of 18 N pushes it to the right, and friction of 10 N opposes it. What is its acceleration?

1. Find resultant force: F = 18 - 10 = 8 N
2. Write the formula: F = m x a
3. Substitute: 8 = 2 x a
4. Rearrange: a = 8 / 2 = 4 m/s²

Now try: What force is needed to accelerate a 900 kg car at 1.2 m/s2? (Answer: F = 900 x 1.2 = 1080 N)

W
Write This Down — Inertia

Inertia is an object's resistance to a change in velocity. The more mass something has, the more inertia it has, and the harder it is to accelerate. This is why F = ma works — a bigger mass needs a bigger force for the same acceleration.

W
Write This Down — Friction

Friction is the force that opposes the sliding of one surface over another. Key facts:

• Friction converts kinetic energy into thermal energy (heat)
• Air resistance (drag) is a form of friction
• Static friction (not yet moving) is greater than dynamic friction (already sliding)
• Friction can be useful (brakes, shoe grip) or a nuisance (wastes energy in machines)
• Reduced by: lubricants, ball bearings, streamlined shapes

!
Don't Confuse — F is the RESULTANT Force

In F = ma, F is the resultant force, not just any single force. If a question gives you a driving force and a friction force, you must subtract friction first to get the resultant, then use F = ma. This is the number one mistake students make.

T
Study Tip — Rearranging F = ma

You need to be able to rearrange this formula three ways:
F = m x a (find force)
m = F / a (find mass)
a = F / m (find acceleration)
The triangle trick: put F on top, m and a on the bottom. Cover the one you want to find.

  • I can write F = ma from memory and know what each letter means
  • I know F is the RESULTANT force, not just any single force
  • I can rearrange F = ma to find m or a
  • I solved the practice problem (F = 1080 N)
  • I know friction converts kinetic energy to heat
Sections 2.9 – 2.10 Weight, Gravity, and Action-Reaction
Weight is not the same as mass. This section covers the formula linking them, and introduces Newton's third law — the one about action and reaction.
W
Write This Down — Weight Formula
W = m x g

W = weight (newtons, N)
m = mass (kilograms, kg)
g = gravitational field strength (N/kg) — on Earth, g = 10 N/kg

W
Write This Down — Mass vs Weight
Mass Weight
Amount of matter in an object The gravitational force on an object
Measured in kilograms (kg) Measured in newtons (N)
Stays the same everywhere Changes depending on gravity
50 kg on Earth = 50 kg on Moon 500 N on Earth, only 80 N on Moon
W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Rocket Acceleration)

A rocket has a mass of 200 kg and a thrust (force from engine) of 3000 N. What is its acceleration at launch? (g = 10 N/kg)

1. Calculate weight: W = m x g = 200 x 10 = 2000 N
2. Resultant force: F = 3000 - 2000 = 1000 N upwards
3. Use F = ma: 1000 = 200 x a
4. Rearrange: a = 1000 / 200 = 5 m/s²
W
Write This Down — Newton's Third Law

Newton's third law: If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal but opposite force on object A.

Another way to say it: To every action there is an equal but opposite reaction.

Key points:
• The forces act on different objects (that's why they don't cancel out)
• They are always equal in size and opposite in direction
• Examples: runner pushes ground backwards, ground pushes runner forwards. Earth pulls you down, you pull Earth up.

!
Don't Confuse — Newton's Third Law Pairs

Action-reaction pairs act on different objects. A book on a table: the book pushes down on the table, and the table pushes up on the book — these are equal and opposite forces on different objects. They don't cancel because they act on different things.

?
Why This Matters — Rockets and Jets

Rockets work because of Newton's third law. The rocket pushes hot gas backwards (action), and the gas pushes the rocket forwards (reaction). A rocket CAN accelerate in space because it pushes against its own exhaust gas — it doesn't need air to push against.

  • I can write W = mg from memory and know the units
  • I know mass stays the same everywhere but weight changes
  • I can state Newton's third law and give an example
  • I know action-reaction pairs act on DIFFERENT objects
Sections 2.11 – 2.12 Momentum and Its Conservation
Momentum links mass and velocity. It is conserved in collisions, which makes it incredibly useful for solving problems where objects crash into each other.
W
Write This Down — Momentum Formula
p = m x v

p = momentum (kg m/s)
m = mass (kg)
v = velocity (m/s)

Momentum is a vector. Direction matters. Use + for one direction and - for the opposite.

W
Write This Down — Force and Momentum
F = Δp / Δt

Resultant force = change in momentum / time taken

This is actually another way of stating Newton's second law. It can also be rearranged as:
F x t = Δp (force x time = change in momentum). The quantity "force x time" is called impulse.

W
Write This Down — Conservation of Momentum

Law of conservation of momentum: When two or more objects act on each other, their total momentum remains constant, provided no external forces are acting.

In other words: total momentum before = total momentum after

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Collision)

Trolley A (1 kg) moves right at 2 m/s. Trolley B (4 kg) moves left at 3 m/s. They collide and stick together. What is their velocity after the collision?

1. Momentum of A: 1 x (+2) = +2 kg m/s
2. Momentum of B: 4 x (-3) = -12 kg m/s
3. Total before: +2 + (-12) = -10 kg m/s
4. Total after (same): -10 kg m/s
5. Combined mass: 1 + 4 = 5 kg
6. Velocity: -10 / 5 = -2 m/s (2 m/s to the left)
!
Don't Confuse — Direction Matters!

In momentum problems, you MUST assign directions. Choose one direction as positive (e.g. right = +). Anything moving the other way gets a negative velocity. If you ignore direction, you will get the wrong answer every time.

T
Study Tip — The Setup

For every momentum problem, always write these four lines:
1. Momentum of object A before = ...
2. Momentum of object B before = ...
3. Total momentum before = ...
4. Total momentum after = same number (conservation!)
Then solve for the unknown velocity.

  • I can write p = mv from memory
  • I know the unit of momentum is kg m/s
  • I can state the law of conservation of momentum
  • I can solve a collision problem using total momentum before = total momentum after
  • I remember to assign + and - directions in momentum problems
Sections 2.13 – 2.14 Vectors, Scalars, and Circular Motion
The final section covers the difference between vectors and scalars, adding forces at angles, and why moving in a circle requires a force even at constant speed.
W
Write This Down — Vectors vs Scalars
Vectors (magnitude + direction) Scalars (magnitude only)
Velocity Speed
Force Mass
Acceleration Distance
Momentum Energy
Displacement Time
W
Write This Down — Adding Vectors in a Straight Line

If forces act in the same direction, add them: 30 N + 40 N = 70 N.
If forces act in opposite directions, subtract: 40 N - 30 N = 10 N (in the direction of the bigger force).

W
Write This Down — Circular Motion

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is still accelerating because its direction is constantly changing (and velocity = speed + direction).

The force that keeps an object moving in a circle is called the centripetal force. It always points towards the centre of the circle.

More centripetal force is needed if:
• the mass is greater
• the speed is greater
• the radius is smaller (tighter circle)

W
Write This Down — Examples of Centripetal Force

• A ball on a string: tension in the string provides the centripetal force
• A car going round a bend: friction between tyres and road
• A planet orbiting the Sun: gravity provides the centripetal force
• An electron orbiting a nucleus: electrostatic force

If the centripetal force disappears (e.g. string breaks), the object flies off in a straight line tangent to the circle, as predicted by Newton's first law.

!
Don't Confuse — "Centrifugal Force"

There is no such thing as centrifugal force on the moving object. When you whirl a ball and the string breaks, the ball moves in a straight line — it is NOT flung outwards. The feeling of being pushed outwards is just your body wanting to continue in a straight line (Newton's first law).

?
Why This Matters

Circular motion questions often appear on Paper 3. The key idea the examiners test: even at constant speed, an object in a circle is accelerating because direction changes. You must explain that acceleration and centripetal force both point towards the centre.

  • I can give 3 examples each of vectors and scalars
  • I know centripetal force points towards the centre of the circle
  • I can name what provides the centripetal force in at least 3 examples
  • I know that constant speed in a circle still means acceleration (direction changes)
