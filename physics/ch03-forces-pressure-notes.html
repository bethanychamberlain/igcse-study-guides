Section 3.1 Forces and Turning Effects
A force can make an object turn around a fixed point (called a pivot). The turning effect depends on both the force and the distance from the pivot.
W
Write This Down — Definition

The moment of a force is its turning effect about a point. It depends on the size of the force and the perpendicular distance from the pivot.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
moment = force x perpendicular distance

Moment is measured in newton metres (N m)
Force is measured in newtons (N)
Distance is measured in metres (m)

Moments can be clockwise or anticlockwise. Always state the direction.

W
Write This Down — The Principle of Moments

The principle of moments: If an object is in equilibrium (balanced), the sum of the clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of the anticlockwise moments about that point.

W
Write This Down — Conditions for Equilibrium

For an object to be in equilibrium, two conditions must be met:
1. The sum of forces in one direction = the sum of forces in the opposite direction.
2. The principle of moments must apply (clockwise moments = anticlockwise moments).

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A person of weight 500 N stands on a plank supported by two trestles A and B, 5 m apart. The person is 2 m from A. Find the upward forces X (at A) and Y (at B).

1. Take moments about A to eliminate X:
2. Clockwise moment: 500 x 2 = 1000 N m
3. Anticlockwise moment: Y x 5
4. Set equal: Y x 5 = 1000, so Y = 200 N
5. Forces balance: X + Y = 500, so X = 300 N
T
Study Tip — Choosing Where to Take Moments

When you take moments about a point, any force acting at that point has a distance of zero, so its moment disappears. This trick lets you eliminate one unknown and solve for the other. Always pick the pivot point that gets rid of the unknown you don't want yet.

!
Don't Confuse — Distance Must Be Perpendicular

The distance in the moment formula must be the perpendicular (at right angles) distance from the force to the pivot. If the force is not at right angles, you need the perpendicular component. In most exam questions the forces are already at right angles, but read carefully.

  • I can define "moment" and write the formula from memory
  • I can state the principle of moments
  • I can state both conditions for equilibrium
  • I solved a moments problem by taking moments about a point
Section 3.2 Centre of Gravity
Every object behaves as if all its weight acts through a single point. Understanding this point explains why things balance or topple.
W
Write This Down — Definition

The centre of gravity (or centre of mass) is the point where the whole weight of an object appears to act. For a uniform object, it is at the geometric centre.

W
Write This Down — Finding It Experimentally

For an irregular flat shape: suspend it from a pin, hang a plumb line, and draw the line. Repeat from a different point. The centre of gravity is where the two lines cross.

W
Write This Down — Stability

An object is stable if, when tilted slightly, it returns to its original position. An object topples when its centre of gravity passes beyond the edge of its base.

To make an object more stable: lower the centre of gravity and/or widen the base.

W
Write This Down — Three Types of Equilibrium
Type What Happens When Tilted Example
Stable Returns to original position Cone on its base
Unstable Topples further away Cone on its tip
Neutral Stays in new position Ball on flat surface
?
Why This Matters

Stability questions come up regularly on exams. The key phrase is: "the centre of gravity must stay over the base." Wider base + lower centre of gravity = more stable. Use these exact words in your answers.

  • I can define centre of gravity
  • I can describe how to find it for an irregular shape
  • I know the three types of equilibrium
  • I know how to make an object more stable (wider base, lower CoG)
Section 3.3 More About Moments
This section applies the principle of moments to harder problems, including heavy (non-negligible) bars and cranes.
W
Write This Down — Heavy Bar Rule

When a bar has weight, treat its weight as a single downward force acting at its centre of gravity. For a uniform bar, this is at the midpoint.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Crane Problem)

A crane's counterbalance weighs 400 N and is 1 m from the pivot. A 100 N load hangs 2 m from the pivot on the other side. Where should the counterbalance be placed?

1. Clockwise moment (load): 100 x 2 = 200 N m
2. Anticlockwise moment (counterbalance): 400 x d
3. Set equal: 400 x d = 200
4. Solve: d = 200 / 400 = 0.5 m from the pivot
T
Study Tip — Verify Your Answer

After solving a moments problem, check by adding up all the moments. Clockwise should equal anticlockwise. If they don't match, you made an error somewhere. This takes 10 seconds and can save you marks.

  • I can handle problems where the bar has weight
  • I solved the crane worked example
Section 3.4 Stretching and Compressing
Forces can stretch or compress objects. This section is about how springs behave under load — and the important limits to that behaviour.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

• Elastic: A material that returns to its original shape when the force is removed.
• Plastic: A material that keeps its new shape when the force is removed (permanently deformed).
• Extension: The difference between stretched length and original (unstretched) length.

W
Write This Down — Hooke's Law

Hooke's law: Below the elastic limit, the extension of a spring is proportional to the load (force) applied.

This means: double the force = double the extension. The graph of extension vs load is a straight line through the origin (up to the limit of proportionality).

W
Write This Down — Spring Constant Formula
F = k x x

F = force/load (newtons, N)
k = spring constant (N/m or N/mm)
x = extension (m or mm)

The spring constant k tells you how stiff the spring is. A higher k means harder to stretch.

W
Write This Down — Key Points on the Graph

• Limit of proportionality: The point beyond which extension is no longer proportional to load (the graph stops being straight).
• Elastic limit: The point beyond which the spring will not return to its original length — it is permanently deformed.

!
Don't Confuse — Extension vs Length

Extension = current length - original length. If the spring starts at 40 mm and stretches to 58 mm, the extension is 18 mm, NOT 58 mm. Always subtract the original length first.

T
Study Tip — Graph Questions

Exam questions often give you a table of load vs length. You need to: (1) calculate extensions by subtracting the unstretched length, (2) plot extension against load, (3) identify where the line stops being straight (limit of proportionality). Practise this with the data in the textbook.

  • I can state Hooke's law from memory
  • I can write F = kx and know what each letter means
  • I know the difference between elastic limit and limit of proportionality
  • I know to calculate extension by subtracting the original length
Section 3.5 Pressure
Pressure measures how concentrated a force is over an area. A small area with the same force means higher pressure — this is why drawing pins have sharp points and tractors have wide tyres.
W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
p = F / A

p = pressure (pascals, Pa)
F = force (newtons, N)
A = area (square metres, m2)

1 Pa = 1 N/m2. Since this is very small, we often use kilopascals (1 kPa = 1000 Pa).

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A concrete block has a mass of 2600 kg and measures 0.5 m x 1.0 m x 2.0 m. What is the maximum pressure it can exert? (g = 10 N/kg)

1. Weight: W = 2600 x 10 = 26 000 N
2. Maximum pressure = smallest area: A = 1.0 x 0.5 = 0.5 m²
3. Pressure: p = 26 000 / 0.5 = 52 000 Pa = 52 kPa
!
Don't Confuse — Maximum vs Minimum Pressure

Maximum pressure = force on the smallest area (the block standing on its smallest face).
Minimum pressure = force on the largest area (the block lying flat).
The force (weight) is the same — it's the area that changes.

?
Why This Matters

Pressure explains real-life design choices. Football boots have studs (small area = high pressure = grip). Skis are wide (large area = low pressure = don't sink). The exam loves asking you to explain examples like these using p = F/A.

  • I can write p = F / A from memory
  • I know the unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa)
  • I can explain why smaller area = higher pressure
Section 3.6 Pressure in Liquids
Liquid pressure has different rules from solid pressure. It depends on depth and density, and it acts in all directions.
W
Write This Down — Four Key Properties

1. Pressure in a liquid acts in all directions.
2. Pressure increases with depth (deeper = more liquid above = more weight = more pressure).
3. Pressure depends on the density of the liquid (denser liquid = higher pressure at the same depth).
4. Pressure does NOT depend on the shape or width of the container.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
p = ρgh

p = pressure (Pa)
ρ (rho) = density of liquid (kg/m3)
g = gravitational field strength (10 N/kg)
h = depth (m)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

What is the pressure at the bottom of a 2 m deep swimming pool? (density of water = 1000 kg/m3)

1. Write the formula: p = ρgh
2. Substitute: p = 1000 x 10 x 2
3. Calculate: p = 20 000 Pa = 20 kPa
!
Don't Confuse — p = F/A vs p = ρgh

Use p = F/A for solid objects pressing on surfaces.
Use p = ρgh for the pressure inside a liquid at a given depth.
The question will make it clear which one you need. If it mentions depth and density, use ρgh.

  • I can state the four properties of liquid pressure
  • I can write p = ρgh from memory
  • I solved the swimming pool worked example
Section 3.7 Pressure from the Air
The atmosphere is a deep ocean of air that exerts pressure on everything. Atmospheric pressure explains how straws, vacuum cleaners, and barometers work.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts

• At sea level, atmospheric pressure is about 100 kPa (100 000 Pa) or 1 atmosphere (1 atm).
• Atmospheric pressure acts in all directions.
• Atmospheric pressure decreases as you go higher (less air above you).
• A barometer measures atmospheric pressure. A mercury barometer supports a column of mercury about 760 mm high.

W
Write This Down — How a Straw Works

When you "suck" a straw, you reduce the air pressure inside the straw. The atmospheric pressure on the surface of the drink is now greater than the pressure inside the straw, so it pushes the liquid up. You are not pulling the liquid — the atmosphere is pushing it.

?
Why This Matters

The straw explanation is a classic exam question. The key is: you don't "suck" liquid up — the atmosphere pushes it up. Same principle for vacuum cleaners: the fan reduces air pressure inside, and the atmosphere pushes air (plus dust) in.

  • I know atmospheric pressure at sea level is about 100 kPa
  • I can explain how a straw works using pressure differences
Section 3.8 Gas Pressure and Volume
When you squash a gas into a smaller space, the pressure goes up. This section covers the mathematical relationship between pressure and volume.
W
Write This Down — Boyle's Law

Boyle's law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume.

This means: if you halve the volume, the pressure doubles. If you double the volume, the pressure halves.

W
Write This Down — Formula
p1 x V1 = p2 x V2

p1 = initial pressure, V1 = initial volume
p2 = final pressure, V2 = final volume
Units must be consistent (both pressures in the same unit, both volumes in the same unit).

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

An air bubble has a volume of 2 cm3 at a depth of 20 m (where total pressure is 3 atm). What is its volume at the surface (1 atm)?

1. Write the formula: p&sub1; x V&sub1; = p&sub2; x V&sub2;
2. Substitute: 3 x 2 = 1 x V&sub2;
3. Calculate: V&sub2; = 6 cm³

The bubble triples in volume because the pressure drops to a third.

W
Write This Down — Kinetic Theory Explanation

Gas particles constantly collide with the container walls, creating pressure. If the volume is halved, there are twice as many particles per unit volume, so there are twice as many collisions per second with each part of the wall. Therefore the pressure doubles.

!
Don't Confuse — "Constant Temperature" Is Essential

Boyle's law only works at constant temperature. If the temperature also changes, the relationship is more complicated. When the exam says "assume no change in temperature," this is your cue to use p1V1 = p2V2.

T
Study Tip — Quick Sense Check

After solving, check: did the pressure go up? Then the volume should have gone down (and vice versa). If both went up or both went down, you made an error. Inverse proportion means one goes up as the other goes down.

  • I can state Boyle's law from memory
  • I can write p1V1 = p2V2 from memory
  • I can explain Boyle's law using the kinetic theory
  • I solved the air bubble worked example
