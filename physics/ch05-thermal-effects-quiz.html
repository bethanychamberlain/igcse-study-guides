Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State two differences between evaporation and boiling.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of:
1. Evaporation occurs at any temperature; boiling occurs only at the boiling point [1].
2. Evaporation occurs at the surface only; boiling occurs throughout the liquid [1].
3. Evaporation is slow and gradual; boiling is rapid with bubbles forming.
This question appears on almost every exam paper. Know at least two differences by heart.

2
Name the three methods of heat transfer. For each one, state whether it needs a medium (material) to travel through.
3 marks
Answer
1. Conduction — needs a medium (works best in solids) [1].
2. Convection — needs a medium (fluids: liquids and gases only) [1].
3. Radiation — does NOT need a medium (can travel through a vacuum) [1].
The key point examiners look for: radiation is the only one that works through a vacuum. If you just name the three without saying which needs a medium, you lose marks.

3
What is absolute zero? Give its value in both °C and K.
2 marks
Answer
Absolute zero is the lowest possible temperature, where particles have the minimum possible energy and cannot move any slower [1]. It is -273 °C or 0 K [1].
You need both the definition AND the values for full marks. Don't write "particles stop moving" — they still have minimum energy at absolute zero.

4
Which surface is the best absorber of thermal radiation: white, matt black, or silver? Which is the best reflector?
2 marks
Answer
Best absorber: matt black [1].
Best reflector: silver / shiny [1].
Remember: good absorbers are also good emitters (matt black is best at both). Good reflectors are poor absorbers (silver/shiny reflects most radiation away).

Intermediate — Can You Explain or Calculate It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to use formulas and/or explain using particle theory.
5
How much thermal energy is needed to raise the temperature of 0.5 kg of water from 20 °C to 100 °C? (Specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J/(kg °C))
3 marks
Answer
E = mcΔθ [1]
E = 0.5 x 4200 x (100 - 20) = 0.5 x 4200 x 80 [1]
E = 168 000 J (or 168 kJ) [1].
Three steps, three marks. Don't forget to calculate Δθ first: 100 - 20 = 80 °C. If you used 100 instead of 80, you lose 2 marks.

6
Explain, using particle theory, why a metal door handle feels colder than a wooden door, even though they are both at room temperature.
3 marks
Answer
Both the metal and wood are at the same temperature (room temperature) [1]. Metal is a good thermal conductor, so it transfers thermal energy away from your hand quickly [1]. Wood is a poor conductor (insulator), so it transfers heat away from your hand slowly. Your hand loses heat faster when touching metal, which makes it feel colder [1].
The trap: students say "metal is colder." It's NOT colder — it's the same temperature. It just conducts your body heat away faster. You must make this clear.

7
Explain why the freezer compartment of a refrigerator is placed at the top.
3 marks
Answer
The freezer cools the air near the top of the fridge [1]. Cold air is denser than warm air, so it sinks downwards through the fridge [1]. This sets up a convection current that circulates cold air around all the food, cooling the entire fridge [1].
The chain: freezer at top → cold air sinks (denser) → convection current cools everything. Each step is a mark. Just saying "cold air sinks" without mentioning density or convection loses marks.

Advanced — Can You Apply and Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, calculations, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
A heating curve shows a substance being heated from solid to gas. The temperature stays constant at 80 °C and again at 250 °C. (a) What is the melting point? (b) What is the boiling point? (c) Explain, in terms of particles, why the temperature stays constant at 80 °C even though heating continues.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Melting point = 80 °C [1].
(b) Boiling point = 250 °C [1].
(c) At 80 °C the substance is changing state (melting) [1]. The energy being supplied is used to overcome/break the bonds (forces of attraction) between particles [1], rather than to increase the kinetic energy of the particles, so the temperature does not rise [1].
The three-part explanation is: (1) it's changing state, (2) energy goes into breaking bonds, (3) NOT into kinetic energy so temperature stays flat. This exact reasoning comes up on almost every exam paper.

9
A 100 W electric heater is used to melt ice at 0 °C. After 300 seconds, 0.10 kg of ice has melted. (a) Calculate the energy supplied by the heater. (b) Using E = mL, calculate the specific latent heat of fusion of ice. (c) The accepted value is 330 000 J/kg. Explain why the experimental value may differ.
5 marks
Answer
(a) E = power x time = 100 x 300 = 30 000 J [1].
(b) E = mL, so L = E / m = 30 000 / 0.10 = 300 000 J/kg [2].
(c) The experimental value is lower than the accepted value because some thermal energy is lost to the surroundings (absorbed by the container, the air, etc.) [1], so not all the energy from the heater goes into melting the ice [1].
Part (c) is about experimental error. The key phrase: "thermal energy lost to surroundings." This makes the calculated L value lower than the true value because you assume all the heater's energy went into melting ice, but some was wasted.

10
A solar panel on a roof has a matt black surface and an area of 2 m2. (a) Explain why the panel is black. (b) Why is there an insulating layer behind the panel? (c) The panel receives 1000 J of energy per square metre per second from the Sun. Calculate the power input in kW. (d) The panel has an efficiency of 60%. It must deliver the same energy as a 3 kW electric heater. Calculate the area of panel needed.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Matt black surfaces are the best absorbers of thermal radiation [1], so the panel absorbs as much energy from the Sun as possible.

(b) The insulating layer reduces thermal energy lost through the back of the panel by conduction [1], so more energy is transferred to the water.

(c) Power = energy per second per m2 x area = 1000 x 2 = 2000 W = 2 kW [1].

(d) Useful power per m2 = 1000 x 0.60 = 600 W/m2 [1].
Area needed = 3000 / 600 = 5 m2 [2].
This is a classic multi-step question. Part (d) requires you to first calculate the useful output per m2 (accounting for 60% efficiency), then divide the required power (3 kW = 3000 W) by that value. Show each step clearly.
