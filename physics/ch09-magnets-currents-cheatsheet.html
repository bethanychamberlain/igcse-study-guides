Magnets and Currents

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Magnetic field The region around a magnet where a magnetic force acts. Field lines run from N to S. Electric field (that is around charges, not magnets)
Ferromagnetic Materials that can be magnetised: iron, steel, nickel, cobalt. Non-magnetic metals like copper, aluminium, brass
Soft iron Easy to magnetise, loses magnetism quickly. Used for electromagnet cores. Steel (hard magnetic — keeps magnetism → permanent magnets)
Solenoid A long coil of wire. When current flows, it creates a field pattern like a bar magnet. —
Motor effect A force on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field. Use LEFT-hand rule. Generator effect (that is motion causing current — use RIGHT hand)
Commutator Split ring in a d.c. motor that reverses the current direction every half-turn to keep the coil spinning. Slip rings (complete rings used in a.c. generators — they do NOT reverse current)
Electromagnetic induction A conductor cutting magnetic field lines induces an e.m.f. (voltage). Use RIGHT-hand rule. Motor effect (that is current causing force, not motion causing current)
Lenz's law The induced current always flows in a direction that opposes the change that caused it. Faraday's law (that tells you the SIZE of the induced e.m.f., not the direction)
Transformer Two coils on a shared iron core. Changes a.c. voltage up or down. Only works with a.c. —
Step-up / Step-down Step-up: more turns on secondary = higher output voltage. Step-down: fewer turns = lower voltage. If voltage goes up, current goes down (and vice versa) — power stays the same

Motor vs Generator — Side by Side

D.C. Motor A.C. Generator
Input Electricity (current) Motion (spinning the coil)
Output Motion (rotation) Electricity (a.c.)
Contacts Commutator (split ring) Slip rings (complete rings)
Hand rule LEFT-hand rule RIGHT-hand rule
How to increase More current, stronger magnet, more turns Spin faster, stronger magnet, more turns, larger coil area
Exam Trap — Which Hand?
Left hand = eLectric motor (current causes force).
Right hand = generatoR (motion causes current).
The exam WILL test whether you know which hand to use.

Step-Up vs Step-Down Transformer

Step-Up Step-Down
Turns More on secondary than primary Fewer on secondary than primary
Voltage Output voltage is HIGHER Output voltage is LOWER
Current Output current is LOWER Output current is HIGHER
Used at Power stations (for long-distance transmission) Substations (to reduce voltage for homes)

Key Formulas (with Units)

Formula What It Tells You Units
Vs / Vp = Ns / Np Transformer equation (voltage ratio = turns ratio) V, turns
Vp × Ip = Vs × Is Transformer power (100% efficient) V, A
Ploss = I2R Power wasted as heat in cables W, A, Ω

Say It in One Sentence

Magnetic field Field lines run from N to S outside the magnet; the closer the lines, the stronger the field.
Electromagnet A solenoid with a soft iron core — switch the current on and off to turn the magnet on and off.
Motor effect A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field feels a force — this is how every electric motor works.
Induction Move a wire through a magnetic field (cutting field lines) and a voltage is induced — no movement, no voltage.
Lenz's law The induced current opposes whatever caused it — push a N pole in and the coil pushes back with its own N pole.
Transformer Two coils on an iron core; a.c. in the primary creates a changing field that induces a voltage in the secondary.
High-voltage transmission Step-up transformers increase voltage (reducing current), which massively reduces I2R heat losses in the cables.

Memory Tricks

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule — TFC
Thumb = Thrust (force/motion). First finger = Field (N to S). seCond finger = Current (conventional, + to −). Hold your LEFT hand with all three at right angles.
Right-Hand Grip Rule for a Coil
Curl your right-hand fingers in the direction of conventional current around the coil. Your thumb points to the N pole.
Why Soft Iron (Not Steel)?
An electromagnet must be switchable. Soft iron loses magnetism when current stops. Steel keeps magnetism — bad for an electromagnet, good for a permanent magnet.
High-Voltage Transmission in 4 Steps
1. Step-up transformer increases voltage. 2. P = IV, so higher V means lower I (same power). 3. Ploss = I2R, so lower I = much less heat wasted. 4. Step-down transformers bring voltage back down for homes. Practise writing this as a paragraph — it is worth 4–6 marks.
No Movement = No Current
If a magnet is held still inside a coil, nothing happens. The wire must be CUTTING field lines. The exam loves asking this.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How can the strength of an electromagnet be increased?" Increase the current or increase the number of turns (or add a soft iron core)
"What is the purpose of the commutator in a motor?" It reverses the current direction every half-turn so the coil keeps spinning the same way
"State Lenz's law" The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change that produced it
"Why do transformers only work on a.c.?" Only a.c. creates a changing magnetic field in the core. D.c. would create a constant field, which does not induce a voltage.
"A transformer has 200 primary turns, 50 secondary turns, and 240 V input. Output voltage?" Vs = Vp × Ns/Np = 240 × 50/200 = 60 V
"Why is electricity transmitted at high voltage?" High V means low I (P = IV). Low I means less power lost as heat (Ploss = I2R).
"The magnet is stationary inside the coil. What happens?" Nothing — no movement means no field lines cut, so no induced e.m.f.
"When is the generator's output at maximum?" When the coil is horizontal (cutting field lines at the fastest rate)
