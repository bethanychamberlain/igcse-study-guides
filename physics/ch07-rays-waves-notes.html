Section 7.1 Light Rays and Waves
Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation. It travels as transverse waves, but we often draw straight lines called rays to show which direction it is going.
W
Write This Down — Key Properties of Light

• Light travels in straight lines
• Light travels as transverse waves
• Light can travel through a vacuum (it does not need a medium)
• Speed of light in a vacuum: 300 000 km/s (3 × 108 m/s) — nothing can travel faster
• Luminous objects emit their own light (Sun, lamps, lasers). Non-luminous objects are visible because they reflect light

W
Write This Down — What Happens When Light Hits a Surface

• Diffuse reflection — rough surfaces scatter light in all directions (e.g. paper)
• Regular reflection — smooth, shiny surfaces reflect light at equal angles (e.g. mirrors)
• Absorption — dark surfaces absorb most of the light (e.g. black paper)
• Transmission — transparent materials let light pass through (e.g. glass, water)

W
Write This Down — Wavelength and Colour

Different wavelengths of light are seen as different colours. The visible spectrum ranges from about 0.0004 mm (violet) to 0.0007 mm (red). White light is a mixture of all colours. Lasers emit monochromatic light (a single wavelength and colour).

  • I know light travels in straight lines as transverse waves at 3 × 108 m/s
  • I can explain the difference between luminous and non-luminous objects
  • I know four things that can happen when light hits a surface
Section 7.2–7.3 Reflection in Plane Mirrors
When light hits a mirror, it bounces off in a predictable way. Understanding the laws of reflection lets you predict where images will form.
W
Write This Down — Laws of Reflection

Law 1: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.
Law 2: The incident ray, reflected ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.

• The normal is a line drawn at right angles (90°) to the mirror surface at the point where the light hits
• The angle of incidence is measured between the incident ray and the normal
• The angle of reflection is measured between the reflected ray and the normal

W
Write This Down — Image in a Plane Mirror

When a plane (flat) mirror forms an image:
• The image is the same size as the object
• The image is the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front
• The image is laterally inverted (left and right are swapped)
• The image is virtual — it cannot be formed on a screen because no rays actually pass through the image position

!
Don't Confuse — Angles Are Measured From the Normal

Students often measure angles from the mirror surface instead of from the normal. The angle of incidence and the angle of reflection are ALWAYS measured from the normal (the line at 90° to the surface), never from the surface itself.

!
Don't Confuse — Real vs Virtual Images

Real image: formed where rays actually converge (meet). Can be picked up on a screen. Example: cinema projector.
Virtual image: formed where rays only appear to come from. Cannot be formed on a screen. Example: image in a plane mirror.

  • I can state the two laws of reflection
  • I know angles are measured from the normal, not the surface
  • I can describe the properties of an image in a plane mirror (same size, same distance, laterally inverted, virtual)
Section 7.4 Refraction of Light
When light passes from one medium into another (e.g. from air into glass), it changes speed. If it enters at an angle, it also changes direction. This bending is called refraction.
W
Write This Down — How Refraction Works

• Light slows down when it enters a denser medium (e.g. air to glass)
• Light speeds up when it enters a less dense medium (e.g. glass to air)
• When light slows down, it bends towards the normal
• When light speeds up, it bends away from the normal
• If light enters at 90° (along the normal), it passes straight through without bending

W
Write This Down — Refractive Index
refractive index = speed of light in vacuum / speed of light in medium

For glass: n = 300 000 / 200 000 = 1.5
For water: n = 1.33
The higher the refractive index, the more the light bends.

W
Write This Down — Dispersion

When white light passes through a prism, it splits into a spectrum of colours (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet). This is called dispersion. It happens because the prism refracts each colour by a slightly different amount. Violet is deviated most; red is deviated least.

T
Study Tip — Drawing Refraction Diagrams

Always draw the normal first (dashed line, at 90° to the surface). Then draw the incident ray. Ask: is the light going into a denser medium? If yes, bend it towards the normal. If leaving, bend it away. Label the angle of incidence and the angle of refraction.

  • I know light bends towards the normal when entering a denser medium
  • I can define refractive index and calculate it
  • I can explain dispersion and name the spectrum colours in order
Section 7.5 Total Internal Reflection
When light travels from a denser medium to a less dense one (e.g. glass to air), something remarkable can happen: at large enough angles, ALL the light is reflected back inside. The surface acts like a perfect mirror.
W
Write This Down — Critical Angle and TIR

Critical angle: The angle of incidence at which the refracted ray travels along the surface (angle of refraction = 90°).

Total internal reflection (TIR): When the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle, there is no refracted ray — all the light is reflected back inside the material.

Two conditions for TIR: (1) light must be travelling from a denser medium to a less dense one, (2) the angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle.

W
Write This Down — Critical Angle Formula
sin c = 1 / n

c = critical angle
n = refractive index of the material
Example: glass has n = 1.5, so sin c = 1/1.5 = 0.67, c = 42°

W
Write This Down — Uses of TIR

• Optical fibres: thin, flexible glass rods that carry light (or infrared) over long distances. Light bounces along inside the fibre by TIR. Used in telecommunications and endoscopes
• Reflecting prisms: used in periscopes, binoculars, and rear reflectors on bikes and cars (angle of incidence of 45° is greater than glass critical angle of 42°)

?
Why This Matters

Optical fibres are how your internet connection, phone calls, and streaming services work. Data is sent as pulses of infrared light along thin glass fibres. They are better than copper cables because: they carry more data, have less signal loss (attenuation), are not affected by electrical interference, and are thinner and lighter.

  • I can define the critical angle and total internal reflection
  • I know the two conditions needed for TIR
  • I can use sin c = 1/n to calculate the critical angle
  • I can name two practical uses of TIR
Section 7.6 Snell's Law and Refraction Calculations
When light refracts, the angles of incidence and refraction are linked by a precise mathematical relationship discovered by the Dutch scientist Willebrord Snell in 1620.
W
Write This Down — Snell's Law
n = sin i / sin r

n = refractive index of the medium
i = angle of incidence (in the less dense medium, e.g. air)
r = angle of refraction (in the denser medium, e.g. glass)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

Light enters water (n = 1.33) at an angle of incidence of 45°. Find the angle of refraction.

1. Write Snell's law: n = sin i / sin r
2. Rearrange: sin r = sin i / n
3. Substitute: sin r = sin 45° / 1.33 = 0.707 / 1.33 = 0.532
4. Find angle: r = sin¹(0.532) = 32°
T
Study Tip — Calculator Check

Make sure your calculator is in degrees mode, not radians. On most scientific calculators, look for "DEG" on the screen. If you get weird answers for sin calculations, check this first.

  • I can write Snell's law from memory: n = sin i / sin r
  • I can rearrange to find any of the three variables
  • I solved the worked example and got r = 32°
Section 7.7–7.9 Lenses and Their Uses
Lenses bend light by refraction and can form images. There are two main types, and you need to know how each one works.
W
Write This Down — Two Types of Lens

Convex (converging) lens: thickest in the middle, thinnest at the edges. Bends parallel rays inwards to meet at the principal focus (F). The distance from the centre of the lens to F is the focal length.

Concave (diverging) lens: thinnest in the middle, thickest at the edges. Bends parallel rays outwards so they appear to come from a point behind the lens (the principal focus).

W
Write This Down — Ray Diagrams (Convex Lens)

To find where a convex lens forms an image, draw any two of these three standard rays:

• Ray 1: A ray through the centre of the lens passes straight through (no bending)
• Ray 2: A ray parallel to the principal axis passes through F after the lens
• Ray 3: A ray through F' (on the object side) leaves the lens parallel to the principal axis

Where two rays meet on the other side = the image position.

W
Write This Down — Images Formed by Convex Lens
Object Position Image Use
Very far away At F; real, inverted, diminished Camera
Beyond 2F Between F and 2F; real, inverted, diminished Camera, eye
Between F and 2F Beyond 2F; real, inverted, enlarged Projector
Closer than F Same side; virtual, upright, enlarged Magnifying glass
W
Write This Down — Correcting Vision

Short sight (cannot see distant objects clearly): corrected with a concave (diverging) lens.
Long sight (cannot see close objects clearly): corrected with a convex (converging) lens.

T
Study Tip — Remember the Correction

Short sight = concave lens (think: short and concave both have a "v" shape dipping inwards). Long sight = convex lens. Or simply: short sight needs to spread rays out (diverge), long sight needs to bring rays together (converge).

  • I can describe the difference between convex and concave lenses
  • I can draw ray diagrams using the three standard rays
  • I know what image is formed when the object is at different distances
  • I know which lens corrects short sight and which corrects long sight
Section 7.10–7.11 The Electromagnetic Spectrum
Light is just one type of electromagnetic (EM) wave. There is a whole family of EM waves, from radio waves to gamma rays, that share common properties but differ in wavelength and frequency.
W
Write This Down — Properties Common to All EM Waves

• They are all transverse waves
• They all travel at the speed of light (3 × 108 m/s) in a vacuum
• They can all travel through a vacuum
• They all transfer energy
• They obey the wave equation: v = fλ

W
Write This Down — The Spectrum (Learn This Order!)
Type (long λ to short λ) Key Uses Key Hazards
Radio waves TV, radio, communication —
Microwaves Mobile phones, Wi-Fi, cooking, satellites Internal heating of body tissue
Infrared Remote controls, thermal imaging, optical fibres Skin burns
Visible light Vision, photography, optical fibres —
Ultraviolet Fluorescence, sterilisation, security marking Skin cancer, eye damage
X-rays Medical imaging, airport security Cell damage, cancer
Gamma rays Cancer treatment, sterilising equipment Cell damage, cancer, mutations
T
Study Tip — Remember the Order

Running Man In Vegas Uses X-tra Gear. (Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible, Ultraviolet, X-rays, Gamma). Longest wavelength / lowest frequency first, shortest wavelength / highest frequency last.

!
Don't Confuse — Wavelength vs Frequency

As you go from radio waves to gamma rays: wavelength decreases and frequency increases. They go in opposite directions. This is because v = fλ and the speed is constant for all EM waves, so if frequency goes up, wavelength must go down.

  • I can list all 7 types of EM wave in order of wavelength
  • I know at least one use and one hazard for each type
  • I know that all EM waves travel at the same speed in a vacuum
  • I know that UV, X-rays, and gamma rays are ionising (can damage cells)
Section 7.12 Sending Signals
Modern communications use electromagnetic waves to send information over long distances. Signals can be analogue or digital.
W
Write This Down — Analogue vs Digital

Analogue signal: varies continuously — the signal can take any value and changes smoothly. Example: the varying voltage from a microphone.

Digital signal: has only two values (on/off, 1/0). The analogue signal is sampled many times per second and converted into binary code (a series of 0s and 1s).

W
Write This Down — Advantages of Digital Signals

• Digital signals can be cleaned up and regenerated by regenerators, removing noise
• Analogue signals amplify the noise along with the signal, so quality degrades over distance
• Digital signals can carry more information in the same bandwidth

W
Write This Down — Optical Fibres in Communications

Optical fibres carry digital signals as pulses of infrared light (or laser light). Advantages over copper cables:
• Carry more signals
• Less attenuation (signal loss) over long distances
• Not affected by electrical interference
• Thinner and lighter
• Cannot be "tapped" (more secure)

  • I can define analogue and digital signals
  • I can state two advantages of digital over analogue
  • I can explain why optical fibres are better than copper cables
