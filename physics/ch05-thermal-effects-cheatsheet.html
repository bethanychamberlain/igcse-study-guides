Thermal Effects

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Temperature Related to the average kinetic energy of particles. Higher temp = faster particles on average. Heat (heat is the transfer of thermal energy; temperature is how hot)
Internal energy The total kinetic energy + potential energy of ALL particles in a material. Temperature (a cup and a bathtub of boiling water have the same temp but different internal energy)
Conduction Thermal energy transferred through a material by particle vibrations (no material moves). Best in solids. Convection (that involves the material moving)
Convection Thermal energy transferred by the movement of heated fluid. Creates circulating convection currents. Conduction (no movement of material in conduction)
Radiation Thermal energy transferred by infrared electromagnetic waves. Does NOT need a medium — works through a vacuum. Conduction/convection (both need a medium; radiation doesn't)
Specific heat capacity (c) Energy needed to raise 1 kg of a material by 1 °C. Unit: J/(kg °C). Water = 4200. Latent heat (that's for changing state, not changing temperature)
Latent heat (L) Energy needed to change the state of 1 kg of a substance at constant temperature. Unit: J/kg. Specific heat capacity (that's for changing temperature, not state)
Evaporation Liquid turns to gas at the surface at any temperature below boiling point. No bubbles. Boiling (fixed temp, whole liquid, bubbles form inside)
Absolute zero The lowest possible temperature: -273 °C = 0 K. Particles have minimum energy. —

Key Formulas — Learn These Cold

What Formula Units
Specific heat capacity E = m × c × Δθ J, kg, J/(kg °C), °C
Latent heat E = m × L J, kg, J/kg
Celsius to Kelvin K = °C + 273 K, °C
Exam Trap — Which Formula?
Changing temperature (no state change)? Use E = mcΔθ. Changing state (melting/boiling/freezing/condensing, no temperature change)? Use E = mL. If a question asks for ice at -10 °C to steam at 100 °C, you need both formulas for different stages.
Exam Trap — Temperature CHANGE, Not Temperature
Δθ is the change. If water heats from 20 °C to 70 °C, Δθ = 50 °C, not 70 °C. Always subtract.

Memory Trick
Good emitters are also good absorbers. Solar panels = black (absorb). Hot-country houses = white (reflect). Emergency blankets = shiny (reflect body heat back to you).

Say It in One Sentence

Temperature How fast the particles are moving on average — higher temperature = faster average motion.
Heat Energy flowing from a hot object to a cold one until they reach the same temperature.
Conduction Energy passed particle-to-particle by vibration — metals are best because of free electrons.
Convection Hot fluid rises (less dense), cold fluid sinks (denser), creating a circulating loop.
Radiation Infrared waves that travel through empty space — how the Sun's heat reaches Earth.
Thermal expansion Heating makes particles vibrate more and take up more space — bridges have gaps for this reason.
Evaporation cools The fastest particles escape from the surface, leaving slower (cooler) ones behind.
Latent heat Energy used to break bonds between particles during a state change — temperature stays flat.

Memory Tricks

Convection Chain
Heating → expands → less dense → rises → cooler fluid sinks to replace it → convection current. Each step in this chain is a mark in the exam.
"Metal Feels Cold" Trap
A metal door handle and a wooden door are the SAME temperature (room temp). The metal just conducts heat away from your hand faster, so it feels colder. It isn't.
Flat Line = Breaking Bonds
On a heating curve, when the line goes flat, picture the energy going into snapping chains between particles, NOT speeding them up. Temperature only rises again when the state change is complete.
SHC vs Latent Heat — The Quick Test
Is the temperature changing? Use E = mcΔθ. Is the substance melting or boiling (temp stays the same)? Use E = mL. One formula for each job.
Speeds Up Evaporation — "THAW"
Temperature higher, Humidity lower, Area (surface) larger, Wind blowing across the surface. Four factors, four marks.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"2 kg of water cools from 70 °C to 20 °C. Energy lost? (c = 4200)" Step 1: E = mcΔθ. Step 2: Δθ = 50 °C. Step 3: E = 2 × 4200 × 50 = 420 000 J
"Energy to melt 2 kg of ice? (L = 330 000 J/kg)" Step 1: E = mL. Step 2: E = 2 × 330 000. Step 3: E = 660 000 J
"Why does temperature stay constant during melting?" Energy is used to break bonds between particles, not to increase kinetic energy, so temperature doesn't rise.
"Explain how a room is heated by convection" Air near radiator is heated → expands → becomes less dense → rises → cooler air sinks and replaces it → convection current heats the room.
"Why are metals good conductors?" They have free electrons that move through the metal, collide with atoms, and transfer energy quickly.
"Why does evaporation cause cooling?" The fastest particles (highest energy) escape from the surface. The remaining particles have lower average KE, so the temperature drops.
"Convert 25 °C to kelvin" K = °C + 273 = 25 + 273 = 298 K
"How does radiation reach us from the Sun?" As infrared electromagnetic waves — radiation does not need a medium, so it can travel through the vacuum of space.
