Section 6.1 Transverse and Longitudinal Waves
When you drop a stone into a pond, ripples spread across the surface carrying energy. But no water actually flows across the pond — the wave effect is just the result of up-and-down motions. Waves transfer energy without transferring matter.
W
Write This Down — Two Types of Wave

Transverse waves: The oscillations (vibrations) are at right angles to the direction the wave travels. Examples: light, all electromagnetic waves, water ripples.

Longitudinal waves: The oscillations are in the same direction (back and forth) as the wave travels. The wave is made of compressions (squashed regions) and rarefactions (stretched regions). Example: sound waves.

W
Write This Down — Key Wave Terms

• Speed — how fast the wave travels (m/s)
• Frequency (f) — the number of waves passing a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz)
• Wavelength (λ) — the distance from one point on a wave to the equivalent point on the next wave (m)
• Amplitude — the maximum distance a point moves from its rest position
• Period — the time for one complete oscillation (s). Period = 1 / frequency

W
Write This Down — The Wave Equation
v = f × λ

v = speed (m/s)
f = frequency (Hz)
λ = wavelength (m) — the Greek letter lambda

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A wave has a frequency of 4 Hz and a wavelength of 2 m. What is its speed?

1. Write the formula: v = f × λ
2. Substitute: v = 4 × 2
3. Calculate: v = 8 m/s

Now try: f = 50 Hz, λ = 0.5 m. You should get v = 25 m/s.

!
Don't Confuse — Transverse vs Longitudinal

Students often say sound is transverse because they see a wavy line on an oscilloscope. That wavy line is a graph of air pressure over time — it is NOT a picture of the wave itself. Sound waves are always longitudinal: the air vibrates back and forth in the same direction the wave travels.

T
Study Tip — Memory Trick

Transverse = Think of a T-shape: the oscillations go across (perpendicular) to the wave direction. Longitudinal = oscillations go aLong the same direction.

  • I can define transverse and longitudinal waves and give an example of each
  • I can define speed, frequency, wavelength, amplitude, and period
  • I can write v = fλ from memory and solve problems with it
Section 6.2 Wave Effects
Waves can be reflected, refracted, and diffracted. These effects can be demonstrated using a ripple tank — a shallow tank of water where tiny waves (ripples) are sent across the surface.
W
Write This Down — Three Wave Effects

Reflection: Waves bounce off a surface at the same angle they hit it. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.

Refraction: Waves change direction when they change speed. In a ripple tank, waves slow down in shallow water, and the wavelength decreases — but the frequency stays the same.

Diffraction: Waves spread out when they pass through a gap or around an obstacle. The most diffraction happens when the gap size is about the same as the wavelength. Wider gaps produce less spreading.

?
Why This Matters

These three effects are evidence that sound and light travel as waves. Sound diffracts around buildings (you can hear around corners). Light refracts when entering glass. Sound reflects off walls to create echoes. Knowing which effect is which — and being able to name it from a description — is a guaranteed exam question.

!
Don't Confuse — Refraction vs Diffraction

Refraction = wave changes speed and bends (e.g. going from deep to shallow water).
Diffraction = wave spreads out through a gap or around an edge (speed doesn't change).
Both involve waves changing direction, but the cause is different.

  • I can define reflection, refraction, and diffraction
  • I know that maximum diffraction happens when gap size equals wavelength
  • I can explain refraction using the wave equation: speed changes, frequency stays the same, so wavelength changes
Section 6.3 Sound Waves
Sound is produced when objects vibrate. The vibrations create compressions and rarefactions that travel through the air.
W
Write This Down — Key Facts About Sound

• Sound waves are longitudinal waves
• Sound is produced by vibrations (e.g. vibrating guitar string, loudspeaker cone, vocal cords)
• Sound needs a medium (material) to travel through — it cannot travel through a vacuum
• Sound can travel through solids, liquids, and gases
• Sound can be reflected (echoes), refracted, and diffracted

W
Write This Down — The Vacuum Experiment

An electric bell is placed inside a glass jar. As air is pumped out, the sound gets quieter and eventually cannot be heard, even though the bell is still vibrating. This proves sound needs a medium — without air particles, there is nothing to pass the vibrations on.

W
Write This Down — Displaying Sound

Sound waves can be displayed using a microphone connected to an oscilloscope (CRO). The trace on the screen is a waveform showing how air pressure changes with time. Remember: the wavy trace is a graph, not a picture of the wave — sound waves are still longitudinal.

W
Write This Down — Reducing Unwanted Sounds

• Hard surfaces reflect sound and can cause echoes (e.g. in large halls).
• Soft materials (curtains, carpets, padded furniture) absorb sound energy and reduce echoes.
• Panels backed with foam or fibrewool can be used between rooms to reduce sound transmission.
• Sound waves can also be diffracted — you can hear someone through an open window because sound spreads through gaps.

T
Study Tip

If an exam question says "Explain why sound cannot travel through space," your answer needs three things: (1) space is a vacuum / has no particles, (2) sound needs a medium, (3) without particles there is nothing to vibrate / pass on the compressions and rarefactions.

  • I know that sound is longitudinal and caused by vibrations
  • I can explain why sound cannot travel through a vacuum
  • I know the waveform on an oscilloscope is a pressure-time graph, not a picture of the wave
  • I can explain how to reduce unwanted sounds
Section 6.4 Speed of Sound and Echoes
Sound is much slower than light — about 330 m/s in air. That's why you see lightning before you hear thunder.
W
Write This Down — Speed of Sound

• Speed of sound in air ≈ 330 m/s (at 0 °C) to 350 m/s (at 30 °C)
• Sound travels faster in solids than liquids, and faster in liquids than gases
• Example speeds: air 330 m/s, water 1400 m/s, concrete 5000 m/s
• Sound travels faster through hot air than cold air
• The speed of sound does not depend on air pressure

W
Write This Down — Measuring the Speed of Sound

A sound is made (e.g. hitting a block with a hammer). Two microphones (A and B) are placed a measured distance apart. A control unit starts a digital timer when microphone A receives the sound and stops it when microphone B receives it. Then: speed = distance / time.

W
Write This Down — Echoes

An echo is a reflected sound heard a short time after the original sound. The sound has to travel to the wall and back again, so:

speed = 2 × distance to wall / echo time

Rearranged: distance to wall = (speed × echo time) / 2

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A person claps near a cliff and hears the echo 1.6 seconds later. Speed of sound = 330 m/s. How far is the cliff?

1. Total distance = speed × time: 330 × 1.6 = 528 m
2. Sound goes there AND back, so divide by 2: 528 / 2 = 264 m
3. Distance to cliff: 264 m
!
Don't Confuse — Divide by 2!

The most common mistake in echo questions is forgetting that the sound travels there and back. Always divide the total distance by 2 to get the distance to the reflecting surface. If you get a ridiculously large answer, check whether you forgot to halve it.

?
Why This Matters — Devices Using the Echo Principle

• Echo-sounders on ships send ultrasound pulses downwards and measure how long they take to bounce back. Depth = (speed in water × time) / 2.
• Radar uses the same principle but with microwaves instead of sound. It detects the positions of aircraft or ships by measuring the “echo times” of microwave pulses reflected from them.
• Parking sensors set off warning bleeps when a car gets too close to an obstacle. Some use sound pulses, others use microwaves.
• Bats navigate in the dark using echo-location — they send out ultrasound pulses and listen for the reflections.

  • I know the speed of sound in air is about 330 m/s
  • I know sound travels fastest in solids, then liquids, then gases
  • I can solve echo problems, remembering to divide by 2
Section 6.5 Characteristics of Sound Waves
You can tell the difference between a loud sound and a quiet one, or a high-pitched note and a low one. These differences show up on an oscilloscope trace.
W
Write This Down — Pitch and Frequency

• Pitch is how high or low a sound is
• High frequency = high pitch. Low frequency = low pitch
• On an oscilloscope: higher frequency shows more waves squeezed closer together (shorter wavelength on the screen)
• Human hearing range: about 20 Hz (very low) to 20 000 Hz (very high)

W
Write This Down — Loudness and Amplitude

• Loudness depends on amplitude
• Greater amplitude = louder sound
• On an oscilloscope: louder sound shows taller waves (larger amplitude)
• Doubling the amplitude means four times the energy delivered per second

W
Write This Down — Reading Oscilloscope Traces
Change on oscilloscope What it means
Waves closer together (more peaks) Higher frequency = higher pitch
Waves further apart (fewer peaks) Lower frequency = lower pitch
Taller waves Greater amplitude = louder
Shorter waves Smaller amplitude = quieter
W
Write This Down — Quality (Timbre)

A guitar and a piano playing the same note sound different. This difference is called quality or timbre. Each instrument has a strong fundamental frequency (the main note), but other weaker frequencies called overtones are mixed in. The combination of overtones gives each instrument its unique sound and waveform shape.

T
Study Tip — Oscilloscope Questions

When comparing two oscilloscope traces, always check TWO things separately: (1) are the peaks the same height? (amplitude/loudness), and (2) are the peaks the same distance apart? (frequency/pitch). The exam will often show you two traces and ask you to describe the differences.

  • I know: high frequency = high pitch, large amplitude = loud
  • I can read an oscilloscope trace and describe pitch and loudness
  • I know the human hearing range: 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz
  • I can explain what quality (timbre) means and what overtones are
Section 6.6 Ultrasound
Any sound with a frequency above 20 000 Hz is called ultrasound. Humans cannot hear it, but it has many practical uses.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Ultrasound is sound with a frequency above 20 000 Hz (above the upper limit of human hearing). Some animals can hear ultrasound — dogs up to about 50 000 Hz, bats up to about 120 000 Hz.

W
Write This Down — Uses of Ultrasound

• Echo-sounding: measuring the depth of water under a ship. Pulses are sent to the sea-bed and the echo time is measured. Depth = (speed × time) / 2
• Pre-natal scanning: imaging an unborn baby in the womb. A transmitter sends pulses of ultrasound into the mother's body and picks up reflections from the baby and different tissue layers. Safer than X-rays because ultrasound does not damage living cells
• Cleaning and breaking: vibrations dislodge dirt and grease from delicate machinery. In hospitals, concentrated beams of ultrasound can also be used to break up kidney stones and gall stones without surgery
• Metal testing: detecting flaws (cracks) inside metals. A pulse sent through the metal produces extra reflections if there is a flaw

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Echo-Sounding)

A ship's echo-sounder sends an ultrasound pulse to the sea-bed. It takes 0.1 s to travel down and back. Speed of sound in water = 1400 m/s. What is the depth?

1. Total distance = speed × time: 1400 × 0.1 = 140 m
2. Sound goes down AND back, divide by 2: 140 / 2 = 70 m
3. Depth of water: 70 m
?
Why This Matters — Ultrasound vs X-rays

The exam will ask: "Why use ultrasound instead of X-rays for scanning a baby?" Two reasons: (1) Ultrasound does not damage living cells, while X-rays can cause cell damage. (2) Ultrasound can distinguish between different layers of soft tissue, which an ordinary X-ray cannot.

T
Study Tip

Echo-sounding calculations are just echo problems with a different speed. Use the same method: total distance = speed × time, then divide by 2. The only difference is the speed of sound in water (1400 m/s) instead of air (330 m/s).

  • I can define ultrasound (frequency above 20 000 Hz)
  • I can name four uses of ultrasound
  • I can explain why ultrasound is preferred over X-rays for scanning babies
  • I can solve echo-sounding depth calculations
