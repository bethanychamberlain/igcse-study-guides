Section 4.1 Work Done
When a force moves an object, energy is transferred. The amount of energy transferred is called "work done."
W
Write This Down — Definition

Work done is the energy transferred when a force moves an object through a distance. Work is measured in joules (J).

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
W = F x d

W = work done (joules, J)
F = force (newtons, N)
d = distance moved in the direction of the force (metres, m)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A person pushes a box with a force of 12 N over a distance of 5 m. How much work is done?

1. Write the formula: W = F x d
2. Substitute: W = 12 x 5
3. Calculate: W = 60 J
?
Why This Matters

Work done = energy transferred. If you push a box and do 60 J of work, you have transferred 60 J of energy from your muscles to the box. This link between work and energy comes up everywhere in this chapter.

T
Study Tip

Practise with different numbers. Try: F = 20 N, d = 8 m. Write all three steps. Then check: W = 20 x 8 = 160 J. Do three more with your own numbers until it feels automatic.

  • I can write W = F x d from memory
  • I know the units: W in J, F in N, d in m
  • I solved a practice problem using all three steps
Section 4.2 Conservation of Energy
This is one of the most important laws in all of physics. It comes up on nearly every paper.
W
Write This Down — The Law

The law of conservation of energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred from one form to another or from one object to another.

W
Write This Down — Energy Forms

The main forms of energy you need to know:

• Kinetic energy (KE) — energy of a moving object
• Gravitational potential energy (GPE) — energy stored due to height
• Thermal (heat) energy — energy due to temperature
• Chemical energy — stored in fuels, food, batteries
• Elastic (strain) energy — stored in stretched/compressed objects
• Electrical energy — carried by electric current
• Sound energy — carried by vibrations
• Light energy — carried by electromagnetic waves

?
Why This Matters

When the exam asks "Describe the energy changes..." you need to say what energy starts as and what it becomes. For example: "Chemical energy in fuel is transferred to kinetic energy of the car and thermal energy (heat and friction)."

!
Don't Confuse

Energy is never "used up" or "lost." When we say energy is "wasted," we mean it has been transferred to a form we don't want (usually heat). The total energy stays the same — it just spreads out.

  • I can state the law of conservation of energy from memory
  • I can name at least 5 forms of energy
  • I know that "wasted" energy is not "lost" — it becomes heat
Section 4.3 Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)
Any object that is above the ground has gravitational potential energy. The higher it is, the more GPE it has.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Gravitational potential energy (GPE) is the energy stored in an object because of its height above the ground. It depends on mass, gravity, and height.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
GPE = m x g x h

GPE = gravitational potential energy (joules, J)
m = mass (kilograms, kg)
g = gravitational field strength (N/kg) — on Earth, g = 10 N/kg
h = height (metres, m)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A 6 kg object is lifted to a height of 4 m. What is its GPE? (g = 10 N/kg)

1. Write the formula: GPE = m x g x h
2. Substitute: GPE = 6 x 10 x 4
3. Calculate: GPE = 240 J

Now try: same object at 6 m height. You should get 360 J.

T
Study Tip

The value of g = 10 N/kg will always be given in the question, so you don't need to memorise it. But you DO need to remember which letter is which: m = mass, g = gravity, h = height. Don't mix up mass and height.

!
Don't Confuse — Mass vs Weight

Mass is how much matter is in an object (kg) — stays the same everywhere.
Weight is the gravitational force on an object (N) — changes with gravity.
In GPE = mgh, you use mass in kg, not weight in N.

  • I can write GPE = mgh from memory
  • I know what each letter stands for and its unit
  • I solved the practice problem (6 kg at 6 m = 360 J)
Section 4.4 Kinetic Energy (KE)
Any moving object has kinetic energy. The faster it moves and the heavier it is, the more KE it has.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Kinetic energy (KE) is the energy an object has because of its movement. It depends on mass and speed.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
KE = 1/2 x m x v2

KE = kinetic energy (joules, J)
m = mass (kilograms, kg)
v = speed/velocity (metres per second, m/s)
v2 means v squared — you multiply v by itself

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A 6 kg ball is rolling at 5 m/s. What is its kinetic energy?

1. Write the formula: KE = 1/2 x m x v²
2. Substitute: KE = 1/2 x 6 x 5²
3a. Square the speed first: 5² = 25
3b. Then multiply: KE = 1/2 x 6 x 25 = 75 J

Now try: same ball at 10 m/s. You should get 300 J. Notice the speed doubled but the KE quadrupled — that's because of the v2.

!
Don't Confuse — The Most Common Mistake

Students forget to square the speed first. If v = 10, then v2 = 100, NOT 10. Always do the squaring step before you multiply by the mass. Write it as a separate line in your working so you don't forget.

T
Study Tip — Calculator Order

On your calculator, do it in this order:
1. Type the speed, press x2 (or multiply it by itself)
2. Multiply by the mass
3. Divide by 2 (or multiply by 0.5)
This avoids bracket errors.

  • I can write KE = 1/2 mv2 from memory
  • I know to square the speed FIRST, then multiply
  • I solved the practice problem (6 kg at 10 m/s = 300 J)
Section 4.5 Efficiency
No machine is perfect. Some energy is always wasted as heat or sound. Efficiency tells you how good a machine is at converting energy usefully.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Efficiency is the percentage of total energy input that is converted into useful energy output. A higher efficiency means less energy is wasted.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
Efficiency = (useful energy output / total energy input) x 100%

The answer is a percentage (%). It can never be more than 100%.

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A light bulb receives 5000 J of electrical energy. It produces 1500 J of light energy. What is its efficiency?

1. Write the formula: Efficiency = (useful output / total input) x 100%
2. Substitute: Efficiency = (1500 / 5000) x 100%
3a. Divide first: 1500 / 5000 = 0.3
3b. Multiply: 0.3 x 100% = 30%
!
Don't Confuse — Which Number Goes on Top?

The useful energy goes on top (numerator). The total energy goes on the bottom (denominator). The useful number is always smaller than the total. If you get a number bigger than 100%, you put them the wrong way round.

Quick check: useful < total, always. If your useful number is bigger than your total, re-read the question.

T
Study Tip — Wasted Energy Shortcut

If they give you total input and ask for wasted energy:
Wasted = Total input - Useful output
In the example above: 5000 - 1500 = 3500 J wasted as heat.

  • I can write the efficiency formula from memory
  • I know: useful on top, total on bottom
  • I know efficiency can never be more than 100%
Section 4.6 Power
Power tells you how fast energy is transferred. A powerful machine does the same work in less time.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Power is the rate of doing work, or the rate of energy transfer. It is measured in watts (W). One watt means one joule per second.

W
Write This Down — Formula + Units
P = W / t

P = power (watts, W)
W = work done or energy transferred (joules, J)
t = time (seconds, s)

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A crane does 1500 J of work in 3 seconds. What is its power?

1. Write the formula: P = W / t
2. Substitute: P = 1500 / 3
3. Calculate: P = 500 W
!
Don't Confuse — "W" Has Two Meanings!

In this chapter, W can mean two different things:
• W = work done (in the formula P = W / t)
• W = watts (the unit of power)
Context tells you which. In "P = W / t", the W is work done. In "P = 500 W", the W means watts. Don't let this trip you up.

T
Study Tip

Think of power like speed, but for energy. Speed = distance / time. Power = energy / time. A 100 W light bulb uses 100 joules every second. A 60 W bulb uses 60 joules every second — it's less powerful.

  • I can write P = W / t from memory
  • I know the unit of power is the watt (W)
  • I won't confuse W (work) with W (watts)
Section 4.7 Energy Conversions: KE and GPE
When something falls, its GPE converts into KE. When something is thrown up, its KE converts into GPE. This is conservation of energy in action.
W
Write This Down — The Key Principle

When no energy is lost to friction or air resistance:
GPE lost = KE gained (object falling)
KE lost = GPE gained (object rising)

This means: mgh = 1/2 mv2

W
Write This Down — Worked Example

A 0.5 kg ball is rolling along the ground with 25 J of kinetic energy. It then rolls up a hill and stops. How high did it go? (g = 10 N/kg)

1. At the top, all KE has become GPE: KE = GPE
2. So: 25 = m x g x h
3. Substitute: 25 = 0.5 x 10 x h
4. Simplify: 25 = 5 x h
5. Rearrange: h = 25 / 5 = 5 m
!
Don't Confuse — When to Use Which Formula

• Object at a height, not moving? Use GPE = mgh
• Object moving on flat ground? Use KE = 1/2 mv2
• Object falling or rising? Set GPE = KE and solve for what's missing

T
Study Tip — Rearranging

In the worked example, you needed to solve for h. The trick: divide both sides by everything that's multiplied by h. If 25 = 0.5 x 10 x h, then h = 25 / (0.5 x 10) = 25 / 5 = 5 m. Practise this rearranging step — it's the part most students get stuck on.

  • I know that GPE lost = KE gained (and vice versa)
  • I can set mgh = 1/2 mv2 and solve for the unknown
  • I practised rearranging the formula to solve for h

Don't Confuse — Energy vs Force vs Power

Energy = the ability to do work (measured in joules, J)
Force = a push or pull (measured in newtons, N)
Power = how fast energy is used (measured in watts, W)

These are three different things. Energy is "how much." Power is "how fast." Force is "how hard you push."
