All Six Possessive Adjectives — Not Just “Mi”

English Before Singular Noun Before Plural Noun
my mi mis
your (tú) tu tus
his / her / your (Ud.) su sus
our nuestro / nuestra nuestros / nuestras
your (vosotros) vuestro / vuestra vuestros / vuestras
their / your (Uds.) su sus
Your Easter Test Error — 1 out of 5
You used “mi” for everything — my, your, his, our. That only works for “my.” You MUST learn all six. The exam will test them.
Key Rules to Drill
1. Mi, tu, su do NOT change for gender. “mi hermano” AND “mi hermana” — same word.
2. They DO change for plural: mi → mis, tu → tus, su → sus.
3. Only nuestro/vuestro change for BOTH gender AND number.
4. Tu (your, no accent) is not tú (you, with accent). Different words!

Describing People — SER vs TENER

Use SER Use TENER
When? Height, build, personality Hair and eyes
Why? “He IS tall” = permanent trait “He HAS brown hair” = possession
Example “Es alto y simpático.” “Tiene el pelo castaño.”
Common Error
NEVER say “es pelo largo” — it makes no sense. Hair and eyes ALWAYS use TENER.

TENER (to have) — Present Tense

Person Form Example
yo tengo Tengo los ojos marrones.
tú tienes ¿Tienes hermanos?
él / ella tiene Tiene el pelo rubio.
nosotros tenemos Tenemos un perro.
vosotros tenéis ¿Tenéis primos?
ellos / ellas tienen Tienen dos hijos.
Irregular Pattern
TENER is a go-verb: the yo form ends in -go (tengo), not -o. The tú/él/ellos forms have ie (tienes, tiene, tienen). Only nosotros and vosotros are “normal.”

Shortened Adjectives — Buen, Mal, Gran

Full Form Short Form When? Example
bueno buen Before masc. singular noun un buen amigo
malo mal Before masc. singular noun un mal día
grande gran Before ANY singular noun una gran familia
Gran Changes Meaning
Before the noun: gran = “great” (quality). After the noun: grande = “big” (size).
“Una gran persona” = a great person. “Una persona grande” = a big/large person.

Say It in One Sentence

Possessives Six possessives exist (mi, tu, su, nuestro, vuestro, su) — stop using “mi” for everything.
Plural = add -s mi → mis, tu → tus, su → sus — the possessive must match the number of the thing owned.
SER = traits Height, build, and personality are permanent → “Es alto, es simpático.”
TENER = hair/eyes “Tiene el pelo negro y los ojos verdes” — never use SER for physical features.
Personal “a” Put a before a person who receives the action: “Quiero a mi madre.”
Y → E “And” changes from y to e before words starting with i-/hi-: “sociable e inteligente.”

Memory Tricks

Possessives — Think of the S Pattern
mi → mis, tu → tus, su → sus. Just add an S for plural. No other changes. The only weird ones are nuestro/vuestro, which change for gender too — but those are rarer.
SER for Labels, TENER for Possessions
If you can put a label on them (“tall person,” “friendly girl”), use SER. If they physically HAVE something (hair, eyes, siblings), use TENER. Labels = SER. Belongings = TENER.
The Exam Formula for Describing Family
Every family member gets FOUR things: Name + Relationship + Appearance + Personality.
“Mi madre se llama Ana. Es alta y delgada. Tiene el pelo castaño. Es muy simpática.”
This exact pattern, repeated for each person, scores top marks.
Tu vs Tú — The Accent Test
Tú (with accent) = “you” the person. Tu (no accent) = “your” the possessive. Think: the accent is the “person” standing on the letter.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
“Describe a tu familia” (Describe your family) Name + relationship + appearance (TENER for hair) + personality (SER)
“¿Tienes hermanos?” (Do you have siblings?) “Sí, tengo un hermano y una hermana” OR “Soy hijo único”
“¿Cómo es tu amigo?” (What is your friend like?) Use SER for traits: “Es simpático, gracioso e inteligente.”
“Describe el aspecto físico” (Describe physical appearance) SER for build + TENER for hair/eyes: “Es alto. Tiene el pelo moreno.”
Blank needs “his/her” su (singular) or sus (plural) — NOT “mi”!
Blank needs “our” nuestro (masc.) or nuestra (fem.) — NOT “mi”!
