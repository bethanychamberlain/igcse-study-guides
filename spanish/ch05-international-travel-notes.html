!
Don't Confuse — Three Different Constructions

Weather uses three different patterns, not just one:
1. Hace + noun: Hace sol, Hace frío (most common)
2. Verb alone: Llueve, Nieva (rain and snow)
3. Hay / Está: Hay tormenta, Está nublado

Do NOT say “es calor” or “está sol.” Weather mostly uses hace.

W
Write This Down — Past Weather (Imperfect)

To describe weather on a past holiday, use the imperfect:

“Hacía sol todos los días.” — It was sunny every day.
“Hacía mucho calor.” — It was very hot.
“Llovía por la tarde.” — It rained in the afternoon.

  • I know at least 8 weather expressions
  • I know which use “hace,” which are verbs alone, and which use “hay/está”
  • I can describe past weather using the imperfect (hacía sol, llovía)

Don't Confuse — IR and SER Have the Same Preterite

Fui can mean “I went” (ir) OR “I was” (ser). Context tells you which:
“Fui a España.” = I went to Spain. (ir — because of “a”)
“Fue increíble.” = It was incredible. (ser — describing something)

?
Why This Matters

The IGCSE writing exam almost always includes “describe a holiday you went on.” You MUST use the preterite for this. Without it, you cannot access the marks for past tense writing. The irregular verbs (fui, hice, tuve) appear in almost every holiday description.

  • I know the regular preterite endings for -AR and -ER/-IR
  • I know the irregular preterites: fui, hice, tuve, estuve
  • I know that ir and ser share the same preterite forms
  • I can write 3+ sentences about a past holiday using the preterite
Section 5 Describing a Holiday — Putting It All Together
The IGCSE wants you to combine three tenses in a holiday description: preterite (what you did), imperfect (what the weather was like / how things were), and present/future (what you think now / what you want to do next).
W
Write This Down — Model Holiday Paragraph

“El verano pasado, fui a España con mi familia. Viajamos en avión. Hacía mucho sol y calor. Nos alojamos en un hotel cerca de la playa. Visité la catedral y comí paella en un restaurante. Fue increíble. Me gustaría volver el año que viene.”

Last summer, I went to Spain with my family. We travelled by plane. It was very sunny and hot. We stayed in a hotel near the beach. I visited the cathedral and ate paella in a restaurant. It was incredible. I would like to go back next year.

!
Don't Confuse — Mucho vs Muy

Muy goes before adjectives and adverbs: “muy bonito” (very pretty).
Mucho goes before nouns or after verbs: “mucho sol” (lots of sun), “como mucho” (I eat a lot).
NEVER say “mucho bonito” — that’s always wrong.

T
Study Tip — The Three-Tense Formula

For any holiday writing task, use this formula:
1. Preterite — what you DID: “Fui a..., Visité..., Comí...”
2. Imperfect — what it WAS LIKE: “Hacía sol, El hotel era grande...”
3. Conditional/Present — your OPINION now: “Fue genial, Me gustaría volver...”

Three tenses in one paragraph = top band marks.

  • I can write a holiday paragraph using preterite + imperfect + opinion
  • I know the difference between muy (+ adjective) and mucho (+ noun)
  • I know at least 4 quantifiers (muy, mucho, bastante, demasiado)
  • I can use “me gustaría” to say what I would like to do
