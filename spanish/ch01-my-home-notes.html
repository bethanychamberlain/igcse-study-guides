Study Tip — Gender Pattern

Notice: words ending in -a are usually feminine (casa, granja, cabaña, hacienda). Words ending in -o are usually masculine (piso, castillo). This pattern holds for most Spanish nouns.

!
Don't Confuse — El vs La

El = masculine “the”. La = feminine “the”. Most room words above are masculine (el salón, el dormitorio). The main exception here is la cocina (feminine). If you use the wrong article, you lose easy marks.

?
Why This Matters — Definite Articles

Spanish has four words for “the”, not just one:

Singular Plural
Masculine el los
Feminine la las

“el dormitorio” → “los dormitorios”
“la cocina” → “las cocinas”

  • I can name at least 8 rooms in Spanish with the correct article
  • I know the four definite articles: el, la, los, las

Section 4 Hay / No Hay — “There is” / “There isn’t”
Hay is one of the most useful words in Spanish. It means both “there is” and “there are” — one word does both jobs.
W
Write This Down — Hay Rules

Hay = there is / there are (same word for singular and plural)
No hay = there isn’t / there aren’t

“En mi dormitorio hay una cama.” — In my bedroom there is a bed.
“Hay tres dormitorios.” — There are three bedrooms.
“No hay jardín.” — There is no garden.
“No hay armarios en mi dormitorio.” — There are no wardrobes in my bedroom.

!
Don't Confuse — Hay vs Está

Hay = there is (introduces something new: “there is a book on the table”)
Está = it is located (talks about something specific: “the book is on the table”)

“Hay un libro en la mesa.” — There is a book on the table. (introducing it)
“El libro está en la mesa.” — The book is on the table. (saying where it is)

Rule: hay + un/una (a). está + el/la (the). If you see “the,” use estar. If you see “a,” use hay.

?
Why This Matters

The IGCSE writing exam almost always asks you to describe a place. Hay is the single most useful word for this — it lets you list what exists in any location. Use it in every paragraph about a room, town, or school.

  • I know that hay means both “there is” and “there are”
  • I can explain the difference between hay and está
  • I can use no hay to say what does NOT exist
Section 5 Prepositions of Place — ¿Dónde está?
Prepositions tell you where things are. You need these to describe rooms, give directions, and answer “where is” questions.
W
Write This Down — Preposition Vocabulary
Spanish English Example
encima de on top of El libro está encima de la mesa.
debajo de under / below El gato está debajo de la cama.
delante de in front of La silla está delante de la mesa.
detrás de behind El armario está detrás de la puerta.
al lado de next to La lámpara está al lado de la cama.
a la derecha de to the right of El baño está a la derecha del salón.
a la izquierda de to the left of La cocina está a la izquierda del comedor.
entre between El salón está entre la cocina y el comedor.
cerca de near Mi casa está cerca del parque.
lejos de far from El colegio está lejos de mi casa.
!
Don't Confuse — De + El = Del

When de comes before el, they combine into del. This is not optional — it is a grammar rule.

“al lado del salón” — next to the living room (de + el = del)
“al lado de la cocina” — next to the kitchen (de + la stays as de la)

Only de + el contracts. De la, de los, and de las do NOT contract.

T
Study Tip — Draw Your Room

Sketch your bedroom. Label each item of furniture. Then write 5 sentences describing where things are using different prepositions. Example: “Mi cama está al lado de la ventana. El escritorio está delante de la estantería.” This is exactly what the exam asks you to do.

  • I can use at least 6 prepositions of place correctly
  • I know that de + el = del
  • I wrote 5 sentences describing my room using prepositions
Section 6 Describing Your Home — Adjective Agreement
In Spanish, adjectives must agree with the noun they describe in gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural). This is something you scored well on in the Easter test — keep it up.
W
Write This Down — Home Description Adjectives
Masculine Feminine English
grande grande big
pequeño pequeña small
moderno moderna modern
antiguo antigua old / antique
luminoso luminosa bright / light-filled
oscuro oscura dark
bonito bonita pretty / nice
cómodo cómoda comfortable
W
Write This Down — Agreement Rules

Rule 1: Adjectives ending in -o change to -a for feminine nouns.
“el salón es pequeño” but “la cocina es pequeña”

Rule 2: Adjectives ending in -e or a consonant do NOT change for gender.
“el salón es grande” and “la cocina es grande”

Rule 3: For plurals, add -s (after a vowel) or -es (after a consonant).
“los dormitorios son pequeños” / “las habitaciones son grandes”

?
Why This Matters

You got adjective agreement perfect (4/4) on the Easter test. That means this is a strength — use it. When you describe your home, every adjective you match correctly to its noun shows the examiner solid grammar. Stack these up and they add marks fast.

  • I know -o adjectives change to -a for feminine nouns
  • I know -e adjectives stay the same for both genders
  • I can describe my home using at least 4 different adjectives correctly
Section 7 Regular Present Tense — -AR, -ER, -IR Verbs
To talk about what you do at home, you need regular present tense verbs. All regular Spanish verbs follow one of three patterns depending on their ending: -ar, -er, or -ir.
W
Write This Down — Regular Present Tense Endings
Person -AR (hablar) -ER (comer) -IR (vivir)
yo habl-o com-o viv-o
tú habl-as com-es viv-es
él / ella habl-a com-e viv-e
nosotros habl-amos com-emos viv-imos
vosotros habl-áis com-éis viv-ís
ellos / ellas habl-an com-en viv-en
W
Write This Down — Home Context Examples

“Cocino en la cocina.” — I cook in the kitchen. (cocinar = -ar)
“Comemos en el comedor.” — We eat in the dining room. (comer = -er)
“Vivo en una casa grande.” — I live in a big house. (vivir = -ir)
“Mi hermano estudia en su dormitorio.” — My brother studies in his bedroom. (estudiar = -ar)

T
Study Tip — The Yo Form Is Your Best Friend

On the exam, most writing tasks ask about your life. That means you mostly need the yo form. Good news: the yo form is always -o for all three verb types. Just drop the -ar/-er/-ir and add -o. Done.

  • I can conjugate a regular -AR verb in all 6 forms
  • I know the -ER and -IR endings are almost identical (only differ in nosotros/vosotros)
  • I can write 3 sentences about what I do at home using different verbs
