Don't Confuse — Librería vs Biblioteca

La librería = bookshop (you BUY books).
La biblioteca = library (you BORROW books).
This is a classic false friend. “Library” looks like “librería” but it’s not!

“Es una ciudad pequeña y tranquila.” — It is a small and quiet town.

!
Don't Confuse — A + El = Al

Just like de + el = del, the preposition a + el = al.
“Voy al parque.” — I go to the park. (a + el = al)
“Voy a la iglesia.” — I go to the church. (a + la stays)

  • I can give directions using at least 5 phrases
  • I know that a + el = al and de + el = del

Study Tip — “En” for Vehicles, “A” for Walking

Use en + vehicle (en coche, en tren). Use a pie for walking. Never say “en pie” or “en a pie.”

Section 4 Comparatives — Comparing Things
!
Don't Confuse — You Scored 0% on This

On the Easter test, you got zero marks on comparatives. You didn’t attempt any comparative sentences. This section fixes that. There are only three patterns and they are all simple.

W
Write This Down — The Three Comparative Patterns
PatternStructureExample
More ... than más + adjective + que “Madrid es más grande que Palm Bay.”
Madrid is bigger than Palm Bay.
Less ... than menos + adjective + que “Palm Bay es menos ruidoso que Madrid.”
Palm Bay is less noisy than Madrid.
As ... as tan + adjective + como “Mi ciudad es tan bonita como tu ciudad.”
My town is as pretty as your town.
W
Write This Down — Irregular Comparatives

Some adjectives have special comparative forms (like English “good → better”):

AdjectiveComparativeExample
bueno (good) mejor (better) “Mi ciudad es mejor que la tuya.”
malo (bad) peor (worse) “El tiempo es peor que ayer.”
grande (big) mayor (bigger/older) “Mi hermano es mayor que yo.”
pequeño (small) menor (smaller/younger) “Soy menor que mi hermana.”
T
Study Tip — Remember the Sandwich

Think of a sandwich: más [FILLING] que. The adjective is the filling. The bread is always the same: más ... que. Just swap the filling for any adjective.
“más grande que” / “más bonita que” / “más tranquilo que”

?
Why This Matters

Comparatives are tested on every single IGCSE paper. They appear in reading comprehension, writing tasks (“compare your town with another”), and speaking. If you can’t form comparatives, you cannot access the higher marks for writing.

  • I know the three patterns: más...que, menos...que, tan...como
  • I know the irregulars: mejor, peor, mayor, menor
  • I can write 3 sentences comparing two towns

Study Tip — The “-ería” Pattern

Many shop names end in -ería. If you know the product, you can often guess the shop: zapato (shoe) → zapatería (shoe shop), pan (bread) → panadería (bakery). All -ería words are feminine.

Section 6 The Perfect Tense — What You Have Done
The perfect tense says what you have done. It is formed with haber (to have) + past participle.

W
Write This Down — Past Participle Formation

-AR verbs: drop -ar, add -ado — visitar → visitado
-ER/-IR verbs: drop -er/-ir, add -ido — comer → comido, vivir → vivido

“He visitado el museo.” — I have visited the museum.
“Hemos comido en un restaurante.” — We have eaten in a restaurant.
“Han vivido aquí cinco años.” — They have lived here five years.

Why This Matters

Using the perfect tense shows the examiner you can use more than one tense. The IGCSE mark scheme explicitly rewards range of tenses. Even dropping in one perfect tense sentence (e.g. “He visitado muchas ciudades”) boosts your grade.

  • I can conjugate haber in all 6 forms
  • I know -AR → -ado and -ER/-IR → -ido
  • I know at least 3 irregular past participles (hecho, visto, escrito)
  • I can write 3 sentences using the perfect tense
