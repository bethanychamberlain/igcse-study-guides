Section 2 Possessive Adjectives — More Than Just “Mi”
!
Don't Confuse — This Is Your Biggest Gap

On the Easter test, you only knew “mi” for possessives and scored 1 out of 5. You used “mi” for everything — “my,” “your,” “his,” “our.” That doesn’t work. Spanish has six different possessive adjectives and you need all of them.

W
Write This Down — All Possessive Adjectives
English Before Singular Noun Before Plural Noun
my mi mis
your (tú) tu tus
his / her / your (usted) su sus
our nuestro/a nuestros/as
your (vosotros) vuestro/a vuestros/as
their / your (ustedes) su sus
W
Write This Down — Examples

“Mi hermano es alto.” — My brother is tall.
“Tu madre es simpática.” — Your mother is nice.
“Su casa es grande.” — His/Her house is big.
“Mis hermanos son jóvenes.” — My brothers are young.
“Nuestro padre es médico.” — Our father is a doctor.
“Nuestra casa está en Palm Bay.” — Our house is in Palm Bay.

!
Don't Confuse — Key Rules

Rule 1: Mi, tu, su do NOT change for gender. “mi hermano” AND “mi hermana” — same word.
Rule 2: They DO change for plural. “mi hermano” but “mis hermanos.”
Rule 3: Only nuestro/vuestro change for BOTH gender AND number: nuestro, nuestra, nuestros, nuestras.
Rule 4: Tu (your) has NO accent. Tú (you) has an accent. Different words!

T
Study Tip — Drill Beyond “Mi”

Write 6 sentences about your family, each using a DIFFERENT possessive: mi padre, tu amigo, su hermana, nuestro perro, mis abuelos, sus primos. If you can only write “mi,” you will lose marks every time the exam asks about other people’s families.

  • I know all 6 possessive adjectives (mi, tu, su, nuestro, vuestro, su)
  • I know to add -s for plural nouns (mi → mis, tu → tus, su → sus)
  • I know that only nuestro/vuestro change for gender (nuestro/nuestra)
  • I can use at least 3 different possessives in sentences

Write This Down — Hair & Eyes (use TENER)

In Spanish you say “I HAVE blue eyes” not “my eyes ARE blue.” Use tener (to have).

SpanishEnglish
el pelo largo / cortolong / short hair
el pelo rizado / lisocurly / straight hair
el pelo rubio / moreno / castaño / pelirrojoblond / dark / brown / red hair
los ojos azules / verdes / marronesblue / green / brown eyes

“Tiene el pelo largo y rubio.” — He/She has long blond hair.
“Tengo los ojos marrones.” — I have brown eyes.

!
Don't Confuse — SER for Build, TENER for Hair/Eyes

“He IS tall” = “Es alto” (SER — permanent trait)
“He HAS brown hair” = “Tiene el pelo castaño” (TENER — possession)

Do NOT say “es pelo largo” — that makes no sense. Hair and eyes always use TENER.

  • I know 4+ adjectives for height/build
  • I use TENER (not SER) for hair and eyes
  • I can describe a person’s appearance in 3+ sentences

!
Don't Confuse — “Y” becomes “E” before “I-”

The word for “and” is normally y, but it changes to e before words starting with i- or hi-.
“sociable e inteligente” NOT “sociable y inteligente.” This is a small rule that earns you accuracy marks.

Why This Matters

The IGCSE writing and speaking exams almost always have a question about family or friends. The examiner wants: name + relationship + appearance + personality. If you can do all four, you’re hitting the top band.

  • I know at least 8 personality adjectives with correct agreement
  • I know that personality uses SER (not ESTAR)
  • I know the y → e rule before i-/hi- words
Section 5 The Personal “A” — A Unique Spanish Rule
Spanish has a rule that doesn’t exist in English: when the direct object of a verb is a person, you put a before them.
W
Write This Down — The Rule

When the thing receiving the action is a person, add a before them.

“Quiero a mi madre.” — I love my mother. (person → add a)
“Conozco a tu hermano.” — I know your brother. (person → add a)
“Veo la televisión.” — I watch television. (not a person → NO a)

T
Study Tip — Simple Test

Before the object of the verb, ask: “Is this a person?” If yes, add a. If no, don’t. That’s it.

  • I know the personal “a” goes before people who are direct objects
  • I can write 2 sentences using the personal a
Section 6 Shortened Adjectives — Buen, Mal, Gran
Some adjectives shorten when they go BEFORE a masculine singular noun. These are called “apocopated” adjectives.
W
Write This Down — The Short Forms
Full FormShortenedWhen?Example
bueno buen Before masc. singular noun un buen amigo (a good friend)
malo mal Before masc. singular noun un mal día (a bad day)
grande gran Before ANY singular noun una gran familia (a great family)
primero primer Before masc. singular noun el primer hijo (the first son)
!
Don't Confuse — Gran vs Grande

Before the noun, gran means “great” (quality): “una gran persona” = a great person.
After the noun, grande means “big” (size): “una persona grande” = a big/large person.
Position changes meaning!

  • I know bueno → buen and malo → mal before masculine singular nouns
  • I know grande → gran before any singular noun, and it changes meaning
