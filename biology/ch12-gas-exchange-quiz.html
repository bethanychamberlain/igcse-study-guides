Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
List four properties of a good gas exchange surface.
2 marks
Answer
Any four from: thin (one cell thick) [½], large surface area [½], moist [½], good blood supply [½], well ventilated [½]. [1 for any two correct, 2 for four correct]
"Thin" and "large surface area" are the two the examiner most wants to see. Always include those first.

2
Put these structures in the correct order for the pathway of air into the lungs: bronchioles, trachea, alveoli, bronchi, larynx.
2 marks
Answer
Larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli [1 for correct sequence, 1 for all five correct].
Remember: the tubes get smaller as you go deeper. Larynx (big) → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles (tiny) → alveoli (sacs at the end).

Name three harmful substances in tobacco smoke and state one effect of each.
3 marks
Answer
Tar — contains carcinogens / coats lungs / damages cilia [1].
Nicotine — is addictive / raises heart rate and blood pressure [1].
Carbon monoxide — binds to haemoglobin, reducing oxygen-carrying capacity of blood [1].
One mark per substance + effect pair. You must name the substance AND its effect to get the mark.

What is the function of the C-shaped cartilage rings in the trachea?
1 mark
Answer
To keep the trachea open / prevent it from collapsing during inhalation [1].
Say "keep the airway open" — that's the key phrase. They are C-shaped (not full rings) so the oesophagus behind can expand when food is swallowed.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain how the structure of alveoli makes them well adapted for gas exchange.
4 marks
Answer
Alveoli have very thin walls (one cell thick), giving a short diffusion distance for gases [1]. There are millions of alveoli, providing a very large surface area for gas exchange [1]. They have a rich blood supply (dense capillary network) which maintains a steep concentration gradient [1]. They are moist, allowing gases to dissolve before diffusing across the surface [1].
Four marks, four features. Each one needs a feature + the reason it helps. Don't just list — explain WHY each feature matters. "Large surface area so more gas molecules can diffuse at once" is the pattern.

6
Describe the changes in the chest that cause air to enter the lungs during inhalation.
4 marks
Answer
The external intercostal muscles contract, pulling the rib cage up and out [1]. The diaphragm muscles contract and the diaphragm flattens / moves down [1]. These movements increase the volume of the chest cavity [1]. The increased volume causes the pressure inside the lungs to decrease (below atmospheric pressure), so air rushes in [1].
The chain: muscles contract → ribs up, diaphragm down → volume increases → pressure decreases → air rushes in. Each link in the chain is a mark. Don't skip any step.

7
Explain why smokers are more likely to develop lung infections than non-smokers.
3 marks
Answer
Smoke damages/paralyses the cilia that line the airways [1]. The cilia can no longer sweep mucus (containing trapped bacteria and dust) away from the lungs [1]. Bacteria accumulate in the lungs and multiply, leading to infections [1].
The logic: cilia damaged → mucus not cleared → bacteria build up → infection. Also accept: tar/smoke stimulates excess mucus production, blocking airways further.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A person with emphysema becomes very breathless during mild exercise. Explain why, referring to the changes in the lungs caused by emphysema and the effect on gas exchange.
5 marks
Answer
In emphysema, the walls of the alveoli are destroyed [1]. This greatly reduces the surface area available for gas exchange [1]. Less oxygen can diffuse into the blood per breath [1]. During exercise, muscles need more oxygen for aerobic respiration, but the damaged lungs cannot supply enough [1]. The person breathes faster and more deeply to try to compensate, but the reduced surface area means they still cannot get enough oxygen, so they become breathless [1].
The key idea the examiner wants: "alveoli destroyed → less surface area → less gas exchange → not enough oxygen for muscles → breathlessness." Each step is a mark.

9
The table below shows the composition of inhaled and exhaled air.

GasInhaledExhaled
Oxygen21%16.9%
Carbon dioxide0.03%4.1%
Nitrogen79%79%

Explain the differences in oxygen and carbon dioxide levels between inhaled and exhaled air, and explain why nitrogen remains the same.
5 marks
Answer
Oxygen decreases because oxygen diffuses from the air in the alveoli into the blood [1]. This is because the concentration of oxygen in the alveolar air is higher than in the deoxygenated blood in the capillaries [1].

Carbon dioxide increases because CO₂ diffuses from the blood into the alveolar air [1]. This is because the blood returning from the tissues has a high concentration of CO₂ (produced by respiration) [1].

Nitrogen stays the same because the body does not use or produce nitrogen — it is chemically inert and is not involved in any metabolic reactions [1].
Always link the gas change to diffusion and concentration gradients. For nitrogen, the key word is "not used by the body" or "inert."

10
A graph from an epidemiological study shows that as the number of cigarettes smoked per day increases, the risk of death from lung cancer increases. A tobacco company argues that this does not prove smoking causes lung cancer. Discuss both sides of this argument.
6 marks
Answer
The tobacco company's argument:
The data shows a correlation (a link), but correlation does not necessarily prove causation [1]. It could be that people who smoke heavily also share other risk factors (e.g. diet, occupation, pollution, genetic predisposition) that increase cancer risk [1]. You cannot do a controlled experiment on humans because it would be unethical to deliberately expose people to a harmful substance [1].

The scientific counter-argument:
Researchers like Sir Richard Doll controlled for many of these factors — he studied doctors (same profession), separated city and countryside dwellers, and still found the link [1]. The correlation has been found in many independent studies across different countries and populations, making it very convincing [1]. Lung cancer is multifactorial, but smoking is accepted as the single greatest risk factor based on the overwhelming weight of evidence [1].
For "discuss" questions, you MUST give BOTH sides. Three marks for the tobacco company's argument (correlation not causation, other factors, can't experiment on humans) and three marks for the scientific response (controlled studies, replicated results, strong evidence). Missing one side loses half the marks.
