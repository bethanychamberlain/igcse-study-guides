Antibiotics

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Drug A chemical substance taken into the body that affects chemical reactions. —
Antibiotic A substance produced by a microorganism that kills or inhibits the growth of bacteria. Antiseptic (used on the body surface, not inside it)
Penicillin The first antibiotic discovered. Produced by the fungus Penicillium. Prevents bacterial cell wall formation. —
Antiseptic Chemical applied to the outside of the body to prevent infection (e.g. hand sanitiser on a wound). Antibiotic (taken INSIDE the body to treat infection)
Disinfectant A strong chemical used on surfaces (worktops, toilets) to kill microbes. NOT safe on skin. Antiseptic (safe on skin) vs Disinfectant (surfaces only)
Antibiotic resistance When a strain of bacteria is no longer killed by an antibiotic, due to a random mutation and natural selection. —
MRSA Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus. Produces a protein that blocks the antibiotic's action on cell walls. —
Mutation A random change in DNA. Can produce a protein that makes a bacterium resistant to an antibiotic. "Mutation" does NOT mean the antibiotic caused the change — it happens randomly before the antibiotic is applied
Natural selection The process by which organisms with favourable traits survive and reproduce more. Explains how resistance spreads. —
Secondary metabolic product A substance (like penicillin) produced when the organism is slowing its growth, not during rapid growth. —

Why Antibiotics Work on Bacteria but NOT Viruses

Bacteria Viruses
Cell wall? Yes — target for penicillin No cell wall
Own metabolism? Yes — can be disrupted No — uses host cell's machinery
Is it a cell? Yes No — just DNA/RNA in a protein coat
Antibiotic effective? Yes No — nothing to target
Exam Trap — Don't Just Say "Because They Only Kill Bacteria"
You need to explain why: viruses have no cell walls and no metabolism of their own, so there is nothing for the antibiotic to target. That's the 2-mark answer.

Say It in One Sentence

Penicillin Prevents bacterial cell walls from forming — without walls, bacteria burst and die.
No virus effect Antibiotics target cell walls and metabolism — viruses have neither, so antibiotics can't touch them.
Resistance step 1 A random mutation gives one bacterium resistance BEFORE the antibiotic is even applied.
Resistance step 2 The antibiotic kills all non-resistant bacteria but the mutant survives and reproduces — natural selection.
MRSA A "superbug" that resists methicillin — especially dangerous in hospitals where patients have weak immune systems.
Finish your course Stopping antibiotics early leaves the strongest bacteria alive — they recover, multiply, and are harder to kill next time.
Sterile fermenter Penicillin production must be sterile so contaminant microbes don't compete for nutrients or produce toxins.

Memory Tricks

Antibiotic vs Antiseptic
Antibiotic = bio = life = goes inside a living body. Antiseptic = prevents sepsis = stays on the outside. "Bio = inside, septic = outside."
The 4-Step Resistance Chain
Mutation → Variation → Selection → Reproduction. "Many Villains Survive and Reproduce." Each step is worth one mark. Write all four for full marks.
The Antibiotic Doesn't CREATE Resistance
Think of it like a flood: the flood doesn't MAKE people tall — it just means only the tall ones survive. The antibiotic doesn't make bacteria resistant; it just lets the already-resistant ones survive.
Secondary Metabolic Product
Penicillin is the fungus's "retirement project" — it's made after the main growth phase slows down, not during rapid growth. Think: grow first, then produce the antibiotic.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"How does penicillin work?" It prevents the formation of bacterial cell walls, so bacteria cannot grow or reproduce
"Why don't antibiotics work on viruses?" Viruses have no cell walls and no metabolism of their own — nothing for the antibiotic to target
"Explain how antibiotic resistance develops" Random mutation → resistant bacterium survives antibiotic treatment (selection) → reproduces → whole population now resistant
"Why should you finish a course of antibiotics?" Stopping early may leave the most resistant bacteria alive; they multiply and the infection becomes harder to treat
"What is MRSA and why is it dangerous?" Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus; it resists common antibiotics and is dangerous in hospitals where patients have weakened immune systems
"Why must the fermenter be kept sterile during penicillin production?" Other microbes would compete for nutrients (reducing yield) and could produce toxic chemicals (making the medicine unsafe)
"Distinguish between antibiotic and antiseptic" Antibiotic: taken inside the body to treat bacterial infection. Antiseptic: applied to body surface to prevent infection.
"Why is penicillin called a secondary metabolic product?" It is produced after the main growth phase of the Penicillium fungus, not during rapid growth
