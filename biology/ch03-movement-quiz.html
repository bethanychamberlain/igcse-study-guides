Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define diffusion.
2 marks
Answer
The net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration [1] to a region of lower concentration [1].
Examiner wants: direction (high to low) + "particles" or "molecules"

2
Define osmosis. (Hint: there are two key parts to this definition.)
2 marks
Answer
The net movement of water molecules [1] through a partially permeable membrane [1], from a dilute solution to a more concentrated solution.
Examiner wants BOTH parts: (1) water only + (2) through a partially permeable membrane. Missing either = lose a mark.

3
What is active transport? Does it need energy?
2 marks
Answer
The movement of particles from a region of lower concentration to higher concentration (against the concentration gradient) [1]. It requires energy from respiration [1].
The word "active" = needs energy. Always mention respiration as the energy source.

4
Give one example of diffusion in the human body.
1 mark
Answer
Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli (air sacs in the lungs) into the blood. Or: Carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the alveoli.
Do NOT say "water moving" — that's osmosis. Safest answer: gas exchange in lungs.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to compare, explain WHY, or apply to a new situation.
5
A plant cell is placed in pure water. Describe what happens to the cell and explain why, using the word "osmosis".
3 marks
Answer
Water enters the cell by osmosis [1] because the water concentration is higher outside the cell than inside [1]. The cell swells and becomes turgid, but does not burst because the cell wall provides support [1].
Must name the process (osmosis), explain the direction (why water moves in), and state the outcome (turgid, not burst, because cell wall).

6
A student says: "Diffusion and active transport are the same thing because they both move particles." Explain two differences between them.
4 marks
Answer
1. Direction: Diffusion moves particles from high to low concentration [1], while active transport moves particles from low to high concentration [1].
2. Energy: Diffusion does not require energy [1], while active transport requires energy from respiration [1].
For "explain two differences" questions, structure each difference as: "[Feature]: [Process A] does X, while [Process B] does Y." Two clear pairs = 4 marks.

7
Root hair cells absorb mineral ions from the soil by active transport. Explain why they cannot absorb minerals by diffusion.
3 marks
Answer
The concentration of mineral ions is higher inside the root hair cell than in the soil [1]. Diffusion can only move particles from high to low concentration [1], so active transport is needed to move minerals against the concentration gradient, using energy from respiration [1].
The key insight: minerals are already more concentrated IN the root than in the soil. Diffusion would move them the wrong way.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
An experiment uses dialysis tubing (a partially permeable membrane) filled with sugar solution, placed in a beaker of pure water. After 30 minutes, the tubing has swollen. Explain these results in terms of osmosis.
5 marks
Answer
The pure water outside the tubing has a higher water concentration than the sugar solution inside [1]. Water molecules are small enough to pass through the partially permeable membrane [1], but sugar molecules are too large to pass through [1]. Water moves into the tubing by osmosis, from the higher water concentration to the lower water concentration [1]. This causes the volume of liquid inside the tubing to increase, making it swell [1].
5-mark questions need 5 distinct points. Cover: concentrations on each side, membrane properties, direction of water movement, name the process, describe the result.

9
Red blood cells are placed in three solutions: (A) pure water, (B) normal saline (same concentration as blood), (C) very concentrated salt solution. Predict and explain what happens to the cells in each solution.
6 marks
Answer
Solution A (pure water): Water concentration is higher outside the cell. Water enters the cell by osmosis [1]. The cell swells and bursts (lysis) because animal cells have no cell wall [1].

Solution B (normal saline): Water concentration is equal on both sides. There is no net movement of water [1]. The cell stays the same shape and size [1].

Solution C (concentrated salt): Water concentration is higher inside the cell. Water leaves the cell by osmosis [1]. The cell shrinks and becomes crenated (wrinkled) [1].
For each solution: state the water concentrations, say which way water moves (or doesn't), describe the result. 2 marks per solution = 6 total.

10
Compare diffusion, osmosis, and active transport. For each process, state: (a) the direction of movement, (b) whether energy is needed, (c) one example in a living organism.
6 marks
Answer
Diffusion: (a) High to low concentration [1]. (b) No energy needed [1]. (c) Oxygen diffuses from alveoli into the blood in the lungs.

Osmosis: (a) Dilute to concentrated solution (water moves from high to low water concentration) [1]. (b) No energy needed [1]. (c) Water enters root hair cells from the soil by osmosis.

Active transport: (a) Low to high concentration [1]. (b) Energy is needed from respiration [1]. (c) Mineral ions are absorbed into root hair cells from the soil.
This is the "if you can answer this, you own the chapter" question. 2 marks per process. If you got all 6, you're exam-ready on this topic.
