Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State two differences between sexual and asexual reproduction.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of: Sexual reproduction involves two parents, asexual involves one [1]. Sexual reproduction involves fertilisation (fusion of gametes), asexual does not [1]. Sexual produces genetically different offspring, asexual produces genetically identical offspring (clones) [1].
Give clear paired comparisons: "Sexual does X, asexual does Y." Don't just describe one type.

2
Name the parts of a flower that: (a) produce pollen, (b) receive pollen, (c) contain the ovules.
3 marks
Answer
(a) Anther [1]. (b) Stigma [1]. (c) Ovary [1].
These three are the most tested flower parts. Anther = male, produces pollen. Stigma = female, receives pollen. Ovary = contains ovules (which become seeds after fertilisation).

3
State the three conditions needed for a seed to germinate.
3 marks
Answer
Water [1], oxygen (for aerobic respiration) [1], and a suitable temperature (for enzyme activity) [1].
Light is NOT needed for germination — this is a very common wrong answer. Remember: WOT (Water, Oxygen, Temperature).

4
In the human female reproductive system, state the function of: (a) the ovary, (b) the oviduct, (c) the uterus.
3 marks
Answer
(a) The ovary produces ova (eggs) and the hormones oestrogen and progesterone [1].
(b) The oviduct (Fallopian tube) carries the ovum from the ovary towards the uterus; fertilisation usually occurs here [1].
(c) The uterus is where the embryo implants and develops during pregnancy [1].
For the oviduct, the fact that fertilisation occurs there is the most important point. Many students say "it connects the ovary to the uterus" — that's true but not enough for a mark.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
A grass flower and a convolvulus (morning glory) flower are both flowering plants, but they look very different. Explain two differences between an insect-pollinated flower and a wind-pollinated flower, and give a reason for each difference.
4 marks
Answer
1. Petals: Insect-pollinated flowers have large, brightly coloured, scented petals [1] to attract insects. Wind-pollinated flowers have small, dull petals because they do not need to attract insects [1].

2. Pollen: Insect-pollinated flowers produce small amounts of sticky, large pollen [1] so it sticks to insects' bodies. Wind-pollinated flowers produce huge amounts of light, smooth pollen [1] so it can be carried easily by the wind (more is needed because wind is less precise).
For each difference, you need the difference AND the reason. "Insect flowers have big petals" = 0 marks. "Insect flowers have big bright petals to attract insects" = 1 mark.

6
Explain the difference between pollination and fertilisation in a flowering plant. Describe the role of the pollen tube in connecting these two processes.
4 marks
Answer
Pollination is the transfer of pollen from an anther to a stigma [1]. Fertilisation is the fusion (joining) of the male gamete nucleus with the female gamete nucleus [1]. After pollination, the pollen grain grows a pollen tube down through the style towards the ovule [1]. The male gamete travels down this tube and enters the ovule through the micropyle to fuse with the female gamete [1].
The pollen tube is the bridge between pollination and fertilisation. Don't skip it — it's worth a mark every time this question appears.

7
Describe the role of the placenta during pregnancy. State two substances that pass from mother to fetus and one substance that passes from fetus to mother.
4 marks
Answer
The placenta is the site of exchange of materials between the mother's blood and the fetus's blood [1]. The mother's blood and fetal blood do not mix — they are separated by a thin membrane.

Mother → fetus: oxygen and glucose (or amino acids/nutrients) [1].
Fetus → mother: carbon dioxide (or urea) [1].

The placenta also provides physical attachment to the uterus wall and produces hormones to maintain pregnancy [1].
Always mention that the bloods do NOT mix. This is a very common exam point. Other acceptable substances: amino acids, antibodies (mother to fetus), urea (fetus to mother).

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Describe the role of the hormones oestrogen and progesterone in the menstrual cycle. Explain what happens to the uterus lining if the ovum is (a) not fertilised, and (b) fertilised.
6 marks
Answer
Oestrogen is produced by the ovary [1]. It causes the uterus lining to repair and thicken after menstruation, developing more blood vessels [1].

Progesterone is produced by the corpus luteum (in the ovary after ovulation) [1]. It maintains the thickened uterus lining, keeping it in good condition for implantation [1].

(a) Not fertilised: The corpus luteum breaks down, so progesterone levels fall. Without progesterone, the uterus lining breaks down and is shed as menstruation [1].

(b) Fertilised: The embryo implants in the uterus lining. Progesterone continues to be produced (first by the corpus luteum, later by the placenta), keeping the lining intact throughout pregnancy [1].
6 marks = 6 ideas. The key distinction: oestrogen BUILDS the lining, progesterone MAINTAINS it. If progesterone drops, the lining falls apart.

9
A fruit grower wants to produce many strawberry plants that are identical to one prize-winning plant. Explain why the grower should use asexual reproduction (runners) rather than growing from seeds. What is one risk of this approach?
5 marks
Answer
Asexual reproduction (using runners) produces offspring that are genetically identical (clones) to the parent plant [1]. This means all the new plants will have the same desirable characteristics as the prize-winning plant [1]. Seeds are produced by sexual reproduction, which involves variation — the offspring would be genetically different and may not have the desired features [1]. Asexual reproduction is also faster and requires only one parent [1].

Risk: Because all the plants are genetically identical, they are all susceptible to the same disease. If one plant is infected, all of them could be killed (as happened in the Irish Potato Famine) [1].
The key idea: clones = guaranteed identical traits BUT vulnerable to disease. The Potato Famine reference is always a strong exam example.

10
A pregnant woman asks why she should avoid smoking and drinking alcohol during pregnancy. Explain how these substances can affect the developing fetus, including the role of the placenta in your answer.
6 marks
Answer
The placenta acts as the site of exchange between the mother's blood and the fetus's blood [1]. Although the two bloods do not mix directly, some harmful substances can cross the placenta membrane [1].

Smoking: Nicotine crosses the placenta and can cause the baby to be born addicted [1]. Carbon monoxide in smoke reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, so less oxygen reaches the fetus, which can result in low birth weight [1].

Alcohol: Alcohol crosses the placenta and can damage the developing nervous system of the fetus [1]. This can cause long-term developmental problems [1].
6 marks: role of placenta (exchange site), substances cross it, nicotine effect, carbon monoxide + low birth weight, alcohol + nervous system damage. Each is a separate mark point.
