Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the two transport tissues found in plants and state what each transports.
2 marks
Answer
Xylem transports water and dissolved mineral ions [1]. Phloem transports sucrose (sugar) and amino acids [1].
Don't just say "food" for phloem — be specific: sucrose and amino acids. And don't say "nutrients" for xylem — it carries water and minerals.

2
Define transpiration.
2 marks
Answer
Transpiration is the loss of water vapour [1] from the leaves of a plant (through the stomata) [1].
Two key parts: (1) it's water vapour being lost, and (2) it's from the leaves/stomata. Saying "plants lose water" is too vague — specify "water vapour" and "from leaves."

3
By what process does water enter root hair cells from the soil?
1 mark
Answer
Osmosis [1].
Not "diffusion" and not "active transport." Water enters by osmosis because the soil solution has a higher water potential than the root hair cell cytoplasm.

State two environmental factors that increase the rate of transpiration.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of: high temperature [1], wind / moving air [1], low humidity [1], high light intensity [1].
All four increase transpiration. For 2 marks, just name any two clearly. Don't say "more sun" — say "high light intensity" or "high temperature" (these are different factors).

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain why root hair cells have many mitochondria.
3 marks
Answer
Root hair cells need to absorb mineral ions from the soil [1]. The concentration of minerals is often lower in the soil than inside the root cell, so the minerals must be taken up by active transport (against the concentration gradient) [1]. Active transport requires energy from respiration, which takes place in mitochondria [1].
The chain: minerals needed → concentration is lower outside → active transport required → active transport needs energy → energy from respiration → respiration happens in mitochondria. Each link is a mark.

6
Compare the structure and function of xylem and phloem tissue. Give two structural differences and one functional difference.
3 marks
Answer
Structural: Xylem cells are dead with no cytoplasm, while phloem cells are alive with thin cytoplasm [1]. Xylem walls are thickened with lignin for strength, while phloem has thin walls with sieve plates between cells [1].

Functional: Xylem transports water and minerals upward only (roots to leaves), while phloem transports sucrose and amino acids in both directions (from sources to sinks) [1].
Other acceptable structural differences: xylem has no end walls (hollow tubes), phloem has companion cells. For function, don't just say "different substances" — mention the direction too for the full mark.

7
Explain why wind increases the rate of transpiration.
3 marks
Answer
Water vapour diffuses out of the stomata and forms a layer of humid air around the leaf surface [1]. Wind blows this humid air away from the leaf [1], which increases / maintains a steep water potential gradient (concentration gradient) between the inside of the leaf and the outside air, so water vapour diffuses out faster [1].
The key idea is the concentration gradient. Without wind, humid air builds up around the leaf and slows diffusion. Wind removes it, keeping the gradient steep. Don't just say "wind dries the leaf" — explain the mechanism.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Describe the complete pathway of water from the soil, through the plant, and into the atmosphere. Name the processes involved at each stage.
6 marks
Answer
(1) Water enters root hair cells from the soil by osmosis because the soil solution has a higher water potential than the cell cytoplasm [1].
(2) Water moves across the root cortex from cell to cell by osmosis until it reaches the xylem [1].
(3) Water is pulled up through the xylem vessels in the stem as a continuous column, held together by cohesion between water molecules and pulled upward by tension caused by water loss from the leaves [1].
(4) Water leaves the xylem and enters the mesophyll cells of the leaf [1].
(5) Water evaporates from the surfaces of the spongy mesophyll cells into the air spaces inside the leaf [1].
(6) Water vapour diffuses out of the leaf through the stomata into the atmosphere — this is transpiration [1].
Six stages, six marks. The key processes to name: osmosis (into root), osmosis (across cortex), cohesion-tension (up xylem), evaporation (in leaf), diffusion (out through stomata). Missing any named process costs you a mark.

9
A student uses a potometer to measure water uptake by a plant shoot under different conditions. In condition A (bright light, wind), the bubble moved 10 cm in 2 minutes. In condition B (dark, still air, high humidity), the bubble moved 10 cm in 50 minutes. Explain the difference in results.
5 marks
Answer
In condition A, bright light causes the stomata to open (to allow CO₂ in for photosynthesis), so water vapour can escape more easily [1]. Wind removes the layer of humid air from around the leaf surface, maintaining a steep concentration gradient for water vapour, so transpiration is faster [1].

In condition B, in the dark the stomata are closed (no photosynthesis needed), so very little water vapour can escape [1]. Still air allows a layer of humid air to build up around the leaf, reducing the gradient [1]. High humidity in the atmosphere means there is a smaller difference in water vapour concentration between the leaf and the air, further reducing the rate of transpiration [1].

Since the potometer measures water uptake, and most water taken up is lost by transpiration, faster transpiration in A means faster water uptake and faster bubble movement.
Five marks: light opens stomata (+), wind removes humid air (+), dark closes stomata (-), still air = humid layer (-), high humidity = reduced gradient (-). One mark per explained factor. Always explain HOW the factor affects transpiration, not just state that it does.

10
A cactus lives in a hot, dry desert environment. Describe three adaptations of the cactus that help it survive in these conditions. For each adaptation, explain how it helps the plant reduce water loss.
6 marks
Answer
(1) Leaves are reduced to spines [1] — this greatly reduces the surface area from which water can be lost by transpiration [1].

(2) The stem is green and fleshy — it carries out photosynthesis instead of leaves [1], and it also stores water for use during droughts [1].

(3) Stomata are sunk into grooves or pits in the stem surface [1] — this traps a layer of humid air near the stomata, reducing the water potential gradient and therefore reducing the rate of transpiration [1].
For 6 marks: 3 adaptations x 2 marks each (one for naming the adaptation, one for explaining how it reduces water loss). Other valid points: thick waxy cuticle (reduces evaporation from stem surface), deep roots (reach water far underground), shallow spreading roots (catch lightest rainfall).
