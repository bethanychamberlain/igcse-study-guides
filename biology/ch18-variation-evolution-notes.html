Section 18.1 Variation
Living organisms differ from one another. These differences are called variations, and they come in two types.
W
Write This Down — Two Types of Variation
Feature Discontinuous Variation Continuous Variation
Definition Clear-cut categories with NO intermediates A range of values with intermediates between extremes
Type of data Qualitative (cannot be measured) Quantitative (can be measured)
Caused by Genes only Genes AND environment
Graph type Bar chart (distinct bars, no smooth curve) Smooth bell curve (normal distribution)
Examples Blood group (A, B, AB, O), sex (male/female) Height, body mass, hand span, chest circumference
!
Don't Confuse — Continuous vs Discontinuous

A simple test: if you can measure it with a number, it's continuous. Height = 165 cm (continuous). Blood group = A (you can't be "halfway between A and B" — discontinuous). The exam will give you a graph and ask you to identify the type — bar chart = discontinuous, smooth curve = continuous.

W
Write This Down — Phenotype Equation

Phenotype = Genotype + Effects of the Environment

This means your observable characteristics come from BOTH your genes AND your environment. A plant may have the genes for chlorophyll, but without light it won't turn green. A person may have genes for a tall build, but without adequate nutrition they won't reach their full height.

T
Study Tip

Write the phenotype equation on a sticky note and put it where you'll see it. It comes up in so many exam questions. If they ask "Why do identical twins raised apart look slightly different?" — it's because they have the same genotype but different environments.

  • I can define continuous and discontinuous variation
  • I can give two examples of each type
  • I know which type is genes-only vs genes-and-environment
  • I can write the phenotype equation from memory
Section 18.2 Causes of Variation
Variation can be caused by mutations in DNA or by the mixing of genes during sexual reproduction.
W
Write This Down — Mutation

Mutation = a change in a gene or chromosome. Mutations can arise from:

• Mistakes in DNA copying during cell division
• Damage from environmental factors (radiation, chemicals)

Two types:
• Gene mutation = change in the base sequence of DNA on one chromosome. Can produce a defective protein or no protein. E.g. sickle cell anaemia (one base change in the haemoglobin gene).
• Chromosome mutation = when cell division fails to distribute chromosomes correctly (e.g. wrong number in gametes).

W
Write This Down — Mutagens

Mutagens = factors that increase the rate of mutation:

• Radiation — gamma rays, ultraviolet light, X-rays
• Chemicals — tars in tobacco smoke, some preservatives, certain plant hormones

A mutagen that causes uncontrolled cell division (cancer) is called a carcinogen.

?
Why This Matters

Not all mutations are harmful. Some are beneficial — they help the organism survive better. Sickle cell is harmful as a homozygote but helpful as a heterozygote (malaria resistance). Beneficial mutations are the raw material for evolution by natural selection.

W
Write This Down — Sexual Reproduction Creates Variation

Sexual reproduction mixes genes in three ways:

• Crossing over — homologous chromosomes exchange segments during meiosis, creating new allele combinations
• Independent assortment — homologous pairs line up randomly at meiosis, so different combinations end up in different gametes
• Random fertilisation — any male gamete can fuse with any female gamete

Together, these three mechanisms make it virtually impossible for any two gametes (except in identical twins) to be the same.

T
Study Tip — Link Back to Chapter 17

This section connects directly to meiosis (17.4). The three sources of variation from sexual reproduction all happen because of meiosis and fertilisation. If you know meiosis well, this section follows naturally.

  • I can define mutation and give an example
  • I know the difference between gene and chromosome mutations
  • I can name two mutagens and define carcinogen
  • I can explain three ways sexual reproduction creates variation
Section 18.3 Adaptation and Darwin
Some organisms are better suited to their environment than others. This is called adaptation, and it's the foundation of evolution.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Adaptation = the way an organism is suited to survive in its environment. An organism well suited to its environment is said to show high fitness.

Adaptive feature = an inherited functional feature that increases an organism's fitness (its chance of surviving and reproducing).

Evolution = the change in a species over time through natural selection.

W
Write This Down — Examples of Adaptation

Polar bear (adapted to cold, arctic conditions):
• White fur = camouflage for hunting + less heat radiation
• Small ears/tail = less surface area for heat loss
• Large body = more cells to generate heat
• Thick fat layer = insulation + food store
• Padded feet = insulation + grip on ice

Camel (adapted to hot, dry desert):
• Fat stored in hump (not under skin) = body can radiate heat
• Thin fur = allows heat loss
• Closable nostrils = keeps sand out
• Large feet with membrane = walk on sand without sinking
• Can drink 100 litres in 10 minutes when water is available

W
Write This Down — Charles Darwin

Charles Darwin (1809-1882) was a British naturalist who sailed on HMS Beagle (1831). His key observations were made on the Galapagos Islands, where he noticed that finches on different islands had different beaks adapted to different food sources, but all seemed related to a common ancestor.

Darwin proposed that species are not fixed — they change over time through natural selection. He published this in The Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection.

!
Don't Confuse — Darwin vs Lamarck

Lamarck thought the environment caused the variations (e.g. giraffes stretched their necks, then passed on longer necks). Darwin said variations arise by chance, and the environment selects the best ones. Lamarck's idea was wrong — acquired characteristics cannot be inherited.

  • I can define adaptation, adaptive feature, and fitness
  • I can describe 3+ adaptations of the polar bear OR camel
  • I know who Darwin was and what he observed on the Galapagos
  • I know the key difference between Darwin and Lamarck
Section 18.4 Natural Selection
Natural selection is the mechanism that drives evolution. It explains how populations change over time.
W
Write This Down — Natural Selection Step by Step

Learn these five steps in order:

  1. Overproduction — organisms produce more offspring than can possibly survive
  2. Variation — within the population, individuals have different characteristics (due to genetic differences)
  3. Struggle for existence — organisms compete for limited resources (food, space, mates)
  4. Survival of the fittest — individuals with characteristics best suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce
  5. Advantageous characteristics passed on — the well-adapted individuals breed and pass their genes to the next generation; over time, these characteristics become more common in the population
?
Why This Matters — Antibiotic Resistance

Antibiotic-resistant bacteria are a real-world example of natural selection happening right now. Some bacteria have a random mutation making them resistant. When antibiotics are used, non-resistant bacteria die, but resistant ones survive and reproduce. Over time, the whole population becomes resistant. This is why "superbugs" like MRSA are such a problem in hospitals.

W
Write This Down — How New Species Form

If two populations of the same species are separated (e.g. by a mountain range), they experience different selection pressures. Over many generations, they accumulate so many different adaptations that they can no longer interbreed. They have become separate species.

Example: Two populations of antelopes separated by mountains. One side has food on high branches (tall antelopes favoured), the other has predators (small, fast antelopes favoured). Eventually they become different species.

T
Study Tip — The 5-Step Chain

Many 5-6 mark questions ask you to "Explain how natural selection leads to evolution." Each of the five steps above is worth one mark. Memorise them as a chain: Over-produce → Vary → Struggle → Survive → Pass on. Practice writing all five from memory.

  • I can list all 5 steps of natural selection from memory
  • I can explain antibiotic resistance as an example of natural selection
  • I can explain how two populations can become separate species
Section 18.5 Artificial Selection
Humans have been selecting organisms with desirable traits for thousands of years. This is artificial selection.
W
Write This Down — Artificial Selection

Artificial selection (selective breeding) = when humans choose organisms with desirable characteristics and allow only those to breed.

Examples:
• Jersey cattle — bred for high cream content in milk
• Dogs — all breeds are the same species, bred for different purposes (hunting, guarding, companionship)
• Wheat — bred so all stems are the same height (easier to harvest) and ears separate easily from the stalk
• Wild mustard → selectively bred into cabbage, cauliflower, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, kale, kohlrabi

W
Write This Down — Natural vs Artificial Selection
Feature Natural Selection Artificial Selection
Agent of selection The natural environment Humans
Speed Slow (many generations) Much quicker
Advantage to organism Helps it survive in the wild May NOT help it survive in the wild (e.g. a Chihuahua)
?
Why This Matters — Maintaining Variation

Artificial selection can reduce genetic variation (all individuals become very similar). This is dangerous because if a new disease appears, the whole population could be wiped out. This is why rare-breed centres and seed banks exist — to preserve genetic diversity as a safety net for the future.

!
Don't Confuse — Selective Breeding vs Genetic Engineering

Selective breeding uses traditional breeding (meiosis + fertilisation) to combine desired traits. Genetic engineering directly changes DNA by inserting genes from another organism. They are different processes. If the question says "selective breeding" or "artificial selection," do NOT write about cutting and pasting genes.

T
Study Tip — Exam Practice

A very common question format: "Merino sheep have thicker wool than wild sheep. Explain how artificial selection could have produced Merino sheep." Just follow the steps: (1) choose sheep with thickest wool, (2) breed them together, (3) from offspring, select those with thickest wool, (4) repeat over many generations. Simple process, easy marks.

  • I can define artificial selection
  • I can give 3 examples of artificial selection
  • I can state 3 differences between natural and artificial selection
  • I know why maintaining genetic variation is important
  • I can describe the steps a farmer would use to selectively breed for a trait
