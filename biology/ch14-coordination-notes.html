Sections 14.1 – 14.2 Homeostasis, Negative Feedback, and Temperature Control
Your body keeps its internal conditions constant so your cells can work properly. This is homeostasis — and it underpins everything else in this chapter.
W
Write This Down — Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a constant internal environment.

Cells are surrounded by tissue fluid. Conditions in the tissue fluid (temperature, glucose, water, pH) must be kept at optimum levels for cells to function. Multiple organs are involved:

• Skin — controls heat exchange
• Kidneys — control water and salt levels (osmoregulation) and remove urea (excretion)
• Liver — regulates levels of many solutes in the blood
• Lungs — regulate CO₂ and O₂ exchange
• Pancreas — controls blood glucose

W
Write This Down — Negative Feedback

Negative feedback is when a change from the normal level (set point) is detected, and a response is triggered that cancels out the change and returns the factor to normal.

The pattern is always: Deviation detected → Control centre triggers response → Response reverses the change → Return to set point.

?
Why This Matters

"Explain negative feedback" is one of the most frequently asked questions across the whole IGCSE Biology exam. Every example in this chapter (temperature, blood glucose, water balance) follows this same pattern. Learn the pattern once, apply it everywhere.

W
Write This Down — Temperature Control

The hypothalamus in the brain is the temperature control centre. Normal body temperature is about 37°C.

If body temperature RISES (too hot):
• Vasodilation — arterioles near the skin surface dilate (widen), more blood flows near the surface, more heat is lost by radiation [1]
• Sweating — sweat glands produce sweat, which evaporates from the skin surface, taking heat energy with it [1]
• Hair erector muscles relax — hairs lie flat, less insulating air trapped

If body temperature FALLS (too cold):
• Vasoconstriction — arterioles near the skin surface constrict (narrow), less blood flows near surface, less heat lost by radiation
• Shivering — muscles contract rapidly, generating heat from respiration
• Hair erector muscles contract — hairs stand up, trapping a layer of insulating air
• Less sweating

!
Don't Confuse — Vasodilation vs Vasoconstriction

Vasodilation = blood vessels dilate (widen) → more blood near skin → more heat lost. Think: "dilate = wide = heat escapes."
Vasoconstriction = blood vessels constrict (narrow) → less blood near skin → less heat lost. Think: "constrict = tight = heat stays in."
Also: the blood vessels do NOT "move closer to" or "further from" the skin. The arterioles change diameter — the capillaries stay in the same place.

T
Study Tip

Draw a simple feedback loop for temperature: "Temperature rises → Hypothalamus detects → Sweating + vasodilation → Temperature falls back to 37°C." Then draw the opposite loop for when temperature falls. Being able to draw these loops from memory is the fastest way to answer exam questions on this topic.

  • I can define homeostasis
  • I can explain negative feedback in my own words
  • I can describe 3 responses when the body is too hot
  • I can describe 3 responses when the body is too cold
  • I know the difference between vasodilation and vasoconstriction
Section 14.3 Control of Blood Glucose
Blood glucose must be kept at about 1 mg per cm³. Too high or too low, and cells can't function properly. Two hormones from the pancreas control this.
W
Write This Down — Insulin and Glucagon

Insulin is released by the pancreas when blood glucose is too high. It causes the liver to convert glucose → glycogen (storage form). This lowers blood glucose back to normal.

Glucagon is released by the pancreas when blood glucose is too low. It causes the liver to convert glycogen → glucose. This raises blood glucose back to normal.

This is negative feedback: the change (high or low glucose) triggers a response that reverses the change.

!
Don't Confuse — The Big Three Mix-Ups

1. Glycogen is NOT glucagon. Glycogen is the storage molecule (a polysaccharide). Glucagon is the hormone. They sound similar but are completely different things.

2. Insulin LOWERS glucose. Glucagon RAISES glucose. Remember: "insulin = glucose goes into storage."

3. The pancreas makes BOTH hormones. Not just insulin. The exam often asks what happens when glucose is too low — the answer is glucagon, not "less insulin."

W
Write This Down — Diabetes

Type I diabetes: The pancreas fails to produce enough insulin. Blood glucose stays too high. Symptoms: excessive thirst, hunger, urine production. Treated by regular injection of insulin.

Type II diabetes: Often linked to diet (too much fat and sugar) and obesity. The body's cells stop responding properly to insulin. Can be controlled by adjusting diet — no injection needed.

If glucose appears in urine, it can be detected using Clinistix (a test strip with an enzyme and a dye).

?
Why This Matters

Blood glucose control is the most common example of negative feedback they test on the exam. If the question says "use the principle of negative feedback to explain how blood glucose is controlled," you need to describe BOTH directions: what happens when glucose is too high AND too low. Give both hormones, both conversions, both organs.

  • I can state the roles of insulin and glucagon
  • I know the difference between glycogen and glucagon
  • I can explain blood glucose control as negative feedback
  • I can describe Type I and Type II diabetes and their treatments
Sections 14.4 – 14.6 The Nervous System, Neurones, and Reflex Arcs
The nervous system is one of two coordination systems in the body. It's the fast one — electrical impulses travelling along nerves in milliseconds.
W
Write This Down — The Nervous System

The central nervous system (CNS) = brain + spinal cord.

The peripheral nervous system = all the nerves connecting the CNS to the rest of the body.

How information flows:
Stimulus → Receptor (detects the change) → Sensory neurone (carries impulse to CNS) → CNS (processes information) → Motor neurone (carries impulse to effector) → Effector (muscle or gland that carries out the response) → Response

W
Write This Down — Neurone Structure

A neurone (nerve cell) has:
• Cell body — contains the nucleus, controls the cell's metabolism
• Dendrites — short branches that collect information from other cells
• Axon — a long fibre that carries the electrical impulse away from the cell body
• Myelin sheath — fatty insulation around the axon that speeds up impulse transmission

Messages pass as electrical impulses (action potentials). At a synapse (the gap between two neurones), a chemical called a neurotransmitter is released, diffuses across the gap, and triggers a new impulse in the next neurone. Synapses ensure impulses travel in one direction only.

W
Write This Down — The Reflex Arc

A reflex action is a rapid, automatic response to a stimulus. It does NOT involve conscious thought — it's faster because the impulse goes through the spinal cord, not the brain.

The reflex arc pathway:
Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory neurone → Relay neurone (in spinal cord) → Motor neurone → Effector (muscle) → Response

Examples: withdrawing hand from hot object, knee-jerk reflex, pupil reflex, coughing, swallowing.

Voluntary actions involve the brain making a conscious decision. Involuntary actions (reflexes) are automatic — no choice involved.

!
Don't Confuse — Three Types of Neurone

Sensory neurone = carries impulse FROM receptor TO CNS. Cell body is on the side, not at the end.
Relay neurone (connector neurone) = carries impulse ACROSS the spinal cord. Short, no myelin sheath.
Motor neurone = carries impulse FROM CNS TO effector. Cell body is at one end.

The exam loves asking you to label these on a diagram. Know which direction each one carries impulses.

T
Study Tip — Draw the Reflex Arc

Draw a hand touching a hot plate. Label: receptor in skin, sensory neurone, relay neurone in spinal cord, motor neurone, effector (muscle in arm). Draw arrows showing the direction of the impulse. Add synapses between the neurones. This single diagram could be worth 5+ marks.

  • I can describe the pathway: stimulus → receptor → coordinator → effector → response
  • I can name and describe the three types of neurone
  • I can describe how a synapse works
  • I can draw and label a reflex arc
  • I know the difference between voluntary and involuntary actions
Section 14.7 The Eye as a Sense Organ
The eye is the most complex sense organ you need to know. It contains receptors (rods and cones), systems for focusing light (lens, cornea), and muscles for controlling light intensity (iris).
W
Write This Down — Eye Structure

Cornea — transparent front layer; does most of the refraction (bending) of light
Iris — the coloured part; contains muscles that control pupil size
Pupil — the hole that lets light in; appears black
Lens — fine-focuses light onto the retina; can change shape
Ciliary muscles — change the shape of the lens
Suspensory ligaments — connect ciliary muscles to the lens
Retina — the light-sensitive layer at the back; contains rods and cones
Optic nerve — carries nerve impulses from the retina to the brain
Blind spot — where the optic nerve exits; no light-sensitive cells here

W
Write This Down — Accommodation (Focusing)

Distant object:
• Light rays are nearly parallel — less refraction needed
• Ciliary muscles relax → suspensory ligaments pull tight → lens is pulled long and thin

Close object:
• Light rays are diverging — more refraction needed
• Ciliary muscles contract → suspensory ligaments go slack → lens becomes short and fat (more powerful)

W
Write This Down — Pupil Reflex and Rods vs Cones

Pupil reflex (bright light): Circular muscles of iris contract, radial muscles relax → pupil gets smaller → protects retina from damage.

Pupil reflex (dim light): Radial muscles contract, circular muscles relax → pupil gets larger → lets more light in.

Rod cells: Sensitive to low light intensity. Give black-and-white vision. Packed around the edges of the retina. Good for night vision but low detail.

Cone cells: Need bright light. Give colour vision (three types: red, green, blue). Packed at the centre of the retina (fovea). Give sharp detail.

!
Don't Confuse — Accommodation

Students often get the ciliary muscle/ligament relationship backwards. Remember: when ciliary muscles contract, they pull inwards, which makes the ligaments go slack, which lets the lens spring into a fat shape. It's counterintuitive — muscles contracting makes ligaments loose. Draw it to convince yourself.

  • I can label a diagram of the eye
  • I can describe accommodation for near and distant objects
  • I can describe the pupil reflex in bright and dim light
  • I know the difference between rods and cones
Sections 14.8 – 14.9 The Endocrine System and Plant Tropisms
The endocrine system is the body's second coordination system — slower than nerves, but with longer-lasting effects. Plants use hormones too, but for growth responses.
W
Write This Down — Nervous vs Endocrine Comparison
Feature Nervous System Endocrine System
Speed Very rapid Can be slow
Nature of message Electrical impulses along nerves Chemical messengers (hormones) in the bloodstream
Duration Short-lived (seconds) Long-lasting (can be years)
Area of response Localised (one area) Widespread (many organs)
Examples Reflexes, blinking, limb movement Growth, puberty, blood glucose control
W
Write This Down — Key Hormones and Glands

Hormones are chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands (ductless glands) directly into the bloodstream. They travel in the blood and only affect target organs that have the correct receptors.

Key glands and hormones:
• Pancreas → insulin and glucagon (blood glucose control)
• Adrenal glands → adrenaline (fight or flight response)
• Ovaries → oestrogen and progesterone (female puberty, menstrual cycle)
• Testes → testosterone (male puberty)

Adrenaline (the "fight or flight" hormone) causes: faster heart rate, deeper breathing, blood diverted from gut to muscles, glycogen converted to glucose. It prepares the body for action.

?
Why This Matters

The "compare nervous and endocrine systems" table is a guaranteed exam question — it appears almost every year. Learn it cold. Also: hormone feedback control uses the same negative feedback principle as temperature and glucose control. It's the same pattern everywhere.

W
Write This Down — Plant Tropisms

A tropism is a growth response of a plant towards or away from a stimulus.

Phototropism = response to light
• Shoots are positively phototropic (grow towards light)
• Roots are negatively phototropic (grow away from light)

Gravitropism = response to gravity
• Roots are positively gravitropic (grow towards gravity, downwards)
• Shoots are negatively gravitropic (grow away from gravity, upwards)

How it works — Auxin:
• Auxin is a plant growth substance produced at the tip of a shoot
• When light comes from one side, auxin moves to the shaded (dark) side
• Auxin stimulates cell elongation in shoots → the dark side grows faster → shoot bends towards the light
• In roots, auxin inhibits cell elongation → the side with more auxin grows less → root bends downwards

!
Don't Confuse — Auxin's Opposite Effects

Auxin does opposite things in shoots and roots. In shoots, more auxin = more growth. In roots, more auxin = less growth. This is why the same hormone makes a shoot bend towards light but a root bend away from it. The exam loves testing whether you understand this difference.

W
Write This Down — Commercial Uses of Plant Hormones

• Rooting powder — auxin stimulates cuttings to grow roots, allowing plant cloning
• Weedkillers — high concentrations of synthetic auxin (2,4-D) disrupt weed growth
• Seedless fruits — hormone sprays can make fruit develop without fertilisation
• Synchronised fruiting — hormone sprays make all fruits ripen at the same time for efficient harvesting
