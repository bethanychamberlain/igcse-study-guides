Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Organic molecule A molecule that contains carbon and hydrogen. All four groups of biological molecules are organic. —
Monosaccharide Simplest carbohydrate. One sugar unit. Example: glucose. Soluble. Polysaccharide (many sugar units joined together — insoluble)
Polysaccharide Many monosaccharides joined in a chain. Examples: starch, glycogen, cellulose. Insoluble. Monosaccharide (single sugar unit — soluble)
Lipid A fat or oil. Made of glycerol + 3 fatty acids. Insoluble in water. Great energy store. Carbohydrate (also C, H, O, but different structure and test)
Amino acid The subunit (building block) of proteins. 20 different kinds exist. Fatty acid (building block of lipids, not proteins)
Condensation Reaction that joins small molecules together. Water is removed (produced). Hydrolysis (the opposite — breaks apart, adds water)
Hydrolysis Reaction that breaks a large molecule into smaller pieces. Water is added (used). Condensation (the opposite — joins together, removes water)
Benedict's reagent Blue liquid used to test for glucose. Must heat. Goes orange/brick-red if positive. Biuret reagent (also blue, but tests for PROTEIN, no heating, goes purple)
Iodine solution Brown liquid. Test for starch. Goes blue-black if positive. No heating needed. —
DCPIP Blue dye used to test for vitamin C. Vitamin C decolourises it (blue disappears). —

Exam Trap — Benedict's vs Biuret
Both start blue. Both start with "B." But they test for different things. Benedict's = glucose, needs heating, goes orange-red. Biuret = protein, no heating, goes purple.

Say It in One Sentence

Glucose The fuel your cells burn for energy — small and soluble so it dissolves in blood and reaches every cell.
Starch How plants save glucose for later — insoluble so it stays put and doesn't dissolve away.
Glycogen How animals save glucose for later — stored in liver and muscles, same idea as starch but for us.
Lipids Pack more energy per gram than carbs, plus they're waterproof — perfect for long-term storage and membranes.
Proteins Chains of amino acids folded into specific shapes — the shape determines the job (enzyme, antibody, keratin).
Condensation Connecting small molecules together like Lego bricks — water pops out as a by-product.
Hydrolysis Snapping Lego bricks apart — water goes in to break the bond ("hydro" = water, "lysis" = splitting).

Memory Tricks

Only Benedict's Needs Heat
Out of all five food tests, only Benedict's requires heating. The rest you just add and shake. If you forget to write "heat in a water bath" for Benedict's, you lose a mark.
G + 3F = Lipid
A lipid = Glycerol + 3 Fatty acids. Just saying "fat" gets zero marks on the exam. You must name both building blocks.
CON-densation = CON-necting
Condensation = connecting molecules (water comes OUT). Hydrolysis = hydro (water) + lysis (splitting) = water goes IN to break bonds. They're exact opposites.
Proteins Have Nitrogen — The Others Don't
Carbs and lipids = C, H, O only. Proteins add N (nitrogen). This is how the biuret test works — it detects the nitrogen. If the exam asks "Which element is in proteins but not carbohydrates?", the answer is nitrogen.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Test this solution for glucose" Add Benedict's, heat in water bath. Positive = orange/brick-red
"Test for starch" Add iodine. Positive = blue-black (no heating)
"Test for protein" Add biuret reagent. Positive = mauve/purple (no heating)
"Test for lipid/fat" Shake with ethanol, pour into water. Positive = milky-white emulsion
"Which element is in protein but not carbohydrate?" Nitrogen (N)
"Describe the structure of a lipid" One molecule of glycerol joined to three fatty acids
"What is hydrolysis?" Breaking a large molecule into smaller ones using water
"Why is a negative control needed?" To prove the reagent/equipment is not contaminated. Use distilled water as the control
