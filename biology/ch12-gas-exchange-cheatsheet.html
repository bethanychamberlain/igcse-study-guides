Gas Exchange

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Gas exchange The swapping of O₂ and CO₂ between an organism and its environment, across a gas exchange surface. Respiration (chemical reactions inside cells) or breathing (physical air movement)
Alveoli Tiny air sacs at the end of bronchioles where gas exchange happens. Walls are one cell thick. Bronchioles (those are the tubes leading TO the alveoli)
Trachea The windpipe. Kept open by C-shaped rings of cartilage. Carries air to the bronchi. —
Bronchi Two tubes (one to each lung) that branch off from the trachea. Bronchioles (smaller branches of the bronchi)
Diaphragm A sheet of muscle below the lungs. Contracts (flattens) during inhalation, relaxes (domes up) during exhalation. —
Haemoglobin Protein in red blood cells that picks up O₂ to form oxyhaemoglobin. Carboxyhaemoglobin (haemoglobin + carbon monoxide — that's the bad one from smoking)
Goblet cells Cells that produce sticky mucus to trap dust, bacteria, and particles in the airways. Ciliated cells (those have cilia that sweep the mucus away)
Cilia Tiny hair-like structures on ciliated cells that beat in a wave to sweep mucus up and away from the lungs. Goblet cells (those make the mucus; cilia move it)
Tar Sticky substance in cigarette smoke. Contains carcinogens. Damages cilia and coats alveoli. Nicotine (that's the addictive chemical, not the cancer-causing one)
Carbon monoxide Gas in cigarette smoke that binds to haemoglobin more strongly than O₂, reducing blood's O₂-carrying capacity. Carbon dioxide (CO₂ is a normal waste product; CO is the toxic one from smoke)
Emphysema Disease where alveolar walls are destroyed, reducing surface area for gas exchange. Causes breathlessness. Bronchitis (that's inflammation of the bronchi with excess mucus)
Epidemiology The study of patterns of disease in populations — used to find links between lifestyle and illness. —

Breathing In vs Breathing Out

Inhalation (Breathing In) Exhalation (Breathing Out)
External intercostals Contract Relax
Internal intercostals Relax Contract (forced breathing only)
Rib cage Moves up and out Moves down and in
Diaphragm Contracts (flattens / moves down) Relaxes (domes up)
Chest volume Increases Decreases
Lung pressure Decreases (below atmospheric) Increases (above atmospheric)
Air movement Air rushes IN Air pushed OUT
Memory Trick — The Syringe
Think of a syringe: pull the plunger out = volume up = pressure drops = air rushes in. Push the plunger in = volume down = pressure rises = air pushed out. That's exactly what the rib cage and diaphragm do.

Inhaled Air vs Exhaled Air

Gas Inhaled Exhaled Why?
Oxygen 21% ~18% O₂ absorbed into blood at alveoli
Carbon dioxide 0.04% ~3–4% CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli
Nitrogen 78% 78% Not used by the body
Water vapour Variable Saturated Water evaporates from moist alveolar lining

Say It in One Sentence

Gas exchange O₂ and CO₂ swapping across a surface by diffusion — not the same as breathing or respiration.
Alveoli Millions of tiny air sacs with thin walls, huge surface area, moist lining, and rich blood supply — perfect for gas exchange.
Air pathway Nose → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli.
Goblet + cilia Goblet cells make mucus to trap dirt; cilia sweep it up and out — your airways' cleaning crew.
Carbon monoxide Binds to haemoglobin 200x more strongly than O₂ — the sneaky damage from smoking that your lungs can't fix.
Emphysema Alveoli walls destroyed by smoking → less surface area → can't get enough O₂ → constant breathlessness.
Correlation ≠ causation A link between smoking and cancer doesn't automatically prove cause — but the evidence is so strong that scientists accept it.

Memory Tricks

5 Features of a Good Gas Exchange Surface
Thin, Large area, Moist, Blood supply, Ventilated. Think "TaLk More Before Vaping" — ironic for a chapter about lung damage.
Airway Order
Nose → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli. "Never Let The Big Boy Ask" — silly, but it sticks.
Smoking's Three Villains
Tar = Tumours (cancer). Nicotine = Need it (addiction). CO = Can't carry Oxygen. Each letter matches the harm.
Diaphragm Direction
Breathe IN = diaphragm goes DOWN (contracts flat). Think "IN = DOWN" (like you're pulling air down into your body).

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Describe features that make alveoli efficient for gas exchange" Thin walls (short diffusion distance), large surface area (millions of alveoli), moist, good blood supply, well ventilated
"Describe how oxygen gets from the alveolus into the blood" O₂ diffuses down its concentration gradient from alveolar air (high) into blood (low), then binds to haemoglobin forming oxyhaemoglobin
"What happens during inhalation?" External intercostals contract, ribs move up and out, diaphragm contracts and flattens, volume increases, pressure drops, air rushes in
"Why does exhaled air have more CO₂ than inhaled air?" CO₂ is produced by respiration in cells, carried in the blood, and diffuses from blood into the alveoli
"How does carbon monoxide in cigarette smoke affect the blood?" CO binds to haemoglobin forming carboxyhaemoglobin, reducing the blood's ability to carry oxygen; the heart must work harder
"Explain how smoking causes emphysema" Tar/smoke damages and destroys alveolar walls, reducing surface area for gas exchange → less O₂ enters blood → breathlessness
"What is the role of goblet cells and cilia?" Goblet cells produce mucus that traps particles; cilia beat to sweep mucus up and out of the airways
"Why can't we prove smoking causes cancer with a lab experiment?" It would be unethical to deliberately expose humans to a known harmful substance. We use epidemiological studies instead.
