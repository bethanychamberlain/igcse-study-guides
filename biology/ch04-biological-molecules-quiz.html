Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the four main groups of organic molecules found in living organisms.
[2]
Answer
Carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids [1 for any two correct, 2 for all four].
Don't write "fats" instead of "lipids" — lipids is the correct scientific term that covers both fats and oils.

What is the difference between a fat and an oil?
[1]
Answer
A fat is solid at room temperature, while an oil is liquid at room temperature [1].
Both are lipids. The only difference the exam cares about is their state at room temperature.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain procedures and reasons, not just name things.
5
Describe the complete procedure for testing a solution to find out if it contains glucose. Include the reagent, method, and expected result.
[4]
Answer
Add 2 cm³ of Benedict's reagent [1] to 2 cm³ of the unknown solution. Heat the mixture in a boiling water bath for 2–3 minutes [1]. If glucose is present, the colour changes from blue to orange/brick-red [1]. If glucose is absent, the solution remains blue [1].
Four marks, four things: (1) name the reagent, (2) say you must heat it, (3) give the positive colour, (4) give the negative colour. Forgetting "heat" is the most common lost mark.

6
Explain why the test for lipids uses ethanol rather than just adding a reagent to the food sample in water.
[3]
Answer
Lipids are insoluble in water [1], so you cannot make an aqueous (water-based) solution of a lipid to test [1]. Ethanol is used because lipids dissolve in ethanol. When the ethanol solution is then poured into water, the lipid comes out of solution and forms a milky-white emulsion [1].
The key logic: lipids won't dissolve in water, so you need ethanol to dissolve them first. Then adding water forces the lipid back out as visible droplets. This "why ethanol?" question appears regularly on exams.

7
Explain the difference between condensation and hydrolysis. Give one example of each.
[4]
Answer
Condensation: Small molecules are joined together to form a larger molecule, and water is produced/removed [1]. Example: glucose molecules joined to form starch (or glycogen) [1].

Hydrolysis: A large molecule is broken down into smaller molecules, and water is used/added [1]. Example: starch broken down into glucose molecules (during digestion) [1].
These are opposites. Condensation builds up (water out). Hydrolysis breaks down (water in). The example doesn't need to be complex — starch/glucose works perfectly for both.

Advanced — Can You Apply It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need you to interpret data, link ideas together, and write clearly. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
A student tests five unknown solutions (A–E) with iodine solution, Benedict's reagent, and biuret reagent. The results are shown below.

Solution Iodine Benedict's (heated) Biuret
ABlue-blackStayed blueStayed blue
BStayed yellowOrangeStayed blue
CStayed yellowStayed bluePurple
DBlue-blackStayed bluePurple
EStayed yellowOrangePurple

(a) Which solution contains only starch?
(b) Which solution contains both starch and protein?
(c) Which solution could be milk? Explain your reasoning.
[5]
Answer
(a) Solution A — positive for iodine (blue-black = starch present), negative for Benedict's and biuret [1].

(b) Solution D — positive for iodine (blue-black = starch) AND positive for biuret (purple = protein) [1].

(c) Solution E could be milk [1]. Milk contains glucose/lactose (a reducing sugar, shown by the orange result with Benedict's) [1] and protein (shown by the purple result with biuret) [1]. Milk does not contain starch, which matches the negative iodine result.
This is the type of data-interpretation question the exam loves. Work through each test result systematically: blue-black = starch, orange = glucose, purple = protein. Then match to what you know about real foods.

9
A student wants to compare the vitamin C content of orange juice and lemon juice using DCPIP. Describe the method she should use, and explain how she should make this a fair test.
[5]
Answer
Method: Place the same volume of DCPIP solution into two test tubes [1]. Add drops of orange juice to one tube and drops of lemon juice to the other, one drop at a time, counting the drops [1]. The juice that needs fewer drops to decolourise (remove the blue colour from) the DCPIP contains more vitamin C [1].

Fair test: She must use the same volume of DCPIP in each tube [1] and the same drop size for both juices. The test should be done at the same temperature [1].
Fair test = keep everything the same except the one thing you're comparing (which juice). Same volume DCPIP, same drop size, same temperature. The result is measured by counting drops, not just looking at a colour.

10
A rat eats maize (corn), which contains starch. Explain how the starch in maize ends up stored as glycogen in the rat's body. In your answer, use the terms hydrolysis, condensation, glucose, and glycogen.
[5]
Answer
The starch in the maize is broken down into glucose by hydrolysis (water is added to break the bonds between glucose units) [1] during digestion [1]. The glucose is soluble, so it is absorbed into the blood and transported to the rat's cells [1]. In the rat's cells, the glucose molecules are joined together by condensation reactions (water is removed) [1] to form glycogen, which is the animal's energy store [1].
This is the diagram from page 36 of your textbook turned into words. The chain: starch → (hydrolysis, add water) → glucose → (condensation, remove water) → glycogen. Each step is a mark. Use ALL four key terms the question gives you.
