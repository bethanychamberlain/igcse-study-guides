Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Enzyme A protein that acts as a biological catalyst — speeds up reactions without being used up. Hormone (also a protein sometimes, but sends signals — doesn't catalyse)
Catalyst A substance that speeds up a reaction and is not changed by the reaction. Reactant (gets used up — a catalyst does not)
Active site The specific region on an enzyme where the substrate binds. Its shape determines specificity. —
Substrate The molecule that fits into the active site and is acted on by the enzyme. Product (what comes OUT of the reaction, not what goes in)
Product The molecule(s) produced after the enzyme has catalysed the reaction. Substrate (what goes IN)
Denatured The active site has changed shape (permanently). The substrate no longer fits. "Killed" or "destroyed" (enzymes aren't alive — they're proteins)
Optimum The temperature or pH at which an enzyme works fastest (maximum rate of reaction). —
Specificity Each enzyme only catalyses one reaction — only one substrate shape fits its active site. "Important" (in biology, specific = one-substrate-only)
Metabolism The sum of all chemical reactions in a living organism. —
Anabolic Reactions that build up large molecules from small ones (needs energy). Catabolic (breaks DOWN large molecules, releases energy)
Catabolic Reactions that break down large molecules into small ones (releases energy). Anabolic (builds UP)
Inhibitor A molecule that makes it harder for the enzyme to bind its substrate (blocks/slows the reaction). Activator (helps the enzyme work better)

Temperature vs pH — How They Affect Enzymes

Below Optimum At Optimum Above Optimum
Temperature Slow — less kinetic energy, fewer collisions Maximum rate (~37°C for humans) Denatured — active site changes shape, irreversible
pH Reduced activity; extreme acid denatures Maximum rate (varies per enzyme) Reduced activity; extreme alkali denatures
Exam Trap — Denatured Does NOT Mean Killed
Enzymes are not alive — they're proteins. Denatured means the active site has permanently changed shape so the substrate can no longer fit. Also: at low temperatures, enzymes are NOT denatured — they're just slow. Warm them up and they work again.

Key Enzymes You Must Know

Enzyme Substrate Product Where? Optimum pH
Amylase Starch Maltose Mouth / small intestine ~7.5
Pepsin Protein Amino acids Stomach ~2.0
Lipase Lipids (fats) Fatty acids + glycerol Small intestine ~8.0
Catalase Hydrogen peroxide Water + oxygen Inside liver cells ~7.0
Where It Works = Its Optimum pH
Pepsin works in the stomach (acid, pH 2). Amylase works in the mouth (neutral, pH 7.5). If you remember the location, you know the pH.

Say It in One Sentence

Enzyme A protein catalyst that speeds up one specific reaction and can be reused over and over.
Active site The dent in the enzyme where the substrate slots in — only one shape fits, like a key in a lock.
Lock and key The substrate (key) fits the active site (lock) because their shapes are complementary — that's why each enzyme is specific.
Denatured The active site got bent out of shape (by heat or extreme pH) and the substrate can't fit any more — permanently broken.
Optimum temp The sweet spot (~37°C for us) where enzymes work fastest — go higher and they break, go lower and they just slow down.
Specificity Amylase only breaks starch. Lipase only breaks fat. Each enzyme has one job and one job only.

Memory Tricks

3-Part Enzyme Definition (Full Marks)
"Enzymes are proteins that act as biological catalysts — they speed up reactions without being used up." Three parts = full marks. Miss one and you lose a mark.
The Temperature Graph Shape
Draw a hill: the left slope goes up gently (getting faster), the peak is at 37°C, and the right side drops off a cliff (denaturation is sudden and permanent). Left side = slow but working. Right side = dead shape.
Boiled Saliva Experiment
Boiled saliva + starch = starch stays. Why? Boiling denatured the amylase — active site changed shape permanently. Even after cooling, it couldn't work. This proves denaturation is irreversible.
Low Temp vs High Temp — The Key Difference
Cold enzyme = slow but not broken (warm it up and it works fine). Hot enzyme = permanently broken (active site shape destroyed, can't be fixed). The exam will test this difference.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Define enzyme" A protein that is a biological catalyst; speeds up reactions without being used up (3 marks = 3 ideas)
"Explain why the reaction rate increases with temperature" More kinetic energy → substrate molecules move faster → more frequent collisions with the active site
"Explain what happens above the optimum temperature" Bonds in the enzyme break → active site changes shape → substrate no longer fits → enzyme is denatured
"What is meant by denatured?" The active site has changed shape so the substrate can no longer fit. This is usually irreversible
"Why is the enzyme specific?" The active site has a complementary shape to only one substrate (lock and key model)
"Why does pepsin work best at pH 2?" pH 2 is its optimum pH; the stomach produces HCl to maintain this acidic environment
"Why are enzymes useful in industry?" Specific (no by-products), reusable (not used up), work at low temperatures (saves energy costs)
"Name the model used to explain enzyme action" The lock and key hypothesis. Substrate (key) fits the active site (lock) by complementary shape
