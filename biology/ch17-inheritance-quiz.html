Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the terms gene and allele.
2 marks
Answer
A gene is a length of DNA that codes for a specific protein [1]. An allele is an alternative form of a gene [1].
Both definitions must be precise. "Gene = thing that controls a trait" is too vague. The key words are "length of DNA" and "codes for a protein."

2
State the four bases found in DNA and their pairing rules.
2 marks
Answer
The four bases are adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), and cytosine (C) [1]. Adenine always pairs with thymine (A-T), and guanine always pairs with cytosine (G-C) [1].
If they ask "What is complementary base pairing?" this IS the answer. Just name all four and state the pairs.

3
What is the difference between a dominant and a recessive allele?
2 marks
Answer
A dominant allele is expressed in the phenotype whenever it is present (in both homozygous and heterozygous individuals) [1]. A recessive allele is only expressed when homozygous (when no dominant allele is present) [1].
Capital letter = dominant, lowercase = recessive. If genotype is Bb, the dominant allele (B) determines the phenotype.

4
State two differences between mitosis and meiosis.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of:
• Mitosis produces 2 daughter cells; meiosis produces 4 [1]
• Mitosis produces identical (diploid) cells; meiosis produces genetically different (haploid) cells [1]
• Mitosis is for growth and repair; meiosis is for producing gametes [1]
• Mitosis involves 1 division; meiosis involves 2 divisions [1]
Any two valid differences score full marks. Make sure each difference includes BOTH sides (what mitosis does AND what meiosis does).

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain HOW and WHY, not just name things.
5
Describe how DNA is replicated. Include the role of base pairing in your answer.
4 marks
Answer
The two strands of the DNA double helix separate / unwind [1]. Each strand acts as a template [1]. Free nucleotides pair up with the exposed bases following the base pairing rules (A-T and G-C) [1]. This produces two identical copies of the original DNA molecule [1].
Four marks = four ideas: (1) strands separate, (2) each strand is a template, (3) complementary base pairing, (4) two identical copies result. Don't forget to mention that the copies are IDENTICAL to the original.

6
In pea plants, the allele for tall (T) is dominant over the allele for short (t). Two heterozygous plants are crossed. Draw a Punnett square and state the expected genotype ratio and phenotype ratio of the offspring.
4 marks
Answer
Parents: Tt × Tt [1 for correct setup]

Punnett square:
        T    t
T   TT   Tt
t    Tt   tt

Genotype ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt [1]
Phenotype ratio: 3 tall : 1 short [1]
Probability of short: 1 in 4 or 25% [1]
You MUST draw the Punnett square to get full marks. Write the parents' genotypes, list the gametes, fill in the grid, then state both ratios. This is the most common genetics question format.

7
Explain why meiosis is necessary for sexual reproduction. Include the terms diploid, haploid, and fertilisation in your answer.
3 marks
Answer
Meiosis produces haploid gametes (with half the chromosome number) [1]. At fertilisation, two haploid gametes fuse to restore the diploid number in the zygote [1]. Without meiosis, the chromosome number would double with each generation [1].
This is a 3-mark "explain why" question. The chain: meiosis halves → fertilisation restores → this keeps the chromosome number constant. If meiosis didn't halve it, organisms would have 92 chromosomes after one generation, 184 after two, etc.

Advanced — Can You Apply It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full genetic diagrams, multi-step reasoning, and clear explanations.
8
Red-green colour blindness is a sex-linked condition caused by a recessive allele on the X chromosome. A woman who is a carrier for colour blindness (XCXc) has children with a man who has normal vision (XCY). Draw a genetic diagram to show the possible genotypes and phenotypes of their children. What is the probability of their son being colour blind?
5 marks
Answer
Parents: XCXc (carrier female) × XCY (normal male) [1]
Gametes: XC or Xc   ×   XC or Y [1]

          XC        Y
XC   XCXC    XCY
Xc    XCXc    XcY

Offspring:
XCXC = normal female; XCXc = carrier female; XCY = normal male; XcY = colour-blind male [1]

Probability of a son being colour blind = 1/2 (50%) [1].
(Half the males will be XcY. Overall probability of any child being a colour-blind boy = 1/4 or 25% [1].)
Key: write alleles ON the X chromosome. The Y has no allele for this gene. The question asks specifically about SONS — half of sons will be colour blind (1 in 2). If it asked about ALL children, it's 1 in 4.

9
Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a recessive allele (HbS). Two parents who are both heterozygous (HbNHbS) have a child. (a) Draw a genetic diagram to predict the possible genotypes. (b) Explain why the heterozygous genotype may be an advantage in regions where malaria is common.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Parents: HbNHbS × HbNHbS [1]
Gametes: HbN or HbS  ×  HbN or HbS

          HbN        HbS
HbN  HbNHbN   HbNHbS
HbS   HbNHbS   HbSHbS

Genotype ratio: 1 HbNHbN : 2 HbNHbS : 1 HbSHbS [1]
Probability of child having sickle cell anaemia = 1/4 (25%) [1]

(b) Heterozygous individuals (HbNHbS) have some sickle-shaped red blood cells [1]. The malaria parasite cannot reproduce effectively in sickle-shaped cells, so heterozygous individuals are resistant to malaria [1]. In malaria-prone regions (e.g. equatorial Africa), this gives them a survival advantage over HbNHbN individuals, who may die of malaria [1].
Part (a) is standard Punnett square work. Part (b) is the classic "explain why the sickle cell allele hasn't disappeared" question. The answer is heterozygous advantage — carriers survive malaria better.

10
Explain, using the terms DNA, gene, triplet, mRNA, and ribosome, how the information in the nucleus leads to the production of a specific protein in the cytoplasm.
5 marks
Answer
A gene is a section of DNA that carries coded instructions as a sequence of bases [1]. The bases are read in groups of three called triplets (codons), each coding for one amino acid [1]. The DNA code is copied into mRNA by a process called transcription [1]. The mRNA leaves the nucleus and travels to a ribosome in the cytoplasm [1]. At the ribosome, the code is read and amino acids are assembled into a protein — this is called translation [1].
The chain: DNA → mRNA (transcription) → ribosome → protein (translation). Each step is a mark. Make sure you mention that triplets code for amino acids — this is the link between bases and proteins.
