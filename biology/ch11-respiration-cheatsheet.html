Respiration

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Respiration Chemical reactions in all living cells that release energy from glucose, controlled by enzymes. Breathing (that's just moving air in/out of lungs — a physical process, not chemical)
Aerobic respiration Respiration that uses oxygen. Happens in the mitochondria. Releases a lot of energy. Anaerobic (that's without oxygen, much less energy)
Anaerobic respiration Respiration without oxygen. Happens in the cytoplasm. Releases much less energy (~1/20). Aerobic (that's with oxygen, much more energy)
Mitochondria The organelles where aerobic respiration takes place. Cells that need lots of energy have many mitochondria. —
ATP Adenosine triphosphate — a small molecule that stores energy released by respiration for use in cell reactions. —
Lactic acid The waste product of anaerobic respiration in humans/animals. Poisonous — causes muscle fatigue. Ethanol/alcohol (that's the waste from yeast anaerobic respiration)
Fermentation Anaerobic respiration in yeast: glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide + energy. Used in brewing and baking. Animal anaerobic respiration (that produces lactic acid, not alcohol)
Oxygen debt The extra oxygen needed after exercise to break down the lactic acid that built up during anaerobic respiration. —
Respirometer A device that measures oxygen consumption. Uses KOH to absorb CO₂ so only O₂ changes are measured. —
Hydrogencarbonate indicator Changes colour when CO₂ dissolves in it: purple (low CO₂) → red → yellow (high CO₂). Limewater (also detects CO₂ but turns milky/cloudy instead of changing colour)

Exam Trap — Which Organism?
Read the question carefully. If it says "in humans" or "in animals," the product is lactic acid. If it says "in yeast" or "fermentation," the products are ethanol + CO₂. They are NOT the same.

Say It in One Sentence

Respiration A chemical process in all living cells that releases energy from glucose — it is NOT breathing.
Aerobic Uses oxygen, happens in mitochondria, releases a lot of energy — the main type of respiration.
Anaerobic No oxygen needed, happens in cytoplasm, releases only 1/20 of the energy — the backup system.
Lactic acid Toxic waste product of anaerobic respiration in muscles — causes fatigue and the burning feeling during sprints.
Oxygen debt The extra O₂ your body needs after exercise to break down the lactic acid that built up — why you keep panting.
Fermentation Anaerobic respiration in yeast — produces ethanol and CO₂, used in brewing beer and making bread rise.
Respirometer Measures O₂ uptake by organisms — KOH absorbs CO₂ so only oxygen changes move the liquid.

Memory Tricks

Aerobic Equation
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water. Think "GO CW" — like "Go, cow!" Aerobic respiration needs oxygen to GO.
Oxygen Debt = A Loan
During exercise you "borrow" energy without paying oxygen. After exercise you "pay it back" by breathing hard. Bigger loan (harder exercise) = longer payback (more panting).
Human vs Yeast Products
Humans make Lactic acid — HL. Yeast makes Alcohol — YA. "HL" and "YA" — short and easy to remember.
Why Root Hair Cells Have Lots of Mitochondria
They need loads of energy for active transport of minerals. More mitochondria = more aerobic respiration = more ATP. If the exam mentions "many mitochondria," think energy and active transport.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What is respiration?" The release of energy from glucose in all living cells, controlled by enzymes. NOT breathing.
"Write the word equation for aerobic respiration" glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy released)
"Where does aerobic respiration take place?" In the mitochondria
"What is the product of anaerobic respiration in humans?" Lactic acid (NOT alcohol — that's yeast)
"Why does breathing rate stay high after exercise?" To supply extra oxygen to break down lactic acid and repay the oxygen debt
"Why does anaerobic respiration release less energy?" Glucose is only partially broken down (lactic acid still contains energy), unlike in aerobic where it is completely broken down
"Why do muscle cells contain many mitochondria?" They need a lot of energy for contraction, supplied by aerobic respiration in the mitochondria
"What is the role of KOH in a respirometer?" It absorbs CO₂, so any change in gas volume is due to oxygen being used up
