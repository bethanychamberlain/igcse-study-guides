Section 1.1 Biology Is the Study of Life and Living Organisms
How do you tell if something is alive? Biologists use a checklist of seven characteristics. If something shows all seven, it's alive.
W
Write This Down — The 7 Characteristics of Living Organisms

Use the mnemonic RINGER (from the textbook) or MRS GREN to remember them:

M — Movement: an action by an organism causing a change of position or place
R — Respiration: chemical reactions in cells that break down nutrient molecules to release energy
S — Sensitivity (Irritability): the ability to detect and respond to changes (stimuli) in the environment
G — Growth: a permanent increase in size and dry mass, from an increase in cell number or cell size
R — Reproduction: making more of the same kind of organism (new individuals of the same species)
E — Excretion: removal of toxic waste products of metabolism from the organism
N — Nutrition: taking in materials for energy, growth and development

?
Why This Matters

The exam often asks you to list the characteristics (easy marks) or to explain why something like a virus or a dried seed is debatable as "living." You need all seven memorised cold.

!
Don't Confuse — Excretion vs Egestion

Excretion = removing waste products of metabolism (chemical reactions inside cells), like CO₂ and urea. Egestion = removing undigested food (faeces). Faeces were never inside cells, so passing faeces is NOT excretion. The exam will try to trick you on this.

T
Study Tip — MRS GREN

Write "MRS GREN" on a sticky note. For each letter, say the characteristic AND give a one-sentence definition. Do it from memory. If you can't do all seven without looking, repeat until you can. This is guaranteed marks on the exam.

  • I can list all 7 characteristics of living organisms from memory
  • I can define each one in one sentence
  • I know the difference between excretion and egestion
Section 1.2 The Variety of Life — Classification and Keys
There are millions of species on Earth. To study them, scientists sort them into groups based on shared features. This is called classification.
W
Write This Down — Why We Classify

Classification = placing organisms into categories based on their observable characteristics. It is important for:

• Conservation: scientists need to identify organisms in habitats being managed
• Understanding evolutionary relationships: organisms that share many features likely share a common ancestor

W
Write This Down — The Five Kingdoms

All living organisms can be placed into one of five kingdoms:

1. Animals — multicellular, no cell wall, heterotrophs (feed on organic molecules)
2. Plants — multicellular, cellulose cell wall, autotrophs (photosynthesise using chlorophyll)
3. Fungi — cell wall made of chitin, saprotrophic nutrition (feed on dead matter by external digestion)
4. Protoctists — cells have a nucleus, mostly unicellular, do not fit in any other kingdom
5. Prokaryotes (Bacteria) — no true nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles, single-celled

W
Write This Down — The Hierarchy of Classification

Organisms are classified into groups that get smaller and more specific:

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

Memory trick: "King Philip Came Over For Good Spaghetti"

As you go down the hierarchy: fewer organisms in each group, but more similarities between them.

W
Write This Down — Binomial Nomenclature

Every species has a two-part scientific name made up of its genus and species. This system was introduced by Carolus Linnaeus.

• The genus name has a capital letter; the species name is lowercase
• Both are written in italics (or underlined if handwritten)
• Example: humans = Homo sapiens, lion = Panthera leo

W
Write This Down — Dichotomous Keys

A dichotomous key is a series of questions with two possible answers (yes/no) that lead you to identify an organism. "Dichotomous" means "branching into two."

Keys can be written as a numbered list or as a branching diagram. They use observable features like body structure (morphology) and anatomy.

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves dichotomous keys. You'll be asked to either use a key to identify organisms or construct your own key from a set of organisms. Practice both — see the end-of-chapter questions in your textbook (Q5 and Q6).

T
Study Tip — Species Definition

A species = a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. This is a classic 2-mark definition. Both parts are needed.

  • I can name the five kingdoms
  • I can list the hierarchy of classification in order
  • I know what binomial nomenclature means and can give an example
  • I can use a dichotomous key to identify an organism
  • I can define "species" (interbreed + fertile offspring)
Section 1.3 Fungi
Fungi are not plants. They look still and rooted, but they feed completely differently.
W
Write This Down — Key Features of Fungi

• Cell wall made of chitin (not cellulose like plants)
• Body made of thread-like hyphae; a network of hyphae = mycelium
• Feed by saprotrophic nutrition: they secrete digestive enzymes onto dead organic matter, then absorb the soluble products
• Reproduce by spores
• Do NOT photosynthesise — no chlorophyll
• Examples: mushrooms, bread mould, yeast

!
Don't Confuse — Fungi vs Plants

Students often put fungi in the Plant Kingdom because they "look like plants" and don't move. But fungi have no chlorophyll (can't photosynthesise), their cell walls are chitin not cellulose, and they feed by absorbing digested food, not making their own. These are the exact differences the exam will ask for.

?
Why This Matters

Fungi are important as decomposers in nutrient cycles, as food (mushrooms), in baking and brewing (yeast), and as agents of disease (athlete's foot). The exam may ask you to explain why fungi are placed in their own kingdom rather than with plants.

  • I know fungi feed by saprotrophic nutrition (external digestion)
  • I can state three differences between fungi and plants
  • I know fungi reproduce by spores
Section 1.4 Plants: The Plant Kingdom
All plants are autotrophs — they make their own food using light energy. This is the defining feature of the Plant Kingdom.
W
Write This Down — Key Features of Plants

• Contain chlorophyll in chloroplasts → carry out photosynthesis
• Have a cellulose cell wall
• Are multicellular
• Store energy as starch

W
Write This Down — Three Main Plant Groups

Mosses: simple, no true roots/stems/leaves, need water for reproduction, no vascular tissue
Ferns: have roots, stems, leaves and vascular tissue, reproduce by spores, need moist/shady habitats
Seed plants (Angiosperms): most successful group, reproduce by seeds, well adapted to life on dry land

Two groups of angiosperms:
• Monocotyledons: one seed leaf (cotyledon), parallel leaf veins (e.g. maize, grass)
• Dicotyledons: two seed leaves, branched leaf veins (e.g. pea, oak)

T
Study Tip

The pattern from mosses → ferns → seed plants shows increasing adaptation to life on dry land. Each group is less dependent on water for reproduction. This "evolution towards dry land" idea connects to the vertebrate story too.

  • I know the key features of all plants (chlorophyll, cellulose cell wall, autotroph)
  • I can name and describe the three main plant groups
  • I know the difference between monocotyledons and dicotyledons
Section 1.5 Invertebrate Animals
Animals are divided into vertebrates (with a backbone) and invertebrates (without). The arthropods are the largest and most important invertebrate group for your exam.
W
Write This Down — Arthropod Features

All arthropods have:
• A hard exoskeleton
• Jointed limbs
• A segmented body

Four classes of arthropods:

Class Body Sections Legs Other Features Example
Insects 3 (head, thorax, abdomen) 3 pairs (6) Usually 2 pairs of wings, compound eyes, antennae Mosquito, butterfly
Arachnids 2 (cephalothorax, abdomen) 4 pairs (8) No wings, no antennae, simple eyes Spider, scorpion
Crustaceans Varies 5+ pairs Hard carapace, often aquatic, 2 pairs of antennae Crab, lobster
Myriapods Many segments Many pairs Long segmented body, antennae Centipede, millipede
W
Write This Down — Metamorphosis

Metamorphosis = "change of body form." Many insects go through complete metamorphosis: egg → larva → pupa → adult. This means the larva and adult don't compete for the same food, allowing more species to coexist.

!
Don't Confuse — Insects vs Spiders

Insects have 3 body sections and 6 legs. Spiders have 2 body sections and 8 legs. Spiders are NOT insects — they are arachnids. The exam will test this, often by giving you a drawing and asking which class it belongs to.

  • I can state the three features shared by all arthropods
  • I can distinguish insects from arachnids (body sections + leg count)
  • I know what metamorphosis means and why it's an advantage
Section 1.6 Vertebrate Animals: Five Classes
Vertebrates all have a backbone made of separate bones called vertebrae. They are divided into five classes that show increasing adaptation to life on dry land.
W
Write This Down — The Five Vertebrate Classes
Class Skin Covering Breathing Reproduction Temperature Example
Fish Wet scales, mucus Gills Eggs in water (external fertilisation) Ectothermic (cold-blooded) Salmon
Amphibians Moist, smooth skin Lungs + skin Eggs in water (external fertilisation) Ectothermic Frog
Reptiles Dry scales Lungs Eggs with leathery shell on land Ectothermic Lizard, crocodile
Birds Feathers Lungs Eggs with hard shell on land Endothermic (warm-blooded) Heron, thrush
Mammals Hair/fur Lungs Live young (most), mammary glands Endothermic Human, mouse
?
Why This Matters

The exam will give you a description or picture of an animal and ask you to identify its class. The skin covering is usually the fastest way to tell: wet scales = fish, smooth moist skin = amphibian, dry scales = reptile, feathers = bird, fur/hair = mammal.

!
Don't Confuse — Fish Scales vs Reptile Scales

Both fish and reptiles have scales, but fish scales are wet (covered in mucus) while reptile scales are dry. Also, fish breathe through gills, reptiles use lungs. The dichotomous key in your textbook uses "Are the scales dry?" to separate them.

T
Study Tip — Copy the Table

  • I can name all five vertebrate classes
  • I can identify a vertebrate class from its skin covering
  • I know which classes are ectothermic and which are endothermic
  • I can recreate the vertebrate comparison table from memory
Section 1.7 Protoctists and Prokaryotes
These are the two kingdoms made up mostly of single-celled organisms. The key difference between them is whether or not they have a nucleus.
W
Write This Down — Protoctists

• Cells have a nucleus (and may have other organelles like chloroplasts)
• Mostly unicellular, some are multicellular (e.g. algae/seaweeds)
• Some feed like animals, some photosynthesise like plants, some do both
• They are placed here because they don't fit neatly into any other kingdom
• Examples: Amoeba, Euglena, Plasmodium (causes malaria), algae

W
Write This Down — Prokaryotes (Bacteria)

• No true nucleus — DNA is free in the cytoplasm as a single circular strand
• No membrane-bound organelles (no mitochondria, no chloroplasts)
• Have a cell wall (but NOT made of cellulose or chitin)
• May have flagella for movement and plasmids (small rings of extra DNA)
• Very small: 1–2 μm
• Some are parasites (feed on living organisms), some are saprotrophs (feed on dead matter)
• Shapes: cocci (spherical), bacilli (rod), spirilli (spiral)

!
Don't Confuse — Protoctists vs Prokaryotes

Both can be single-celled, but:
• Protoctists HAVE a nucleus; prokaryotes do NOT
• Protoctists may have organelles (chloroplasts, etc.); prokaryotes have NO membrane-bound organelles
If a unicellular organism has a nucleus, it's a protoctist, not a prokaryote.

T
Study Tip

The prefix "pro" in prokaryote means "before" and "karyon" means "nucleus" — so prokaryote literally means "before nucleus" (they evolved before cells with nuclei). This helps you remember that prokaryotes have NO nucleus.

  • I can state the key feature of protoctists (have a nucleus, don't fit elsewhere)
  • I can describe the structure of a typical bacterium
  • I know that prokaryotes have no true nucleus and no membrane-bound organelles
  • I can name an example of a protoctist and a prokaryote
Section 1.8 Viruses
Viruses are not placed in any of the five kingdoms. They are not considered truly "living" because they cannot carry out life processes on their own.
W
Write This Down — Virus Structure

• Genetic material: a strand of DNA or RNA (not both)
• Surrounded by a protein coat
• May have spikes on the surface to attach to host cells
• No cytoplasm, no nucleus, no organelles, no cell membrane
• Much smaller than bacteria

W
Write This Down — Why Viruses Are Not "Living"

• They can only reproduce inside a living host cell
• Outside a host, they show none of the seven characteristics of life (no respiration, no nutrition, no excretion, etc.)
• All viruses are parasites — they cause harm to their host
• Examples: influenza virus, HIV, COVID-19 (coronavirus), tobacco mosaic virus

!
Don't Confuse — Bacteria vs Viruses

This is one of the most common exam mix-ups:

Feature Bacteria Virus
Living? Yes — shows all 7 characteristics No — only active inside a host
Cell? Yes — it is a cell (with cytoplasm, cell wall, membrane) No — not a cell
Nucleus? No true nucleus (DNA free in cytoplasm) No nucleus (just a strand of DNA or RNA)
Reproduction Can reproduce on its own (binary fission) Can only reproduce inside a host cell
Size Larger (1–2 μm) Much smaller
Treatment Antibiotics Antibiotics do NOT work on viruses
?
Why This Matters

"State one difference between a bacterium and a virus" is a classic exam question. Learn the table above — any one row gives you a valid answer. The safest answer: bacteria are cells and can reproduce on their own; viruses are not cells and can only reproduce inside a living host cell.

  • I can describe the structure of a virus (genetic material + protein coat)
  • I know why viruses are not placed in any kingdom
  • I can state at least three differences between bacteria and viruses

T
Study Tip — The Dichotomous Key for Kingdoms

Use these questions to place any organism into its kingdom:
1. Does it have cells with a nucleus? No → Prokaryote.
2. Is it made of many different cells? No → Protoctist.
3. Is it a heterotroph? No → Plant.
4. Does it have cell walls and feed by external digestion? Yes → Fungus. No → Animal.
Practice walking through this key with different organisms until it's automatic.
