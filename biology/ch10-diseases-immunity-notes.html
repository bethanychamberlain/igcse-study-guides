Section 10.1 Diseases and Immunity
Disease is what happens when the body can no longer maintain its normal internal conditions. Some diseases are caught from other organisms; others are not.
W
Write This Down — What is Disease?

Disease is the state of the body when it cannot cope with changes by the normal homeostatic methods. A diseased person shows signs (observable, e.g. raised temperature) and symptoms (felt by the patient, e.g. feeling tired, pain).

W
Write This Down — Two Types of Disease

Infectious (transmissible) diseases: caused by another living organism (a pathogen). Can be passed from one individual to another. Examples: cholera, influenza, malaria, AIDS.

Non-infectious diseases: NOT caught from another person. Causes include:
• Degenerative — e.g. heart attacks, cataracts (ageing)
• Deficiency — e.g. scurvy (lack of vitamin C)
• Inherited/metabolic — e.g. sickle cell anaemia, diabetes
• Self-induced — e.g. lung cancer from smoking, cirrhosis from alcohol
• Allergy — e.g. hay fever
• Environmental — e.g. skin cancer from UV radiation

W
Write This Down — How Infectious Diseases Spread

Infectious diseases can be transmitted by:
• Droplets in the air — e.g. influenza, COVID-19
• Contaminated water — e.g. cholera
• Contaminated food — e.g. Salmonella food poisoning
• Direct contact — e.g. athlete's foot (fungus)
• Animal vectors — e.g. malaria (via Anopheles mosquito)
• Body fluids — e.g. HIV/AIDS, hepatitis B

!
Don't Confuse

Signs vs symptoms: a sign is something a doctor can observe or measure (e.g. high temperature, rash). A symptom is something the patient feels (e.g. pain, nausea). The exam will test if you know the difference.

T
Study Tip

Make a two-column list: "Infectious" and "Non-infectious." For each, write three examples with their cause. This is a quick way to check you can tell them apart under exam pressure.

  • I can define disease and distinguish signs from symptoms
  • I can explain the difference between infectious and non-infectious diseases
  • I can list six ways that infectious diseases spread
Section 10.2 Pathogens
A pathogen is any organism that causes disease. They come in very different sizes and types, and they cause harm in different ways.
W
Write This Down — What is a Pathogen?

A pathogen is an organism that causes disease. Pathogens are parasites — they live on or in a host and cause it harm.

How pathogens cause harm:
• By secreting toxins (poisons) — e.g. Salmonella releases toxins that irritate the gut lining
• By multiplying rapidly and damaging cells directly — e.g. viruses cause host cells to burst
• By triggering the immune response — swelling, soreness, fever

W
Write This Down — Types of Pathogen
Pathogen Type Size Example Disease
Virus ~1 nm (1/1000 of a µm) Influenza, AIDS
Bacterium (prokaryote) ~1 µm Cholera, food poisoning
Protoctist Up to 1 mm Malaria, dysentery
Fungus Variable (can be large) Athlete's foot, ringworm
!
Don't Confuse

Viruses are NOT living organisms in the traditional sense — they cannot reproduce on their own and must use a host cell's machinery to replicate. The exam might say "viruses differ from true living organisms because..." — the answer is they cannot survive or reproduce outside a host cell.

W
Write This Down — Controlling the Spread

Methods to control the spread of infectious disease:
• Hygienic food preparation — wash hands, cook food thoroughly
• Good personal hygiene
• Proper waste disposal and sewage treatment
• Antibiotics can treat bacterial diseases (NOT viruses)

?
Why This Matters

A very common exam question: "Antibiotics kill bacteria but NOT viruses." If asked why, explain that antibiotics work by disrupting bacterial cell processes (e.g. cell wall formation), and viruses don't have these structures because they reproduce inside host cells.

  • I can define pathogen and give four types with examples
  • I know three ways pathogens cause harm
  • I know that antibiotics work on bacteria but NOT viruses
  • I can suggest methods to control the spread of disease
Section 10.3 Preventing Disease: Safe Food
Food poisoning is one of the most common infectious diseases. Understanding how to prevent it is both a life skill and an exam topic.
W
Write This Down — Food Poisoning

Food poisoning is caused by eating food contaminated with harmful microorganisms (especially bacteria like Salmonella and Clostridium botulinum).

Symptoms: abdominal pain, diarrhoea, vomiting (usually 8-36 hours after eating).
Danger: diarrhoea and vomiting can cause dehydration, which is especially dangerous for young children and elderly people.

High-risk foods: undercooked meats and poultry, cooked rice, shellfish, dishes with raw eggs (e.g. homemade ice cream).

W
Write This Down — Preventing Food Poisoning

Three key principles:
• Avoid contamination — wash hands, clean surfaces, separate raw and cooked foods
• Prevent bacteria multiplying — refrigerate food, don't leave food at room temperature
• Destroy bacteria — cook food thoroughly (especially meat)

T
Study Tip

For exam questions on food safety, always think in terms of the three steps: avoid, prevent, destroy. This gives you a clear framework for any "suggest how to reduce the risk of food poisoning" question.

  • I can name the symptoms and dangers of food poisoning
  • I can describe three principles for preventing food poisoning
Section 10.4 Fighting Disease Together
Fighting disease is not just a personal responsibility — it requires action at individual, community, and worldwide levels.
W
Write This Down — Three Levels of Responsibility

Personal responsibility:
• Good personal hygiene, balanced diet, regular exercise
• Not smoking, controlling alcohol intake

Community responsibility:
• Providing safe drinking water and treating sewage
• Removing refuse (waste), providing medical care
• Monitoring health and hygiene standards

Worldwide responsibility (WHO):
• Vaccination programmes (e.g. elimination of smallpox)
• Reducing malaria through mosquito control
• Providing safe water and improving nutrition

?
Why This Matters

The exam sometimes asks "Suggest how the spread of a named disease could be reduced." Structure your answer around these three levels: what the individual can do, what the community can do, and what governments/WHO can do. This gives you a clear, multi-mark answer.

  • I can describe personal, community, and worldwide responsibilities for fighting disease
  • I know that WHO helped eliminate smallpox through vaccination
Section 10.5 Blood and Defence
The body has multiple lines of defence against pathogens. If they get past the external barriers, the blood fights back.
W
Write This Down — First Lines of Defence

The body prevents pathogen entry with physical and chemical barriers:
• Skin — the epidermis is waxy and impermeable; a continuous physical barrier
• Mucus and cilia — in the respiratory tract, mucus traps pathogens and cilia sweep them away
• Hydrochloric acid in the stomach — kills most bacteria in food
• Lysozyme in tears — an enzyme that destroys bacterial cell walls
• Bactericidal wax in the ears — kills bacteria

W
Write This Down — Blood Clotting

If the skin is broken, blood clotting seals the wound to:
• Prevent further blood loss
• Prevent entry of pathogens

How clotting works:
• Damaged platelets and torn capillaries release enzymes
• These enzymes convert fibrinogen (inactive protein in plasma) into fibrin (active protein)
• Fibrin forms a mesh of fibres that traps red blood cells
• This dries to form a scab

W
Write This Down — Phagocytes

Phagocytes are white blood cells that engulf and digest pathogens (a process called phagocytosis).

How phagocytosis works:
• Phagocyte detects the pathogen (recognises it as foreign)
• Flows around the pathogen, enclosing it in a vacuole
• Digestive enzymes are released into the vacuole
• The pathogen is broken down and destroyed

Adaptations of phagocytes:
• Irregular (lobed) nucleus — allows them to squeeze through capillary walls
• Contain digestive enzymes in their cytoplasm
• Attracted to sites of infection by chemical signals

!
Don't Confuse — Two Types of White Blood Cell

Phagocytes physically eat pathogens (engulf and digest). Lymphocytes make antibodies (proteins). These are completely different mechanisms. The exam will test whether you know which does what. Remember: Phagocytes = physically eat. Lymphocytes = launch antibodies.

T
Study Tip

Sketch the clotting process as a flow chart: Damaged platelets → release enzymes → fibrinogen converted to fibrin → mesh traps RBCs → scab forms. Then sketch phagocytosis in four steps. Drawing these processes helps you remember the sequence for multi-mark questions.

  • I can name five physical/chemical barriers that prevent pathogen entry
  • I can describe the clotting process step by step
  • I can describe phagocytosis and name adaptations of phagocytes
  • I know the difference between phagocytes and lymphocytes
Section 10.6 Antibodies and the Immune Response
Antibodies are the body's precision weapons. Each type targets a specific pathogen, and the body remembers how to make them for future attacks.
W
Write This Down — Antigens and Antibodies

Antigen: a protein or carbohydrate on the surface of a pathogen that the immune system recognises as foreign.

Antibody: a Y-shaped protein produced by lymphocytes in response to a specific antigen. Each antibody is specific — it only binds to one type of antigen.

How antibodies destroy pathogens:
• The forked end of the antibody recognises and binds to the antigen on the pathogen
• This can cause pathogens to clump together (agglutination), making them easier for phagocytes to engulf
• Some antibodies can burst pathogen cell membranes directly
• Others neutralise toxins produced by pathogens

W
Write This Down — Memory Cells and Immunity

After an infection, some lymphocytes remain in the body as memory cells. If the same pathogen enters the body again:

• The memory cells recognise its antigen immediately
• They produce antibodies much faster and in greater quantities than the first time
• The pathogen is destroyed before it can cause symptoms
• The body is said to be immune to that disease

W
Write This Down — Types of Immunity
Active Immunity Passive Immunity
How? Body makes its own antibodies Body receives ready-made antibodies
Natural Catching the disease and recovering Antibodies from mother (placenta or breast milk)
Artificial Vaccination (injecting weakened/dead pathogen) Injection of antibodies (e.g. anti-tetanus serum)
Duration Long-term (memory cells formed) Short-term (no memory cells formed)
W
Write This Down — How Vaccination Works

A vaccine contains a weakened or dead form of the pathogen (or its antigens). It does NOT cause the disease, but it stimulates lymphocytes to produce antibodies and form memory cells.

If the real pathogen enters the body later, the immune response is faster and stronger because memory cells are already present. This is why vaccination prevents disease.

Herd immunity: If enough people in a population are vaccinated, there are very few hosts for the pathogen to infect, so even unvaccinated individuals are protected. This is why vaccination must continue even when a disease seems rare.

!
Don't Confuse — Active vs Passive

The key difference: in active immunity, the body makes its own antibodies (and forms memory cells, so it lasts a long time). In passive immunity, the body receives someone else's antibodies (no memory cells are formed, so protection is only temporary). The exam loves asking this.

?
Why This Matters

The immune response is one of the most heavily tested topics. A common multi-mark question: "Explain how vaccination leads to immunity." You need to say: vaccine contains weakened/dead pathogen → lymphocytes produce antibodies → memory cells are formed → if real pathogen enters, response is faster/stronger → person doesn't get ill. Each step is a mark.

T
Study Tip — The Graph Question

The exam often shows a graph of antibody concentration over time after vaccination and a booster. The key features to describe: (1) slow, small response after first injection, (2) faster, larger response after second injection (booster), (3) antibody level stays high for longer. Practise sketching this graph and explaining why the second response is bigger (memory cells).

  • I know that antibodies are specific — each one fits only one type of antigen
  • I can explain how memory cells provide long-term immunity
  • I can fill in the active vs passive immunity table from memory
  • I can explain how vaccination works step by step
  • I can explain why vaccination must continue even when a disease is rare
  • I can sketch and explain a vaccination antibody graph
