Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
State the difference between continuous and discontinuous variation. Give one example of each.
2 marks
Answer
Continuous variation shows a range of values between two extremes with no clear categories (e.g. height, body mass) [1]. Discontinuous variation shows distinct categories with no intermediates (e.g. blood group, sex/gender) [1].
The key distinction: continuous = range, discontinuous = categories. If you can measure it with a number and get any value in a range, it's continuous.

2
Define the term mutation.
2 marks
Answer
A mutation is a change in a gene or chromosome [1] that can result in a change in a characteristic / a different protein being produced [1].
Two parts: (1) what it IS (a change in DNA), and (2) what it DOES (changes a characteristic). Both needed for full marks.

3
What is meant by adaptation? Give one example of an adaptive feature.
2 marks
Answer
Adaptation is the way an organism is suited to survive in its environment [1]. Example: polar bears have white fur for camouflage in snow / thick fat for insulation against the cold / camels have large feet to walk on sand without sinking [1].
Any valid adaptive feature from any organism scores the example mark. Make sure you say HOW it helps the organism, not just what it looks like.

4
Merino sheep have much thicker wool than wild sheep. Merino sheep are the result of:
A mutation of a gene   B natural selection   C phenotypic variation   D selective breeding
1 mark
Answer
D — selective breeding (artificial selection) [1].
Humans deliberately chose sheep with thicker wool and bred them over many generations. This is artificial / selective breeding, not natural selection (which is driven by the environment, not by humans).

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY and HOW, not just name things.
5
Explain the relationship between genotype, environment, and phenotype. Use an example in your answer.
3 marks
Answer
Phenotype = genotype + effects of the environment [1]. The genotype is the full set of genes an organism possesses, and the phenotype is its observable characteristics [1]. Example: a plant may have genes to produce chlorophyll (genotype), but if grown in the dark, it will not turn green (the environment affects the phenotype) [1].
State the equation, define the terms, and give a clear example showing how the environment changes the outcome. Another good example: identical twins raised in different countries may have different body masses due to diet.

6
Suggest two factors that could increase the rate of mutation. Name a specific example of a mutation and its effect on the organism.
3 marks
Answer
Factors (mutagens): radiation (e.g. UV light, gamma rays, X-rays) [1] and chemicals (e.g. tars in tobacco smoke) [1].

Example: Sickle cell anaemia — a gene mutation (change in one base of the haemoglobin gene) causes abnormal haemoglobin to be produced, resulting in sickle-shaped red blood cells [1].
Name specific mutagens, not just "chemicals" or "radiation" in isolation. Say WHAT type of radiation or WHICH chemical. For the example, link the mutation to its protein AND its effect on the organism.

7
A plant breeder wants to produce a variety of wheat with larger grains. Describe the steps the breeder would follow using artificial selection.
4 marks
Answer
1. From existing wheat plants, select those with the largest grains [1].
2. Breed these selected plants together (cross-pollinate them) [1].
3. From the offspring, again select only those with the largest grains [1].
4. Repeat this process over many generations until a variety with consistently large grains is produced [1].
Four steps, four marks. The key words are: select, breed, select from offspring, repeat. This exact pattern works for ANY artificial selection question — just swap in the desired trait (thicker wool, more milk, larger fruit, etc.).

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full explanations, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Warfarin is a poison used to kill rats. It prevents blood clotting, so even small wounds cause the rat to bleed to death. Some rats are not affected by warfarin, and their numbers are increasing. Using the theory of natural selection, explain why this is happening.
5 marks
Answer
Within the rat population, there is variation — some rats have a random mutation that makes them resistant to warfarin [1]. When warfarin is used, non-resistant rats die [1]. The resistant rats survive because the poison does not affect them [1]. The surviving resistant rats breed and pass on the resistance allele to their offspring [1]. Over many generations, the proportion of resistant rats in the population increases [1].
This is the natural selection chain applied to a real scenario: variation (mutation) → selection pressure (warfarin) → non-resistant die → resistant survive and breed → resistant genes become more common. Each link = 1 mark. This is EXACTLY the same pattern as antibiotic-resistant bacteria.

9
An island was invaded by a species of bird that preyed on butterflies. In the population of butterflies, only those individuals that produced a toxic substance as a protection survived. (a) Name the type of selection occurring. (b) Explain, step by step, how the butterfly population changed over time.
5 marks
Answer
(a) Natural selection [1].

(b) There was variation in the butterfly population — some butterflies had a gene/mutation allowing them to produce a toxic substance [1]. The birds acted as a selection pressure — they ate the non-toxic butterflies [1]. Toxic butterflies survived because the birds avoided them [1]. The surviving toxic butterflies reproduced and passed on the gene for toxin production to their offspring, so over time, most butterflies in the population were toxic [1].
Part (a) is 1 free mark — don't write "artificial selection" (there are no humans choosing butterflies!). Part (b) follows the standard 4-step chain: variation → selection pressure → survival → passing on genes.

10
Bacterial infections that were easily treated with antibiotics 50 years ago are now a problem in hospitals. Using your knowledge of mutation and natural selection, explain why antibiotic-resistant bacteria have become more common. Suggest one action that could slow the spread of resistance.
6 marks
Answer
Within a population of bacteria, a random mutation may occur that gives one bacterium resistance to an antibiotic [1]. When the antibiotic is used, non-resistant bacteria are killed [1]. The resistant bacterium survives because it is not affected by the antibiotic [1]. It reproduces rapidly (bacteria reproduce very quickly by binary fission) [1]. The offspring inherit the resistance gene, so the population of resistant bacteria increases over time [1].

Action to slow the spread: Only prescribe antibiotics when necessary / patients should always finish the full course of antibiotics / develop new antibiotics / improve hygiene in hospitals to prevent transmission [1].
5 marks for the explanation (mutation → antibiotic kills non-resistant → resistant survive → rapid reproduction → resistance spreads), 1 mark for a sensible suggestion. This question comes up almost every year — it is one of the most important in all of IGCSE Biology.
