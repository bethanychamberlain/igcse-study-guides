Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Homeostasis Maintenance of a constant internal environment (temperature, glucose, water, pH). —
Negative feedback A change from the set point is detected → a response is triggered that reverses the change → return to normal. —
Insulin Hormone from the pancreas. Lowers blood glucose by converting glucose → glycogen in the liver. Glucagon (that RAISES glucose). Glycogen (that's the storage molecule, not a hormone).
Glucagon Hormone from the pancreas. Raises blood glucose by converting glycogen → glucose in the liver. Glycogen (storage molecule, not the hormone!)
Vasodilation Arterioles near the skin widen → more blood flows near surface → more heat lost. Happens when too hot. Vasoconstriction (that narrows vessels to keep heat IN)
Vasoconstriction Arterioles near the skin narrow → less blood flows near surface → less heat lost. Happens when too cold. Vasodilation (that widens vessels to release heat)
Synapse The gap between two neurones. A chemical neurotransmitter crosses it. Ensures one-way impulse transmission. —
Reflex arc The pathway: receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone → motor neurone → effector. Automatic, fast, no brain. Voluntary action (that DOES involve the brain making a conscious decision)
Accommodation The eye changing the shape of the lens to focus on near or distant objects. —
Auxin Plant growth substance. Stimulates elongation in shoots but inhibits it in roots. Moves to the shaded side. —
Tropism A growth response of a plant towards or away from a stimulus (light = phototropism, gravity = gravitropism). —
Adrenaline Hormone from adrenal glands. "Fight or flight" — increases heart rate, breathing rate, blood to muscles. —

Nervous System vs Endocrine System

Nervous System Endocrine System
Speed Very rapid (milliseconds) Can be slow (seconds to hours)
Message type Electrical impulses along nerves Chemical hormones in the blood
Duration Short-lived (seconds) Long-lasting (hours to years)
Area affected Localised (one precise area) Widespread (many organs)
Examples Reflexes, blinking, movement Growth, puberty, blood glucose
Exam Trap — This Table Appears Every Year
"Compare the nervous and endocrine systems" is one of the most frequently asked questions. Learn all five rows cold.

Body Too Hot vs Body Too Cold

Too Hot Too Cold
Blood vessels Vasodilation (widen, more heat lost) Vasoconstriction (narrow, less heat lost)
Sweat glands More sweating (evaporation cools skin) Less sweating
Muscles — Shivering (rapid contraction = heat)
Body hairs Lie flat (less air trapped) Stand up (trap insulating air)
Detected by Hypothalamus in the brain (the thermostat)

Say It in One Sentence

Homeostasis Keeping the inside of your body constant so enzymes and cells can work at their best.
Negative feedback Something changes → body detects it → body reverses the change → back to normal. Rinse and repeat.
Insulin Blood glucose too HIGH → pancreas releases insulin → liver converts glucose to glycogen → glucose drops.
Glucagon Blood glucose too LOW → pancreas releases glucagon → liver converts glycogen to glucose → glucose rises.
Reflex arc Receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone (spinal cord) → motor neurone → effector — fast, automatic, no brain.
Rods vs cones Rods = dim light, black-and-white, edges of retina. Cones = bright light, colour vision, packed in the fovea.
Auxin Moves to the shaded side of a shoot → that side grows faster → shoot bends towards the light.

Memory Tricks

Insulin vs Glucagon
Insulin = glucose goes into storage (glucose → glycogen, blood glucose goes DOWN). Glucagon = glucose is gone from the blood, so the liver releases more (glycogen → glucose, blood glucose goes UP).
Vasodilation = "Dilate = Wide = Heat Escapes"
Dilate = Door opens wide = heat goes out. Constrict = Closed tight = heat stays in. Also: blood vessels do NOT move — the arterioles change diameter.
Accommodation — The Counterintuitive One
Ciliary muscles contract = ligaments go slack = lens goes fat (for near objects). It seems backwards, but think: muscles pulling inward create slack in the strings holding the lens.
Auxin's Opposite Effects
In shoots: more auxin = MORE growth (shoot bends towards light). In roots: more auxin = LESS growth (root bends downward). Same hormone, opposite effect. Think: "Shoots love auxin, roots hate it."

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Define homeostasis" The maintenance of a constant internal environment
"Explain negative feedback using blood glucose as an example" Glucose rises → pancreas detects → insulin released → liver converts glucose to glycogen → glucose falls. AND the reverse with glucagon. Give both directions.
"Describe what happens when the body is too hot" Vasodilation (more blood near skin, more heat lost), sweating (evaporation cools skin), hairs lie flat
"What is the difference between Type I and Type II diabetes?" Type I: pancreas doesn't produce enough insulin, treated by injection. Type II: cells stop responding to insulin, managed by diet.
"Describe the reflex arc for touching a hot object" Receptor in skin → sensory neurone → relay neurone (spinal cord) → motor neurone → effector (arm muscle) → hand pulls away
"Why do reflexes not involve the brain?" They bypass the brain and go through the spinal cord, making them faster — which protects the body from harm
"Describe accommodation for a near object" Ciliary muscles contract → suspensory ligaments go slack → lens becomes short and fat (more powerful refraction)
"Explain how auxin causes a shoot to bend towards light" Light from one side → auxin moves to shaded side → more growth on shaded side → shoot bends towards the light
