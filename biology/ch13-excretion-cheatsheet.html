Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Excretion Removal of toxic waste products of metabolism, and substances in excess of requirements, from the body. Egestion (that's removing undigested food — faeces were never inside cells)
Deamination The removal of the amino group from excess amino acids in the liver, producing urea. —
Urea The toxic waste product formed in the liver from deamination of excess amino acids. Removed by the kidneys. Urine (that's the liquid containing urea, water, and salts)
Nephron The functional unit of the kidney — a tiny tube where blood is filtered and useful substances are reabsorbed. —
Ultrafiltration High-pressure filtration at the glomerulus. Small molecules are forced out of the blood into Bowman's capsule. Selective reabsorption (that takes useful things BACK into the blood)
Selective reabsorption Useful substances (all glucose, all amino acids, most water) are taken back into the blood from the tubule. Ultrafiltration (that pushes things OUT of the blood)
Glomerulus A ball of capillaries in the nephron where ultrafiltration occurs. Blood arrives under high pressure. —
Bowman's capsule The cup-shaped structure that surrounds the glomerulus and collects the filtrate. —
ADH Anti-diuretic hormone. Controls how much water the collecting duct reabsorbs. More ADH = more water saved. —
Osmoregulation Control of the water content of the body — balancing water gained (drinks, food, respiration) and water lost. Excretion (that's about removing waste, not balancing water)
Dialysis Treatment for kidney failure. Blood flows past a partially permeable membrane; urea diffuses out into dialysis fluid. Kidney transplant (that's surgery to replace the kidney entirely)

Dialysis vs Kidney Transplant

Dialysis Kidney Transplant
How it works Blood passes through a machine; urea diffuses out across a membrane Healthy donor kidney surgically placed in patient
Frequency 3 times/week, 6–8 hours each One-time surgery
Advantages No surgery; no donor needed Normal life; long-term cheaper
Disadvantages Hugely disruptive; expensive long-term Tissue rejection risk; need immunosuppressants
Key science Diffusion down concentration gradient Tissue type matching; immune suppression
Exam Trap — Why Doesn't Glucose Leave During Dialysis?
The dialysis fluid already contains glucose at the same concentration as normal blood. No concentration gradient = no diffusion. This is deliberate — it keeps useful substances in the blood.

What's Where? — Blood vs Filtrate vs Urine

Substance Blood Plasma Filtrate Urine
Protein Present Absent (too big) Absent
Glucose Present Present Absent (fully reabsorbed)
Amino acids Present Present Absent (fully reabsorbed)
Urea Low Low High (concentrated)
Water High High Variable (ADH-controlled)
Exam Trap — Glucose in Urine
If glucose appears in the urine, this is a sign of diabetes — the blood glucose is so high that the kidneys cannot reabsorb it all.

Say It in One Sentence

Excretion Getting rid of waste your cells MADE — not undigested food (that's egestion).
Deamination The liver strips the nitrogen part off excess amino acids and turns it into urea.
Ultrafiltration High-pressure blood forces everything small out into the nephron — a "dump everything" step.
Selective reabsorption The nephron picks back up what the body needs — all glucose, all amino acids, most water.
ADH More ADH = collecting duct reabsorbs more water = smaller volume of concentrated urine.
Kidney's two jobs Excretion (removing urea) and osmoregulation (controlling water balance) — both happen in the nephron.
180 dm³ filtered The kidneys filter 180 litres of water daily but only 1.5 litres leaves as urine — over 99% is reabsorbed.

Memory Tricks

Excretion vs Egestion
Excretion = exit of waste from cells (metabolic waste). Egestion = ejecting food that was never inside cells. If it was never absorbed, it's egestion.
Filter Then Pick Up
Think of the nephron as emptying your whole bag onto the table (ultrafiltration), then picking up only what you want to keep (selective reabsorption). What's left on the table is urine.
Protein Too Big
Imagine the glomerulus filter like a kitchen sieve. Water, salt, sugar (glucose) fall through. But a tennis ball (protein) is too big to fit through the holes. That's why there's no protein in the filtrate.
ADH = Anti-Diuretic = Anti-Peeing
"Diuretic" means something that makes you pee. "Anti-diuretic" = anti-peeing. More ADH = less peeing = more water kept in the body. Simple.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Define excretion" Removal of toxic waste products of metabolism and substances in excess from the body
"Name two excretory products and how they are removed" CO₂ (from respiration, removed by lungs) and urea (from deamination in liver, removed by kidneys)
"Explain why protein is absent from the filtrate" Protein molecules are too large to pass through the gaps in the glomerular capillary walls
"Explain why glucose is absent from urine" Glucose is completely reabsorbed by selective reabsorption in the first coiled tubule
"Explain how a dialysis machine removes urea" Urea diffuses from the blood (high concentration) through a partially permeable membrane into the dialysis fluid (low concentration)
"Why does the dialysis fluid contain glucose?" To ensure no concentration gradient for glucose, so glucose does not diffuse out of the blood
"Why might a transplanted kidney be rejected?" The recipient's immune system recognises the donor kidney as foreign and attacks it (tissue rejection)
"What does ADH do?" Increases the permeability of the collecting duct to water, so more water is reabsorbed into the blood
