Section 11.1 Respiration Provides Energy for Life
Every living cell needs energy. Respiration is the process that releases it — it happens in all living cells, all the time, not just when you breathe.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Respiration is the release of energy from food substances (such as glucose) in all living cells. It is a set of chemical reactions that break down nutrient molecules, controlled by enzymes.

!
Don't Confuse — Respiration vs Breathing

Respiration is a chemical process that happens inside cells. Breathing (ventilation) is a physical process — moving air in and out of the lungs. They are NOT the same thing. The exam will try to catch you on this.

W
Write This Down — Energy Conversions

Photosynthesis converts light energy → chemical energy (stored in food).
Respiration converts chemical energy (in food) → energy for work in cells.

Much of the energy eventually leaves organisms as heat.

W
Write This Down — What Cells Use Energy For

Cells use energy from respiration for:
• Growth — building large molecules from smaller ones (e.g. protein synthesis from amino acids)
• Active transport — moving molecules against a concentration gradient (e.g. mineral uptake by roots)
• Cell division — replication and separation of chromosomes
• Muscle contraction — movement
• Nerve impulses — pumping ions in and out of nerve cells
• Maintaining body temperature — heat released from reactions

W
Write This Down — ATP

The energy released by respiration is stored in small ‘packets’ in a molecule called adenosine triphosphate (ATP). ATP acts as a short-term energy store that drives reactions inside the cell.

?
Why This Matters

An exam question might ask: “Why do root hair cells contain many mitochondria?” The answer links directly here — they need lots of energy (from respiration) for active transport of mineral ions.

  • I can define respiration without looking
  • I know the difference between respiration and breathing
  • I can list at least 4 uses of energy in living organisms
  • I know what ATP is and why it matters
Section 11.1 continued Aerobic Respiration
The main type of respiration — the one that releases the most energy — requires oxygen.
W
Write This Down — Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration is the release of energy from glucose in the presence of oxygen. It takes place in the mitochondria of cells.

Word equation:
glucose + oxygen → energy + carbon dioxide + water

Chemical equation:
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → energy + 6CO₂ + 6H₂O

T
Study Tip — Learn the Equation

Write the word equation from memory 5 times on a blank sheet. If you can do it without looking, you know it. The word equation is worth 2 marks on its own. The chemical equation is a bonus that shows top-level understanding.

  • I can write the word equation for aerobic respiration from memory
  • I know aerobic respiration happens in the mitochondria
Section 11.2 Contraction of Muscles Requires Energy
Muscles need energy to contract. During hard exercise, aerobic respiration alone can’t keep up — so cells switch to a backup system.
W
Write This Down — Muscles and Energy

Muscles are collections of muscle fibres. When many fibres shorten at the same time, the muscle contracts and pulls on bones to create movement.

Muscles need a constant supply of glucose and oxygen (delivered by the blood) for aerobic respiration. They also have extensive capillary beds to supply nutrients and carry away waste (CO₂ and heat).

W
Write This Down — Anaerobic Respiration

Anaerobic respiration is the release of energy from glucose without using oxygen. It happens when muscles are working so hard that the heart and lungs cannot supply enough oxygen.

Word equation (in humans):
glucose → energy + lactic acid

!
Don't Confuse — Two Types of Anaerobic Respiration

In humans/animals: glucose → lactic acid (+ energy)
In yeast (fermentation): glucose → alcohol + carbon dioxide (+ energy)

They are different products. The exam will ask which organism — read the question carefully. Yeast fermentation is used in brewing and baking.

W
Write This Down — Drawbacks of Anaerobic Respiration

• It releases only about one-twentieth (1/20) of the energy per glucose molecule compared to aerobic respiration — much less efficient.
• Lactic acid is poisonous — if it builds up in muscles it inhibits contraction, causing fatigue (and eventually, death if not removed).

?
Why This Matters

This is why you get a “burning” feeling in your muscles during a sprint — that’s lactic acid building up. It’s also why you keep panting after you stop running.

  • I can write the word equation for anaerobic respiration in humans
  • I know the word equation for anaerobic respiration in yeast
  • I can state two drawbacks of anaerobic respiration
Section 11.2 continued Oxygen Debt and Recovery
After hard exercise, your body has to “pay back” the oxygen it couldn’t supply during the workout.
W
Write This Down — Oxygen Debt

Oxygen debt is the extra oxygen that must be taken in after exercise to break down the lactic acid that built up during anaerobic respiration.

Lactic acid is carried in the blood to the heart, liver, and kidneys, where it is oxidised to pyruvate using the extra oxygen. This releases energy by aerobic respiration.

W
Write This Down — Rest, Exercise, Recovery
Phase What happens Heart rate Breathing
Rest Aerobic respiration meets all energy needs; no lactic acid builds up ~70 bpm ~15 breaths/min
Hard exercise Anaerobic respiration kicks in; lactic acid accumulates; oxygen debt builds ~140 bpm ~50 breaths/min
Recovery Deep, fast breathing continues to supply extra O₂ to break down lactic acid and repay the oxygen debt ~140 falling to normal ~50 falling to normal
T
Study Tip — Think of a Loan

Oxygen debt is like a loan. During exercise you “borrow” energy without paying oxygen. After exercise you “pay it back” by breathing heavily to supply extra oxygen. If an exam question asks “Why does breathing rate stay high after exercise?” — the answer is oxygen debt.

  • I can define oxygen debt
  • I know what happens to lactic acid during recovery
  • I can explain why breathing stays fast after exercise stops
Section 11.3 The Measurement of Respiration
You can’t see respiration happening, but you can measure its products — heat, CO₂ release, and oxygen consumption.
W
Write This Down — Measuring Anaerobic Respiration in Yeast

Yeast in a sugar solution produces carbon dioxide (and ethanol) during anaerobic respiration. You can measure the volume of CO₂ collected in a measuring cylinder over time.

A layer of liquid paraffin on top of the yeast suspension keeps oxygen out, ensuring only anaerobic respiration occurs.

W
Write This Down — The Respirometer

A respirometer measures oxygen consumption by living organisms (e.g. germinating seeds, maggots).

How it works:
• Potassium hydroxide (KOH) absorbs CO₂ produced during respiration
• Since CO₂ is removed, any change in gas volume must be due to oxygen being used up
• The drop of coloured liquid in the capillary tube moves as volume decreases
• The distance moved per unit time = rate of oxygen consumption

W
Write This Down — Detecting CO₂ Release

Hydrogencarbonate indicator changes colour when CO₂ dissolves in it:
• Purple at high pH (alkaline) → Red at neutral → Yellow/orange at low pH (acidic, more CO₂)
Limewater turns cloudy/milky when CO₂ is bubbled through it.

?
Why This Matters

The respirometer and CO₂ indicator experiments are common practical questions. Know the role of KOH (absorbs CO₂) and why you need a control (dead organisms or glass beads to show that changes are due to living respiration, not temperature/pressure changes).

T
Study Tip — Vacuum Flask Experiment

In the textbook, living peas in a vacuum flask get warmer over time but dead peas don’t. This proves respiration releases heat. If an exam gives you this data, say: “The living peas are respiring, releasing heat energy. The dead peas are not respiring, so no heat is released.” Always mention the control (flask B with dead peas).

  • I know how to measure anaerobic respiration in yeast
  • I can explain how a respirometer works and the role of KOH
  • I know what hydrogencarbonate indicator and limewater detect
