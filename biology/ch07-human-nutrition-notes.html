Sections 7.1 – 7.2 Food Groups and a Balanced Diet
All living organisms need food. Humans are heterotrophic — we can't make our own food and must take in organic substances. A balanced diet has seven components.
W
Write This Down — The Seven Components of a Balanced Diet

A balanced diet provides all nutrients in the correct proportions. The ideal ratio is roughly 1/7 fat, 1/7 protein, 5/7 carbohydrate. The seven components are:

1. Carbohydrates — main energy source. Sources: rice, potatoes, wheat, pasta. Digested to glucose. Excess stored as glycogen then as fat.
2. Lipids (fats) — energy store, insulation (electrical around nerve cells, thermal beneath skin), cell membranes, steroid hormones. Sources: meat, eggs, milk, nuts. Contain saturated or unsaturated fatty acids.
3. Proteins — many functions: enzymes, transport molecules (e.g. haemoglobin), structural materials (e.g. muscle), hormones (e.g. insulin), defence (antibodies). Sources: meat, fish, eggs, beans, soya. Digested to amino acids. 20 amino acids; some are essential (must come from diet as the body cannot make them).
4. Vitamins — needed in small amounts. Vitamin C (citrus fruits, green leafy vegetables) prevents scurvy. Vitamin D (liver, dairy, eggs, fish liver oil; also made by body in sunlight) prevents rickets (soft bones).
5. Minerals — Iron (red meat, liver, leafy vegetables) needed to make haemoglobin; deficiency = anaemia. Calcium (milk, cheese, fish) for bones and teeth; deficiency = weak bones, poor blood clotting, muscle spasms.
6. Water — 70% of body. Solvent for reactions, transport medium. Lose 5% = unconsciousness. Lose 10% = fatal.
7. Dietary fibre — indigestible cellulose from plant cell walls. Gives bulk to faeces, prevents constipation, may reduce bowel cancer risk.

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions love asking you to link a deficiency to a disease. Memorise these four pairs: Vitamin C → scurvy, Vitamin D → rickets, Iron → anaemia, Protein → kwashiorkor/marasmus.

T

  • I know the difference between autotrophic and heterotrophic nutrition
Sections 7.3 – 7.5 Energy from Food and Malnutrition
Food is fuel. The energy in food can be measured, and different people need different amounts. Getting the balance wrong causes malnutrition.
W
Write This Down — Energy in Food

Energy is released from food by respiration. Energy values per gram: Fats = 39 kJ/g, Proteins = 20 kJ/g, Carbohydrates = 17 kJ/g.

Energy is measured using a food calorimeter (burn food under water, measure temperature rise). The formula:

Energy value (cal/g) = (temperature rise x volume of water) / mass of food

Convert to kJ: multiply calories per gram by 4.2, then divide by 1000.

W
Write This Down — Energy Needs and Malnutrition

Energy requirement = BMR + activity. The basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the energy needed just to stay alive. Energy needs depend on age, sex, and occupation/activity.

Malnutrition = literally "bad feeding." It can mean:

• Too much food → obesity → risks: diabetes, breathing difficulties, atherosclerosis (narrowing of the arteries), arthritis
• Too little food → undernutrition → body tissues waste away
• Wrong proportions → deficiency diseases

Kwashiorkor — protein deficiency; swollen abdomen, poor muscle development. Often in children weaned onto high-carb, low-protein diet.
Marasmus — general starvation; not enough energy or protein; body tissues waste away, very thin with wrinkled skin.

!
Don't Confuse

Malnutrition is NOT the same as undernutrition. Malnutrition includes overeating and eating the wrong proportions. An obese person eating too much fat and sugar is malnourished even though they are eating plenty of food.

  • I know which food type gives the most energy per gram (fats)
  • I can describe how to measure energy in food using a calorimeter
  • I know what affects energy requirements (age, sex, activity)
  • I can distinguish between kwashiorkor and marasmus
Sections 7.6 – 7.7 The Alimentary Canal and Ingestion
Nutrition is the taking in of nutrients which are organic substances and mineral ions, then absorbing and assimilating them. Food must be broken down before the body can use it. The alimentary canal (gut) is a specialised tube running from mouth to anus, and each region has a specific job.
W
Write This Down — The Six Processes of Nutrition

Nutrition involves six processes in sequence:

1. Ingestion — taking food into the mouth
2. Mechanical digestion — teeth break food into smaller pieces (no chemical change)
3. Chemical digestion — enzymes break large insoluble molecules into small soluble ones
4. Absorption — small molecules cross gut wall into blood or lymph
5. Assimilation — digested food molecules become part of body cells, used for energy, growth and repair
6. Egestion — undigested food passes out through the anus as faeces

W
Write This Down — Organs of the Alimentary Canal
Organ Function
Mouth Ingestion. Teeth chew food (mastication), tongue mixes with saliva to form a bolus.
Oesophagus Muscular tube. Moves bolus to stomach by peristalsis (waves of muscle contraction).
Stomach Muscular bag. Churns food with gastric juice to form chyme. Gastric glands produce three secretions: mucus (protects stomach wall), pepsin (protease enzyme), and hydrochloric acid (acidic conditions for pepsin, kills bacteria).
Liver Produces bile (stored in gall bladder). Bile emulsifies fats and neutralises stomach acid.
Pancreas Produces pancreatic juice containing amylase, protease (trypsin), and lipase. Also produces hydrogencarbonate to neutralise acid.
Small intestine Duodenum: first part, receives bile and pancreatic juice, continues digestion. Ileum: longest part, where digested food is absorbed into the blood and lymphatic system via villi.
Large intestine Reabsorbs water from gut contents; also absorbs some vitamins and minerals. The colon absorbs water; rectum stores faeces.
Anus Exit point for faeces. Controlled by sphincter muscles.
W
Write This Down — Teeth and Mastication

Four types of teeth: Incisors (cutting/biting), Canines (holding/cutting), Premolars (chewing/crushing), Molars (chewing/crushing). Adults have 32 teeth.

Tooth structure: Enamel (hardest tissue, protects crown), Dentine (bulk of tooth, contains calcium), Pulp cavity (blood vessels + nerves), Cement (anchors root to jaw).

Dental decay: Bacteria in plaque convert sugars to acids → acids dissolve enamel → dentine exposed → infection reaches pulp. Prevented by: low sugar diet, brushing twice daily, avoiding fizzy drinks.

?
Why This Matters

Peristalsis is often tested as a diagram question: draw circular muscles contracting behind the bolus, pushing it forward. The key word is "waves of muscular contraction." Fibre gives the muscles something to push against.

!
Don't Confuse — Egestion vs Excretion

Egestion is removing undigested food that was never absorbed — it passes straight through the gut. Excretion is removing metabolic waste products that were made inside cells (like CO₂ and urea). Faeces are egested, NOT excreted.

  • I can name each organ of the alimentary canal and its function
  • I know the four types of teeth and what each does
  • I can explain how dental decay happens
  • I know the difference between egestion and excretion
Section 7.8 Enzymes and Chemical Digestion
This is the most exam-heavy part of the chapter. You must know which enzyme breaks down which food, where it works, and what it produces. You scored poorly on this in the Easter test — so pay close attention.
W
Write This Down — Why Digestion Is Needed

Digestion is the breakdown of large, insoluble molecules into small, soluble molecules. This is necessary because:

• Large molecules are too big to cross the gut wall into the blood
• Large molecules are insoluble and cannot be transported in blood plasma

Digestion uses hydrolysis reactions (breaking bonds by adding water), catalysed by enzymes.

W
Write This Down — The Role of Bile

Bile is made in the liver, stored in the gall bladder, and released into the duodenum. It is NOT an enzyme. Bile does two things:

• Emulsifies fats — breaks large fat globules into tiny droplets, increasing surface area for lipase to work on
• Neutralises acid — contains hydrogencarbonate which raises pH of chyme from acidic (stomach) to alkaline (needed for intestinal enzymes)

!
Don't Confuse — This Loses Students Marks

Bile is NOT an enzyme. It does not chemically digest anything. It physically breaks up fat into smaller droplets (emulsification). The actual chemical digestion of fat is done by lipase. If the exam asks "name an enzyme that digests fat," the answer is lipase, NOT bile.

!
Don't Confuse — Starch Digestion Happens in Stages

Starch is not converted directly to glucose. It goes: Starch → (amylase) → Maltose → (maltase) → Glucose. Two enzymes, two steps. The exam will penalise you if you write "amylase converts starch to glucose."

Section 7.9 Absorption and Assimilation
Once food is digested into small soluble molecules, it must be absorbed into the blood and then used by cells. The small intestine is beautifully adapted for this job.
W
Write This Down — Villi and Absorption

Absorption happens mainly in the ileum (lower small intestine). The ileum is adapted for absorption:

• It is very long (~6 m) — food takes a long time to pass through, giving time for absorption
• The inner surface is folded into tiny finger-like projections called villi (singular: villus) — massively increases surface area
• Each villus has microvilli on its surface — increases surface area by a further 20 times
• The epithelium (wall) of each villus is one cell thick — short diffusion distance
• Each villus has a dense network of capillaries (for glucose and amino acids) and a lacteal (for fatty acids and glycerol)

W
Write This Down — What Gets Absorbed Where

• Glucose and amino acids → absorbed into blood capillaries in the villus → travel via the hepatic portal vein to the liver
• Fatty acids and glycerol → absorbed into the lacteal (part of the lymphatic system)
• Water is reabsorbed mainly in the large intestine (colon)

W
Write This Down — The Liver and Assimilation

Assimilation = digested food molecules become part of body cells. The liver plays a central role:

• Stores excess glucose as glycogen; converts glycogen back to glucose when blood sugar drops
• Transamination — converts one amino acid into another that the body needs
• Deamination — removes the amino group from excess amino acids, converting it to urea (excreted by kidneys)
• Produces bile for fat digestion
• Detoxification — breaks down alcohol and other toxins
• Stores iron from old red blood cells

?
Why This Matters

"Describe the adaptations of the villus for absorption" is a classic 4-mark question. The four marks are: (1) large surface area (villi + microvilli), (2) thin wall (one cell thick = short diffusion distance), (3) good blood supply (capillary network maintains concentration gradient), (4) lacteal for fats. Learn these four points as a set.

Putting It All Together The Journey of a Ham Sandwich
If you can trace a ham sandwich through the entire digestive system, you understand the whole chapter. The exam often asks exactly this.
W
Write This Down — Trace the Journey

Bread (starch): Mouth → salivary amylase breaks starch to maltose → digestion pauses in stomach (too acidic for amylase) → pancreatic amylase continues starch → maltose in duodenum → maltase on ileum wall converts maltose → glucose → absorbed into capillaries → liver stores as glycogen

Ham (protein): Mouth → chewed mechanically → stomach → pepsin (protease) + HCl break protein to amino acids → trypsin in duodenum continues → amino acids absorbed into capillaries → liver (transamination or deamination)

Butter (fat): Mouth/stomach → no enzyme acts on fat yet → duodenum → bile emulsifies fat into small droplets → lipase breaks fat to fatty acids + glycerol → absorbed into lacteal

  • I can trace protein from mouth to amino acids absorbed in ileum
