Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Gene A length of DNA that codes for a specific protein (and therefore a characteristic). Allele (an allele is a specific VERSION of a gene)
Allele An alternative form of a gene. E.g. the gene for eye colour has alleles for brown and blue. Gene (the gene is the "question," the allele is the "answer")
Dominant The allele that shows in the phenotype whenever it is present. Written as a CAPITAL letter (e.g. B). Recessive (recessive only shows when homozygous — bb)
Homozygous Both alleles are the same (e.g. BB or bb). Heterozygous (two different alleles — Bb)
Heterozygous The two alleles are different (e.g. Bb). Shows the dominant phenotype. Homozygous (same alleles)
Genotype The combination of alleles an organism has (e.g. Bb). Phenotype (that's what you can see/observe)
Phenotype The observable characteristic (e.g. brown eyes). Genotype (that's the letters, not the appearance)
Codominance When both alleles are expressed in the heterozygote, producing a third phenotype. Neither allele is dominant. Dominant/recessive (in codominance, BOTH show)
Diploid (2n) Full chromosome number in body cells (46 in humans — 23 pairs). Haploid (half the number — in gametes)
Haploid (n) Half the chromosome number, found in gametes (23 in humans). Diploid (full number — in body cells)
Sex-linked A gene located on the X chromosome but NOT on the shorter Y. E.g. colour blindness. —

Mitosis vs Meiosis — Side by Side

Mitosis Meiosis
Daughter cells 2 4
Genetically identical? Yes (clones) No (all different)
Chromosome number Same as parent (diploid) Half (haploid)
Purpose Growth and repair Making gametes
Where? All body tissues Testes and ovaries only
Number of divisions 1 2
Memory Trick
Mitosis = Makes identical cells. Meiosis = halves the number and makes gametes.

Say It in One Sentence

DNA structure A double helix with base pairs A-T and G-C — about 600 base pairs code for one average protein.
Gene → Protein A gene codes for a protein, and that protein determines a characteristic (e.g. keratin → hair).
DNA replication The double helix unzips, each strand acts as a template, free nucleotides pair up → two identical copies.
Transcription DNA code is copied into mRNA in the nucleus (RNA uses uracil instead of thymine).
Translation mRNA travels to a ribosome where the code is read and amino acids are assembled into a protein.
Test cross Cross an organism showing the dominant phenotype with a homozygous recessive (bb) — if ANY offspring are recessive, the parent was Bb.
Sex determination XX = female, XY = male. The father's sperm determines the sex (he passes X or Y).

Memory Tricks

Base Pairing: Apple Tree, Gold Coin
Apple Tree = A pairs with T. Gold Coin = G pairs with C. Given one strand (e.g. ATGCCA), write the other by swapping partners (TACGGT).
Replication / Transcription / Translation
Replication = DNA makes more DNA. Transcription = DNA makes mRNA. Translation = mRNA makes Protein. Three processes, three products — don't mix them up.
Punnett Square Setup
Always follow 5 steps: (1) state symbols, (2) write parental genotypes, (3) write gametes, (4) draw the Punnett square, (5) list genotype AND phenotype ratios. Miss any step = lost marks.
Colour Blindness Is on the X
Males only have ONE X, so one copy of the recessive allele = colour blind (XcY). Females need TWO copies (XcXc) — that's why it's much rarer in females.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What is the difference between a gene and an allele?" A gene codes for a characteristic; an allele is a specific version of that gene (e.g. brown vs blue for eye colour)
"What is the genotype of a carrier?" Heterozygous (e.g. Aa) — carries the recessive allele but shows the dominant phenotype
"Two unaffected parents have an affected child" Both parents must be carriers (heterozygous) and the trait is recessive
"Why is colour blindness more common in males?" Males have only one X chromosome — one copy of the recessive allele = affected. Females need two copies.
"What is codominance?" Both alleles are expressed in the heterozygote — produces a third phenotype. Example: blood group AB (IAIB)
"Bb × Bb — what ratio?" Genotype: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb. Phenotype: 3 dominant : 1 recessive. 25% chance of recessive.
"What are stem cells?" Unspecialised cells that can divide by mitosis and differentiate into specialised cell types
"Why is sickle cell common in malarial areas?" Heterozygous carriers (HbSHbA) are resistant to malaria — so natural selection keeps the allele in the population
