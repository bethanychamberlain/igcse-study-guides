Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
List the seven characteristics of living organisms.
7 marks
Answer
Movement [1], Respiration [1], Sensitivity (irritability) [1], Growth [1], Reproduction [1], Excretion [1], Nutrition [1].
MRS GREN. One mark per characteristic named. If you can only remember six, you've already dropped a mark — learn all seven.

2
Name the five kingdoms used to classify living organisms.
5 marks
Answer
Animals [1], Plants [1], Fungi [1], Protoctists [1], Prokaryotes [1].
Spelling matters. "Protoctists" not "protists" (though some mark schemes accept both). "Prokaryotes" not "bacteria" — bacteria is an example, prokaryote is the kingdom name.

3
State two features that all plants share.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of: they contain chlorophyll / carry out photosynthesis [1]; they have a cellulose cell wall [1]; they are multicellular [1]; they are autotrophs [1].
The two safest answers are chlorophyll/photosynthesis and cellulose cell wall. These distinguish plants from every other kingdom.

4
Butterflies are insects for which of the following reasons?
A Because they lay eggs
B Because they can fly
C Because they have three main body parts
D Because they feed on nectar
1 mark
Answer
C — Because they have three main body parts (head, thorax, abdomen) [1].
Many animals lay eggs (A) or fly (B — bats fly but are mammals), and many organisms feed on nectar (D). The defining feature of insects is three body sections + six legs.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain features and give reasons, not just name things.
5
Fungi are not placed in the Plant Kingdom. State three reasons why fungi are classified separately from plants.
3 marks
Answer
Any three of:
• Fungi do not contain chlorophyll / cannot photosynthesise [1]
• Fungi have cell walls made of chitin, not cellulose [1]
• Fungi feed by saprotrophic nutrition (external digestion and absorption), not by making their own food [1]
• Fungi do not have roots, stems, or leaves [1]
The classic "why are fungi not plants?" question. You need three distinct points. "No chlorophyll" and "no photosynthesis" count as ONE point, not two.

6
State one way in which a bacterium differs from all of the organisms listed below, and state one way in which a virus differs from a bacterium.

Organisms: spider, insect, fungus, fern, protoctist.
2 marks
Answer
Bacterium vs others: A bacterium has no true nucleus / no membrane-bound organelles [1].

Virus vs bacterium: A virus is not a cell / has no cytoplasm / cannot reproduce on its own (can only reproduce inside a living cell) / has no cell wall [1].
For the first part, all listed organisms (spider, insect, fungus, fern, protoctist) have cells with a nucleus — bacteria do not. For the second, the safest answer is "a virus is not a cell" or "a virus can only reproduce inside a host cell."

7
A student observes an organism with the following features: it is multicellular, it has no cell wall, and it feeds on organic molecules. Which kingdom does it belong to? Explain your answer using the features given.
3 marks
Answer
It belongs to the Animal Kingdom [1]. It has no cell wall, which rules out plants (cellulose cell wall) and fungi (chitin cell wall) [1]. It feeds on organic molecules (heterotroph), which rules out plants (autotrophs) [1].
This is a "use the dichotomous key" question in disguise. Walk through the features one by one, ruling out kingdoms as you go.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Viruses are not classified in any of the five kingdoms. Explain why scientists do not consider viruses to be living organisms, with reference to the characteristics of life and the structure of viruses.
5 marks
Answer
Viruses do not show the seven characteristics of living organisms on their own [1]. They cannot carry out respiration, nutrition, growth, or excretion independently [1]. They can only reproduce inside a living host cell, using the host's cellular machinery [1]. Viruses have a very simple structure — just genetic material (DNA or RNA) surrounded by a protein coat [1]. They have no cytoplasm, no organelles, and no cell membrane, so they cannot carry out metabolic reactions [1].
5 marks, 5 ideas: (1) don't show characteristics of life independently, (2) can't do respiration/nutrition/etc., (3) can only reproduce in a host, (4) simple structure, (5) no cytoplasm/organelles for metabolism. Chain your points together logically.

9
The five classes of vertebrates are fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals. Describe how these classes show a gradual adaptation from life in water to life on dry land. Refer to skin covering, gas exchange, and reproduction in your answer.
6 marks
Answer
Skin covering: Fish have wet/mucus-covered scales suited to water. Amphibians have moist, permeable skin that must stay damp. Reptiles have dry, waterproof scales that prevent water loss on land [1]. Birds have feathers for insulation and flight [1]. Mammals have hair/fur for insulation.

Gas exchange: Fish use gills to extract oxygen from water [1]. Amphibians use lungs and skin. Reptiles, birds, and mammals use lungs only, which are more efficient on land [1].

Reproduction: Fish and amphibians lay eggs in water (external fertilisation) [1]. Reptiles lay eggs with waterproof shells on land. Birds lay eggs with hard shells. Mammals have internal development and feed young with milk — completely independent of water for reproduction [1].
The pattern is: each class is less dependent on water. Fish = fully aquatic, amphibians = need water nearby, reptiles = first fully land-adapted, birds and mammals = most independent. Two marks per category (skin, gas exchange, reproduction).

10
Scientists use the binomial system to name organisms. (a) State what is meant by the binomial system. (b) Explain why using a universal scientific naming system is more useful than using common names. (c) State the correct order of the hierarchy of classification from largest group to smallest.
6 marks
Answer
(a) The binomial system gives every organism a two-part scientific name [1] made up of the genus (capital letter) and species (lowercase), written in italics [1].

(b) Common names vary between languages and regions — for example, a lion is called "león" in Spanish and "simba" in Swahili [1]. A scientific name like Panthera leo is understood by scientists worldwide, avoiding confusion [1].

(c) Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species [2].
(a) "Two-part name" is essential for mark 1. Genus + species for mark 2. (b) The point is universal understanding — give a concrete example if you can. (c) All seven in order for 2 marks; if one is wrong or missing, usually 1 mark.
