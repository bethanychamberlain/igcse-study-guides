Section 6.1 Photosynthesis and Plant Nutrition
Plants don't eat — they make their own food using sunlight. This is photosynthesis, and it's the foundation of almost all life on Earth.
W
Write This Down — Key Terms

Plants are autotrophic (self-feeding) — they take simple substances from their environment and use light energy to build them into complex food compounds.

Chloroplasts are the organelles where photosynthesis takes place. They contain a green pigment called chlorophyll.

Chloroplasts act as energy transducers — they convert light energy into chemical energy (stored in glucose).

W
Write This Down — THE Equation (Learn This Exactly)

Word equation:
carbon dioxide + water ⟶ glucose + oxygen
(with light energy and chlorophyll above/below the arrow)

Symbol equation:
6CO₂ + 6H₂O ⟶ C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
(light energy / chlorophyll above the arrow)

!
Don't Confuse — This Got You on the Easter Test

You scored 33% on photosynthesis. The most common error: writing the equation wrong or leaving out parts. The word equation MUST include "light energy" and "chlorophyll" — without them you lose marks. Also: the product is glucose, not "sugar" or "food" (those are too vague for the exam).

W
Write This Down — What Plants Do With Glucose

Glucose from photosynthesis is used to make:
• Sucrose — transport form of carbohydrate (carried in phloem)
• Starch — storage form of carbohydrate
• Cellulose — structural carbohydrate (in cell walls)
• Amino acids and proteins — need nitrate and sulfate ions from soil
• Lipids (fats and oils) — for long-term energy storage and cell membranes
• Nucleic acids (including DNA) — need phosphate from soil

?
Why This Matters

The exam may ask: "Why do animals depend on plants?" Two reasons: (1) plants provide food compounds for animals to eat, and (2) plants release oxygen needed for aerobic respiration in all organisms.

Section 6.1 continued Testing Leaves for Starch
Some glucose is stored as starch in plant cells. We can test for it to prove photosynthesis has occurred.
W
Write This Down — The Starch Test (Step by Step)

Before testing: Plant must be destarched — kept in a dark cupboard for 48 hours so it uses up all stored starch.

Testing procedure:
1. Boil the leaf in water (~2 min) — kills the cells, breaks down cell walls, allows iodine to penetrate.
2. Warm in ethanol — removes the green chlorophyll (so it won't mask the colour result). Ethanol turns green.
3. Dip in warm water — softens the now-brittle leaf.
4. Place on white tile, add iodine solution — blue-black = starch present; brown/orange = no starch.

W
Write This Down — What the Experiments Prove

Experiment A (black paper covers part of leaf — blocks light): Covered part has no starch → light is needed for photosynthesis.
Experiment B (soda lime removes CO₂; hydrogencarbonate provides CO₂): No starch without CO₂ → carbon dioxide is needed.
Experiment C (variegated leaf — green and white parts): Only green parts produce starch → chlorophyll is needed.

!
Don't Confuse

Iodine tests for starch, not glucose. If the exam says "test for glucose," the answer is Benedict's solution (heat and look for orange/red). Different tests — don't mix them up.

  • I can describe the starch test procedure (all 4 steps)
  • I know why we destarch the plant first
  • I can explain what experiments A, B, and C prove
Section 6.2 The Rate of Photosynthesis
How fast is photosynthesis happening? We can measure it by counting oxygen bubbles or collecting oxygen gas.
W
Write This Down — Qualitative vs Quantitative

Qualitative test (iodine/starch): tells you whether photosynthesis occurred, but not how fast.

Quantitative test: measures the rate of photosynthesis. Done by measuring the volume of oxygen released in a fixed time, or by counting oxygen bubbles per minute from pondweed.

W
Write This Down — Pondweed Experiment

Apparatus: Pondweed (e.g. Elodea) in a beaker of water, inverted funnel, test tube collecting gas, light source.
• Independent variable (what you change): light intensity (move lamp closer/further)
• Dependent variable (what you measure): volume of oxygen released per minute
• Control variables (keep constant): temperature, CO₂ concentration, species of plant, number of leaves

T
Study Tip

The exam loves to ask about this experiment. Remember: the assumption is that each bubble is the same size. If bubbles vary in size, counting them is less accurate than collecting and measuring the total volume.

  • I know the difference between qualitative and quantitative tests
  • I can describe the pondweed oxygen experiment
  • I can name the independent, dependent, and control variables
Section 6.3 Leaf Structure and Photosynthesis
The leaf is a photosynthesis factory. Every part is adapted for the job. The exam will ask you to name layers and explain their adaptations.
W
Write This Down — Leaf Layers (Top to Bottom)
Layer Structure Adaptation for Photosynthesis
Waxy cuticle Waterproof waxy coating, thicker on top surface Reduces water loss; transparent so light passes through
Upper epidermis Single layer of cells, no chloroplasts, transparent Lets light pass straight through to palisade cells; protects against disease
Palisade mesophyll Tall, thin cells packed tightly in columns near the top Packed with many chloroplasts for maximum light absorption
Spongy mesophyll Loosely packed round cells with large air spaces Air spaces allow diffusion of CO₂ and O₂ through the leaf
Vein (vascular bundle) Xylem + phloem Xylem delivers water and minerals; phloem carries away glucose (as sucrose)
Lower epidermis Contains stomata (tiny pores) Stomata allow CO₂ in and O₂ out; mostly on bottom to reduce water loss
W
Write This Down — Stomata and Guard Cells

Stomata (singular: stoma) are tiny pores in the lower epidermis. They allow CO₂ in and O₂ out.

Guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata:
• When the plant has plenty of water: guard cells become turgid (swollen), they curve apart, and the stoma opens.
• When the plant is short of water: guard cells become flaccid (floppy), they close together, and the stoma closes.
• Stomata close in the dark (no photosynthesis, so no need for CO₂).

!
Don't Confuse — This Is a Common Exam Error

Stomata are mainly on the lower epidermis, not the upper. Why? The lower surface is less exposed to the sun, so less water evaporates through the open stomata. If the exam asks "Why are most stomata on the lower surface?", the answer is: to reduce water loss.

?
Why This Matters — You Scored 33% on Leaf Structure

The exam will show a cross-section diagram of a leaf and ask you to label it and explain adaptations. If you can name all six layers and give one adaptation each, that's 12 marks of easy knowledge right there. Draw and label the diagram yourself — don't just read it.

T
Study Tip — Draw It From Memory

Section 6.4 The Control of Photosynthesis — Limiting Factors
Photosynthesis depends on several factors. The slowest one acts as a bottleneck — this is the limiting factor.
W
Write This Down — Requirements for Photosynthesis

For photosynthesis to occur, a plant needs:
• Light (provides energy to split water molecules)
• Chlorophyll (absorbs the light energy; requires magnesium ions from soil)
• Carbon dioxide (raw material/substrate)
• Water (raw material/substrate)
• Suitable temperature (for enzyme activity)

W
Write This Down — Limiting Factor Definition

A limiting factor is the factor that is in shortest supply, and therefore restricts the rate of the overall process. It is the factor nearest to its minimum value.

The three main limiting factors are:
1. Light intensity — more light = faster photosynthesis, until another factor limits it
2. Carbon dioxide concentration — the substrate most often in short supply
3. Temperature — affects the rate of enzyme-controlled reactions in photosynthesis

W
Write This Down — Reading the Graph

The classic limiting factor graph (CO₂ concentration vs rate at two temperatures):
• At low CO₂: increasing CO₂ increases the rate → CO₂ is the limiting factor.
• The line levels off (plateaus) at high CO₂ → CO₂ is no longer limiting; something else is (temperature or light).
• At 25°C the line plateaus higher than at 15°C → at the plateau, temperature was the limiting factor.

!
Don't Confuse

Water is rarely a limiting factor for photosynthesis. Why? Because many other plant processes depend on water too — the plant will wilt and close its stomata long before water shortage limits photosynthesis directly.

T
Study Tip — How to Answer "Identify the Limiting Factor"

If the graph line is still going up, the factor on the x-axis is limiting. If the line has levelled off, the factor on the x-axis is no longer limiting — something else is. The exam will often give you data at two temperatures and ask which factor is limiting at each stage. Follow the slope.

  • I can define "limiting factor"
  • I can name the three main limiting factors
  • I can read a limiting factor graph and identify what's limiting at each stage
  • I know why water is rarely a limiting factor
Section 6.5 Greenhouses — Controlling Photosynthesis
Greenhouse growers use their knowledge of limiting factors to maximise crop yields.
W
Write This Down — How Greenhouses Optimise Conditions

Light: Artificial lights extend the day length and increase intensity on cloudy days. Photosynthesis is most efficient at red and blue wavelengths (green light is reflected, which is why leaves look green).

CO₂: Concentration is raised to about 0.1% (three times normal air). This can be done by burning paraffin heaters (which also raises temperature) or releasing CO₂ from cylinders. Result: up to 50% increase in yield.

Temperature: Thermostatically controlled heaters keep it at the optimum for enzyme activity. Too hot and enzymes denature (link back to Chapter 5).

High-yielding strains: Selective breeding or genetic engineering for high yield, pest resistance, and uniform ripening.

?
Why This Matters

The exam often asks: "Explain how a greenhouse grower can increase the yield of tomatoes." You need to link specific action (e.g. add CO₂) to biological reason (CO₂ is a substrate for photosynthesis, so more CO₂ = faster rate = more glucose = more growth).

  • I can explain how light, CO₂, and temperature are controlled in greenhouses
  • I can link each greenhouse control to the biology behind it
Section 6.6 Photosynthesis and the Environment
Plants both photosynthesise and respire. The balance between these two processes affects the gases around them.
W
Write This Down — Photosynthesis vs Respiration

Photosynthesis: CO₂ + H₂O → glucose + O₂ (needs light)
Respiration: glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (happens all the time)

In the light: Photosynthesis is faster than respiration → overall, the plant takes in CO₂ and releases O₂.
In the dark: Only respiration occurs → the plant takes in O₂ and releases CO₂.

W
Write This Down — Compensation Point

The compensation point is the light intensity at which the rate of photosynthesis exactly equals the rate of respiration. At this point:
• There is no net uptake or release of CO₂ or O₂.
• Above the compensation point, the plant gains glucose (growth).
• Below it, the plant uses up stored glucose.

W
Write This Down — Hydrogencarbonate Indicator

Hydrogencarbonate indicator changes colour based on CO₂ levels:
• CO₂ decreases (plant photosynthesising in light) → indicator turns purple
• CO₂ increases (plant respiring in dark) → indicator turns yellow
• Normal CO₂ level = red (starting colour)

!
Don't Confuse

Plants always respire — day and night. They don't "switch off" respiration when photosynthesising. In the light, both processes happen at the same time, but photosynthesis is faster so it masks respiration.

  • I can compare photosynthesis and respiration equations
  • I can define the compensation point
  • I know what hydrogencarbonate indicator does (purple/red/yellow)
  • I know plants respire ALL the time, not just in the dark
Section 6.7 Plants and Minerals
Plants need more than just CO₂ and water. They absorb mineral ions from the soil to build proteins, chlorophyll, and DNA.
W
Write This Down — Key Minerals and Their Functions
Mineral Ion What It's Needed For Deficiency Symptoms
Nitrate (NO₃⁻) Making amino acids and proteins (including enzymes) Stunted growth, weak stem, yellowing leaves (starts from bottom)
Magnesium (Mg²⁺) Making chlorophyll Leaves turn pale green/yellow (chlorosis), starting from bottom of plant
W
Write This Down — Fertilisers

In cultivated soil, minerals are removed when crops are harvested. Farmers replace them using:
• Natural fertilisers: sewage sludge, animal manure, compost
• Artificial fertilisers: NPK (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium)

Problem: Excess fertiliser can wash into rivers and lakes (eutrophication). Fertiliser boosts algae growth → algae die → bacteria decompose them using up all the oxygen → fish and other organisms die.

!
Don't Confuse

Nitrate is for protein. Magnesium is for chlorophyll. The exam will give you a plant with yellow leaves and ask what's missing — if the whole plant is stunted with yellowing leaves from the bottom, it's likely nitrate. If leaves are just turning pale green/yellow but growth is more normal, it's magnesium.

T
Study Tip — Memory Trick

Nitrate = proteiN. Magnesium = chlorophyll (which Makes things green). If magnesium is missing, the plant can't make chlorophyll, so leaves lose their green colour. Simple cause and effect.

  • I know what nitrate ions are needed for (amino acids/proteins) and the deficiency symptoms
  • I know what magnesium ions are needed for (chlorophyll) and the deficiency symptom (chlorosis)
  • I can explain eutrophication
  • I know the difference between natural and artificial fertilisers
