Section 17.1 Variation and Inheritance
Living organisms vary. Some differences are inherited from parents, others are acquired during life.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Inheritance is the transmission of genetic information from generation to generation.

Inherited characteristics (hereditary) = features passed on through genes. Examples: eye colour, hair colour, tendency for genetic diseases like cystic fibrosis, permanent skin colour.

Acquired characteristics = features developed during life that are NOT passed on. Examples: a scar, a suntan, muscle size from exercise, knowledge and skills.

!
Don't Confuse

Body mass is acquired (changed by diet), but the tendency to be a certain build is inherited. The exam may try to trick you with this. If the question says "permanent colour of the skin" = inherited. "A temporary suntan" = acquired.

W
Write This Down — Sexual Reproduction & Inheritance

In sexual reproduction:

• The only part of the sperm that enters the egg is the nucleus
• Gametes (sex cells) are formed by cell division
• The young organism develops from a single fertilised egg by cell division

So understanding inheritance means understanding the nucleus and how it behaves during cell division.

  • I can define inheritance
  • I can give examples of inherited vs acquired characteristics
  • I know that only the nucleus of the sperm enters the egg
Section 17.2 DNA, Proteins, and Characteristics
The characteristics of an organism depend on the proteins it makes — and proteins are coded for by DNA.
W
Write This Down — Chromosomes and Genes

Chromosome = a thread-like structure of DNA carrying genetic information in the form of genes.

Gene = a length of DNA that codes for a specific protein.

The nucleus of a human cell contains 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). Each chromosome contains a very long, coiled strand of DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid).

W
Write This Down — DNA Structure

DNA is a double helix made of two strands twisted around each other like a ladder.

The "rungs" of the ladder are made of pairs of nucleotide bases:

• Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T)
• Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C)

This is called complementary base pairing. About 600 base pairs code for one average protein.

?
Why This Matters

The chain is: Gene → Protein → Characteristic. Hair cells make keratin (a structural protein). Enzymes like amylase in saliva are proteins. Antibodies are proteins. If the gene changes, the protein changes, and the characteristic changes.

W
Write This Down — DNA Replication

DNA replication = the process by which DNA makes an exact copy of itself before cell division.

Steps:
1. The two strands of the double helix separate (unzip)
2. Each strand acts as a template
3. Free nucleotides pair up with the exposed bases using the base pairing rule
4. Result: two identical copies of the original DNA

T
Study Tip — Base Pairing Memory Trick

Apple Tree, Gold Coin. A-T and G-C. If they give you one strand (e.g. ATGCCA), you can work out the other (TACGGT) by swapping each letter for its partner.

  • I can define chromosome and gene
  • I know the four bases and their pairing rules (A-T, G-C)
  • I can describe the steps of DNA replication
  • I understand: gene → protein → characteristic
Section 17.3 How the Code Is Carried
DNA stays in the nucleus, but proteins are made at ribosomes in the cytoplasm. A messenger molecule carries the code across.
W
Write This Down — Triplets and Amino Acids

Each gene carries coded instructions as triplets (code words of three bases). Each triplet codes for one specific amino acid. The sequence of triplets determines the sequence of amino acids, which determines the protein made.

The four bases (A, T, G, C) can form enough different triplets to code for all 20 amino acids found in cells.

W
Write This Down — Transcription and Translation

Transcription = DNA code is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA) in the nucleus. Key difference: RNA uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T), so the base pair is A-U.

Translation = mRNA travels to a ribosome in the cytoplasm, where the code is read and used to build a protein (a chain of amino acids).

Summary: DNA → mRNA → Protein → Characteristic

!
Don't Confuse — Replication vs Transcription vs Translation

• Replication makes a DNA copy using DNA
• Transcription makes mRNA using DNA
• Translation makes protein using mRNA
Three different processes — don't mix them up. The exam loves asking "Name the process" for each one.

  • I know what a triplet (codon) is
  • I can define transcription and translation
  • I know that RNA uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T)
Section 17.4 Cell Division — Mitosis
The body needs to make exact copies of cells for growth and repair. This is mitosis.
W
Write This Down — Mitosis

Mitosis is copying division. It produces two identical daughter cells with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell.

Steps:
1. DNA is replicated exactly
2. Each chromosome becomes two identical chromatids joined at the centromere
3. Chromosomes line up at the equator (middle) of the cell
4. Spindle fibres pull the chromatids apart to opposite poles
5. The cell membrane pinches in, forming two identical cells

W
Write This Down — Where Mitosis Happens

• In animals: tissues provide new cells for growth and repair (e.g. liver cells, skin cells)
• Stem cells divide by mitosis to produce new specialised cells
• In plants: cell division at meristems (tips of roots and shoots) for length, and cambium for girth

?
Why This Matters

Every cell in your body (except gametes) was produced by mitosis from the original zygote. An adult human has about 50 billion cells, all containing the same genetic information. This is why mitosis must be accurate — errors can lead to cancer.

  • I can describe the steps of mitosis
  • I know that mitosis produces two identical cells
  • I know where mitosis occurs in plants and animals
Section 17.4 (continued) Cell Division — Meiosis
Gametes (sex cells) must have half the normal chromosome number. Meiosis makes this happen.
W
Write This Down — Key Terms

Diploid number (2n) = the full number of chromosomes in a body cell (46 in humans, i.e. 23 pairs).

Haploid number (n) = half the number, found in gametes (23 in humans).

Homologous pairs = matching pairs of chromosomes, one from each parent, carrying the same genes in the same positions.

W
Write This Down — Meiosis

Meiosis is reduction division. It produces four genetically different daughter cells, each with half the chromosome number (haploid).

Key steps:
1. DNA is replicated
2. Homologous pairs line up together at the equator
3. First division: homologous pairs are separated → two cells, each with one chromosome from each pair
4. Second division: chromatids are separated (like mitosis) → four haploid cells

W
Write This Down — The Big Comparison
Feature Mitosis Meiosis
Daughter cells 2 4
Genetically identical? Yes (clones) No (all different)
Chromosome number Same as parent (diploid) Half (haploid)
Purpose Growth, repair Making gametes
Where? All body tissues Testes and ovaries only
Number of divisions 1 2
!
Don't Confuse — Mitosis vs Meiosis

Mitosis = Makes identical cells (Mi = identical). Meiosis = Meiosis halves the number and makes gametes. If the question says "gametes" or "sex cells" — it's meiosis. If it says "growth" or "repair" — it's mitosis.

T
Study Tip — Draw This Table From Memory

This mitosis vs meiosis comparison is worth 4-6 marks across the exam. Close the book, draw the table on blank paper. If you can fill all 12 cells, you own this topic.

  • I can define diploid, haploid, and homologous pair
  • I can describe the key steps of meiosis
  • I can list at least 4 differences between mitosis and meiosis
  • I can recreate the comparison table from memory
Section 17.5 Stem Cells
Not all cells become specialised immediately. Stem cells retain the ability to differentiate into many types.
W
Write This Down

Stem cells are unspecialised cells that can divide by mitosis and differentiate into specialised cell types.

• Embryonic stem cells — can differentiate into almost any cell type
• Adult stem cells — found in bone marrow, liver, brain muscle, blood — can only differentiate into a limited range of cells

?
Why This Matters

Stem cell research could help treat leukaemia, Type 1 diabetes, spinal cord damage, and Parkinson's disease. But using embryonic stem cells is controversial because it involves destroying embryos. This is a common "discuss" question on the exam — you need to give arguments for and against.

  • I can define stem cell and differentiation
  • I know the difference between embryonic and adult stem cells
Section 17.6 Inheritance — Key Genetic Terms
Before you can do genetic crosses, you need to know the vocabulary. This is the most definition-heavy section of the whole chapter.
W
Write This Down — Definitions You MUST Know

Gene = a length of DNA that codes for a specific protein (and therefore a characteristic).

Allele = an alternative form of a gene. E.g. the gene for eye colour has alleles for brown and blue.

Homozygous = both alleles are the same (e.g. BB or bb).

Heterozygous = the two alleles are different (e.g. Bb).

Dominant allele = the allele that shows up in the phenotype whenever it is present (written as a CAPITAL letter, e.g. B).

Recessive allele = the allele that is only expressed when homozygous (written as a lowercase letter, e.g. b). It is "hidden" when the dominant allele is present.

Genotype = the combination of alleles an organism has (e.g. Bb).

Phenotype = the observable characteristic (e.g. brown eyes).

!
Don't Confuse — Gene vs Allele

A gene is the instruction for a characteristic (e.g. "eye colour"). An allele is a specific version of that instruction (e.g. "brown" or "blue"). The gene is the question, the allele is the answer. Examiners love asking the difference — 2 free marks if you know it.

T
Study Tip — Flashcards

These 8 definitions come up in almost every genetics question. Write each term on one side of a card and the definition on the other. Shuffle. Test yourself until you can get them all right without hesitation. This is pure memorisation — flashcards work best.

  • I can define: gene, allele, homozygous, heterozygous
  • I can define: dominant, recessive, genotype, phenotype
  • I know the difference between a gene and an allele
Section 17.7 Genetic Crosses and Punnett Squares
Punnett squares are the tool you use to predict offspring. Learn the method once and it works for every genetics question.
W
Write This Down — How to Set Up a Cross

Always follow this format:
1. State the allele symbols — "Let B = brown (dominant), b = blue (recessive)"
2. Write the parental genotypes — e.g. Parents: Bb × Bb
3. Write the gametes — each parent can pass on either allele
4. Draw the Punnett square — put one parent's gametes across the top, the other down the side
5. List the genotypes and phenotypes of the offspring

W
Write This Down — Worked Example (Bb × Bb)

Let B = brown eyes (dominant), b = blue eyes (recessive).
Parents: Bb × Bb
Gametes: B or b   ×   B or b

Bb
BBBBb
bBbbb

Genotype ratio: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb
Phenotype ratio: 3 brown : 1 blue
Probability of blue eyes: 1 in 4 (25%)

!
Don't Confuse — Ratios Are Probabilities

A 3:1 ratio does NOT mean that if you have 4 children, exactly 3 will have brown eyes and 1 will have blue. Each child is an independent event — there is always a 1/4 (25%) chance of blue for each child. The ratio is only seen reliably in very large numbers.

W
Write This Down — Test Cross

A test cross (back cross) is used to find out if an organism showing the dominant phenotype is BB or Bb. Cross it with a homozygous recessive (bb):

• If ALL offspring show the dominant phenotype → parent was BB
• If ANY offspring show the recessive phenotype → parent was Bb

W
Write This Down — Pedigree Diagrams

A pedigree (family tree) shows how a trait is inherited across generations. Key symbols:
• Circle = female, Square = male
• Filled/shaded = affected by the condition
• Horizontal line between two symbols = parents (mated pair)
• Vertical line down = offspring

Key rule: If two unaffected parents have an affected child, both parents must be carriers (heterozygous) and the trait is recessive.

T
Study Tip — Practice Crosses

Do at least 5 Punnett square problems from different angles: BB × bb, Bb × Bb, Bb × bb. Then try some pedigree problems. Speed matters on the exam — you should be able to set up and complete a Punnett square in under 2 minutes.

  • I can set up a genetic cross with correct notation
  • I can draw and complete a Punnett square
  • I can state genotype and phenotype ratios from a cross
  • I know what a test cross is and when it is used
  • I can read and interpret a pedigree diagram
Section 17.8 Inherited Conditions and Codominance
Some genetic conditions are caused by recessive alleles, and some traits show codominance where neither allele is dominant.
W
Write This Down — Inherited Medical Conditions

Albinism = caused by a recessive allele. The person cannot produce the pigment melanin. Genotype must be aa (homozygous recessive). Heterozygous individuals (Aa) are carriers — they have normal skin colour but carry the recessive allele.

Sickle cell anaemia = caused by a recessive allele that produces abnormal haemoglobin. Homozygous (HbAHbA) = normal. Heterozygous (HbSHbA) = some symptoms but resistant to malaria. Homozygous (HbSHbS) = severe anaemia.

W
Write This Down — Codominance

Codominance = when both alleles are expressed in the heterozygote, producing a third phenotype. Neither allele is dominant over the other.

Example — ABO blood groups: Three alleles: IA, IB, and IO.

GenotypePhenotype (Blood Group)
IAIA or IAIOGroup A
IBIB or IBIOGroup B
IAIBGroup AB (codominant)
IOIOGroup O

IA and IB are both dominant over IO, but codominant with each other.

?
Why This Matters

The sickle cell / malaria link is a classic exam question. In areas where malaria is common (equatorial Africa), being heterozygous (carrier) is actually an advantage — you have some anaemia but are resistant to malaria. This is an example of how natural selection can maintain a harmful allele in a population.

  • I can explain albinism as a recessive condition
  • I understand the sickle cell / malaria connection
  • I can define codominance and use blood group as an example
Section 17.9 Sex Determination and Sex-Linked Genes
Whether you are male or female is determined by one pair of chromosomes — the sex chromosomes.
W
Write This Down — Sex Chromosomes

Humans have 22 pairs of autosomes + 1 pair of sex chromosomes = 46 total.
• Female = XX
• Male = XY

The man's sperm determines the sex of the child (he can pass on X or Y). The woman can only pass on X.

X (mother)X (mother)
X (father)XX (girl)XX (girl)
Y (father)XY (boy)XY (boy)

Theoretical sex ratio: 1 male : 1 female (50% chance of each).

W
Write This Down — Sex-Linked Characteristics

Some genes are located on the X chromosome but NOT on the shorter Y chromosome. These are called sex-linked genes.

Red-green colour blindness is sex-linked (recessive allele on the X chromosome):
• XC = normal vision, Xc = colour blind
• Males only have ONE X chromosome, so if they inherit Xc, they will be colour blind (XcY)
• Females need TWO copies (XcXc) to be colour blind — so it is much rarer in females
• A female who is XCXc is a carrier (not affected but can pass it on)

?
Why This Matters

This explains why colour blindness and haemophilia are much more common in males. Males only need one copy of the recessive allele (they have no second X to "mask" it). This is a favourite exam topic — expect a Punnett square crossing a carrier female with a normal male.

T
Study Tip — Sex-Linked Crosses

For sex-linked Punnett squares, always write the alleles ON the X and Y chromosomes (e.g. XCXc × XCY). Never just write "Cc" — you must show they are on the X. The Y has no allele for colour vision, so leave it blank (just Y).

  • I know that XX = female and XY = male
  • I can draw a Punnett square for sex determination
  • I can explain why colour blindness is more common in males
  • I can do a sex-linked cross with carrier female × normal male
