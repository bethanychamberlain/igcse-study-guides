Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define excretion.
2 marks
Answer
Excretion is the removal of toxic materials [1], the waste products of metabolism, and substances in excess of requirements from the body [1].
Key phrase: "waste products of metabolism." If you just say "getting rid of waste" you won't get both marks. You must say it's metabolic waste, not undigested food.

2
Name two waste products of metabolism. For each one, state where it is produced and how it is removed from the body.
4 marks
Answer
1. Carbon dioxide — produced during respiration (in all cells) [1], removed by the lungs [1].
2. Urea — produced in the liver during deamination of excess amino acids [1], removed by the kidneys in urine [1].
Each waste product needs: name + source + removal organ. Don't say "faeces" — that's egestion, not excretion.

3
State the two main functions of the kidneys.
2 marks
Answer
1. To remove urea (and other waste products) from the blood — excretion [1].
2. To regulate the water content of the blood — osmoregulation [1].
Use the proper terms: "excretion" and "osmoregulation." Both are worth a mark each.

4
What is the name of the functional unit (tiny tube) found in the kidney? State one function it carries out.
2 marks
Answer
The nephron (kidney tubule) [1]. It filters substances from the blood / carries out ultrafiltration and selective reabsorption [1].
Either "filters the blood" or "reabsorbs useful substances" counts for the second mark.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain HOW and WHY, not just name things.
5
Describe what happens during ultrafiltration at the glomerulus. Explain why proteins are not found in the filtrate.
3 marks
Answer
Blood enters the glomerulus (a ball of capillaries) under high pressure [1]. Small molecules such as water, glucose, urea, salts, and amino acids are forced out of the blood into the Bowman's capsule [1]. Protein molecules are too large to pass through the capillary walls / membrane, so they stay in the blood [1].
Three ideas: high pressure, small molecules forced out, proteins too large to pass through. Each is a mark.

6
The table shows the concentration of substances in blood plasma, the filtrate in the Bowman's capsule, and urine. Explain why glucose is present in the filtrate but absent from the urine.
3 marks
Answer
Glucose is a small molecule so it passes through during ultrafiltration into the filtrate [1]. As the filtrate passes along the first coiled tubule, all the glucose is reabsorbed back into the blood [1] by selective reabsorption (because glucose is a useful substance the body needs for respiration) [1].
This is a very common exam question. The key phrase is "selective reabsorption" — the kidney keeps what's useful and lets waste pass through.

7
On a hot day, a person sweats more but produces less urine, even if they drink the same amount of water. Explain why the volume of urine decreases on a hot day.
3 marks
Answer
On a hot day, more water is lost through sweating [1]. The body detects a decrease in water levels and releases more ADH (anti-diuretic hormone) [1]. ADH causes the collecting duct to reabsorb more water back into the blood, so less water is left to form urine, and the urine produced is smaller in volume and more concentrated [1].
The chain: hot day → more sweating → more ADH released → more water reabsorbed from collecting duct → less urine.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A patient with kidney failure is treated using a dialysis machine. Explain how the machine removes urea from the blood while keeping glucose in the blood.
5 marks
Answer
The patient's blood flows on one side of a partially permeable membrane [1]. Dialysis fluid flows on the other side. Urea is at a higher concentration in the blood than in the dialysis fluid, so it diffuses across the membrane from the blood into the fluid [1]. The dialysis fluid contains glucose at the same concentration as normal blood [1], so there is no concentration gradient for glucose, and it does not diffuse out [1]. The blood and dialysis fluid flow in opposite directions (countercurrent) to maintain a steep concentration gradient along the whole length of the membrane [1].
Five key ideas: partially permeable membrane, urea diffuses down concentration gradient, dialysis fluid has normal glucose concentration, no glucose gradient so glucose stays, countercurrent flow maintains the gradient.

9
A kidney transplant is considered better than dialysis for treating kidney failure. Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of a kidney transplant compared with dialysis treatment.
5 marks
Answer
Advantages of transplant:
• The patient's life is less disrupted — no need for 3 dialysis sessions per week, each lasting 6–8 hours [1].
• In the long term, a transplant is much cheaper than ongoing dialysis treatment [1].

Disadvantages of transplant:
• Risk of tissue rejection — the recipient's immune system may attack and destroy the donor kidney [1].
• Blood groups and tissue types of donor and recipient must be carefully matched to reduce rejection [1].
• The recipient must take immunosuppressant drugs for the rest of their life, which reduces their ability to fight infections [1].
"Discuss" means give both sides. Always give at least two advantages and two disadvantages. Mention tissue rejection and immunosuppressants — examiners love these terms.

10
The concentration of urea in the blood in the renal artery is higher than in the renal vein. The concentration of urea in the urine is much higher than in both. Explain these observations using your knowledge of how the nephron works.
5 marks
Answer
The renal artery carries blood to the kidney, which still contains all the urea [1]. In the glomerulus, urea is filtered out of the blood by ultrafiltration (along with water, glucose, salts) [1]. By the time the blood leaves via the renal vein, much of the urea has been removed, so the renal vein has a lower urea concentration [1]. As the filtrate passes through the tubule, most of the water is reabsorbed back into the blood (selective reabsorption) [1], but the urea is not reabsorbed. Since the water is removed but the urea stays in the tubule, the urea becomes much more concentrated in the urine [1].
The key insight: urea concentration in urine is high NOT because the kidney adds urea, but because water is reabsorbed while urea is not — so the urea gets more and more concentrated as the water leaves.
