Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the seven components of a balanced diet.
2 marks
Answer
Carbohydrates, lipids (fats), proteins, vitamins, minerals, water, and dietary fibre [1 for at least 5 correct, 2 for all 7].
Don't forget water and fibre — students always leave these out. "Lipids" or "fats" both accepted.

2
State the function of dietary fibre in the human diet.
2 marks
Answer
Fibre provides bulk to the food / faeces [1], which helps the muscles of the gut wall push food along by peristalsis [1]. A shortage of fibre can cause constipation.
The key link: fibre = bulk = peristalsis works properly. Just saying "it helps digestion" is too vague for a mark.

3
Name the enzyme that begins the digestion of starch in the mouth, and state what it produces.
2 marks
Answer
Salivary amylase [1] breaks starch down into maltose [1].
NOT glucose. Starch → maltose → glucose is a two-step process. Amylase only does the first step. Maltase (in the small intestine) does the second.

4
What is peristalsis?
2 marks
Answer
Peristalsis is waves of muscular contraction [1] that push food along the alimentary canal / gut [1].
Two marks, two ideas: (1) it's muscular contraction in waves, (2) it moves food along. Just saying "muscles push food" misses the "wave" idea.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain why food must be digested before it can be absorbed.
3 marks
Answer
Food molecules such as starch and proteins are too large [1] to pass through the wall of the small intestine / gut wall [1]. They must be broken down into small, soluble molecules (e.g. glucose, amino acids) that can cross the gut wall and enter the blood [1].
Three key ideas: molecules are too large, they are insoluble, they must become small and soluble to cross the gut wall. "So the body can use them" alone is too vague.

6
Describe the role of bile in digestion. Include where it is made, where it is stored, and what it does.
4 marks
Answer
Bile is made in the liver [1] and stored in the gall bladder [1]. It is released into the duodenum (small intestine). Bile emulsifies fats — it breaks large fat globules into smaller droplets, increasing the surface area for lipase to work on [1]. Bile also contains hydrogencarbonate which neutralises the acid from the stomach, providing the alkaline conditions needed for intestinal enzymes [1].
Remember: bile is NOT an enzyme. It does not chemically break down fat. Lipase does the chemical digestion; bile just makes the droplets smaller so lipase works faster.

7
A child in a developing country has a swollen abdomen, thin limbs, and poor muscle development. Name this condition, state its cause, and explain why the abdomen is swollen.
3 marks
Answer
This is kwashiorkor [1], caused by a diet that lacks sufficient protein [1]. The abdomen is swollen because the liver cannot make enough plasma proteins, so water from the blood plasma leaks into the body tissues / the liver is swollen from trying to work with inadequate protein supply [1].
Don't confuse with marasmus (general starvation — lack of ALL food, not just protein). Kwashiorkor = enough energy food but not enough protein.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Describe how the small intestine is adapted for the efficient absorption of digested food. Include at least four adaptations.
5 marks
Answer
(1) The small intestine is very long (~6 m), so food has a long time in contact with the absorbing surface [1].
(2) The inner lining is folded into thousands of villi, which greatly increase the surface area for absorption [1].
(3) Each villus has microvilli on its surface, further increasing surface area [1].
(4) The wall of each villus is one cell thick (thin epithelium), providing a short diffusion distance [1].
(5) Each villus has a dense network of blood capillaries (to absorb glucose and amino acids) and a lacteal (to absorb fatty acids and glycerol), which maintain a steep concentration gradient by carrying absorbed molecules away quickly [1].
This is a classic 4-5 mark question. For each adaptation, state the feature AND explain how it helps absorption. Just listing features without explanation gets fewer marks.

9
Describe, in the correct sequence, how the protein and starch in a ham sandwich are broken down ready for absorption. Name the enzymes involved and where they act.
6 marks
Answer
Starch (bread):
In the mouth, salivary amylase begins breaking starch into maltose [1].
In the duodenum (small intestine), pancreatic amylase continues converting starch to maltose [1].
On the wall of the ileum, maltase converts maltose to glucose [1].

Protein (ham):
In the stomach, pepsin (a protease) begins breaking proteins into amino acids, in acidic conditions (HCl, pH 2) [1].
In the duodenum, trypsin (a protease from the pancreas) continues breaking proteins into amino acids, in alkaline conditions (pH 8) [1].
Throughout, food is moved along by peristalsis [1].
Six marks: three for starch pathway (amylase in mouth, amylase in duodenum, maltase in ileum) and three for protein pathway (pepsin in stomach + acid, trypsin in duodenum + alkaline, peristalsis). Name the enzyme AND location for each mark.

10
The liver receives blood from the small intestine via the hepatic portal vein. Describe three functions of the liver in dealing with absorbed food molecules.
6 marks
Answer
(1) Glucose regulation: The liver converts excess glucose into glycogen for storage [1]. When blood glucose levels drop, glycogen is converted back to glucose [1].

(2) Deamination: The liver removes the amino group from excess amino acids [1] and converts it into urea, which is excreted by the kidneys [1].

(3) Bile production: The liver manufactures bile [1], which is important for the emulsification of fats and neutralisation of stomach acid in the duodenum [1].
Other acceptable functions include: transamination (converting one amino acid to another), detoxification (breaking down alcohol/toxins), storing iron from old red blood cells. For 6 marks, give 3 functions with 2 marks of detail each.
