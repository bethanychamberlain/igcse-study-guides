Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Xylem Dead, hollow tubes strengthened with lignin. Transport water and minerals upward from roots to leaves. Phloem (alive, transports sugars both ways)
Phloem Living sieve tubes with companion cells. Transports sucrose and amino acids from source to sink (both directions). Xylem (dead, one-way upward only)
Translocation The transport of dissolved sugars in phloem from sources to sinks. Transpiration (that's water loss from leaves, not sugar movement)
Transpiration Loss of water vapour from leaves, mainly through stomata on the lower surface. Evaporation (transpiration is specifically from plant leaves)
Root hair cell Long thin extension of root epidermal cell. Huge surface area. Many mitochondria for active transport of minerals. —
Osmosis Net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential to lower water potential through a partially permeable membrane. Diffusion (diffusion is any substance moving down its concentration gradient — osmosis is specifically water through a membrane)
Active transport Movement of substances against the concentration gradient, using energy from respiration. Osmosis/diffusion (those are passive — no energy needed)
Potometer Apparatus that measures water uptake by a cut shoot (approximately = transpiration rate). An air bubble moves along a capillary tube. It measures water uptake, not water loss directly
Xerophyte A plant adapted to survive in dry conditions (e.g. cactus, marram grass). —
Wilting Plant becomes limp when water loss exceeds water uptake — cells lose turgor pressure and become flaccid. —

Xylem vs Phloem — Side by Side

Xylem Phloem
Transports Water + mineral ions Sucrose + amino acids
Direction One way: UP (roots → leaves) Both ways (source → sink)
Cells Dead — no cytoplasm Alive — sieve tubes + companion cells
Wall Thick, lignin (waterproof, strong) Thin; sieve plates with pores
Structure Hollow tubes, end walls removed Sieve tubes + companion cells
Process Transpiration stream Translocation
Exam Trap — Phloem Does NOT Just Go Down
Students often say "xylem goes up, phloem goes down." Wrong. Phloem goes both ways — from source to sink. In summer: leaves (source) → roots (sink). In spring: roots (source) → new leaves (sink).

Factors That Increase Transpiration Rate

Factor Effect Why
High temperature Increases rate More kinetic energy → water evaporates faster
Wind Increases rate Blows humid air away → steeper diffusion gradient
Low humidity Increases rate Bigger water potential gradient between leaf and air
High light intensity Increases rate Stomata open for photosynthesis → water escapes

Say It in One Sentence

Transpiration Water evaporates from spongy mesophyll cells, diffuses out through stomata, and is replaced by water pulled up through the xylem.
Xylem Dead hollow pipes waterproofed with lignin — no cytoplasm means no obstruction to water flow.
Phloem Living sieve tubes that move dissolved sugars from wherever they are made or stored to wherever they are needed.
Root hair cell Long thin projection that absorbs water by osmosis and minerals by active transport from the soil.
Potometer Measures how fast a bubble moves along a tube connected to a cut shoot — faster bubble = faster water uptake.
Wilting When water loss exceeds water uptake, cells go flaccid and the plant droops — which actually slows further water loss.
Xerophyte Plants in dry habitats with thick cuticles, sunken stomata, rolled leaves, or spines to reduce water loss.

Memory Tricks

X = eXit (water goes up and out)
Xylem = water eXits the plant (goes up to leaves and out through stomata). Phloem = phood (sugar) moves around. Both are in vascular bundles together.
WHTL — The 4 Transpiration Factors
Wind, Heat, (low) Humidity, Light. All four increase transpiration. On a hot, windy, dry, sunny day, transpiration is at its fastest.
The Water Journey in 4 Steps
1. Soil → root hair cell (osmosis). 2. Root → xylem (osmosis, cell to cell). 3. Xylem → leaf (tension pulls water up, cohesion holds the column together). 4. Leaf → atmosphere (evaporation then diffusion through stomata = transpiration).
Why Root Hair Cells Have Mitochondria
Mitochondria = energy factory. Energy is needed for active transport of mineral ions against the concentration gradient. No mitochondria = no energy = no mineral uptake. This is also why waterlogged (no oxygen) roots can't absorb minerals.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Compare xylem and phloem" Xylem: dead, lignin walls, water UP. Phloem: alive, sieve plates, sucrose BOTH ways (translocation)
"Why are xylem cells dead?" No cytoplasm = no obstruction to water flow. Hollow tubes are more efficient for transport
"How does water enter root hair cells?" By osmosis — soil has higher water potential than root cell cytoplasm
"How do mineral ions enter root hair cells?" By active transport (against concentration gradient, using energy from respiration)
"Explain how wind increases transpiration" Wind blows away humid air from around the leaf, maintaining a steep diffusion gradient
"What does a potometer measure?" Water uptake by a cut shoot (approximately equal to transpiration rate)
"Describe two adaptations of xerophytes" Thick waxy cuticle (reduces evaporation) + rolled leaves/sunken stomata (trap humid air, reducing gradient)
"What is the evidence for water transport in xylem?" Celery in coloured water — dye travels up the xylem vessels, visible in cross-section
