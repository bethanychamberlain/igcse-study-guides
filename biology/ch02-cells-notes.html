Section 2.1 Organisms Are Made Up of Cells
All living things are made of cells. Cells are the basic unit of life. You need to know what's inside animal and plant cells — and what each part actually does.
W
Write This Down — Features Shared by All Cells

Both animal and plant cells have these three structures:

• Cell surface membrane — surrounds the cytoplasm, controls what enters and exits the cell, separates the cell's contents from its surroundings
• Cytoplasm — jelly-like substance where chemical reactions happen; contains water, dissolved substances like sugars and salts, and organelles
• Nucleus — contains genetic material (DNA, which makes up genes on chromosomes); carries the coded instructions that control the activities and characteristics of the cell

!
Don't Confuse — This Is a Common Exam Mistake

The cell wall and the cell surface membrane are NOT the same thing. The membrane is thin and controls what goes in and out. The cell wall is a thick outer layer made of cellulose that provides structural support. Only plant cells have a cell wall. Both plant and animal cells have a membrane.

W
Write This Down — Extra Features in Plant Cells Only

Plant cells have everything animal cells have, plus these extras:

• Cellulose cell wall — rigid outer layer that provides structural support, keeps the cell's shape regular, protects against damage from osmotic water intake. It is freely permeable to water and dissolved substances (unlike the membrane, which is selective)
• Large permanent vacuole — filled with cell sap (water + dissolved ions and molecules); provides turgor pressure to keep the cell firm
• Chloroplasts — contain the green pigment chlorophyll (for absorbing light) and the enzymes needed for photosynthesis (making glucose from light energy)
• Starch granules — found in cytoplasm or chloroplasts; the storage form of carbohydrates in plants

?
Why This Matters — Explaining Functions Gets Marks

On the Easter test you could name cell parts but lost marks when asked to explain what they do. The exam doesn't just ask "What is in a plant cell?" — it asks "State the function of the cell wall" or "Explain why plant cells have chloroplasts." You must link the structure to its job. Every time.

W
Write This Down — Animal Cell Features

Animal cells have features that relate to heterotrophic nutrition (they eat food rather than making it):

• Glycogen granules — the storage form of carbohydrates in animal cells (plants store starch, animals store glycogen)
• Secretory vesicles — small packages containing cell products like hormones or enzymes, much more common in animal cells
• Vacuoles in animal cells are small and temporary (e.g. for digestion in phagocytes, or contractile vacuoles to remove excess water) — NOT the large permanent vacuole you see in plant cells

T
Study Tip — The Shape Clue

Animal cells have irregular shapes because they have no cell wall to hold them rigid. Plant cells tend to have regular, box-like shapes because the cellulose cell wall maintains their structure. If you see a diagram on the exam and need to tell which is which, the shape is your first clue.

  • I can name the three structures shared by ALL cells (membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus)
  • I can state the function of each of those three structures
  • I can list the four extra features found only in plant cells
  • I can explain what each plant cell structure does (not just name it)
  • I know the difference between the cell wall and the cell membrane

Section 2.1 continued Structures Within the Cytoplasm
Some organelles are too small to see with a light microscope. An electron microscope can reveal structures like mitochondria and rough endoplasmic reticulum inside the cytoplasm.
W
Write This Down — Mitochondria

Mitochondria (singular: mitochondrion) are made of folded membranes that provide a large surface area. They are the sites of aerobic respiration — the process that releases energy for the cell to use.

Think of mitochondria as the cell's powerhouses. Cells that need lots of energy (muscle cells, nerve cells, liver cells) have many mitochondria.

W
Write This Down — Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum

Rough endoplasmic reticulum (rough ER) is made of flattened membranes with ribosomes on its surface. Ribosomes are the sites of protein synthesis. Rough ER is common in cells that make many proteins (e.g. liver cells which produce enzymes and blood proteins).

?
Why This Matters

The exam often asks: "Why does a liver cell contain many mitochondria?" The answer is always: because the liver cell has a high rate of metabolic activity and needs a lot of energy from respiration. The question is really testing whether you know what mitochondria do.

  • I can state the function of mitochondria (site of aerobic respiration / energy release)
  • I can state the function of rough ER (has ribosomes for protein synthesis)
  • I can explain why active cells have more mitochondria
Section 2.1 continued Microscopes and Magnification
Cells are too small to see with the naked eye, so we use microscopes. You need to know the magnification formula and how to use it.
W
Write This Down — Magnification Formula

Magnification = Measured length ÷ Actual length

Or rearranged: Actual length = Measured length ÷ Magnification

Units: A typical animal cell is about 25 µm in diameter. 1 mm = 1000 µm. Always make sure both measurements are in the same unit before dividing.

T
Study Tip — MAM Triangle

Remember the formula with a triangle: M on top, A and M on the bottom. Magnification = Measured ÷ Actual. Cover the one you want to find, and the other two show you the calculation. The textbook also gives a memory trick: M-A-M (the first letters spell MAM).

W
Write This Down — Light vs Electron Microscope

• A light microscope uses visible light, gives useful magnification up to about ×400. You can see cells, nuclei, and chloroplasts.
• An electron microscope gives much higher magnification. You can see mitochondria, rough ER, and ribosomes.
• Stains improve contrast: methylene blue shows the nucleus in animal cells; iodine solution shows structures in plant cells.

!
Don't Confuse — Units Trap

If the question gives the measured length in mm and the actual length in µm, you MUST convert to the same unit first. Forgetting to convert is the most common reason students get magnification calculations wrong. Always check your units before you divide.

  • I can write the magnification formula from memory
  • I can convert between mm and µm (1 mm = 1000 µm)
  • I know the difference between a light microscope and an electron microscope
Section 2.2 The Organisation of Living Organisms
Large organisms are multicellular — made of many cells. Different cells become specialised to do particular jobs. Their structure is adapted to their function.
W
Write This Down — Specialised Animal Cells

Red blood cell — transports oxygen from the lungs to the tissues for aerobic respiration. Adaptations: contains haemoglobin (carries oxygen), has no nucleus (more room for haemoglobin), biconcave disc shape (large surface area), very flexible (can squeeze through narrow capillaries).

Ciliated cell — has tiny hairs called cilia that can beat and move mucus from one place to another. The mucus traps dust and microbes. Found lining the airways.

Motor nerve cell — conducts nerve impulses. Adaptations: long fibre called an axon for carrying impulses over distance, fatty sheath for electrical insulation, branched endings to connect with many other cells.

W
Write This Down — Specialised Plant Cells

Root hair cell — absorbs minerals and water from the soil. Adaptation: long, thin extension (the root hair) that gives a large surface area for absorption.

Xylem vessel — transports water and supports the plant. Adaptations: no cytoplasm (so water can flow freely), no end walls (cells join to form a continuous tube), walls strengthened with a waterproof substance called lignin.

Palisade mesophyll cell — specialised for photosynthesis. Contains many chloroplasts and is packed tightly near the top of the leaf where light is strongest.

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions about specialised cells always follow the same pattern: they describe or draw a cell, then ask "How is this cell adapted for its function?" You need to link each structural feature to a reason. For example: "The root hair cell has a long extension because this increases the surface area so that more water can be absorbed." Structure → reason → advantage. That pattern gets full marks.

!
Don't Confuse — Red Blood Cells Are Special

Red blood cells have no nucleus. Do NOT write "all cells have a nucleus" on the exam — red blood cells are the big exception. The reason they have no nucleus is to make more room for haemoglobin, so they can carry more oxygen.

T
Study Tip — Flash Card Each Cell

Make one flash card per specialised cell. On the front: the cell name. On the back: its job, and then each adaptation with the reason. Test yourself until you can rattle off all the adaptations without looking. This is one of the highest-value topics — it comes up almost every exam.

  • I can describe adaptations of the red blood cell and link each to its function
  • I can describe adaptations of the ciliated cell
  • I can describe adaptations of the motor nerve cell
  • I can describe adaptations of the root hair cell
  • I can describe adaptations of the xylem vessel
Section 2.2 continued Levels of Organisation
Cells don't work alone. They are organised into increasingly complex structures that work together.
W
Write This Down — The Five Levels (Smallest to Largest)

Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism

• Cell — the basic unit of life (e.g. an epithelial cell)
• Tissue — a group of cells with similar structures and functions working together (e.g. epithelial tissue lines the gut)
• Organ — a group of different tissues working together to perform a specific function (e.g. the small intestine)
• Organ system — a group of organs with related functions working together to perform body functions (e.g. the digestive system)
• Organism — a complete living thing made of all its organ systems working together (e.g. a human)

Why This Matters — Division of Labour

In a multicellular organism, different cells, tissues, and organs perform different functions — this is called division of labour. The advantage is that each part can become highly efficient at its specific job. But it also means the parts must be coordinated — that's what the nervous and endocrine systems do.

T

Pick one organ system and trace it from cell to organism. For example: epithelial cell → epithelial tissue (lining the intestine) → small intestine (organ) → digestive system (organ system) → human (organism). If you can do this from memory for one example, you understand the concept.
