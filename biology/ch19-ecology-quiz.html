Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1–2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the terms population, community, and ecosystem.
3 marks
Answer
Population: all the members of the same species in the same area at the same time [1].
Community: all the populations of different species living in the same area [1].
Ecosystem: a unit containing the community of organisms and their non-living environment, interacting together [1].
One mark per definition. "Same species" is essential for population. "Interacting together" is the key phrase for ecosystem.

Name two abiotic factors that might determine whether a habitat is suitable for a living organism.
2 marks
Answer
Any two from: temperature [1], light intensity [1], water availability, oxygen/CO₂ concentration, humidity, pH of soil.
Abiotic = non-living. Do NOT write "predators" or "food" — those are biotic. Temperature and light intensity are the safest picks.

3
What do the arrows in a food chain represent?
1 mark
Answer
The arrows show the direction of energy flow (or transfer of energy) through the food chain [1].
Do NOT say "who eats whom." The arrows point FROM the organism being eaten TO the organism eating it — showing the direction energy moves.

4
Define decomposer and state the type of organisms that act as decomposers.
2 marks
Answer
Decomposers are organisms that break down dead organisms and wastes, obtaining energy and raw materials from them [1]. They are bacteria and fungi [1].
Saying "microorganisms" alone is usually accepted, but naming both bacteria AND fungi is safer for full marks.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3–4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain why food chains rarely have more than four or five trophic levels.
3 marks
Answer
Energy is lost at each trophic level [1], mainly as heat from respiration [1]. By the fourth or fifth level, there is not enough energy remaining to support another population of organisms [1].
Three ideas: energy lost at each step, lost as heat through respiration, insufficient energy remains. The classic chain: energy in → respiration heat loss → not enough left.

6
In a mark-recapture experiment, 30 beetles are caught, marked, and released. The next day, 52 beetles are caught, and 12 of those are marked. Calculate the estimated total population.
3 marks
Answer
Population = (number marked × number in second sample) ÷ number of marked recaptured [1]
Population = (30 × 52) ÷ 12 [1]
Population = 1560 ÷ 12 = 130 beetles [1].
One mark for the formula, one for correct substitution, one for the answer. Always show your working — even if the final answer is wrong, the formula and substitution marks still count.

7
Describe how carbon dioxide in the atmosphere can become part of the carbon compounds in an animal's body.
3 marks
Answer
CO₂ is absorbed by plants during photosynthesis [1] and converted into organic compounds such as carbohydrates, fats, and proteins [1]. Animals then obtain these carbon compounds by feeding on the plants (or on other animals that ate plants) [1].
Three steps: photosynthesis takes in CO₂, plants make organic molecules, animals eat the plants. Follow the carbon step by step.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5–6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Measurements suggest that the carbon dioxide concentration in the atmosphere is rising. Using your knowledge of the carbon cycle, explain how this is happening.
4 marks
Answer
Humans are burning large quantities of fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) [1]. Combustion releases CO₂ that was "locked up" underground for millions of years [1]. At the same time, deforestation reduces the number of trees available to absorb CO₂ by photosynthesis [1]. The rate of CO₂ release (combustion + respiration) now exceeds the rate of CO₂ removal (photosynthesis), so concentrations are rising [1].
Four marks, four points: fossil fuel combustion, stored carbon released, deforestation reduces photosynthesis, imbalance between release and removal. This is one of the most commonly tested questions in the entire IGCSE.

9
Describe how nitrogen in a dead animal can eventually become part of a protein in a plant. Include the names of the types of bacteria involved.
5 marks
Answer
Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down the proteins in the dead animal into amino acids and then into ammonium ions [1]. Nitrifying bacteria convert the ammonium ions first to nitrite and then to nitrate (a process called nitrification) [1]. This process requires oxygen [1]. The nitrate ions are absorbed by plant roots (by diffusion and active transport) [1] and used by the plant to make amino acids and then proteins [1].
Follow the chain: dead animal → decomposers → ammonium → nitrifying bacteria → nitrate → plant absorbs → makes protein. Name the bacteria at each step for full marks.

10
Explain why growing vegetables for human consumption instead of raising beef cattle results in fewer energy losses. Suggest why eating less meat in wealthy countries could help feed the world's growing population.
5 marks
Answer
Eating plants directly involves fewer trophic levels (one step instead of two) [1]. At each step, energy is lost as heat from respiration in the organisms [1]. Cattle also use energy for movement and maintaining body temperature, which is wasted [1]. If humans eat the plants directly, more of the original energy from photosynthesis is available as food [1]. So the same area of farmland can feed more people with crops than with cattle, helping to feed a larger global population [1].
Key idea: shorter food chain = less energy wasted = more food per hectare. The cattle question always comes back to energy loss at each trophic level.
