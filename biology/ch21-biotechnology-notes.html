Section 21.1 Bacteria in Biotechnology
Bacteria are incredibly useful to humans. They reproduce fast, make useful products, and their DNA is easy to manipulate. Understanding why bacteria are useful is the key to this chapter.
W
Write This Down — Why Bacteria Are Useful

Bacteria are useful in biotechnology because:

• They reproduce very rapidly (by binary fission — can double every 20 minutes).

• They can produce complex molecules like enzymes and hormones (e.g. insulin).

• Their genetic code is the same as in all other organisms, so human genes work in bacteria.

• They have plasmids (small circles of extra DNA) that can carry genes between cells.

• People are less concerned about experiments on bacteria than on larger animals.

W
Write This Down — Fermenters (Bioreactors)

Bacteria and other microorganisms are grown in fermenters under controlled conditions:

• Nutrient input — energy source (carbohydrate) + growth materials (amino acids or ammonium salts)

• Temperature control — water jacket keeps it warm enough for enzyme activity but not so hot that proteins denature

• Paddle stirrers — mix contents so microbes stay in contact with nutrients; ensure even temperature

• Gas outlet — releases CO₂ or other gases to prevent pressure build-up

• Probes — monitor pH, temperature, and oxygen; linked to computer control

• Sterile conditions — to prevent contamination by unwanted microorganisms

!
Don't Confuse — Batch vs Continuous Culture

Batch culture: fixed input of nutrients, collect products, clean, restart. More expensive but contamination only ruins one batch.

Continuous culture: fresh nutrients added continuously, products collected at intervals. More economical but contamination causes greater losses.

?
Why This Matters

Fermenter diagrams are a common exam question. They'll give you a labelled diagram and ask you to explain what each part does. The answer is always about controlling conditions for optimal enzyme activity and preventing contamination.

  • I can list at least four reasons why bacteria are useful in biotechnology
  • I can describe the key features of a fermenter
  • I know the difference between batch and continuous culture
Section 21.2 Enzymes from Microorganisms
Microorganisms produce enzymes that humans use in industry, medicine, and everyday products. Immobilising enzymes makes them reusable and more economical.
W
Write This Down — Uses of Enzymes
Enzyme Use
Protease Softens gluten in biscuit-making; removes protein stains in biological washing powders
Lipase Removes fatty/greasy stains in washing powders; helps chocolate flow in sweet-making
Amylase Breaks down starch-based stains in washing powders
Pectinase Breaks down pectin in plant cell walls — clears and increases yield of fruit juices
Catalase Used in wound dressings — converts hydrogen peroxide to oxygen and water
Lactase Breaks down lactose in milk — produces lactose-free milk for lactose-intolerant people
W
Write This Down — Immobilised Enzymes

Immobilised enzymes = enzymes fixed to an inert (non-reactive) support material such as resin beads or fibres.

Advantages:

• Product does not contain enzyme molecules — easier to purify.

• Enzyme can be reused many times — continuous production, lower cost.

• Enzyme is stabilised — less likely to break down.

• Optimum conditions for the enzyme can be used without worrying about the whole organism.

T
Study Tip — Biological Washing Powders

If the exam asks "Why do biological washing powders work at lower temperatures?", the answer is: because enzymes work at moderate temperatures (around 30–40°C). At these lower temperatures, less energy is used, clothes don't shrink, AND the enzymes break down stains that normal detergents can't.

  • I can match at least four enzymes to their uses
  • I can explain what immobilised enzymes are and give two advantages
Section 21.3 Baking and Brewing
Yeast is a single-celled fungus that carries out anaerobic respiration (fermentation). This is the basis of both bread-making and alcohol production.
W
Write This Down — The Fermentation Equation

glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide + energy

This is anaerobic respiration in yeast. Also called alcoholic fermentation.

W
Write This Down — Baking vs Brewing

Baking (bread):

• Yeast ferments sugar in the dough, producing CO₂ bubbles that make the dough rise.

• Baking at 180°C kills the yeast, stops fermentation, evaporates alcohol, and forms a crust.

• Good bread is spongy (trapped CO₂) and fresh-smelling (partly alcohol vapour).

Brewing (alcohol):

• Yeast ferments sugar to produce ethanol (the useful product) and CO₂.

• At 8–9% alcohol concentration, the ethanol is toxic to the yeast and kills it, stopping fermentation. Strains of yeast tolerant to higher concentrations have been developed, but wine rarely exceeds about 12% alcohol.

• Spirits with higher alcohol content require distillation.

!
Don't Confuse

In baking, the useful product is the CO₂ (makes bread rise). In brewing, the useful product is the ethanol (the alcohol). Same reaction, different product wanted.

?
Why This Matters

The exam may ask "Why does wine rarely have more than 12% alcohol?" The answer: at high alcohol concentrations, the ethanol becomes toxic to the yeast and kills it, so fermentation stops. This is a classic 2-mark question.

  • I can write the word equation for anaerobic respiration in yeast
  • I know the role of CO₂ in baking and ethanol in brewing
  • I can explain why wine doesn't exceed ~12% alcohol
Section 21.4 Genetic Engineering
Genetic engineering means taking a gene from one organism and putting it into another. The most important example for the exam is the production of human insulin by bacteria.
W
Write This Down — Steps of Genetic Engineering

Step 1: The desirable gene (e.g. human insulin gene) is identified and cut out of the human chromosome using a restriction enzyme. This leaves sticky ends.

Step 2: A plasmid (small circle of DNA in a bacterium) is cut open at specific points using the same restriction enzyme, creating matching sticky ends.

Step 3: The human gene is inserted into the plasmid. The enzyme ligase "glues" (splices) the gene into the plasmid. This creates recombinant DNA.

Step 4: The recombinant plasmid is inserted back into a host bacterium.

Step 5: The bacterium is cultured in a fermenter. As it divides, all daughter cells contain the human gene and produce the desired protein (e.g. insulin).

Step 6: The product is extracted and purified.

W
Write This Down — Key Vocabulary

• Restriction enzyme = cuts DNA at specific points, leaving sticky ends.

• Ligase = joins/splices DNA fragments together.

• Plasmid = small circle of DNA in bacteria; acts as a vector (carrier) for the gene.

• Vector = anything that carries a gene from one organism to another (plasmid, virus).

• Recombinant DNA = DNA that contains genes from two different organisms.

W
Write This Down — Products of Genetic Engineering

• Human insulin — for treating diabetes. Purer than animal insulin, less likely to be rejected.

• Human growth hormone

• Factor VIII — blood clotting factor for haemophilia

• BGH (bovine growth hormone) — used to speed up the growth of beef cattle

• Hepatitis B vaccine — the gene coding for the surface antigen of hepatitis B virus is isolated and inserted into yeast cells. The yeast cells produce the antigen, which is purified into a vaccine. It is safe because no whole virus is present. Usually given in two doses (first gives 1 year’s protection, a booster extends this to 10 years).

?
Why This Matters

"Why is genetically engineered insulin better than insulin from pigs?" is a classic exam question. Answer: it's the human version of the protein, so it works more efficiently in a person and is less likely to be rejected by the body's defences. It can be produced in large quantities, at about 1% of the cost of insulin from pig pancreas. The process can be switched on or off easily, as the bacteria can be stored until needed again.

T
Study Tip — Learn the Steps as a Story

Think of it as a recipe: (1) cut gene out with restriction enzyme scissors, (2) cut plasmid open with the same scissors, (3) glue gene into plasmid with ligase, (4) put plasmid back into bacterium, (5) grow bacteria in a fermenter, (6) collect the product. Practise writing these steps from memory until you can do it without looking.

  • I can describe all six steps of genetic engineering
  • I know what restriction enzymes, ligase, plasmids, and vectors are
  • I can name at least three products made by genetic engineering
  • I can explain why GE insulin is better than animal insulin
Section 21.5 Gene Transfer and GM Organisms
Genetic engineering isn't just for bacteria. Genes can be transferred into plants and animals too, creating transgenic organisms. But this raises ethical questions.
W
Write This Down — Transgenic Organisms

A transgenic organism = an organism that has received genes from a different species.

Examples of GM plants:

• Improved shelf life (gene that inhibits enzymes causing ripening — e.g. "Flavr Savr" tomatoes)

• Pest resistance (gene for insecticidal crystal protein — kills caterpillars)

• Herbicide resistance (crop survives when herbicide kills surrounding weeds)

• Drought resistance (thicker, more waxy cuticles)

• Golden Rice — engineered to produce beta-carotene (vitamin A) for areas with vitamin A deficiency

• Nitrogen fixation genes transferred to non-leguminous crops (could reduce need for fertilisers)

W
Write This Down — Gene Therapy

Gene therapy = transferring a healthy human gene into a person's cells that contain a mutant allele causing disease.

Example: cystic fibrosis — the healthy gene is identified, placed into an empty influenza virus coat (used as a gene vector). The virus infects the diseased lung cells (which are easy to reach via the trachea and bronchi) and releases the healthy gene. The cell can now make the correct protein.

W
Write This Down — Benefits vs Concerns of GM
Benefits Concerns
Higher yields from fewer resources Pest-resistant genes could spread to wild plants, creating "superweeds"
Crops can grow in extreme environments, reducing famine Engineered bacteria could escape with unpredictable consequences
More predictable results than selective breeding Companies might patent organisms, blocking shared benefits
Healthier foods (e.g. Golden Rice with vitamin A) Ethical concerns about "designer" organisms and where to draw the line
T
Study Tip — Discuss Questions

The exam may ask you to "discuss the advantages and disadvantages of genetic engineering." Always give BOTH sides — at least two of each. Finish with a conclusion: "Overall, GE has significant benefits but must be carefully regulated to avoid environmental risks."

  • I know what a transgenic organism is
  • I can give at least three examples of GM crop modifications
  • I can describe gene therapy using cystic fibrosis as an example
  • I can discuss at least two benefits and two concerns of GM technology
