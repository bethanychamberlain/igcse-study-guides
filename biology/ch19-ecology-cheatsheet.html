Ecology and Ecosystems

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Ecosystem A unit containing the community of organisms AND their non-living (abiotic) environment interacting together. Community (community = living things only; ecosystem includes non-living too)
Population All the members of the same species in the same area at the same time. Community (community = ALL species in an area)
Community All the populations of different species living in one area. Population (one species only)
Producer An organism that makes its own food by photosynthesis (usually a green plant). Consumer (consumers eat other organisms)
Trophic level The position of an organism in a food chain (producer = level 1, primary consumer = level 2, etc.). —
Decomposer Fungi and bacteria that break down dead organisms by saprotrophic feeding (secrete enzymes externally, absorb products). Consumer (decomposers don't "eat" in the normal sense — they digest externally)
Nitrogen fixation N₂ gas from the air converted to ammonium/nitrate by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (in root nodules of legumes). Nitrification (that starts with ammonium already in the soil, not atmospheric N₂)
Nitrification Ammonium ions in the soil converted to nitrite, then nitrate, by nitrifying bacteria. Needs oxygen. Nitrogen fixation (that starts with N₂ gas from the air)
Denitrification Nitrate converted back to N₂ gas by denitrifying bacteria. Occurs in waterlogged (anaerobic) soils. Nitrification (that goes ammonium → nitrate; denitrification goes nitrate → N₂ gas)
Abiotic factor A non-living factor: temperature, light, water, oxygen concentration, wind. Biotic factor (living: predators, food, competition, disease)

Carbon Cycle vs Nitrogen Cycle

Carbon Cycle Nitrogen Cycle
Enters living things via Photosynthesis (CO₂ → glucose) Nitrogen fixation (N₂ → nitrate) + root absorption
Returns to atmosphere via Respiration, combustion, decomposition Denitrification (nitrate → N₂ gas)
Role of decomposers Respire, releasing CO₂ Break down proteins → ammonium ions
Key bacteria? No (mainly physical/biological processes) Yes — 4 types essential

Say It in One Sentence

Arrow direction Arrows in food chains point the direction energy flows — from the eaten TO the eater (grass → rabbit → fox).
Energy loss At every trophic level, ~80–90% of energy is lost as heat from respiration — that's why food chains rarely exceed 4–5 levels.
Saprotrophic feeding Decomposers secrete enzymes ONTO dead material, digest it externally, then absorb the simple products.
Carbon balance Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ faster than photosynthesis can remove it — that's the one-sentence version of climate change biology.
Lincoln index Population = (marked × recaptured total) ÷ marked recaptured. Used for mobile animals you can't just count.
Quadrat rule Place quadrats randomly (use a random number generator), repeat many times, and calculate the mean — never hand-pick locations.

Memory Tricks

Population vs Community vs Ecosystem
Population = one species. Community = all species living together. Ecosystem = community + the non-living environment. Think: P ⊂ C ⊂ E (each one nests inside the next).
Abiotic = A for "Absent of life"
Abiotic = non-living (temperature, light, water). Biotic = living (predators, disease, food). If the exam says "name two abiotic factors," don't write "predators" — that's biotic!
Nitrogen Cycle Bacteria
The four N-cycle processes: Fix it (N₂ → ammonium), Decompose it (dead matter → ammonium), Nitrify it (ammonium → nitrate), Denitrify it (nitrate → N₂). "Fix, Decompose, Nitrify, Denitrify."
Decomposition Needs OWT
Decomposers work fastest with: Oxygen, Warmth, waTer. Without these, decay is slow and smelly (anaerobic).

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Why do food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels?" Energy is lost as heat from respiration at each level — not enough energy left to support another level
"Name two abiotic factors" Temperature, light intensity, water availability (NOT predators — that's biotic)
"Which pyramid can never be inverted?" Pyramid of energy — always a true pyramid shape
"What is the difference between nitrogen fixation and nitrification?" Fixation: N₂ gas from air → ammonium/nitrate. Nitrification: ammonium already in soil → nitrate.
"Why do farmers drain waterlogged fields?" To prevent denitrification — denitrifying bacteria work in waterlogged (anaerobic) soils and remove useful nitrate
"Why do farmers plant legumes?" Legumes have nitrogen-fixing bacteria in root nodules that convert N₂ to nitrate, enriching the soil
"Explain why CO₂ levels are rising" Combustion of fossil fuels releases CO₂ faster than photosynthesis can remove it
"What would happen if the fox population decreased?" Rabbit population would increase (less predation) → plant population would decrease (more grazing). Always trace the chain.
