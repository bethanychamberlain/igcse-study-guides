Section 3.1 Diffusion
Diffusion is the simplest way particles move. No energy needed — particles just naturally spread out.
W
Write This Down — Diffusion

Diffusion is the net movement of molecules within a gas or liquid, from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration (down a concentration gradient), as a result of their random movement, until an equilibrium is reached.

The random movement of the particles is due to their own kinetic energy. The cells themselves do not have to expend any energy for diffusion to take place.

Examples:
• Oxygen diffuses from alveoli (lungs) into the blood
• Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into the alveoli
• Glucose and amino acids pass from inside the gut into the blood

?
Why This Matters

On the exam, they'll ask you to give an example of diffusion. The oxygen/CO₂ in the lungs example is the safest one — it's unambiguous and always scores marks.

!
Don't Confuse

Osmosis is a type of diffusion — specifically, it is the diffusion of water molecules through a partially permeable membrane. If a question says "water moves through a membrane from dilute to concentrated," that is osmosis. But diffusion can also involve water and other molecules; the key difference is that osmosis always involves a partially permeable membrane.

W
Write This Down — How Organisms Speed Up Diffusion

Living organisms have adaptations to increase the rate of diffusion:

• Short diffusion distances — the membranes in the lungs are very thin so that oxygen and carbon dioxide can diffuse quickly between the blood and the lung air spaces
• Concentration gradients are maintained — glucose molecules that cross from the gut into the blood are quickly removed by the circulating blood, so their concentration does not build up and equilibrium is not reached
• Large diffusion surfaces — the surface of the placenta is highly folded to increase the surface area for the diffusion of molecules between the mother and the developing fetus

T
Study Tip

Cover up the definition and say it in your own words. If you can't explain it without looking, you don't know it yet. Repeat until you can.

  • I can define diffusion without looking (include "random movement" and "equilibrium")
  • I can give two real examples of diffusion
  • I know that diffusion does NOT require energy (particles move due to kinetic energy)
  • I can list three ways organisms speed up diffusion
Section 3.2 Osmosis
Osmosis is a special case of diffusion. It's only about water, and only through a membrane.
W
Write This Down — Osmosis

Osmosis is the diffusion of water molecules from a region of higher concentration of water molecules to a region of lower concentration of water molecules, through a partially permeable membrane.

Partially permeable membrane = a membrane with tiny holes that lets small molecules (like water) through but blocks large ones (like sugar or protein). Examples: cell membranes, dialysis tubing in experiments.

!
Don't Confuse — This Got You on the Easter Test

You wrote that osmosis is "specifically for moving water" — that's half right but only gets 1 mark. The second mark requires: "through a partially permeable membrane." Always mention both parts.

W
Write This Down — What Happens to Cells

In pure water (dilute solution): water enters the cell by osmosis → cell swells
• Animal cell: takes in water, swells, and may burst (haemolysis)
• Plant cell: becomes turgid (firm) — cell wall prevents bursting

In concentrated solution: water leaves the cell by osmosis → cell shrinks
• Animal cell: becomes crenated (shrivelled)
• Plant cell: becomes flaccid, then plasmolysed (membrane pulls away from wall)

?
Why This Matters

This is why you can't drink seawater — the salt concentration outside your cells is too high, so water leaves your cells by osmosis and they shrivel up. It's also why slugs die when you put salt on them.

T
Study Tip — Draw It

  • I can define osmosis including BOTH key parts (water + membrane)
  • I know what happens to animal vs plant cells in dilute solution
  • I know what happens to animal vs plant cells in concentrated solution
  • I drew a diagram showing osmosis in action
Section 3.3 Active Transport
Sometimes cells need to move substances against the natural flow. That takes energy.
W
Write This Down

Active transport is a method by which particles can cross membranes against a concentration gradient (from lower to higher concentration). Protein carriers in the cell surface membrane pick up and carry particles across the membrane, using energy from respiration.

To summarise, active transport:
• can move molecules against a concentration gradient
• requires energy
• involves protein carriers in membranes

!
Don't Confuse — This Was Your Biggest Mix-Up

On Q5, you were asked: "Which process moves substances from low to high concentration, using energy?" You picked diffusion. But diffusion is the opposite — high to low, no energy.

Remember: Active transport is the active one — it takes effort (energy). Diffusion is passive — it happens on its own.

W
Write This Down — Examples

• Root hair cells absorb mineral ions from soil by active transport (soil has low concentration, root has high)
• Glucose is absorbed in the gut by active transport when concentration in gut is lower than in blood

?
Why This Matters

Active transport explains why root hairs need mitochondria — they need energy for active transport. If an exam question asks "Why do root hair cells have many mitochondria?", the answer links back here.

T
Study Tip — The "Uphill" Trick

Think of concentration like a hill. Diffusion rolls downhill (easy, no effort). Active transport pushes uphill (hard, needs energy). If the question mentions "energy" or "against the gradient" — it's active transport. Always.

  • I can define active transport (against gradient, energy, protein carriers)
  • I know it goes LOW to HIGH (opposite of diffusion)
  • I know it needs energy from respiration and uses protein carriers in the membrane
  • I can give two examples
Section 3.3 continued Phagocytosis
Some particles are too large to cross a membrane by diffusion or active transport. A few specialised cells have a different method.
W
Write This Down — Phagocytosis

Phagocytosis is the process by which a cell engulfs large particles by the cell surface membrane flowing around them. The particle is taken into the cell inside a vacuole.

• Used by white blood cells to engulf and destroy bacteria and other pathogens
• This is how the immune system removes invading microorganisms

  • I know that phagocytosis is used when particles are too large for diffusion or active transport
  • I can describe how white blood cells use phagocytosis
