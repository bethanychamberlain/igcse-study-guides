Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define pathogen.
1 mark
Answer
A pathogen is an organism that causes disease [1].
Short and simple. Don't over-complicate it. "An organism that lives in a host" is not enough — you must say it CAUSES DISEASE.

2
Name four types of pathogen and give one disease example for each.
4 marks
Answer
Virus — e.g. influenza or AIDS [1].
Bacterium — e.g. cholera or food poisoning [1].
Protoctist — e.g. malaria or dysentery [1].
Fungus — e.g. athlete's foot or ringworm [1].
One mark per correct pair (pathogen type + valid disease). You can also mention parasitic worms (tapeworm) as a fifth type.

3
Give three natural ways the body prevents pathogens from entering.
3 marks
Answer
Any three of:
Skin — acts as a physical barrier [1].
Mucus and cilia in the respiratory tract — trap and sweep away pathogens [1].
Hydrochloric acid in the stomach — kills bacteria [1].
Lysozyme in tears — destroys bacterial cell walls [1].
Blood clotting — seals wounds to prevent pathogen entry [1].
One mark per barrier. Name the barrier AND briefly explain what it does. Just saying "skin" without "acts as a physical barrier" may not get the mark.

4
State one difference between a phagocyte and a lymphocyte.
1 mark
Answer
Phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens, whereas lymphocytes produce antibodies [1].
You can also say: phagocytes have an irregular/lobed nucleus while lymphocytes have a large round nucleus. Either is correct.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Describe the process of blood clotting, starting from when the skin is cut.
4 marks
Answer
When the skin is cut, platelets are damaged and release enzymes [1]. These enzymes convert the inactive plasma protein fibrinogen into fibrin [1]. Fibrin forms a mesh of fibres across the wound [1]. Red blood cells become trapped in the mesh, which dries to form a scab that seals the wound and prevents pathogen entry [1].
Four steps in the chain: platelets release enzymes → fibrinogen becomes fibrin → mesh forms → scab seals wound. The fibrinogen → fibrin step is worth a mark on its own and many students forget it.

6
Describe the process of phagocytosis. Include two structural features of phagocytes that help them carry out their function.
4 marks
Answer
The phagocyte detects and moves towards the pathogen [1]. It engulfs the pathogen by flowing around it and enclosing it in a vacuole [1].

Structural adaptations:
The irregular/lobed nucleus allows the cell to squeeze through gaps in capillary walls to reach the site of infection [1].
The cell contains digestive enzymes which are released into the vacuole to break down and destroy the pathogen [1].
Two marks for the process (detects + engulfs), two marks for the adaptations (nucleus shape + enzymes). Always link the adaptation to its function.

7
Explain the difference between active and passive immunity. Give one example of each.
4 marks
Answer
In active immunity, the body produces its own antibodies in response to an antigen [1]. This gives long-term protection because memory cells are formed. Example: vaccination, or catching a disease and recovering [1].

In passive immunity, the body receives ready-made antibodies from another source [1]. This gives only short-term protection because no memory cells are formed. Example: antibodies from mother's breast milk, or injection of anti-tetanus serum [1].
Key contrast: active = body makes OWN antibodies (long-term). Passive = body RECEIVES antibodies (short-term). Then one valid example of each.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Explain how vaccination leads to immunity against a specific disease. In your answer, use the terms: antigen, lymphocyte, antibody, and memory cell.
5 marks
Answer
A vaccine contains a weakened or dead form of the pathogen (or its antigens) [1]. The antigens on the pathogen are recognised by lymphocytes in the blood [1]. The lymphocytes are stimulated to produce specific antibodies that match the antigens [1]. Some lymphocytes remain in the body as memory cells [1]. If the same pathogen enters the body in the future, the memory cells recognise its antigens and produce antibodies much faster and in greater quantities, destroying the pathogen before it can cause disease [1].
Five-step chain: (1) vaccine contains weakened pathogen/antigens, (2) lymphocytes recognise antigens, (3) antibodies produced, (4) memory cells formed, (5) faster response on re-infection. Each step = 1 mark.

9
A child receives a first vaccination at 4 months and a booster at 8 months. Describe how the antibody concentration in the blood changes over time after each injection. Explain why the response to the booster is different from the first injection.
5 marks
Answer
After the first injection, there is a delay before antibodies appear in the blood (it takes several days for lymphocytes to recognise the antigen and begin producing antibodies) [1]. The antibody concentration rises slowly, reaches a moderate peak, then gradually declines [1].

After the booster at 8 months, the antibody concentration rises much faster, reaches a higher peak, and remains elevated for much longer [1]. This is because memory cells from the first vaccination are already present in the body [1]. They recognise the antigen immediately and rapidly divide to produce large numbers of antibodies (the secondary immune response) [1].
Describe the graph shape for BOTH responses (slow/small first, fast/large second), then explain the difference is due to MEMORY CELLS. This is one of the most common exam questions on the whole topic.

10
A person is vaccinated against measles, tetanus, and rubella as a child. As an adult, they catch chicken pox. Explain why they are immune to measles but not to chicken pox. Also explain why some people argue vaccination should continue even when a disease appears to have been eliminated.
6 marks
Answer
Why immune to measles but not chicken pox:
Antibodies are specific — each type of antibody only binds to one type of antigen [1]. The measles vaccine stimulated the production of antibodies (and memory cells) specific to the measles antigen [1]. The chicken pox virus has different antigens on its surface, so the measles antibodies and memory cells do not recognise it [1]. The person has never been exposed to chicken pox antigens, so they have no memory cells for it and no immunity [1].

Why continue vaccination:
If vaccination stops, the number of immune individuals in the population decreases [1]. Even if a disease seems eliminated locally, travellers could bring the pathogen from other countries. Without herd immunity (enough vaccinated people to prevent spread), the disease could return and spread rapidly among the now-unprotected population [1].
Key idea: specificity (antibodies only fit matching antigens). Then for the continuation argument: herd immunity, travel risk, and the example of smallpox (eliminated worldwide by sustained vaccination).
