Circulation

Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Double circulation Blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit: once to the lungs (pulmonary), once to the body (systemic). —
Artery Thick-walled, elastic blood vessel carrying blood away from the heart at high pressure. Vein (thin walls, valves, carries blood towards the heart)
Vein Thin-walled blood vessel with valves, carrying blood back towards the heart at low pressure. Artery (thick walls, no valves, high pressure)
Capillary Tiny vessel (one cell thick) that connects arteries to veins. Where exchange of substances happens. —
Haemoglobin The iron-containing protein inside red blood cells that binds to oxygen (forming oxyhaemoglobin). Red blood cell (haemoglobin is inside the RBC — it's not the cell itself)
Plasma The liquid part of blood. Transports dissolved glucose, amino acids, urea, CO₂, hormones, and heat. Tissue fluid (tissue fluid is plasma squeezed out of capillaries — it lacks RBCs and large proteins)
Phagocyte White blood cell that engulfs and digests pathogens (phagocytosis). Has lobed nucleus. Lymphocyte (that makes antibodies — different job)
Lymphocyte White blood cell that produces antibodies (specific proteins) to fight infection. Phagocyte (that physically eats pathogens — different job)
Platelet Cell fragment that triggers blood clotting when a vessel is damaged. Converts fibrinogen to fibrin. —
Coronary artery The artery that supplies the heart muscle itself with oxygenated blood. Blockage = heart attack. Aorta (the aorta carries blood to the whole body — coronary arteries branch off it to feed the heart muscle)
Atheroma Fatty deposit that builds up inside coronary arteries, narrowing them and restricting blood flow. —

Artery vs Vein vs Capillary — Side by Side

Artery Vein Capillary
Direction Away from heart Towards heart Links arteries to veins
Wall Thick, muscular, elastic Thin One cell thick
Lumen Narrow Wide Very narrow (one RBC wide)

Valves? No (except semilunar) Yes — prevent backflow No

*Exceptions: pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood; pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood.

Exam Trap — The Pulmonary Exception
The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood and the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood. "Artery" and "vein" refer to direction (away from / towards heart), NOT the type of blood inside.

Components of Blood — What Does What

Component Function Key Feature
Red blood cells Carry O₂ (as oxyhaemoglobin) No nucleus, biconcave disc, contains haemoglobin
Phagocytes (WBC) Engulf and digest pathogens Lobed nucleus, digestive enzymes
Lymphocytes (WBC) Produce antibodies Large round nucleus
Platelets Blood clotting Cell fragments, trigger fibrinogen → fibrin
Plasma Transports dissolved substances and heat Carries glucose, amino acids, urea, CO₂, hormones

Say It in One Sentence

Double circulation Blood goes through the heart twice per loop: right side pumps to lungs, left side pumps to the body.
Left ventricle Has the thickest wall because it pumps blood the furthest distance (to the entire body), needing the most pressure.
Capillaries One cell thick, highly branched, slow blood flow — perfect conditions for exchange of substances with tissue fluid.
Tissue fluid Plasma squeezed out of capillaries under pressure; bathes cells for exchange but has no RBCs or large proteins.
Pacemaker A patch of tissue in the right atrium wall that sends electrical signals to make the heart beat in rhythm.
Heart attack Coronary artery blocked by atheroma → heart muscle starved of O₂ → muscle cells die → heart stops pumping properly.

Memory Tricks

A = Away, V = Via veins back
Artery = Away from heart. Vein = back Via veins to the heart. The biggest artery is the aorta. The biggest vein is the vena cava.
Trace the Red Blood Cell Loop
Vena cava → Right atrium → Right ventricle → Pulmonary artery → Lungs → Pulmonary vein → Left atrium → Left ventricle → Aorta → Body → Vena cava. Say it out loud five times. That's the entire double circulation.
Ph = Physical eater, L = Launches antibodies
Phagocytes physically eat (engulf and digest) pathogens. Lymphocytes launch antibodies (proteins). Two completely different defence mechanisms.
Why the Left Side Is Thicker
Think: Left = Long journey. The left ventricle pumps blood all the way around the body. The right ventricle only pumps to the nearby lungs. Longer distance = more pressure needed = thicker muscle wall.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Why do large organisms need a circulatory system?" Small surface area to volume ratio — diffusion alone is too slow to reach cells deep inside
"Why is the left ventricle wall thicker?" It pumps blood further (whole body vs just lungs), so needs more pressure, so needs more muscle
"Name the component of blood that carries oxygen" Red blood cells (which contain haemoglobin)
"Name two substances carried in plasma" Glucose and urea (also: amino acids, CO₂, hormones)
"How are capillaries adapted for exchange?" One cell thick (short diffusion distance), highly branched (large surface area), close to every cell
"What happens during a heart attack?" Coronary artery blocked by atheroma → heart muscle lacks oxygen → muscle cells die
"Give two risk factors for CHD" Smoking and high-cholesterol diet (also: lack of exercise, stress, genetic factors)
"What is the role of valves in veins?" Prevent backflow of blood — blood in veins is at low pressure, so it needs valves to keep it moving forward
