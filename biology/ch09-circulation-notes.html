Section 9.1 Blood as the Transport Medium
Large organisms can't rely on simple diffusion — they need a mass flow system to move substances quickly around the body. Blood is that system's transport medium.
W
Write This Down — Why We Need a Circulatory System

Large organisms have a small surface area to volume ratio. Cells deep inside the body are too far from the surface to get oxygen and glucose by diffusion alone. A mass flow system (the circulatory system) carries large volumes of fluid to all parts of the organism.

A mass flow system has four components:
• A medium — the blood
• A system of tubes — arteries, veins, capillaries
• A pump — the heart
• Sites of exchange — the capillaries

W
Write This Down — Components of Blood

Blood = plasma (55%) + cells (45%). The plasma is a straw-coloured liquid that carries dissolved substances.

Component Function Key Adaptation
Red blood cells (erythrocytes) Transport O₂ from lungs to tissues; carry CO₂ back Contain haemoglobin; no nucleus (more space); biconcave disc shape; small & flexible
White blood cells — phagocytes Engulf and digest pathogens Irregular nucleus to squeeze through capillary walls; contain digestive enzymes
White blood cells — lymphocytes Produce antibodies (proteins that fight infection) Large nucleus for gene control of antibody production
Platelets Cell fragments involved in blood clotting Release clotting enzymes when blood vessel is damaged
Plasma Transports dissolved glucose, amino acids, urea, CO₂, hormones, and heat High water content = high specific heat capacity for heat distribution
?
Why This Matters

A very common exam question: "Name the component of blood that transports oxygen" — the answer is red blood cells (not plasma, not haemoglobin on its own). Another classic: "Name two substances transported in the plasma" — glucose and urea are the safest picks.

!
Don't Confuse

Haemoglobin is the protein inside red blood cells that carries oxygen. It is not a cell itself. Oxygen is carried by red blood cells (which contain haemoglobin). CO₂ is mostly carried dissolved in the plasma.

T
Study Tip

Draw and label a centrifuged blood sample (test tube with layers). From top: plasma (straw-coloured), white blood cells and platelets (thin white layer), red blood cells (bottom, red). This diagram comes up regularly.

  • I can name the four components of a mass flow system
  • I can name the components of blood and their functions
  • I know that red blood cells contain haemoglobin and have no nucleus
Section 9.2 The Circulatory System
Blood flows around the body in a system of blood vessels. Humans have a double circulation — blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit.
W
Write This Down — Double Circulation

In a double circulatory system, blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit of the body:

Circuit 1 (Pulmonary): Heart → Lungs → Heart
(Right ventricle pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery; oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium via the pulmonary vein)

Circuit 2 (Systemic): Heart → Body → Heart
(Left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood to the body via the aorta; deoxygenated blood returns to the right atrium via the vena cava)

!
Don't Confuse — The Two Exceptions

The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood (even though arteries normally carry oxygenated blood). The pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood (even though veins normally carry deoxygenated blood). The names refer to the direction of flow (artery = away from heart, vein = towards heart), NOT the type of blood.

Section 9.3 Capillaries and Tissue Fluid
Capillaries are where the real action happens — substances are exchanged between the blood and the body's cells through tissue fluid.
W
Write This Down — Capillary Adaptations

Capillaries are adapted for exchange in several ways:
• Walls are only one cell thick — short diffusion distance
• Highly branched — large surface area for diffusion
• Constantly supplied with fresh blood — maintains concentration gradients
• Lumen is so narrow that red blood cells squeeze through in single file, slowing them down and allowing more time for exchange

W
Write This Down — Tissue Fluid

Tissue fluid is formed when blood plasma is squeezed out of the capillaries under pressure. It bathes the cells and allows exchange by diffusion.

• Tissue fluid contains dissolved oxygen, glucose, amino acids (useful substances)
• It does NOT contain red blood cells or large plasma proteins — they are too big to pass through capillary walls
• Cells take what they need and release waste (CO₂, urea) into the tissue fluid
• Most tissue fluid returns to the capillaries; some drains into lymph vessels and is returned to the blood near the heart

?
Why This Matters

If tissue fluid doesn't return properly, tissues swell up — this is called oedema. The exam might ask you to explain why capillary walls being one cell thick is important. Answer: it gives a short diffusion distance so substances can cross quickly.

T
Study Tip

Think of capillaries like a delivery system. Arteries are the motorway (fast, high pressure), capillaries are the local streets (slow, one cell wide, where the parcels actually get dropped off), and veins are the return road. Sketch the exchange at a capillary bed — arrows showing O₂ and glucose going out, CO₂ coming in.

  • I can list three adaptations of capillaries for exchange
  • I can explain how tissue fluid is formed
  • I know that tissue fluid does NOT contain red blood cells or plasma proteins
  • I know excess tissue fluid drains into lymph vessels
Section 9.4 The Heart
The heart is a muscular pump with four chambers. It works as a double pump — the right side pumps blood to the lungs, the left side pumps blood to the body.
W
Write This Down — Heart Structure

Four chambers:
• Right atrium — receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the vena cava
• Right ventricle — pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery
• Left atrium — receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via the pulmonary vein
• Left ventricle — pumps oxygenated blood to the body via the aorta

Other key structures:
• Septum — wall dividing left and right sides; prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood
• Valves — atrioventricular valves (between atria and ventricles) and semilunar valves (at the exits to arteries) prevent backflow
• Tendons — hold the atrioventricular valves in place so they don't flip inside out

W
Write This Down — Why the Left Ventricle is Thicker

The left ventricle has a much thicker muscular wall than the right ventricle because it must pump blood at high pressure all the way around the body. The right ventricle only pumps blood the short distance to the lungs. The body circuit has about 5 times the pressure of the lung circuit.

!
Don't Confuse — Diagram Orientation

In heart diagrams, the left side of the heart appears on the RIGHT of the diagram (and vice versa). This is because the diagram shows the heart as if you were looking at someone facing you. Don't let this trip you up on the exam.

W
Write This Down — The Pacemaker

The heartbeat is controlled by the pacemaker (also called the sinoatrial node), a specialised piece of tissue in the wall of the right atrium. It sends electrical signals that:
• Make the atria contract just before the ventricles (so blood flows atria → ventricles)
• Adjust heart rate to match the body's demand for oxygen and nutrients
If the natural pacemaker fails, an artificial electronic pacemaker can be implanted.

?
Why This Matters

A classic exam question: "Why is the wall of the left ventricle thicker than the right?" Make sure you say it pumps blood further (to the whole body vs just the lungs) so it needs more pressure, which requires more muscle. Three-part answer = full marks.

T
Study Tip — Trace the Blood

Practise tracing the path of a red blood cell around the whole body: vena cava → right atrium → right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs → pulmonary vein → left atrium → left ventricle → aorta → body → vena cava. If you can say this from memory, you understand double circulation.

  • I can name all four chambers and the vessels connected to each
  • I can explain why the left ventricle wall is thicker
  • I know the role of the septum and the valves
  • I can trace the path of blood through the entire double circulation
  • I know what the pacemaker does and where it is
Section 9.5 Coronary Heart Disease
The heart muscle itself needs a blood supply. When that supply gets blocked, things go wrong fast.
W
Write This Down — Coronary Arteries

The heart muscle gets its own blood supply through the coronary arteries, which branch off the aorta. These deliver oxygenated blood and glucose to the heart muscle cells for aerobic respiration (the heart needs constant energy to keep beating).

W
Write This Down — What is CHD?

Coronary heart disease (CHD) occurs when the coronary arteries become narrowed or blocked by a fatty deposit called atheroma. This restricts blood flow to the heart muscle.

If the coronary artery is blocked:
• Heart muscle cells are deprived of oxygen and glucose
• Poisonous waste (e.g. lactic acid) builds up
• Part of the heart muscle stops contracting — this is a heart attack
• Can be fatal because other organs lose their blood supply

W
Write This Down — Risk Factors and Treatments

Risk factors for CHD:
• Poor diet — high cholesterol, saturated fatty acids
• Poor lifestyle — smoking, lack of exercise, stress
• Genetic factors — family history, being male

Treatments:
• Coronary artery bypass — a blood vessel from another part of the body is grafted to bypass the blocked section
• Angioplasty with stent — a tiny metal cage is inserted into the narrowed artery to hold it open

?
Why This Matters

CHD is one of the biggest killers in the developed world. The exam often gives you data (graphs of smoking vs CHD risk, or cholesterol vs death rate) and asks you to describe patterns or explain the link. Always connect it back to: fatty deposits → narrowed artery → reduced blood flow → heart muscle lacks oxygen.

T
Study Tip — Prevention vs Treatment

Know the difference between prevention (don't smoke, exercise, eat less fat) and treatment (bypass surgery, stent). The exam might ask for one of each. Also remember: "coronary" always refers to the heart's own blood supply, not the main circulation.

  • I know what coronary arteries are and why the heart needs them
  • I can explain what happens during a heart attack
  • I can list three risk factors for CHD
  • I can describe bypass surgery and stent insertion
  • I know the difference between preventing and treating CHD
