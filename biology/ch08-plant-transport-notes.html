Section 8.1 Transport Systems in Plants — Xylem and Phloem
Plants need to move water, minerals, and sugars around their bodies. They have two specialised transport tissues, bundled together in vascular bundles.
W
Write This Down — Xylem vs Phloem
Feature Xylem Phloem
What it transports Water and dissolved mineral ions Sucrose (sugar) and amino acids
Direction Always upward (roots → leaves) Both ways (source → sink)
Cells alive or dead? Dead — no cytoplasm, no organelles Alive — thin cytoplasm, must remain alive or transport stops
Cell wall Thickened with lignin (waterproof, strong) Thin walls; sieve plates with pores between cells
Structure Long hollow tubes (end walls removed) — no obstruction to water flow Sieve tubes with companion cells that carry out life processes
Process name Transpiration stream Translocation
W
Write This Down — Sources and Sinks

Phloem transports sugars from sources (where sugars are made or stored) to sinks (where sugars are used or stored).

• In summer: leaves (source, photosynthesis) → roots (sink, storage)
• In spring: roots (source, stored starch) → new leaves (sink, growth)
This means phloem moves materials in both directions, depending on the season.

!
Don't Confuse

Xylem = water UP. Phloem = sugar BOTH WAYS. Students often say "xylem goes up, phloem goes down." That's wrong — phloem goes both up AND down depending on where the source and sink are.

?
Why This Matters

The xylem/phloem comparison table is a classic exam question. You can also be asked: "Why are xylem cells dead?" Answer: because dead cells have no cytoplasm, so there is no obstruction to the flow of water. The hollow tubes + lignin = perfect water pipes.

T
Study Tip — Celery Experiment

Remember the celery-in-coloured-water experiment? The coloured dye travels up through the xylem. If you cut the celery cross-section, you can see the stained dots — those are the xylem vessels. This proves xylem carries water upward.

  • I can compare xylem and phloem in a table from memory
  • I know xylem cells are dead and strengthened with lignin
  • I understand sources and sinks, and why phloem goes both ways
  • I know the term "translocation" for sugar transport in phloem
Section 8.2 Uptake of Water and Minerals by Roots
Plants absorb water and minerals from the soil through their roots. The root hair cells are specially adapted for this job.
W
Write This Down — Root Hair Cells

Root hair cells are extensions of the epidermal cells of the root. They are adapted to absorb water and minerals:

• Long, thin projections that extend between soil particles → enormous surface area for absorption
• Thin cell wall → short distance for water to cross
• Contain many mitochondria → provide energy for active transport of mineral ions

W
Write This Down — How Water and Minerals Enter

Water enters by osmosis: The soil solution has a higher water potential than the root hair cell cytoplasm (which contains dissolved sugars and salts). Water moves down the water potential gradient, through the partially permeable cell membrane, into the cell.

Minerals enter by two methods:

• Diffusion — if the mineral concentration in soil is higher than in the root cell
• Active transport — if the mineral concentration in soil is lower than in the root cell. This requires energy from respiration, which is why root hair cells have many mitochondria.

!
Don't Confuse — This Links Back to Chapter 3

Water enters root hairs by osmosis (not active transport). Minerals enter by active transport (against concentration gradient) OR diffusion (down concentration gradient). The exam loves asking which process is which — don't mix them up.

?
Why This Matters

If a question asks "Why does waterlogging kill plants?", here's the chain: waterlogged soil → no air spaces → no oxygen for root cells → no aerobic respiration → no energy for active transport → plant can't absorb minerals → plant dies. Also, flooding with salt water lowers the water potential of the soil, preventing osmosis.

  • I can describe three adaptations of root hair cells
  • I know water enters by osmosis and minerals by active transport (or diffusion)
  • I can explain why root hair cells need many mitochondria
Section 8.3 Transpiration — Water Movement Through the Plant
98% of the water a plant takes up is lost through its leaves. This might seem wasteful, but it actually drives the whole water transport system.
W
Write This Down — What Is Transpiration?

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the leaves of a plant, mainly through the stomata (tiny pores on the leaf surface, mostly on the lower surface).

The transpiration stream is the continuous flow of water through the plant:

1. Water evaporates from the surfaces of spongy mesophyll cells inside the leaf
2. Water vapour diffuses out through the stomata
3. Water is pulled up the xylem in the stem (due to tension from water loss above and cohesion between water molecules)
4. Water enters the roots from the soil by osmosis

W
Write This Down — Factors That Affect Transpiration Rate
Factor Effect on Transpiration Why
High temperature Increases rate More kinetic energy → water evaporates faster from leaf cells
Wind Increases rate Blows away humid air from leaf surface → steeper concentration gradient
Low humidity Increases rate Bigger water potential gradient between leaf and atmosphere
High light intensity Increases rate Stomata open (for photosynthesis) → water vapour can escape

Remember: anything that increases evaporation or opens stomata increases transpiration.

W
Write This Down — Measuring Transpiration: The Potometer

A potometer measures water uptake by a cut shoot (which is approximately equal to water loss by transpiration). An air bubble is introduced into a capillary tube and its movement is timed. Faster bubble movement = faster water uptake = faster transpiration.

?
Why This Matters

"Describe an experiment to investigate the effect of wind on transpiration rate" is a classic practical question. Use a potometer with and without a fan. Measure the distance the bubble moves in a set time. The fan increases transpiration rate — the bubble moves faster.

!
Don't Confuse

A potometer measures water uptake, not water loss. These are approximately equal because ~98% of water taken up is lost by transpiration. But strictly, the potometer measures uptake. The exam may try to catch you on this.

T
Study Tip — The 4 Factors

Use the mnemonic WHTL — Wind, Heat, (low) Humidity, Light. All four INCREASE transpiration. If the exam gives you a scenario (hot windy day vs cold still day), just check which factors are present.

  • I can define transpiration
  • I can describe the four steps of the transpiration stream
  • I know four factors that affect transpiration rate and can explain WHY
  • I know what a potometer is and what it measures
Section 8.4 The Leaf, Stomata, and Water Loss
Stomata are the plant's dilemma: they must open to let CO₂ in for photosynthesis, but every time they open, water escapes. Plants have evolved clever adaptations to balance this trade-off.
W
Write This Down — Stomata

Stomata (singular: stoma) are tiny pores on the leaf surface, mostly on the lower surface (to reduce evaporation from sun-warmed upper surface). Each stoma is surrounded by two guard cells which can open and close the pore.

When guard cells are turgid (full of water) → stomata open → CO₂ enters, water vapour escapes.
When guard cells are flaccid (lost water) → stomata close → reduces water loss.

W
Write This Down — Leaf Adaptations to Reduce Water Loss

Plants in dry environments (xerophytes) have special features:

• Thick, waxy cuticle — waterproof layer on leaf surface reduces evaporation
• Stomata in pits/grooves — traps humid air near the stomata, reducing the concentration gradient
• Rolled leaves — traps humid air on inner surface near stomata
• Reduced leaves (spines) — e.g. cacti; smaller surface area for water loss. The stem is green and carries out photosynthesis instead
• Deep or shallow roots — to reach water (deep water table) or catch light rainfall

W
Write This Down — Wilting

When water loss by transpiration exceeds water uptake from roots:

• Cells lose water → become flaccid (lose turgor pressure)
• Plant becomes limp — this is wilting
• Wilting actually helps the plant because it reduces the leaf surface area exposed to sun and wind, slowing further water loss

?
Why This Matters

"Describe how the structure of a marram grass leaf reduces water loss" is a favourite exam question. The answer uses the adaptations above: rolled leaves trap humid air, stomata are in grooves, thick waxy cuticle. Every adaptation must include how it reduces water loss, not just what it is.

T
Study Tip — The Cobalt Chloride Experiment

Cobalt chloride paper is blue when dry, pink when wet. If you tape it to both sides of a leaf, the paper on the lower surface turns pink faster — proving more stomata are on the underside. This is a simple but common practical question.

  • I know what stomata are and why they are mostly on the lower surface
  • I can explain how guard cells open and close stomata
  • I can list at least four adaptations of xerophytes and explain how each reduces water loss
  • I know what wilting is and why it happens
Putting It All Together The Complete Water Journey
If you can trace the journey of a water molecule from the soil to the atmosphere, you understand the entire chapter.
W
Write This Down — Trace the Water Journey

1. Soil → Root hair cell: Water enters by osmosis (soil has higher water potential than root cell cytoplasm).

2. Root → Xylem: Water moves across the root cortex by osmosis, cell to cell, until it reaches the xylem vessels.

3. Xylem → Leaf: Water is pulled up the xylem as a continuous column. This is driven by tension (pull from water evaporating above) and held together by cohesion (water molecules stick to each other).

4. Leaf → Atmosphere: Water evaporates from the surfaces of spongy mesophyll cells into the air spaces inside the leaf. Water vapour then diffuses out through the stomata into the atmosphere. This is transpiration.

T

Draw a plant with roots, stem, and leaf. Trace the water journey from soil to atmosphere with arrows. At each stage, label the process (osmosis, cohesion/tension, evaporation, diffusion). If you can do this from memory, you own this chapter.
