Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Photosynthesis The process by which plants use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. Respiration (the opposite — breaks glucose down to release energy)
Chloroplast The organelle where photosynthesis takes place. Contains chlorophyll. Chlorophyll (the green pigment inside the chloroplast, not the organelle itself)
Chlorophyll Green pigment that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis. Chloroplast (chlorophyll is inside the chloroplast)
Limiting factor The factor in shortest supply that restricts the rate of photosynthesis (light, CO₂, or temperature). —
Compensation point The light intensity where photosynthesis rate exactly equals respiration rate — no net gas exchange. Limiting factor (compensation point is about balance, not bottleneck)
Stomata Tiny pores on the lower leaf surface that allow CO₂ in and O₂ out. Guard cells (guard cells open and close the stoma — they are not the pore itself)
Palisade mesophyll Tall, tightly packed cells near the top of the leaf, packed with chloroplasts for maximum light absorption. Spongy mesophyll (loosely packed with air spaces for gas diffusion)
Starch The storage form of carbohydrate in plants. Insoluble, so it doesn't affect osmosis. Glucose (glucose is soluble and made first; starch is how it is stored)
Nitrate ion Mineral absorbed from soil, needed to make amino acids and proteins. Magnesium ion (that one is for chlorophyll, not protein)
Eutrophication Excess fertiliser washes into water → algal bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose them using up oxygen → fish die. —

Photosynthesis vs Respiration — Side by Side

Photosynthesis Respiration
Word equation CO₂ + H₂O → glucose + O₂ glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
Energy Takes in light energy (endothermic) Releases energy (exothermic)
When? Only in the light All the time (day and night)
Where? In chloroplasts In mitochondria
Gas change Takes in CO₂, releases O₂ Takes in O₂, releases CO₂
Exam Trap — Plants Always Respire
Plants do NOT "switch off" respiration during the day. Both processes happen at the same time in the light, but photosynthesis is faster so it masks respiration. In the dark, only respiration occurs.

Leaf Layers — Top to Bottom

Layer Key Feature Adaptation
Waxy cuticle Waterproof, transparent Reduces water loss; lets light through
Upper epidermis Transparent, no chloroplasts Lets light pass to palisade cells
Palisade mesophyll Tall cells, many chloroplasts Maximum light absorption
Spongy mesophyll Loose cells, air spaces Gas diffusion (CO₂ in, O₂ out)
Vein (vascular bundle) Xylem + phloem Water in (xylem), sugar out (phloem)
Lower epidermis Contains stomata Gas exchange; on underside to reduce water loss

Say It in One Sentence

Photosynthesis Plants use light energy and chlorophyll to turn carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.
Chloroplast An energy transducer that converts light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.
Starch test Destarch the plant, then: boil leaf → ethanol (removes chlorophyll) → warm water → iodine (blue-black = starch present).
Guard cells Turgid (swollen) = stoma opens; flaccid (floppy) = stoma closes to reduce water loss.
Limiting factor Whichever factor is in shortest supply acts as the bottleneck that slows down the whole process.
Compensation point The exact light intensity where photosynthesis and respiration rates are equal — no net gas exchange.
Nitrate → protein Plants need nitrate ions from the soil to build amino acids and proteins; without them, growth is stunted.

Memory Tricks

The Word Equation (You MUST Memorise This)
Carbon dioxide + Water ⟶ Glucose + Oxygen. Remember: CW-GO (like "see, we go!"). Don't forget to write "light energy" and "chlorophyll" above/below the arrow — leaving them out loses you marks.
N = proteiN, M = Makes green
Nitrate = proteiN. Magnesium = chlorophyll (which Makes things green). If the plant has yellow leaves, think: is it stunted (nitrate) or just pale (magnesium)?
Reading a Limiting Factor Graph
If the line is still going up, the x-axis factor is limiting. If the line has levelled off, something else is now the bottleneck. Two lines at different temperatures? The higher line shows temperature was the limiting factor at the plateau.
Hydrogencarbonate Indicator Colours
Think traffic lights in reverse: Purple = CO₂ went down (photosynthesis winning). Red = normal. Yellow = CO₂ went up (respiration winning, or in the dark).

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Write the word equation for photosynthesis" carbon dioxide + water ⟶ glucose + oxygen (with light energy and chlorophyll)
"Where in the cell does photosynthesis occur?" In the chloroplasts, mainly in palisade mesophyll cells
"Why are most stomata on the lower epidermis?" To reduce water loss — the lower surface gets less direct sunlight
"Identify the limiting factor from the graph" If the line is still rising, the x-axis factor is limiting. If it has levelled off, another factor is limiting.
"What mineral is needed for chlorophyll?" Magnesium ions. Without it, leaves turn yellow (chlorosis).
"Why does a destarched plant have no starch?" It was kept in the dark for 48 hours, so it used up all stored starch through respiration and could not photosynthesise to make more.
"Name the green parts of a variegated leaf that produce starch" Only the green parts (with chlorophyll) produce starch, proving chlorophyll is needed for photosynthesis.
"How do greenhouses increase crop yield?" Artificial lighting, CO₂ enrichment (~0.1%), and heating — each removes a limiting factor so photosynthesis rate increases.
