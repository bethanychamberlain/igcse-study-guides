Section 15.1 Drugs, Antibiotics, and Penicillin
A fungus such as Penicillium absorbs food molecules from its environment for its own metabolism. Sometimes it secretes substances to kill off disease-causing or competitive microorganisms. A product made by one microorganism to kill off another is called an antibiotic.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Drug = a chemical substance taken into the body where it can affect chemical reactions.

Antibiotic = a product made by one microorganism to kill off another microorganism. Antibiotics work in various ways to inhibit the development of bacterial infections, without harming human cells.

Penicillin = the first antibiotic to be discovered, named after the organism Penicillium that produced it. Penicillin prevents the bacterial cell walls forming (human cells do not have cell walls, so human cells are not harmed).

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves the question: "How does penicillin work?" The answer is always: it prevents the formation of bacterial cell walls. One sentence, easy marks. Don't overcomplicate it.

!
Don't Confuse — Antibiotics vs Antiseptics vs Disinfectants

Antibiotic = taken inside the body to treat a bacterial infection (e.g. penicillin tablet).

Antiseptic = chemicals applied to the outside of the body, such as the skin, to kill microbes and prevent infection. ‘Anti’ = prevent, ‘sepsis’ = infection.

Disinfectant = chemicals that kill microbes, but more powerful and generally used on surfaces such as worksurfaces or lavatory bowls, NOT on skin.

T
Study Tip — Memory Hook

Antibiotic = bio means life = goes inside a living body. Antiseptic = prevents sepsis = stays on the outside. If you remember "bio = inside, septic = outside," you won't mix them up.

  • I can define drug, antibiotic, and antiseptic
  • I know that penicillin prevents bacterial cell wall formation
  • I can explain the difference between antibiotics, antiseptics, and disinfectants
Section 15.1 (continued) Why Antibiotics Don't Work on Viruses
This is one of the most commonly tested ideas in the whole chapter. It comes up on almost every paper.
W
Write This Down

Antibiotics only work on bacteria, NOT on viruses. This is because:

• Antibiotics target features that are specific to bacterial cells (like cell walls).

• Viruses are not cells — they have no metabolism of their own and no cell walls.

• Human cells also lack cell walls, which is why penicillin does not harm human cells.

Therefore: There is no benefit in taking antibiotics for viral diseases like influenza (flu) or the common cold.

?
Why This Matters

In real life, people often ask for antibiotics when they have a cold. Doctors refuse because colds are caused by viruses — antibiotics would do nothing. Taking them unnecessarily also increases the risk of antibiotic resistance (next section).

!
Don't Confuse

"Antibiotics kill viruses" is WRONG. If the exam asks why antibiotics don't work on viruses, don't just say "because they only kill bacteria." You need to explain why: viruses have no cell walls and no metabolism of their own, so there is nothing for the antibiotic to target.

  • I can explain why antibiotics don't work on viruses (no cell wall, no metabolism)
  • I know that taking antibiotics for a cold is pointless
Section 15.1 (continued) Production of Penicillin
Penicillin is made on an industrial scale using large fermenters. It's a secondary metabolic product, meaning it's made after the main growth phase of the fungus.
W
Write This Down — How Penicillin is Produced

Penicillin is a secondary metabolic product — it is produced when the Penicillium fungus is slowing down its growth, not during rapid growth.

Conditions needed:

• Food for energy and protein synthesis

• Oxygen for aerobic respiration

• Correct temperature (24°C) and pH (7.5)

• Sterile conditions — no other microbes to compete for nutrients or produce toxic chemicals

After collection: The batch is filtered to remove the fungus, and penicillin is extracted, crystallised, and put into capsules.

?
Why This Matters

The exam might ask why the fermenter must be kept sterile. Two reasons: (1) other microbes would compete for nutrients, slowing penicillin production, and (2) contaminant microbes could produce toxic chemicals that would make the medicine unsafe.

T
Study Tip — The Timeline

Think of it in order: fungus grows first (uses nutrients) → growth slows down → penicillin is produced → collect and filter → extract and crystallise → put into capsules. Sketch this as a simple timeline in your notes.

  • I know penicillin is a secondary metabolic product (produced after growth slows)
  • I can list the conditions needed in the fermenter
  • I can explain why sterile conditions are essential
Section 15.1 (continued) Antibiotic Resistance and MRSA
This is the "big idea" of the chapter and connects to natural selection. The exam almost always has a question about this.
W
Write This Down — How Resistance Develops

Step 1: In a normal population of bacteria, a random mutation occurs that makes one bacterium resistant to the antibiotic.

Step 2: When the antibiotic is used, it kills all the non-resistant bacteria, but the resistant one survives.

Step 3: The resistant bacterium reproduces by cell division. The whole new population is now resistant.

Step 4: The antibiotic no longer works against this strain.

!
Don't Confuse — This Is Crucial

The antibiotic does NOT create the resistance. The mutation happens randomly before the antibiotic is applied. The antibiotic acts as a selection pressure — it selects for the bacteria that already have resistance. This is natural selection / artificial selection in action.

W
Write This Down — MRSA

MRSA = Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus. Methicillin is a synthetic version of penicillin. It is a very useful antibiotic in controlling Staphylococcus and Streptococcus infections. Unfortunately, some bacteria have resistance to this antibiotic — they produce a protein which prevents the blockage of cell wall production.

MRSA is especially dangerous in hospitals because patients have weakened immune systems.

W
Write This Down — Why Resistance is Getting Worse

Resistance is more likely to develop when:

• Antibiotics are used unnecessarily (e.g. for viral infections where they do nothing)

• People do not finish their course of prescribed antibiotics — this leaves some bacteria alive, and these may be the more resistant ones that recover and multiply

?
Why This Matters

This explains why doctors tell you to finish all your antibiotics even if you feel better. If you stop early, you've killed the weakest bacteria but left the strongest alive. Those survivors reproduce, and now you have a harder-to-treat infection.

T
Study Tip — The 4-Step Chain

For exam answers on resistance, use this chain: Mutation → Variation → Selection → Reproduction. Each step is worth a mark. Practice writing it out as a 4-line answer. If you can nail this chain, you can answer any resistance question.

  • I can explain the 4 steps of how antibiotic resistance develops
  • I know the antibiotic SELECTS for resistance, it doesn't CREATE it
  • I know what MRSA stands for and why it is dangerous
  • I can give two reasons why resistance is increasing
