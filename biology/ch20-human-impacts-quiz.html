Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1–2 marks each on the exam.
1
What is monoculture? State one advantage and one disadvantage.
3 marks
Answer
Monoculture is growing a single crop over a large area [1].
Advantage: e.g. specialised machinery can be used / highly selected strains matched to conditions [1].
Disadvantage: e.g. disease spreads easily between identical plants / loss of genetic variety / soil minerals depleted [1].
One mark per part. Any valid advantage or disadvantage accepted.

Name two greenhouse gases.

Answer

These are the two main ones. Water vapour is also a greenhouse gas but CO₂ and methane are the safest answers for IGCSE.

3
Define sustainable development.
2 marks
Answer
Sustainable development means using resources at a rate that allows them to be replaced/renewed [1], so that the environment is not damaged for future generations [1].
Two key ideas: (1) resources are replenished at the same rate they're used, (2) future generations can still benefit.

State two ways in which overfishing can be reduced.
2 marks
Answer
Any two from: use larger net mesh sizes (to let young fish escape and reproduce) [1], set quotas on the size of catches [1], fish farming / restocking to replenish wild populations, closed seasons to allow breeding.
Mesh sizes and quotas are the two most commonly expected answers.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3–4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain how acid rain is formed and state two effects it has on the environment.
4 marks
Answer
Burning fossil fuels releases sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides into the atmosphere [1]. These gases dissolve in rainwater to form sulfuric acid and nitric acid, which falls as acid rain [1].
Effects: Soils become acidic, causing leaching of minerals and inhibiting decomposition [1]. Lakes become acidic, causing death of fish and invertebrates [1].
Formation = 2 marks (name the gases + they dissolve in rain). Effects = 1 mark each. Could also mention damage to forest trees or buildings.

6
State three consequences of large-scale deforestation.
3 marks
Answer
Any three from:
• Soil erosion — tree roots no longer stabilise the soil [1].
• Flooding — without trees to absorb rainfall, water runs off rapidly [1].
• Increased atmospheric CO₂ — fewer trees to absorb CO₂ by photosynthesis [1].
• Species extinction — loss of habitat for forest-dwelling organisms [1].
• Reduced soil fertility — nutrients removed with the trees [1].
• Climatic changes (drier atmosphere, less rainfall) [1].
Three required, but know more so you can choose the three you're most confident about.

7
A pesticide called DDT was used to kill insects on crops. Scientists found very high concentrations of DDT in the tissues of birds of prey. Explain how this happened.
3 marks
Answer
DDT is not broken down (non-biodegradable) and accumulates in the tissues/fat of organisms [1]. At each trophic level in the food chain, the DDT becomes more concentrated because each predator eats many prey organisms [1]. Birds of prey are top carnivores, so they accumulate the highest concentration of DDT [1].
Key term: bioaccumulation (or biomagnification). Three marks: DDT is stored/not broken down, concentration increases at each level, top predators get the highest dose.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5–6 marks each. Full sentences, multiple points, clear reasoning.
8
A farmer applies large quantities of nitrate fertiliser to his fields. A river runs alongside the fields. Describe the sequence of events that could lead to the death of fish in the river.
6 marks
Answer
Rain washes excess nitrate fertiliser from the soil into the river (leaching) [1]. The extra nutrients cause algae to grow rapidly on the surface (algal bloom) [1]. The algae block light from reaching plants below the surface, and these plants die [1]. Decomposing bacteria feed on the dead plant material and multiply rapidly [1]. The bacteria use up large amounts of oxygen for aerobic respiration (high biological oxygen demand) [1]. Fish and other aquatic organisms cannot obtain enough oxygen and die [1].
This is the eutrophication sequence. Six marks, six steps. Practise writing this chain from memory — it comes up almost every year. The process is called eutrophication.

9
The greenhouse effect is increasing. Explain the causes and describe three possible effects of global warming.
5 marks
Answer
Causes: Increased combustion of fossil fuels releases more CO₂ [1]. Methane is released from cattle farming, rice fields, and landfill sites [1]. These greenhouse gases trap infrared radiation (heat) near the Earth's surface.

Effects (any three):
• Melting of polar ice and rising sea levels, flooding coastal areas [1].
• More extreme weather events — stronger winds, heavier rainfall [1].
• Desertification — evaporation of water from fertile areas [1].
Also accepted: pests spreading to new areas, changes to crop-growing seasons.
Causes = 2 marks (CO₂ from fossil fuels + methane from farming). Effects = 1 mark each, need three.

10
The tiger is an endangered species. Describe why the tiger has become endangered and outline a conservation strategy that could help protect it.
5 marks
Answer
Why endangered:
• Hunting for fur, bones, and teeth used in traditional medicines [1].
• Habitat loss — forests cleared for agriculture; competition with villagers for land [1].

Conservation strategy:
• Set up protected areas such as national parks or fenced sanctuaries [1].
• Captive breeding programmes in zoos to maintain the population and genetic variation [1].
• Legal protection — ban trade in tiger products through CITES [1].
Also accepted: education programmes for local communities, financial compensation for villagers who lose livestock.
Template: (1) why endangered (hunting + habitat loss), (2) what is being done (protected areas, captive breeding, legal ban on trade). Apply this template to any endangered species question.
