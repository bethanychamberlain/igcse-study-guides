Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Name the four components of blood and state one function of each.
4 marks
Answer
Red blood cells — transport oxygen (using haemoglobin) [1].
White blood cells — defend against pathogens / produce antibodies [1].
Platelets — involved in blood clotting [1].
Plasma — transports dissolved substances such as glucose, urea, CO₂, and hormones [1].
Don't say "red blood cells carry haemoglobin" — they CONTAIN it. Say they "transport oxygen." For plasma, name a specific substance.

2
State two differences between arteries and veins.
2 marks
Answer
Any two of:
Arteries have thick muscular walls; veins have thin walls [1].
Arteries carry blood at high pressure; veins carry blood at low pressure [1].
Arteries carry blood away from the heart; veins carry blood towards the heart [1].
Veins have valves; arteries do not (except semilunar) [1].
Arteries have a narrow lumen; veins have a wide lumen [1].
Always give BOTH sides of the comparison. Don't just say "arteries have thick walls" — say "arteries have thick walls WHILE veins have thin walls."

3
Name the chamber of the heart that receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.
1 mark
Answer
Left atrium [1].
Blood returns from the lungs via the pulmonary vein and enters the left atrium. Don't say "left ventricle" — that's the pumping chamber.

4
What is the name of the blood vessel that supplies the heart muscle with oxygenated blood?
1 mark
Answer
Coronary artery [1].
Not the aorta — the coronary arteries branch OFF the aorta and run across the surface of the heart. They supply the heart's own muscle cells.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain why the wall of the left ventricle is thicker than the wall of the right ventricle.
3 marks
Answer
The left ventricle pumps blood to the whole body (via the aorta) [1], which is a much greater distance than the right ventricle, which only pumps blood to the lungs [1]. The left ventricle therefore needs to generate higher pressure, which requires a thicker, more muscular wall [1].
Three-part chain: left ventricle pumps to body (further) → needs more pressure → needs more muscle. Each step is a mark.

6
Explain how capillaries are adapted for the exchange of materials between blood and tissues. Give three adaptations.
3 marks
Answer
Walls are only one cell thick, giving a short diffusion distance [1].
They are highly branched (forming networks/beds), giving a large surface area for exchange [1].
They are constantly supplied with fresh blood, maintaining concentration gradients for diffusion [1].
For each adaptation, state the feature AND then explain HOW it helps exchange. "One cell thick" alone is only half the mark — you must add "short diffusion distance."

7
Explain what happens during a heart attack, starting from the coronary artery becoming blocked.
4 marks
Answer
The coronary artery becomes blocked (e.g. by fatty deposits / atheroma) [1].
Blood cannot reach the heart muscle cells, so they are deprived of oxygen and glucose [1].
The heart muscle cells cannot carry out aerobic respiration, and poisonous wastes (e.g. lactic acid) build up [1].
Part of the heart muscle stops contracting, which can be fatal because other organs in the body lose their blood supply [1].
Follow the chain: blocked artery → no oxygen/glucose to heart muscle → no respiration → muscle stops contracting. Each link in the chain is a mark.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning. This is where exam grades are won or lost.
8
Describe the pathway of a red blood cell from the vena cava, through the heart and lungs, back through the heart and out to the body. Name every chamber, valve type, and blood vessel it passes through.
5 marks
Answer
Vena cava → right atrium → (through atrioventricular valve) → right ventricle [1] → (through semilunar valve) → pulmonary artery → lungs (picks up oxygen) [1] → pulmonary vein → left atrium [1] → (through atrioventricular valve) → left ventricle [1] → (through semilunar valve) → aorta → body [1].
This is a classic 5-mark question. Practise writing it out from memory. The key pitfall: make sure you go through the lungs BETWEEN the two sides of the heart. Students often skip straight from right ventricle to left atrium.

9
Explain how tissue fluid is formed and how it is returned to the blood. In your answer, explain why tissue fluid does not contain red blood cells.
5 marks
Answer
Blood arrives at the capillary bed at high pressure from the arteries [1]. The pressure forces plasma out through the thin capillary walls, forming tissue fluid that bathes the surrounding cells [1]. Red blood cells and large plasma proteins are too large to pass through the capillary walls, so tissue fluid does not contain them [1]. Most tissue fluid returns to the capillaries by osmosis (due to the water potential gradient created by plasma proteins) [1]. Some excess fluid drains into lymph vessels and is eventually returned to the blood near the heart [1].
Five ideas: (1) high pressure forces plasma out, (2) this fluid bathes cells, (3) RBCs too large to pass, (4) most returns by osmosis, (5) excess drains into lymph. Each is a mark.

10
Explain why humans have a double circulatory system rather than a single one. Discuss the advantages, and explain why a fish can survive with a single circulation but a human cannot.
6 marks
Answer
In a double circulation, blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit [1]. After picking up oxygen in the lungs, blood returns to the heart and receives a second "boost" of pressure [1]. This means blood reaches the body tissues at high pressure, so a large volume of blood flows quickly past the cells, delivering oxygen and glucose efficiently [1].

In a single circulation (as in fish), blood only passes through the heart once. After passing through the gills, blood pressure drops significantly [1], so blood flows slowly to the rest of the body. This is sufficient for fish because they have lower metabolic rates and are supported by water, so their oxygen demands are lower [1].

Humans are active, warm-blooded animals with high metabolic rates. They need a rapid, high-pressure blood supply to deliver enough oxygen and glucose to their tissues [1]. A single circulation would not maintain sufficient pressure.
Key points: double = blood gets two boosts of pressure; single = pressure drops after gills; humans need high pressure because of high metabolic rate; fish can cope because of lower demands.
