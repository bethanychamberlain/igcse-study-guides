Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1–2 marks each on the exam.
1
State two reasons why bacteria are particularly useful in biotechnology.
2 marks
Answer
Any two from: they reproduce very rapidly (by binary fission) [1]; they can produce complex molecules such as enzymes and hormones [1]; they have plasmids that can carry genes from one cell to another; their genetic code is the same as in other organisms.
The two most commonly expected: rapid reproduction + ability to make useful products.

Write the word equation for anaerobic respiration in yeast.
2 marks
Answer
glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide (+ energy) [2]
Both products needed for full marks. Writing "alcohol" instead of "ethanol" is usually accepted but "ethanol" is the more precise term.

3
Name the enzyme used to cut a gene out of DNA and the enzyme used to join the gene into a plasmid.
2 marks
Answer
Cut: restriction enzyme [1]. Join: ligase [1].
These two enzyme names are essential. "Restriction enzyme" cuts (leaving sticky ends). "Ligase" joins/splices. Learn them as a pair.

4
State two reasons why immobilised enzymes are used in industrial processes.
2 marks
Answer
Any two from: the enzyme can be reused many times (reducing costs) [1]; the product does not contain enzyme, making purification easier [1]; the enzyme is more stable and less likely to break down; allows continuous production.
"Reused" and "easier purification" are the two safest answers.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3–4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Explain why a constant temperature must be maintained in a fermenter during the production of an antibiotic.
3 marks
Answer
The microorganisms use enzymes to carry out metabolic reactions that produce the antibiotic [1]. If the temperature is too low, enzyme activity is too slow and production is inefficient [1]. If the temperature is too high, the enzymes (and other proteins) are denatured and stop working, killing the microorganisms [1].
The answer always links back to enzymes: too cold = slow, too hot = denatured. The fermenter has a water jacket to maintain the optimum temperature.

6
In bread-making, explain the role of yeast. Why is the dough kept warm, and what happens when the bread is baked at 180°C?
4 marks
Answer
Yeast carries out anaerobic respiration (fermentation), converting sugar into ethanol and carbon dioxide [1]. The CO₂ bubbles are trapped in the sticky dough, causing it to rise (expand) [1]. The dough is kept warm (~28°C) because this is the optimum temperature for the yeast's enzymes [1]. Baking at 180°C kills the yeast (stops fermentation), evaporates the alcohol, and hardens the outer surface into a crust [1].
Four marks: what yeast does (fermentation), how CO₂ makes bread rise, why warm (enzymes), what baking does (kills yeast, forms crust).

7
Explain why the chamber of a fermenter must be sterilised before use.
2 marks
Answer
To kill any unwanted microorganisms [1] that might compete with the desired microorganism for nutrients, or contaminate the product (making it impure or dangerous) [1].
Two ideas: remove competing/contaminating organisms + prevent contamination of the product.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5–6 marks each. Full sentences, multiple points, clear reasoning.
8
Describe how genetic engineering is used to produce human insulin in bacteria. Include the names of the enzymes involved.
6 marks
Answer
The insulin gene is identified in a human cell (e.g. pancreas cell) [1]. The gene is cut out of the chromosome using a restriction enzyme, leaving sticky ends [1]. A plasmid from a bacterium is cut open using the same restriction enzyme [1]. The human gene is inserted into the plasmid and joined using the enzyme ligase, creating recombinant DNA [1]. The recombinant plasmid is placed back into a host bacterium [1]. The bacteria are cultured in a fermenter under optimum conditions; as they divide, they produce human insulin which is then extracted and purified [1].
Six steps, six marks. This is the single most important sequence in this chapter. Name both enzymes (restriction enzyme and ligase), mention the plasmid, and end with the fermenter. Practise until you can write it from memory.

9
Suggest why insulin produced by genetic engineering is preferable to insulin extracted from the bodies of other animals (e.g. pigs).
4 marks
Answer
The product is the human version of insulin, so it works more efficiently and is less likely to be rejected by the body's immune system [1]. It can be produced in large quantities, making it more readily available [1]. It is less expensive — GE insulin costs about 1% as much as animal-derived insulin [1]. It can be produced on demand, rather than only when animals are slaughtered [1].
Four marks: human version (less rejection), large quantities, cheaper, available on demand. Also acceptable: avoids ethical concerns about using animals, purer product.

10
Discuss the benefits and potential problems of using genetically modified (GM) crops. Give at least two of each.
5 marks
Answer
Benefits:
• Crops can be engineered for pest resistance (e.g. insecticidal crystal protein), reducing the need for pesticides [1].
• Crops can grow in extreme environments (drought resistance), opening new areas for cultivation and reducing risk of famine [1].
• Crops can be made more nutritious (e.g. Golden Rice produces vitamin A) [1].

Problems:
• Genes for pest or herbicide resistance could spread to wild relatives (cross-pollination), creating "superweeds" that are difficult to control [1].
• Engineered organisms could escape from laboratories or farms with unpredictable consequences for ecosystems [1].
Also accepted: ethical concerns, patent issues, loss of traditional crop varieties, unknown long-term effects.
"Discuss" means both sides. Give at least two benefits and two problems. A brief concluding sentence like "GM technology has great potential but needs careful regulation" can earn a quality-of-written-communication mark.
