Section 12.1 Gas Exchange Supplies Oxygen for Respiration
Cells need oxygen for aerobic respiration and must get rid of CO₂ waste. Gas exchange is the process that makes this happen.
W
Write This Down — Gas Exchange Definition

Gas exchange is the swapping of oxygen and carbon dioxide between an organism and its environment. It takes place through a gas exchange surface (also called a respiratory surface).

!
Don't Confuse — Gas Exchange vs Respiration vs Breathing

Respiration = chemical reactions inside cells that release energy from glucose.
Gas exchange = O₂ and CO₂ crossing a surface (e.g. alveoli walls).
Breathing = the physical movement of air in and out of the lungs (ventilation).
These are three different things. The exam loves to test whether you mix them up.

W
Write This Down — Properties of a Good Gas Exchange Surface
Property Reason
Thin (one cell thick) Short diffusion distance for gases
Large surface area Many gas molecules can diffuse at the same time
Moist Gases dissolve in moisture before diffusing; cells die if dry
Good blood supply Carries gases to/from respiring cells, maintains concentration gradient
Well ventilated Fresh air maintains concentration gradients for O₂ and CO₂
?
Why This Matters

This table is exam gold. A classic 4-mark question: “Describe the features of alveoli that make them efficient for gas exchange.” Just list the properties and link each one to its reason.

  • I can define gas exchange
  • I know the 5 properties of a good gas exchange surface
  • I can tell the difference between respiration, gas exchange, and breathing
Section 12.1 continued The Human Gas Exchange System
Air travels through a series of tubes before reaching the alveoli, where gas exchange actually happens.
W
Write This Down — The Pathway of Air

Nose/mouth → Larynx (voice box) → Trachea (windpipe, kept open by C-shaped cartilage rings) → Bronchi (one bronchus to each lung) → Bronchioles (fine branches) → Alveoli (air sacs where gas exchange occurs)

W
Write This Down — Alveoli

Alveoli (singular: alveolus) are tiny air sacs at the ends of bronchioles. They are where gas exchange takes place.

Why alveoli are so efficient:
• Thin walls — only one cell thick, so gases diffuse across a very short distance
• Huge surface area — millions of alveoli give a total area the size of a tennis court
• Moist lining — gases dissolve before diffusing
• Rich blood supply — dense network of capillaries maintains a steep concentration gradient
• Well ventilated — breathing keeps fresh air flowing in

W
Write This Down — How Gas Exchange Works at the Alveolus

• Oxygen diffuses from the air in the alveolus (high concentration) into the blood in the capillary (low concentration)
• Oxygen is picked up by haemoglobin in red blood cells, forming oxyhaemoglobin
• Carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood (high concentration) into the air in the alveolus (low concentration)
• Both gases move by diffusion — down their concentration gradients

W
Write This Down — Protecting the Airways

Goblet cells produce sticky mucus that traps dust, microbes, and particles.
Ciliated cells have tiny hair-like cilia that beat in a coordinated wave, sweeping the mucus (and trapped particles) up and away from the lungs towards the throat.

T

Draw a simple diagram of one alveolus surrounded by a capillary. Label: alveolar wall, capillary wall, red blood cells, oxygen arrows (into blood), CO₂ arrows (into alveolus). Drawing this forces you to think about the direction of gas movement.

  • I can explain why alveoli are efficient for gas exchange (at least 4 reasons)

Section 12.2 Breathing Ventilates the Lungs
Breathing moves air in and out of the lungs. It’s a mechanical process involving muscles and pressure changes — nothing to do with the chemical reactions of respiration.
W
Write This Down — Breathing In (Inhalation)

1. External intercostal muscles contract → pull rib cage up and out
2. Diaphragm muscles contract → diaphragm moves down (flattens)
3. Chest cavity volume increases
4. Pressure inside the lungs decreases (below atmospheric pressure)
5. Air rushes in to equalise the pressure

W
Write This Down — Breathing Out (Exhalation)

1. Internal intercostal muscles contract (during forced breathing) → rib cage moves down and in
2. Diaphragm muscles relax → diaphragm moves up (returns to dome shape)
3. Chest cavity volume decreases
4. Pressure inside the lungs increases (above atmospheric pressure)
5. Air is pushed out

!
Don't Confuse — Which Muscles Do What

External intercostals contract during breathing in.
Internal intercostals contract during forced breathing out (e.g. coughing, sneezing).
Normal breathing out is mostly passive — the muscles just relax and the lungs spring back.

Gas Inhaled air Exhaled air Why
Oxygen 21% ~18% O₂ diffuses from alveoli into blood
Carbon dioxide 0.04% ~3-4% CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli
Nitrogen 78% 78% Not used by the body
Water vapour Variable Saturated Water evaporates from moist alveolar surfaces
W
Write This Down — Exercise and Breathing

During exercise, muscles respire faster → more O₂ needed, more CO₂ produced. The body responds by:
• Increasing breathing rate (more breaths per minute)
• Increasing tidal volume (deeper breaths = more air per breath)
• Increasing heart rate (pumps blood faster to carry O₂ to muscles and CO₂ away)

T
Study Tip — The Pressure Story

Think of it as a syringe. Pull the plunger out = volume increases = pressure drops = air rushes in. Push the plunger in = volume decreases = pressure increases = air pushed out. That’s exactly what the rib cage and diaphragm do.

  • I can describe what happens during breathing in (5 steps)
  • I can describe what happens during breathing out (5 steps)
  • I know the differences between inhaled and exhaled air
  • I can explain how exercise affects breathing rate and tidal volume
Section 12.3 Smoking and Disease
Smoking damages almost every part of the gas exchange system. Know the chemicals and the diseases they cause.
W
Write This Down — Harmful Chemicals in Tobacco Smoke
Chemical What it does
Tar A sticky substance that coats the lungs. Contains carcinogens (cancer-causing chemicals). Damages cilia and builds up in alveoli.
Nicotine The addictive substance. Increases heart rate and blood pressure. Narrows blood vessels.
Carbon monoxide (CO) Binds to haemoglobin more strongly than oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin. Reduces the blood’s ability to carry oxygen. Heart must work harder.
Smoke particles Irritate the airways, causing inflammation and extra mucus production.
W
Write This Down — Diseases Caused by Smoking

Lung cancer: Tar contains carcinogens that cause uncontrolled cell division in lung tissue.
Chronic bronchitis: Smoke irritates the bronchi → inflammation → excess mucus → persistent cough. Cilia are damaged so mucus is not cleared → infections are more likely.
Emphysema: Alveoli walls are destroyed → surface area for gas exchange is greatly reduced → person becomes breathless because not enough O₂ can enter the blood.

W
Write This Down — Addiction

Physical addiction: the body cannot function properly without nicotine because it has replaced a natural body chemical.
Psychological addiction: smoking becomes linked with comfort or stress relief — the person reaches for a cigarette automatically.

?
Why This Matters

Carbon monoxide is the sneaky one. It doesn’t damage the lungs directly — it reduces the blood’s oxygen-carrying capacity. This is why smokers get breathless more easily and why their hearts have to beat faster to deliver enough oxygen.

  • I can name 4 harmful chemicals in tobacco smoke and what each does
  • I can describe how smoking causes lung cancer, bronchitis, and emphysema
  • I know the difference between physical and psychological addiction
Section 12.4 How Do We Know Smoking Causes Disease?
We can’t do experiments on humans, so scientists use epidemiological studies — large-scale surveys that look for patterns between smoking and disease.
W
Write This Down — Epidemiology

Epidemiology is the study of patterns in the distribution of diseases. It looks at who gets ill, where, and why — to find links between lifestyle factors and disease.

Sir Richard Doll’s study:
• Studied doctors (to rule out occupation as a factor)
• Separated data for city vs countryside (to rule out pollution)
• Found a clear correlation: the more cigarettes smoked per day, the higher the risk of lung cancer

!
Don't Confuse — Correlation vs Causation

A correlation between two variables does not prove that one causes the other. It might be that heavy smokers share another risk factor (e.g. diet, genetics). However, the evidence for smoking is so strong — because so many other variables have been eliminated — that scientists accept smoking as a major cause of lung disease.

?
Why This Matters

You cannot deliberately give humans cancer for an experiment — that would be unethical. So epidemiological studies (observing large populations over time) are the best evidence we have. The exam may ask: “Why can’t we use a typical laboratory experiment to prove smoking causes lung cancer?” Answer: it would be unethical to deliberately expose humans to a known harmful substance.

T
Study Tip — Graph Interpretation

If the exam shows a graph of cigarettes smoked vs lung cancer deaths, describe the trend (as cigarettes increase, deaths increase), state it shows a positive correlation, and then add the caution: “correlation does not prove causation, but the link is strongly supported by multiple studies.”

  • I can define epidemiology
  • I know what Richard Doll's study showed and how he controlled variables
  • I understand the difference between correlation and causation
