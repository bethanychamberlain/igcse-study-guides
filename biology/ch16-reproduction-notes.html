Sections 16.1 – 16.2 Asexual vs Sexual Reproduction
All living organisms reproduce. There are two fundamentally different methods, and the exam will ask you to compare them.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Reproduction = the generation of new individuals of the same species.

Sexual reproduction = involves the fusion of gametes (sex cells) from two parents. Produces offspring that are genetically different from each other and from the parents.

Asexual reproduction = involves only one parent, no gametes, no fertilisation. All offspring are genetically identical to the parent (clones).

Gametes = sex cells. They are haploid (have half the normal number of chromosomes).

Fertilisation = the fusion (joining together) of a male gamete and a female gamete to produce a zygote (which is diploid).

W
Write This Down — Copy This Table
Feature Sexual Asexual
Parents Two (male + female) One
Gametes? Yes No
Fertilisation? Yes No
Offspring Genetically different (variation) Genetically identical (clones)
Cell division Meiosis (to make gametes) Mitosis
Advantage Variation → adaptation Fast, only one parent needed
Disadvantage Slower, needs two parents No variation → vulnerable to disease
Example Humans, flowering plants Strawberry runners, bacteria
?
Why This Matters

The Irish Potato Famine (1845) is the textbook's example of why asexual reproduction can be dangerous: all the potatoes were clones, so when a disease hit, it killed all of them. This is a good example to use in an exam answer about disadvantages of asexual reproduction.

!
Don't Confuse — Clone Definition

A clone is a group of cells (or organisms) that are genetically identical. It is NOT just "a cell produced by asexual reproduction" — that's incomplete. The key word is genetically identical.

  • I can define sexual and asexual reproduction
  • I can define gamete, fertilisation, and zygote
  • I can list advantages and disadvantages of each type
  • I can explain why clones are vulnerable to disease (no variation)
Sections 16.2 – 16.5 Plant Reproduction: Flowers, Pollination, Fertilisation, and Germination
Flowering plants reproduce sexually using highly specialised structures called flowers. You need to know the parts, the process, and the differences between insect- and wind-pollinated flowers.
W
Write This Down — Parts of a Flower

Male parts (stamen):

• Anther — produces pollen grains (male gametes)

• Filament — stalk that holds up the anther

Female parts (carpel):

• Stigma — sticky platform where pollen lands

• Style — stalk connecting stigma to ovary

• Ovary — contains ovules (which contain the female gametes)

Other parts:

• Petals — attract insects (coloured, scented, with nectaries)

• Sepals — protect the flower bud

• Nectary — produces nectar (sugary solution) to attract insects

W
Write This Down — Pollination

Pollination = the transfer of pollen from an anther to a stigma.

Self-pollination = pollen transferred within the same flower (efficient but less variation).

Cross-pollination = pollen transferred to a different plant of the same species (risky but more genetic variation).

W
Write This Down — Insect vs Wind Pollination
Feature Insect-Pollinated Wind-Pollinated
Petals Large, bright, scented Small, dull, no scent
Pollen Small amounts, sticky, large grains Huge amounts, light, smooth grains
Anthers Stiff, inside flower Dangling outside flower
Stigma Flat, inside flower Long, feathery, hanging outside
Nectar? Yes No
!
Don't Confuse — Pollination vs Fertilisation

Pollination = pollen lands on the stigma. Fertilisation = the male and female nuclei actually fuse (join). Pollination comes FIRST. They are NOT the same thing. This is a very common exam mistake.

W
Write This Down — Fertilisation in Plants

After pollination, a pollen tube grows down through the style to reach the ovule. The male gamete travels down the pollen tube and fuses with the female gamete in the ovule. This is fertilisation.

After fertilisation:

• The ovule becomes a seed (contains the embryo + food store + seed coat/testa)

• The ovary becomes a fruit (helps disperse the seed)

• The petals, sepals, stamens, stigma, and style wither away

W
Write This Down — Germination

Germination = the development of a seed into a new young plant.

Conditions needed: water, oxygen (for aerobic respiration), and a suitable temperature (for enzymes).

What happens: Water enters through the micropyle → activates enzymes → enzymes convert insoluble food stores to soluble ones → seed swells and testa splits → radicle (root) grows down, plumule (shoot) grows up.

T
Study Tip — Memory Hooks

"Ovule → Seed, Ovary → Fruit" — memorise this pair. The exam will definitely test it.

Germination needs WOT: Water, Oxygen, Temperature. Light is NOT needed (that's a common wrong answer).

  • I can label the parts of a flower and state their functions
  • I can define pollination and distinguish it from fertilisation
  • I can compare insect-pollinated and wind-pollinated flowers
  • I know what happens after fertilisation (ovule → seed, ovary → fruit)
  • I can state the conditions needed for germination (water, oxygen, temperature)
Section 16.6 Human Reproductive Systems
You need to know the structures and functions of both the male and female reproductive systems. The exam will ask you to label diagrams and compare gametes.
W
Write This Down — Male Reproductive System

• Testes — produce sperm and testosterone. Held outside the body in the scrotum (2–3°C cooler than body = ideal for sperm production).

• Sperm duct (vas deferens) — carries sperm from testis to urethra.

• Prostate gland — produces seminal fluid (99.5% of semen; sperm = 0.5%).

• Urethra — carries semen (or urine) to the tip of the penis. A valve prevents both at the same time.

• Penis — delivers sperm to the female reproductive system.

W
Write This Down — Female Reproductive System

• Ovaries — produce ova (eggs) and the hormones oestrogen and progesterone.

• Oviduct (Fallopian tube) — carries the ovum from the ovary towards the uterus. Fertilisation usually occurs here (in the first third).

• Uterus (womb) — where the embryo/fetus develops during pregnancy.

• Cervix — ring of muscle at the neck of the uterus; keeps fetus inside; dilates during birth.

• Vagina (birth canal) — receives the penis during intercourse; the baby passes through during birth.

W
Write This Down — Comparing Gametes
Feature Sperm Ovum (egg)
Size Very small Much larger
Numbers Millions (300 million per ejaculation) One per month
Mobility Motile (has a flagellum/tail) Non-motile (does not move on its own)
Food store Very little Large cytoplasm with food stores
Special features Acrosome (enzymes), many mitochondria Jelly coat, haploid nucleus
?
Why This Matters

The exam loves to ask: "Why do sperm have many mitochondria?" Answer: to release energy (from respiration) for swimming/movement of the flagellum. Another favourite: "Why are millions of sperm produced but only one ovum per month?" Answer: most sperm die on the journey; producing millions ensures at least one reaches the ovum.

  • I can label the male reproductive system and state each part's function
  • I can label the female reproductive system and state each part's function
  • I can compare sperm and ova (size, number, mobility, features)
  • I know where fertilisation occurs (oviduct)
Sections 16.7 – 16.8 The Menstrual Cycle and Conception
The menstrual cycle is a 28-day hormone-controlled process. This is one of the hardest topics in biology because there are four hormones to keep track of. Break it into phases.
W
Write This Down — The Four Phases

1. Menstruation (Days 1–5): The uterus lining breaks down and is shed, along with blood. Triggered by a decrease in progesterone.

2. Repair phase (Days 5–14): The uterus lining thickens and develops more blood vessels. Triggered by an increase in oestrogen.

3. Ovulation (Day 14): A mature ovum is released from the ovary into the oviduct. Triggered by a surge of LH (luteinising hormone) from the pituitary gland.

4. Receptive/Premenstrual phase (Days 14–28): The uterus lining is maintained, ready for implantation. Maintained by progesterone (from the corpus luteum). If no fertilisation occurs, progesterone drops and the cycle starts again.

W
Write This Down — The Four Hormones

FSH (follicle stimulating hormone) — from the pituitary gland → stimulates a follicle to develop in the ovary.

Oestrogen — from the ovary → repairs and thickens the uterus lining; triggers LH release.

LH (luteinising hormone) — from the pituitary gland → triggers ovulation and development of the corpus luteum.

Progesterone — from the corpus luteum (in the ovary) → maintains the uterus lining for implantation. Also inhibits FSH (feedback inhibition — prevents another egg developing).

!
Don't Confuse — Oestrogen vs Progesterone

Oestrogen = builds up the lining (repair phase). Progesterone = maintains the lining (after ovulation). When progesterone drops, the lining breaks down = menstruation. They are NOT interchangeable.

W
Write This Down — Conception

Copulation (sexual intercourse) delivers sperm to the female reproductive system. The ovum travels along the oviduct, moved by peristalsis (muscle contractions) and cilia (tiny hair-like structures). Fertilisation usually occurs in the first third of the oviduct.

The fertilised ovum (zygote) divides repeatedly as it travels towards the uterus. After about 6 days, it becomes a ball of cells (embryo) and embeds in the uterus lining. This embedding is called implantation. Conception = the moment implantation is complete.

T
Study Tip — Timeline Sketch

Draw a timeline from Day 1 to Day 28. Mark: menstruation (Days 1–5), ovulation (Day 14), and the lining thickness. Write each hormone's name where it peaks. This visual is worth drawing before every practice exam — it takes 30 seconds and prevents mistakes.

  • I can name the four phases of the menstrual cycle
  • I can state the role of each hormone (FSH, LH, oestrogen, progesterone)
  • I know that ovulation occurs on Day 14
  • I can explain the difference between fertilisation and conception
  • I know where fertilisation occurs (oviduct) and what implantation is
Sections 16.9 – 16.11 Pregnancy, Placenta, and Birth
Once the embryo implants, it develops for about 9 months (the gestation period). The placenta is the key organ that keeps the fetus alive.
W
Write This Down — The Placenta

The placenta is a disc-shaped organ that forms from the embryo and the uterus lining. It is connected to the fetus by the umbilical cord.

Functions of the placenta:

• Exchange of materials: oxygen and nutrients (glucose, amino acids) pass from mother → fetus; carbon dioxide and urea pass from fetus → mother.

• Barrier: maternal and fetal blood do NOT mix. They are separated by a thin membrane. This protects the fetus from the mother's blood pressure and immune system.

• Produces hormones (oestrogen and progesterone) to maintain pregnancy.

• Antibodies cross the placenta late in pregnancy, giving the baby some immunity.

!
Don't Confuse — Blood Mixing

The mother's blood and the fetus's blood NEVER mix. Materials cross the placenta by diffusion across the membrane. If a question asks "Do the bloods mix?", the answer is always no.

W
Write This Down — Protecting the Fetus

• Amniotic sac — membrane surrounding the fetus.

• Amniotic fluid — protects against mechanical shock, drying out, and temperature changes.

Harmful substances that CAN cross the placenta:

• Nicotine (from smoking) — reduces oxygen transport, causing low birth weight.

• Alcohol — can damage the developing nervous system.

• Rubella virus (German measles) — damages brain and nervous system.

W
Write This Down — Birth (Labour)

Labour has three stages, controlled by hormones:

1. Uterus muscles begin contractions, stimulated by oxytocin (from the pituitary gland). Progesterone levels fall (it was preventing contractions). The amniotic sac breaks ("waters breaking") and the cervix dilates.

2. Stronger contractions push the baby through the cervix and vagina (birth canal), usually head first.

3. The placenta detaches from the uterus wall and is delivered as the afterbirth. The umbilical cord is clamped and cut.

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions often ask about harmful substances crossing the placenta. A common question: "Explain why a pregnant woman should not smoke." Answer: nicotine crosses the placenta → reduces oxygen carried by the blood → less oxygen for the fetus → lower birth weight.

T
Study Tip

Draw a simple diagram of the placenta with arrows showing what crosses in each direction. Mother → fetus: oxygen, glucose, amino acids, antibodies. Fetus → mother: CO₂, urea. Label the villi, umbilical cord, and note "bloods do not mix."

  • I can state the four functions of the placenta
  • I know that maternal and fetal blood do NOT mix
  • I can name three harmful substances that cross the placenta
  • I can describe the three stages of labour
  • I know that oxytocin stimulates contractions and progesterone falls
Section 16.12 Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)
STIs are diseases spread by body fluids through sexual contact. You need to know examples, symptoms, and prevention methods.
W
Write This Down — Two Key STIs
Feature Gonorrhoea HIV / AIDS
Caused by Bacterium Virus (HIV)
Symptoms Pain when urinating, discharge Flu-like illness; immune system fails over time
Transmission Unprotected sex Unprotected sex, blood contact, mother to child, shared needles
Treatment Antibiotics (but resistant strains developing) No cure; antiviral drugs slow progression
W
Write This Down — Prevention

• Use a condom (barrier method) during sexual intercourse

• Know the sexual history of partners

• Have a medical check if symptoms appear

• Community-level: education programmes, testing, vaccine development

!
Don't Confuse

HIV is the virus. AIDS is the condition that develops later when the immune system fails. They are not the same thing. HIV+ does not mean the person has AIDS yet.

Also: gonorrhoea is bacterial (treatable with antibiotics). HIV is viral (no cure, antibiotics won't work).

  • I can name one bacterial STI and one viral STI
  • I know the difference between HIV and AIDS
  • I can explain why gonorrhoea can be treated with antibiotics but HIV cannot
  • I can state how STIs are prevented
