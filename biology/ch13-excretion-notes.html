Section 13.1 Excretion: Removal of Waste Products of Metabolism
Living cells produce waste products that can be toxic. The body has to get rid of them or they'll cause damage.
W
Write This Down — Definition

Excretion is the removal of toxic materials, the waste products of metabolism, and substances in excess of requirements from the body.

!
Don't Confuse — Excretion vs Egestion

Excretion is getting rid of waste products made by the body's cells (metabolic waste). Egestion is getting rid of undigested food (faeces). Faeces were never inside cells — they just passed through the gut. The exam will try to trick you on this.

W
Write This Down — Three Main Waste Products

1. Carbon dioxide — produced during respiration. Dissolves in plasma and tissue fluid to form a weak acid (carbonic acid, H₂CO₃), which can denature enzymes and other proteins at high concentrations. Removed by the lungs.

2. Urea — produced in the liver during deamination of excess amino acids. Can denature enzymes. Removed by the kidneys in urine.

3. Salts — which can be in excess in the diet, can have an effect on the water potential of the blood. Removed by the kidneys.

?
Why This Matters

"Name two waste products of metabolism and state how each is removed" is a classic 4-mark question. Learn the three waste products, where each is made, and which organ removes it. That's easy marks.

  • I can define excretion without looking
  • I know the difference between excretion and egestion
  • I can name three waste products, their sources, and how they are removed
Section 13.1 (continued) The Role of the Kidney
The kidneys are the main excretory organs. They do two jobs: excretion (removing urea) and osmoregulation (controlling water content).
W
Write This Down — Kidney Functions

The kidneys are specialised organs that:

• Remove the toxic waste product urea from the circulating blood — they carry out excretion

• Regulate the water content of the blood — they carry out osmoregulation

The kidney is under close control by a feedback system so that water saving is always exactly balanced to the body's needs.

W
Write This Down — Kidney Structure

Blood supply:
• Renal artery — brings blood with high urea concentration to the kidney
• Renal vein — carries blood with lower urea concentration away from the kidney

Internal structure:
• Cortex (outer region) — contains glomeruli which filter excess fluid and waste products out of the blood
• Medulla (inner region) — contains long regions of kidney tubules which can vary the amount and composition of the urine
• Ureter — carries urine from kidney to bladder
• Bladder — stores up to about 500 cm³ of urine before expelling it from the body
• Sphincter — ring of muscle which controls flow of urine from bladder to urethra
• Urethra — carries urine from bladder to outside

Section 13.1 (continued) The Nephron (Kidney Tubule)
Each kidney contains hundreds of thousands of tiny tubes called nephrons. Every nephron works the same way, so if you understand one, you understand the whole kidney.
W
Write This Down — How the Nephron Works

Step 1 — Ultrafiltration (at the glomerulus):
The glomerulus is a ball of capillaries. Blood arrives under high pressure from a branch of the renal artery. Small molecules (water, urea, glucose, salts, amino acids) are forced out of the blood into the Bowman's capsule. Large molecules like proteins and blood cells are too big to pass through — they stay in the blood.

Step 2 — Selective reabsorption (in the tubule):
As the filtrate passes along the first coiled tubule, useful substances are reabsorbed back into the blood: all glucose, all amino acids, and most water. This is called selective reabsorption.

Step 3 — Water adjustment (collecting duct):
The collecting duct can reabsorb more water depending on the body's needs, controlled by ADH (anti-diuretic hormone). What's left is urine — a solution of urea, excess salts, and water.

!
Don't Confuse — Filtration vs Reabsorption

Ultrafiltration pushes everything small out of the blood (including useful things). Selective reabsorption takes the useful things back. Think of it as: "filter everything out, then pick up what you want to keep."

?
Why This Matters — Key Exam Data

About 180 dm³ of water is filtered through the nephrons every day. Only about 1.5 dm³ is lost as urine. That means over 99% of the water is reabsorbed. If they give you a table of concentrations in blood plasma, filtrate, and urine, you need to know:

• Protein: 0 in filtrate (too big to be filtered)
• Glucose: present in filtrate but 0 in urine (fully reabsorbed)
• Urea: much higher concentration in urine than in filtrate (water reabsorbed, urea concentrated)

Section 13.2 Dialysis and the Treatment of Kidney Failure
The body must balance water intake and water loss. When the kidneys fail, we need technology to do their job.
W
Write This Down — Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation is the control of the water content of the body. Water is the most common substance in the body (about 40 dm³).

Water intake (per day): drinks ~1500 cm³, water in food ~800 cm³, from respiration ~200 cm³.

Water loss (per day): urine ~1500 cm³, sweat ~500 cm³, exhaled air ~400 cm³, faeces ~100 cm³.

The water excreted in urine is adjusted so that total water intake and total water loss are balanced.

The volume and concentration of urine is affected by: water intake, temperature (more sweating = less urine), and exercise (more sweating = less urine).

ADH (anti-diuretic hormone) controls how much water the collecting duct reabsorbs. More ADH = more water reabsorbed = smaller volume of more concentrated urine.

W
Write This Down — Kidney Failure

Kidney failure: Damage to the kidneys, perhaps through infection or following an accident, can stop the nephrons working efficiently. The body can no longer control the composition or amount of urine formed, so the content of the blood plasma and tissue fluid is not kept at its optimum. Death may follow quite quickly if the kidney failure is not corrected. Two types of treatment are available:

1. Dialysis (artificial kidney):
• Blood is taken from the patient's artery under high pressure
• A partially permeable membrane separates the blood from the dialysis fluid
• Urea and excess salts diffuse from blood (high concentration) across the membrane into dialysis fluid (low concentration)
• The dialysis fluid has a composition that maintains blood glucose concentration, balances salt intake and removal, and removes urea from the blood — useful solutes and water do not diffuse out
• Blood and dialysis fluid flow in opposite directions (countercurrent) to maintain a concentration gradient
• A chamber removes blood clots and re-warms blood to body temperature before it returns through the vein
• A pump controls pressure and flow rate; typically needed 3 times per week, 6–8 hours each session

2. Kidney transplant:
• A healthy kidney from a donor is surgically transferred to a person with kidney failure (the recipient)
• Problem: tissue rejection — the recipient's immune system will attack the donor kidney and slowly destroy it
• Blood groups and tissue types of donors and recipients are carefully matched to reduce the likelihood of rejection
• The recipient must take drugs to stop rejection happening
• Advantages over dialysis: in the long term, a transplant is much cheaper; the patient's life is less disrupted once they have recovered from the operation

?
Why This Matters

"Compare dialysis and kidney transplant" is a common exam question. The key advantage of a transplant is that the patient can live a normal life afterwards. The key disadvantage is tissue rejection. The key advantage of dialysis is that it doesn't require surgery or a donor. The key disadvantage is the disruption — 3 sessions per week, 6–8 hours each.

!
Don't Confuse — How Dialysis Works

Dialysis removes urea by diffusion, NOT by filtration. The urea moves down its concentration gradient from blood (high) through the membrane into dialysis fluid (low). The reason glucose stays in the blood is that the dialysis fluid already has the same concentration of glucose as normal blood — so there's no concentration gradient for glucose to diffuse along.
