Section 19.1 Ecology and Ecosystems — Key Terms
Ecology is about how organisms interact with each other and their surroundings. Start by nailing the definitions — the exam loves asking you to define these.
W
Write This Down — Core Definitions

Ecology = the study of living organisms in relation to their environment.

Habitat = a part of the environment that can provide food, shelter, and a breeding site for a living organism (e.g. a patch of grassland).

Population = all the members of the same species in the same area at the same time (e.g. all the wildebeest on a plain).

Community = all the populations of living organisms in one area (e.g. acacia trees, zebra, wildebeest, and grass together). The community is the biotic environment.

Ecosystem = a unit containing the community of organisms and their non-living (abiotic) environment interacting together.

!
Don't Confuse

Population vs community: A population is one species. A community is ALL the species living together. If the question says "all the organisms in an area," that's a community, not a population.

W
Write This Down — Biotic vs Abiotic

Biotic factors (living) = predators, food availability, mates, competition, disease.

Abiotic factors (non-living) split into:

• Physical: oxygen and CO₂ concentration, light intensity, water availability

• Climatic: temperature, humidity, wind

?
Why This Matters

The exam often asks "Name two abiotic factors." Students lose marks by naming biotic ones like "predators." Abiotic = non-living. Temperature, light, water — these are the safe answers.

T
Study Tip

Write each definition on a flashcard. On the back, put an example. Test yourself by covering the definition and trying to say it from the example alone. These five definitions come up every year.

  • I can define ecology, habitat, population, community, and ecosystem
  • I know the difference between biotic and abiotic factors
  • I can give two examples of each type of factor
Section 19.2 Food Chains and Food Webs
Food chains show energy flow. Food webs show how multiple food chains connect. The arrows always point in the direction energy flows — from the eaten to the eater.
W
Write This Down — Key Terms

Food chain = shows the flow of energy from one organism to the next.

Trophic level = the position of an organism in a food chain.

Producer = an organism (usually a green plant) that makes its own food by photosynthesis.

Consumer = an organism that obtains energy by feeding on other organisms.

• Primary consumer = herbivore (eats plants)

• Secondary consumer = carnivore (eats herbivores)

• Tertiary consumer / top carnivore = eats other carnivores

Decomposer = fungi and bacteria that break down dead organisms and wastes.

!
Don't Confuse — Arrow Direction

The arrows in a food chain point in the direction energy flows, NOT "who eats whom." So: grass → rabbit → fox means "energy goes FROM grass TO rabbit TO fox." If you draw it backwards, you lose the mark.

W
Write This Down — Energy Transfer Is Inefficient

At every step in a food chain, energy is lost as heat from respiration.

• Only about 5–8% of the Sun's energy falling on leaves is "fixed" by photosynthesis.

• Energy transfer to primary consumer is only 5–10% (much of the plant is indigestible, not all parts are eaten).

• Energy transfer to secondary consumer is 10–20% (animal material is more digestible).

This is why food chains rarely have more than 4 or 5 trophic levels — there is not enough energy left to support another level.

?
Why This Matters

This explains why it's more efficient for humans to eat plants directly rather than feeding plants to animals and then eating the animals. Each step loses 80–90% of the energy as heat.

W
Write This Down — Food Webs

A food web = many interconnected food chains in an ecosystem. The more complicated the food web, the more stable the community — because if one species declines, predators can switch to other prey.

Removing one species from a food web has knock-on effects. For example, removing all sharks from a coral reef food web would let the populations of sea turtles and octopuses increase, which could reduce butterflyfish, which could increase coral.

T
Study Tip — Practise "What Happens If..."

Look at any food web diagram. Pick one organism and imagine it disappears. Trace the effects up and down. The exam loves these questions: "What would happen to the population of X if Y were removed?" Practise talking through the chain of consequences.

  • I can define producer, consumer (primary/secondary/tertiary), and decomposer
  • I know that arrows point in the direction of energy flow
  • I can explain why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels
  • I can predict what happens when a species is removed from a food web
Section 19.3 Pyramids of Numbers, Biomass, and Energy
Food chains give qualitative information (who eats whom). Pyramids give quantitative information (how many, how much mass, how much energy).
W
Write This Down — Three Types of Pyramid
Pyramid type What it measures Can it be inverted?
Numbers Number of organisms at each trophic level Yes — e.g. one oak tree supporting thousands of insects
Biomass Total mass of organisms at each trophic level at one time Rarely, but can be if organisms reproduce and replace themselves quickly
Energy Amount of energy flowing through each level over a period (e.g. kJ/m²/year) Never — always a true pyramid shape
!
Don't Confuse — "Inverted" Pyramids

A pyramid of numbers can be inverted (upside-down) when the producer is very large (one oak tree) or when parasites are involved (many bird lice on fewer birds). A pyramid of energy is NEVER inverted. Know which types can flip.

W
Write This Down — Sampling Methods

To build pyramids, you need to count organisms. Methods include:

• Quadrat — a square frame placed randomly on the ground; count organisms inside. Must be random (use random number generator) and repeated to get a mean.

• Transect — a line across a habitat; record organisms at regular intervals. A belt transect samples between two parallel lines.

• Pitfall trap — jar sunk into soil to catch ground arthropods.

• Pooter — device for collecting small insects from bushes and trees.

• Mark-recapture — for mobile animals. Catch, mark, release. Recapture later. Use the Lincoln index:

Population = (number marked × number in second sample) ÷ number of marked individuals recaptured

?
Why This Matters

The mark-recapture calculation is a common exam question. They'll give you the numbers and ask you to calculate the population size. Practise this formula until you can use it without thinking.

  • I can describe the three types of ecological pyramid
  • I know which pyramids can be inverted and why
  • I can describe how to use a quadrat and why sampling must be random
  • I can use the Lincoln index formula
Section 19.4 Decay and Nutrient Recycling
When organisms die, their bodies don't just vanish. Decomposers break them down and return nutrients to the soil for reuse — this is recycling at its most fundamental.
W
Write This Down — The Decomposition Process

Scavengers (e.g. vultures) eat the dead body first, breaking it into smaller pieces.

Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) then break down the remains by saprotrophic feeding — they secrete enzymes onto the dead material, digest it externally, then absorb the simple products.

Key enzymes involved: lipases (fats → fatty acids + glycerol), amylases (starch → maltose → glucose), proteases (protein → amino acids).

W
Write This Down — Conditions Affecting Decay

Decomposition is faster when:

• Temperature is warm (optimum for enzyme activity)

• Oxygen is present (aerobic respiration in decomposers). Without oxygen, decay is slow and smelly (methane and hydrogen sulfide produced).

• Water is available (many decomposition reactions are hydrolysis reactions).

T
Study Tip — Link to Compost Heaps

If the exam asks about compost heaps, connect the dots: gardeners spray water (moisture for reactions), build on sticks (air gaps for oxygen), and the heap heats up (respiration by decomposers generates heat). Every compost question is really a decomposition question.

  • I can describe saprotrophic feeding
  • I know the three conditions needed for rapid decomposition
Section 19.5 The Carbon Cycle
Carbon is endlessly recycled between living organisms and the atmosphere. The key processes that move it are photosynthesis, respiration, feeding, decomposition, and combustion.
W
Write This Down — Carbon Cycle Steps

Carbon enters living things via:

• Photosynthesis — plants use CO₂ from the air to make organic compounds (carbohydrates, fats, proteins).

Carbon returns to the atmosphere via:

• Respiration — all living organisms (plants, animals, decomposers) release CO₂.

• Combustion — burning fossil fuels or biomass releases CO₂.

• Decomposition — decomposers respire as they break down dead matter.

Carbon moves between organisms via:

• Feeding — animals eat plants or other animals.

• Death and excretion — provides material for decomposers.

!
Don't Confuse

"Plants only photosynthesize" is WRONG. Plants both photosynthesize and respire. Overall, they fix more carbon than they release (otherwise they couldn't grow), but they do respire 24/7.

W
Write This Down — Fossil Fuels and the Carbon Cycle

Over millions of years, some dead organisms did NOT decompose (conditions were anaerobic, low pH, extreme cold). Their carbon became "locked up" in fossil fuels (coal, oil, peat, gas).

When humans burn fossil fuels, this stored carbon is released rapidly as CO₂, increasing atmospheric CO₂ concentration. This disrupts the balance of the carbon cycle.

?
Why This Matters

Questions about rising CO₂ levels almost always link to the carbon cycle. The answer is: "Combustion of fossil fuels releases CO₂ faster than photosynthesis can remove it." That's the one-sentence version of climate change biology.

  • I can name the processes that add carbon to the atmosphere
  • I can name the process that removes carbon from the atmosphere
  • I can explain how fossil fuel combustion disrupts the carbon cycle
  • I could draw or label a carbon cycle diagram from memory
Section 19.6 The Nitrogen Cycle
Plants need nitrogen to make proteins and DNA, but they can't use nitrogen gas (N₂) directly. It must be converted to nitrate first. This involves several types of bacteria.
W
Write This Down — Four Key Bacterial Processes
Process What happens Bacteria involved
Nitrogen fixation N₂ gas → ammonium ions → nitrate Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g. in root nodules of legumes like peas and beans)
Decomposition Dead organisms → amino acids → ammonium ions Decomposing bacteria and fungi
Nitrification Ammonium ions → nitrite → nitrate (needs oxygen) Nitrifying bacteria
Denitrification Nitrate → nitrogen gas (occurs without oxygen) Denitrifying bacteria (in waterlogged soils)
!
Don't Confuse — Nitrification vs Nitrogen Fixation

Nitrogen fixation = N₂ gas from the air → ammonium → nitrate. It starts with atmospheric nitrogen.

Nitrification = ammonium ions already in the soil → nitrite → nitrate. It starts with ammonium in the soil.

They sound similar, but they start from different places. The exam will test whether you know the difference.

?
Why This Matters — Farming Link

Farmers drain waterlogged fields to prevent denitrification (which removes useful nitrate). They plant legumes (peas, beans) in crop rotation to add nitrate via nitrogen fixation. They add fertilisers to directly increase soil nitrate. All of this connects to the nitrogen cycle.

T
Study Tip — Draw It

The nitrogen cycle diagram is worth learning to draw. Start with "N₂ in atmosphere" at the top, then show the four processes as arrows connecting nitrogen gas, ammonium, nitrite, nitrate, plant protein, animal protein, dead matter. Practise until you can sketch it from memory.

  • I can name and describe all four bacterial processes in the nitrogen cycle
  • I know the difference between nitrogen fixation and nitrification
  • I can explain why farmers drain waterlogged fields and plant legumes
Section 19.7 The Water Cycle
Unlike carbon and nitrogen, most water recycling happens through physical processes (evaporation, condensation) rather than biological ones. Only a tiny proportion passes through living organisms.
W
Write This Down — Water Cycle Steps

• Evaporation — heat from the Sun turns liquid water (from seas, lakes, soil) into water vapour.

• Evapotranspiration — water evaporates from plant leaves (transpiration) into the atmosphere.

• Condensation — water vapour cools and forms water droplets, which collect as clouds.

• Precipitation — water falls as rain, snow, or hail.

• Melting/refreezing — ice turns to liquid water and vice versa.

The driving force is heat energy from the Sun.

W
Write This Down — Why Water Is Special for Life

• High specific heat capacity — cells resist temperature changes.

• High latent heat of vaporisation — evaporation from surfaces cools organisms.

• Incompressible — provides support (e.g. for fish bodies, eyeballs).

• Excellent solvent/transport medium — carries glucose, oxygen, amino acids in blood.

• Biological reagent — needed for photosynthesis and digestion (hydrolysis).

• Lubricant — e.g. saliva, synovial fluid in joints.

T
Study Tip

The water cycle is the easiest of the three cycles. Focus more study time on the carbon and nitrogen cycles — those are where the harder exam questions come from.

  • I can describe the main steps of the water cycle
  • I can list at least four biological functions of water
