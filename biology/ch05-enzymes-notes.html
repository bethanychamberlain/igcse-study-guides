Section 5.1 Enzymes Are Biological Catalysts
Every chemical reaction in your body is controlled by enzymes. Without them, reactions would be too slow to keep you alive.
W
Write This Down — Key Definitions

Metabolism = the sum of all the chemical reactions going on within a living organism.

Anabolic reactions = build up large molecules from smaller ones (require energy input).
Example: condensation of glucose into glycogen in the liver.

Catabolic reactions = break down large molecules into smaller ones (release energy).
Example: breakdown of glucose into CO₂ and water in respiration.

W
Write This Down — Enzyme Definition

Enzymes are proteins that function as biological catalysts. They speed up chemical reactions without being changed or used up themselves.

• The molecules that react in the enzyme-catalysed reaction are called substrates.
• The molecules produced are called products.
• Different enzymes are involved in anabolic and catabolic reactions.

?
Why This Matters

The exam will often say "biological catalyst" and expect you to know that means enzyme, or ask you to define an enzyme. You need three ideas in your answer: protein + speeds up reactions + not changed by the reaction. All three = full marks.

T
Study Tip

Cover the definition and say it out loud. If you wrote "Enzymes speed up reactions" on your Easter test, that would only get 1 mark. You need to also say they are proteins and that they are not used up.

  • I can define metabolism, anabolic, and catabolic
  • I can define enzyme using all three key ideas (protein, catalyst, unchanged)
  • I know what substrates and products are
Section 5.1 continued Enzymes and Cells
Enzymes are made inside cells. Some work inside the cell, others are released outside it.
W
Write This Down — Two Types of Enzyme

Intracellular enzymes work inside the cell.
• Example: catalase breaks down harmful hydrogen peroxide in liver cells.

Extracellular enzymes are made inside cells then released to work outside.
• Example: lipase (breaks down fats into fatty acids and glycerol)
• Example: amylase (breaks down starch into maltose)

W
Write This Down — Specificity

Enzymes are specific — each enzyme works on one kind of substrate only. For example, proteases break down proteins but have no effect on carbohydrates or lipids.

This specificity comes from the complementary shape of the active site and the substrate.

!
Don't Confuse

"Specific" does NOT mean "important" in biology. It means the enzyme will only catalyse one particular reaction. Amylase only works on starch — put it with protein and nothing happens.

  • I can give an example of an intracellular enzyme and what it does
  • I can give two examples of extracellular enzymes
  • I can explain what enzyme specificity means
Section 5.1 continued The Lock and Key Model
This is how enzymes actually work at a molecular level. The exam loves this model — learn it cold.
W
Write This Down — The Lock and Key Hypothesis

An enzyme is a protein folded into a complex 3D shape. The part of the enzyme where the substrate binds is called the active site.

How it works (step by step):
1. The substrate molecule fits exactly into the active site of the enzyme (like a key fits into a lock).
2. This forms an enzyme-substrate complex.
3. The active site brings the substrate molecules closer together (or strains bonds to break them).
4. The substrates react to form products.
5. The products leave the active site.
6. The enzyme is unchanged and can be used again many thousands of times.

?
Why This Matters

The lock and key model explains specificity: only a substrate with the correct shape can fit into the active site, just as only one key fits a particular lock. If the exam asks "Why is the model called lock and key?", the answer is: the substrate (key) has a complementary shape to the active site (lock).

W
Write This Down — Enzymes Lower Activation Energy

Enzymes lower the energy needed for the reaction to take place (the activation energy). This makes the reaction much more likely to happen at body temperature.

T
Study Tip — Draw It

Draw the lock and key model from memory: enzyme with active site → substrate fits in → enzyme-substrate complex → products released → enzyme free again. Label every part. If you can draw and label it, you can answer any question on it.

  • I can name and describe the active site
  • I can explain the lock and key model step by step
  • I can explain why it's called "lock and key" (complementary shapes)
  • I drew the lock and key diagram from memory
Section 5.1 continued Factor 1: Temperature
Temperature is the factor most commonly tested in the exam. You need to know what happens at every point on the graph.
W
Write This Down — Temperature and Enzyme Activity

As temperature increases from low:
• Substrate molecules move faster (more kinetic energy).
• They collide with the enzyme's active site more often and with more energy.
• So the rate of reaction increases.

At the optimum temperature:
• The rate of reaction is at its maximum.
• For most human enzymes this is around 37°C.
• For most plant enzymes it is around 25°C.

Above the optimum temperature:
• The enzyme molecules vibrate so much that the bonds holding the 3D shape break.
• The active site changes shape — the substrate can no longer fit.
• The enzyme is denatured.
• The rate of reaction drops rapidly to zero.

W
Write This Down — The Graph Shape

Picture this graph (you must be able to sketch it):
• X-axis: Temperature (°C)
• Y-axis: Enzyme activity (rate of reaction)
• Shape: The line rises steadily, peaks at the optimum, then drops sharply on the right side.
• The left side of the peak is a gentle slope (reaction getting faster).
• The right side is a steep drop (denaturation is sudden and permanent).

!
Don't Confuse — This Is Critical

Denatured does NOT mean "killed" or "destroyed." Enzymes are not alive — they're proteins. Denatured means the active site has changed shape so the substrate no longer fits. This is usually irreversible (permanent).

Also: at low temperatures, enzymes are NOT denatured. They're just slow because the molecules have less kinetic energy. If you warm them up again, they work fine.

?
Why This Matters — You Scored 33% on Enzymes

On the Easter test, you confused the effect of temperature on enzymes. The exam will almost always include a graph question about this. If you can describe what's happening at every point on the temperature graph — left side, peak, right side — you'll pick up 4-5 marks easily.

T
Study Tip — The Boiled Saliva Experiment

In the textbook experiment, saliva (containing amylase) was boiled then added to starch. Result: starch was NOT digested. Why? Boiling denatured the amylase — its active site changed shape permanently. Even after cooling, it couldn't work. This is the proof that denaturation is irreversible.

  • I can explain why reaction rate increases with temperature (more kinetic energy, more collisions)
  • I know the optimum temperature for human enzymes (~37°C)
  • I can define denaturation (active site changes shape, substrate no longer fits)
  • I know denatured does NOT mean killed — and that it IS irreversible
  • I can sketch the temperature-activity graph from memory
Section 5.1 continued Factor 2: pH
pH affects enzymes in a similar way to temperature — there's an optimum, and going too far from it causes denaturation.
W
Write This Down — pH and Enzyme Activity

Each enzyme has its own optimum pH where it works fastest. Moving away from this optimum (too acidic or too alkaline) changes the 3D shape of the enzyme and can denature it.

Key examples:
• Pepsin (stomach protease): optimum pH 2.0 (very acidic — the stomach produces hydrochloric acid)
• Amylase (mouth/small intestine): optimum pH 7.5 (slightly alkaline)

W
Write This Down — The pH Graph Shape

• X-axis: pH
• Y-axis: Enzyme activity (rate of reaction)
• Shape: A bell curve (hill shape) centred on the optimum pH.
• Activity rises as pH approaches the optimum, peaks, then falls on both sides.
• At extreme pH values (very high or very low), the enzyme is denatured.

!
Don't Confuse

Not all enzymes have the same optimum pH. The exam might give you a table of pH vs activity and ask you to read off the optimum. Just find the highest activity value — the pH at that point is the optimum.

T
Study Tip — Remember the Examples

Pepsin = pH 2 = stomach (acid). Amylase = pH 7.5 = mouth (neutral/slight base). If you remember where the enzyme works, you can figure out its optimum pH. Stomach = acidic. Mouth = neutral.

  • I know each enzyme has its own optimum pH
  • I can name pepsin's optimum pH (2.0) and where it works (stomach)
  • I can name amylase's optimum pH (7.5) and where it works (mouth/small intestine)
  • I know that extremes of pH denature enzymes (same idea as temperature)
Section 5.1 continued Activators and Inhibitors
Some molecules help enzymes work; others block them. These are less commonly tested but still worth knowing.
W
Write This Down

Activators make it more likely that the enzyme binds to its substrate.
• Example: chloride ions are essential for salivary amylase to work.

Inhibitors make it more difficult for the enzyme to bind to its substrate.
• Example: cyanide ions block the active sites of enzymes involved in respiration (this is why cyanide is a poison).

  • I know what activators and inhibitors do
Section 5.1 continued Humans Exploit Enzymes
Because enzymes are specific and can be used over and over, they are valuable in industry, food preparation, and medicine.
W
Write This Down

Enzymes are useful because they are:
• Specific — they only catalyse one reaction, so no unwanted by-products
• Reusable — they are not used up, so a small amount can process a lot of substrate
• Effective at low temperatures — no need for expensive high-energy heating

Example uses: biological washing powders (proteases/lipases to break down stains), food production, medicine (e.g. an enzyme from snake venom can break down blood clots).

  • I can explain why enzymes are useful in industry (specific, reusable, work at low temps)
