Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Restriction enzyme Cuts DNA at specific recognition sites, leaving "sticky ends" that can join with matching fragments. Ligase (ligase JOINS DNA; restriction enzymes CUT it)
Ligase An enzyme that joins (splices) two DNA fragments together by bonding their sticky ends. Restriction enzyme (restriction enzymes cut; ligase glues)
Plasmid A small circle of extra DNA in bacteria. Used as a vector (carrier) to transfer genes into host cells. Chromosome (plasmids are separate from the main bacterial chromosome)
Vector Anything that carries a gene from one organism to another (e.g. plasmid, virus). —
Recombinant DNA DNA that contains genes from two different organisms (e.g. human insulin gene in a bacterial plasmid). —
Transgenic organism An organism that has received genes from a different species (e.g. Golden Rice, insulin-producing bacteria). Selectively bred organism (that uses traditional breeding, not gene transfer)
Fermenter A large container for growing microorganisms under controlled conditions (temperature, pH, nutrients, sterility). Fermentation (that's the anaerobic respiration process in yeast)
Fermentation Anaerobic respiration in yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + energy. Fermenter (that's the equipment, not the reaction)
Immobilised enzyme An enzyme fixed to an inert support material (e.g. resin beads). Can be reused, product is purer. —
Gene therapy Transferring a healthy gene into a person's cells that contain a mutant allele causing disease (e.g. cystic fibrosis). Genetic engineering (GE puts genes into bacteria/plants; gene therapy treats human patients)

Batch Culture Continuous Culture
Nutrients Fixed input at start Added continuously

GM Technology — Benefits vs Concerns

Benefits Concerns
Higher yields from fewer resources Resistance genes could spread to wild plants ("superweeds")
Crops can grow in extreme environments Engineered bacteria could escape with unpredictable effects
Healthier foods (e.g. Golden Rice with vitamin A) Companies might patent organisms, blocking shared benefits
More predictable results than selective breeding Ethical concerns about "designer" organisms

Say It in One Sentence

GE insulin Human insulin gene is cut out, spliced into a bacterial plasmid, and the bacteria are grown in a fermenter to mass-produce insulin.
Sticky ends When a restriction enzyme cuts DNA, it leaves uneven ends that can pair with matching sticky ends from another fragment.
Yeast in baking CO₂ from fermentation makes the dough rise; the alcohol evaporates during baking.
Yeast in brewing Ethanol is the useful product; wine rarely exceeds 12% because the alcohol kills the yeast.
Pectinase Breaks down pectin in plant cell walls to make fruit juice clearer and increase the yield.
Golden Rice Engineered to produce beta-carotene (vitamin A) for populations with vitamin A deficiency.
Immobilised enzymes Fixed to beads so they can be reused, the product stays pure, and costs drop — win-win-win.

Memory Tricks

Genetic Engineering — 6-Step Recipe
(1) Cut gene out with restriction enzyme, (2) Cut plasmid open with the SAME enzyme, (3) Glue gene into plasmid with ligase, (4) Put plasmid back into bacterium, (5) Grow bacteria in a fermenter, (6) Collect the product. Think: Cut, Cut, Glue, Put, Grow, Collect.
Baking = CO₂, Brewing = Ethanol
Same yeast reaction (glucose → ethanol + CO₂), but the useful product is different. Baker wants the gas (bubbles). Brewer wants the liquid (alcohol). The other product is a by-product in each case.
Why GE Insulin Beats Pig Insulin
Three reasons: (1) it's the human version so it works better, (2) produced in large quantities on demand, (3) no animal slaughter needed. If you remember "Human, Huge amounts, Humane," you've got 3 marks.
Fermenter Features = Control + Sterile
Every fermenter feature exists for one of two reasons: (1) controlling conditions for optimal enzyme activity (temperature jacket, pH probe, stirrer) or (2) preventing contamination (steam sterilisation, sealed system). If they ask "Why does it have X?", pick one of these two reasons.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Describe the steps of genetic engineering" Cut gene with restriction enzyme, cut plasmid with same enzyme, join with ligase, insert into bacterium, culture in fermenter, extract product
"Why is genetically engineered insulin better than animal insulin?" It's the human version (more effective, less rejection), produced in large quantities, no animal slaughter needed
"Why does wine rarely have more than 12% alcohol?" At high concentrations, ethanol becomes toxic to the yeast and kills it, stopping fermentation
"What is the role of the water jacket in a fermenter?" Controls temperature — keeps it warm enough for enzyme activity but not so hot that proteins denature
"Give two advantages of immobilised enzymes" Enzyme can be reused (lower cost) and the product is easier to purify (no enzyme contamination)
"Why do biological washing powders work at lower temperatures?" Enzymes work at moderate temperatures (30–40°C) — less energy used, break down stains that normal detergents cannot
"Give a benefit and a concern of GM crops" Benefit: higher yields / pest resistance. Concern: resistance genes could spread to wild plants ("superweeds")
"What is gene therapy?" Inserting a healthy gene into cells that have a mutant allele, using a virus as a vector. Example: cystic fibrosis
