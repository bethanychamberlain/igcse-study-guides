Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the term enzyme.
3 marks
Answer
An enzyme is a protein [1] that acts as a biological catalyst [1], speeding up chemical reactions without being changed or used up itself [1].
Three ideas = three marks. "Protein" + "catalyst/speeds up reactions" + "not used up." Miss any one and you lose a mark.

What is the name of the part of an enzyme where the substrate binds?
1 mark
Answer
The active site [1].
One word answer. Don't write "active area" or "binding site" — the exact term is "active site."

3
Name one intracellular enzyme and one extracellular enzyme, and state what each one does.
2 marks
Answer
Intracellular: catalase — breaks down hydrogen peroxide in liver cells [1].
Extracellular: amylase — breaks down starch into maltose (or lipase — breaks down fats into fatty acids and glycerol) [1].
Name + function = 1 mark each. Just writing the name without what it does won't score.

4
What does it mean when we say enzymes are specific?
2 marks
Answer
Each enzyme only works on one type of substrate [1] because the substrate must have a complementary shape to the enzyme's active site [1].
Two ideas: (1) one substrate only, (2) because of complementary shape / lock and key fit. Both needed for full marks.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Describe the lock and key model of enzyme action. Include the terms active site, substrate, enzyme-substrate complex, and product in your answer.
4 marks
Answer
The substrate has a complementary shape to the enzyme's active site [1]. The substrate binds to the active site to form an enzyme-substrate complex [1]. The enzyme catalyses the reaction and products are formed [1]. The products leave the active site and the enzyme is free to be used again [1].
Four terms required, four marks. Make sure each term is used correctly in context, not just listed.

6
Explain the effect of temperature on enzyme activity. In your answer, describe what happens below, at, and above the optimum temperature.
4 marks
Answer
Below optimum: as temperature rises, substrate molecules gain kinetic energy and move faster, so they collide with the active site more frequently — rate of reaction increases [1].

At optimum (~37°C for humans): the rate of reaction is at its maximum [1].

Above optimum: the enzyme vibrates so much that the bonds holding its shape break [1]. The active site changes shape (the enzyme is denatured) so the substrate can no longer fit, and the rate drops to zero [1].
Four ideas: kinetic energy increases rate, maximum at optimum, bonds break above optimum, active site changes shape (denatured). Say "active site changes shape" not just "enzyme breaks."

7
Amylase digests starch, but proteases do not. Explain why.
3 marks
Answer
Enzymes are specific [1]. The active site of amylase has a complementary shape to starch molecules [1]. Protease has a differently shaped active site that fits protein, not starch, so starch cannot bind to it [1].
Key phrase: "complementary shape." Don't just say "they're different" — explain WHY using the lock and key idea.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A student set up four test tubes, each containing starch solution. Tube A had saliva added at 30°C, Tube B had saliva at 20°C, Tube C had boiled then cooled saliva at 20°C, and Tube D had water at 20°C. After 20 minutes, tubes A and B tested negative for starch (no blue-black colour with iodine), while C and D still tested positive. Explain these results.
5 marks
Answer
Tubes A and B: Saliva contains the enzyme amylase, which digests starch into maltose [1]. Both A (30°C) and B (20°C) showed digestion because the enzyme was active at both temperatures [1].

Tube C: Boiling the saliva denatured the amylase — the active site permanently changed shape [1], so even after cooling it could no longer bind to starch. Starch remained [1].

Tube D: Water contains no enzyme, so no digestion occurred — this is the control to show that starch does not break down on its own [1].
Five marks for five ideas: amylase digests starch, works at both temps, boiling denatures permanently, active site changed shape, D is the control. The control explanation is often missed — don't skip it.

The table shows the relative activity of a human enzyme at different pH values, kept at 35°C.

pH357911
Activity4123194

(a) What is the optimum pH for this enzyme? [1]
(b) Explain why the reaction mixtures were all kept at 35°C. [2]
(c) Describe and explain the results. [3]
6 marks
Answer
(a) The optimum pH is 7 (highest activity = 31) [1].

(b) Temperature is kept constant so it is a controlled/fixed variable [1]. This ensures that any change in activity is only due to changes in pH, not temperature [1].

(c) Activity increases from pH 3 to pH 7 as conditions approach the optimum [1]. Above pH 7 the activity decreases because the pH is moving away from the optimum and extreme pH values denature the enzyme [1] — the active site changes shape so the substrate can no longer fit [1].
Part (b) is about fair testing — "to keep it a fair test" alone is worth 1 mark, but explaining WHY (so only pH varies) gets the second. Part (c): describe the trend AND explain it using denaturation.

10
Human enzymes catalyse reactions at 37°C, but not at 73°C. Explain why, using the lock and key model in your answer.
5 marks
Answer
At 37°C, the enzyme's active site has a specific three-dimensional shape [1]. The substrate has a complementary shape and fits into the active site like a key fits a lock [1], forming an enzyme-substrate complex and allowing the reaction to occur.

At 73°C, the high temperature causes the enzyme to vibrate excessively, breaking the bonds that hold its shape [1]. The active site changes shape (the enzyme is denatured) [1]. The substrate can no longer fit into the active site, so no enzyme-substrate complex can form and no reaction occurs [1].
Five marks: active site has specific shape, substrate fits (complementary), high temp breaks bonds, active site changes shape (denatured), substrate no longer fits. Use the phrase "complementary shape" and "active site changes shape" — these are examiner trigger words.
