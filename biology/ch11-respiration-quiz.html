Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define respiration.
2 marks
Answer
Respiration is the release of energy [1] from food/glucose in all living cells [1].
Two key ideas: "release of energy" and "in all living cells." If you just say "breathing" you get zero — breathing is NOT respiration.

2
Write the word equation for aerobic respiration.
2 marks
Answer
glucose + oxygen → energy + carbon dioxide + water [1 for reactants, 1 for products].
You must include ALL parts. Forgetting "water" loses a mark. Write "energy +" before the products — energy is released, not a chemical product, but examiners accept it in the word equation.

3
Name two uses of energy in living organisms.
2 marks
Answer
Any two from: muscle contraction/movement [1], active transport [1], growth/protein synthesis, cell division, maintaining body temperature, passage of nerve impulses.
Pick the two you're most confident spelling correctly. "Muscle contraction" and "active transport" are the safest choices.

4
In which part of the cell does aerobic respiration take place?
1 mark
Answer
Mitochondria [1].
Not "mitochondrion" (that's singular but still accepted). Do NOT say "nucleus" or "cytoplasm" — those are wrong for aerobic respiration.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
State the word equation for anaerobic respiration in humans, and give the word equation for anaerobic respiration in yeast. State one difference between the products.
4 marks
Answer
In humans: glucose → energy + lactic acid [1]
In yeast: glucose → energy + alcohol (ethanol) + carbon dioxide [1]
Difference: In humans the product is lactic acid, but in yeast the products are alcohol and carbon dioxide [1]. Yeast produces CO₂ but human anaerobic respiration does not [1].
The most common mistake: mixing up the products. Humans make lactic acid. Yeast makes alcohol + CO₂. Never the other way round.

6
A runner continues to breathe heavily for several minutes after finishing a race. Explain why.
3 marks
Answer
During the race, the muscles could not get enough oxygen for aerobic respiration, so anaerobic respiration occurred [1]. This produced lactic acid, which built up in the muscles [1]. After the race, the runner breathes heavily to take in extra oxygen to break down (oxidise) the lactic acid — this is called repaying the oxygen debt [1].
Three marks, three ideas: (1) anaerobic respiration happened, (2) lactic acid built up, (3) extra oxygen needed to break it down = oxygen debt. Use the term "oxygen debt" — it's a key term.

7
In an experiment, germinating peas are placed in a vacuum flask. The temperature rises from 20°C to 28°C over 5 days. Dead peas in another flask stay at 20°C. Explain these results.
3 marks
Answer
The living (germinating) peas are respiring, releasing energy, some of which is lost as heat [1]. The vacuum flask prevents heat loss to the surroundings, so the temperature rises [1]. The dead peas are not respiring, so no heat is released and the temperature stays constant — this acts as a control to show the temperature rise is due to respiration [1].
Always mention the control. Saying "the dead peas show the temperature change is caused by respiration, not by the environment" is worth a mark.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A respirometer is used to measure the rate of oxygen consumption by germinating seeds at different temperatures. At 15°C the rate is 0.3 mm/s, at 35°C it is 1.1 mm/s, and at 65°C it is 0.0 mm/s. Explain these results.
5 marks
Answer
Respiration is controlled by enzymes [1]. As temperature increases from 15°C to 35°C, the enzyme molecules and substrates have more kinetic energy, so they collide more frequently and the rate of respiration increases [1]. 35°C is close to the optimum temperature for these enzymes, giving the highest rate [1]. At 65°C, the enzymes have been denatured — their active sites have changed shape permanently [1], so they can no longer catalyse the reactions of respiration and the rate falls to zero [1].
The chain: enzymes control respiration → higher temp = more kinetic energy = faster reactions → optimum around 35°C → above optimum = denatured = zero. Use the word "denatured" — not "killed" or "destroyed."

9
Compare aerobic and anaerobic respiration in terms of: (a) oxygen requirement, (b) location in the cell, (c) energy yield, (d) products. Then explain why anaerobic respiration releases less energy.
6 marks
Answer
(a) Oxygen: Aerobic requires oxygen; anaerobic does not [1].
(b) Location: Aerobic takes place in the mitochondria; anaerobic occurs in the cytoplasm [1].
(c) Energy: Aerobic releases a large amount of energy; anaerobic releases much less — about 1/20th [1].
(d) Products: Aerobic produces carbon dioxide and water; anaerobic produces lactic acid (in animals) or alcohol and carbon dioxide (in yeast) [1].

Why less energy: In anaerobic respiration, glucose is only partially broken down [1]. The products (lactic acid or alcohol) still contain a lot of stored chemical energy that has not been released [1].
For the "why less energy" part, the key idea is "glucose is not fully broken down" — the products still contain energy. This is worth 2 marks on its own.

10
Describe how you would use a respirometer to investigate the effect of temperature on the rate of respiration in germinating seeds. Include the apparatus, method, how you would ensure a fair test, and how you would process the results.
6 marks
Answer
Apparatus: Respirometer with a capillary tube, potassium hydroxide (KOH) to absorb CO₂, germinating seeds in a gauze basket, a thermostatically controlled water bath [1].

Method: Place a known mass of germinating seeds in the respiratory chamber. Include KOH to absorb CO₂. Close the system and allow equilibrium. Measure the distance the coloured liquid moves in the capillary tube over a set time period [1].

Fair test: Use the same mass of seeds each time [1]. Change only the temperature (independent variable) using the water bath. Keep the time interval constant. Repeat at several temperatures (e.g. 15°C, 25°C, 35°C, 45°C) [1].

Results: Calculate the rate of oxygen consumption as distance moved / time (mm per second or mm per minute) [1]. Plot a graph of rate of oxygen consumption (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis) [1].
Practical questions like this are high-value. Key marks: name the apparatus, describe what you measure, explain the role of KOH, state what variables you control, and describe how you process results. Mentioning "repeats for reliability" can earn an extra mark.
