Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define homeostasis.
1 mark
Answer
Homeostasis is the maintenance of a constant internal environment [1].
Short and precise. "Constant internal environment" is the key phrase. Don't say "keeping the body the same" — that's too vague.

2
State one difference between a sensory neurone and a motor neurone.
1 mark
Answer
Any one of:
• A sensory neurone carries impulses from the receptor to the CNS, while a motor neurone carries impulses from the CNS to the effector [1].
• A sensory neurone has its cell body on the side of the axon, while a motor neurone has its cell body at one end [1].
Direction is the safest answer: sensory = to CNS, motor = from CNS. That's always right.

3
Name the two hormones that control blood glucose concentration. For each, state whether it raises or lowers blood glucose.
2 marks
Answer
Insulin — lowers blood glucose [1].
Glucagon — raises blood glucose [1].
Both are made by the pancreas. Remember: "insul-IN = glucose goes IN-to storage (lowers it)."

4
Name three parts of the eye and state the function of each.
3 marks
Answer
Any three of:
• Cornea — refracts (bends) light entering the eye [1]
• Iris — controls the amount of light entering the eye by changing the size of the pupil [1]
• Lens — fine-focuses light onto the retina; can change shape [1]
• Retina — contains light-sensitive cells (rods and cones) that detect light [1]
• Optic nerve — carries nerve impulses from the retina to the brain [1]
Name + function for each. Don't just list parts — you need to say what each one does.

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain HOW and WHY, not just name things.
5
A person's body temperature rises above 37°C. Describe two responses of the skin that help to reduce body temperature back to normal.
4 marks
Answer
1. Vasodilation: The arterioles near the skin surface dilate (widen) [1], so more blood flows close to the surface of the skin and more heat is lost by radiation to the surroundings [1].

2. Sweating: Sweat glands produce sweat onto the skin surface [1]. The sweat evaporates, which requires heat energy, so heat is transferred from the body to the environment, cooling the skin [1].
For each response: name the mechanism AND explain HOW it removes heat. Just saying "sweating" without explaining evaporation won't get full marks.

6
Describe the pathway of a reflex arc when a person touches a hot object. Name the type of neurone at each stage.
4 marks
Answer
Heat is detected by a receptor in the skin (a thermoreceptor) [1]. A sensory neurone carries the impulse to the spinal cord (CNS) [1]. A relay neurone (connector neurone) passes the impulse across the spinal cord [1]. A motor neurone carries the impulse to the effector (a muscle in the arm), which contracts to pull the hand away [1].
The full chain: receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone → motor neurone → effector. Every link is a mark. Don't forget the relay neurone in the middle — students often skip it.

7
Explain how the eye focuses on a close object. Name the structures involved and describe what happens to each.
3 marks
Answer
The ciliary muscles contract [1]. This causes the suspensory ligaments to become slack (loose) [1]. The lens is no longer being pulled, so it springs into a short, fat (more convex) shape, which refracts light more strongly to focus the diverging light rays from a close object onto the retina [1].
Three marks, three steps: ciliary muscles contract, ligaments go slack, lens becomes fat. The counterintuitive part is that muscles contracting makes ligaments LOOSE — say it clearly.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
A person eats a meal high in sugar. Using the concept of negative feedback, explain how the blood glucose concentration is returned to its normal level.
5 marks
Answer
After the meal, blood glucose concentration rises above normal [1]. This increase is detected by cells in the pancreas [1]. The pancreas responds by secreting the hormone insulin into the bloodstream [1]. Insulin causes the liver (and muscle/fat cells) to convert glucose into glycogen for storage, removing glucose from the blood [1]. This is negative feedback because the response (lowering glucose) cancels out the original change (glucose being too high), returning blood glucose to its normal level [1].
Five-step chain: glucose rises → pancreas detects → insulin released → glucose converted to glycogen in liver → glucose returns to normal. Then explicitly state it's negative feedback because the response reverses the change.

9
Compare the nervous system and the endocrine system as methods of coordination in the human body. Give four differences and one similarity.
5 marks
Answer
Differences:
• Nervous system uses electrical impulses along nerves; endocrine uses chemical messengers (hormones) in the bloodstream [1].
• Nervous responses are very rapid (milliseconds); endocrine responses can be slow [1].
• Nervous responses are short-lived (seconds); endocrine responses can last for years (e.g. growth) [1].
• Nervous responses are usually localised (one specific area); endocrine responses are often widespread (affect many organs) [1].

Similarity:
• Both systems are used to coordinate and control the body's responses to stimuli / both involve communication between different parts of the body [1].
This table-style comparison question appears almost every year. Learn four differences and one similarity. Set it out clearly — don't write a paragraph; use bullet points.

10
A young shoot is placed on its side in the dark. After a few days, the shoot grows upwards. Explain how the plant hormone auxin causes this response, and state the name for this type of growth response.
5 marks
Answer
This is an example of negative gravitropism — the shoot grows away from the direction of gravity [1]. Auxin is produced at the tip of the shoot and diffuses downwards due to gravity [1]. This means more auxin accumulates on the lower side of the shoot [1]. In shoots, auxin stimulates cell elongation, so the cells on the lower side grow faster (elongate more) than those on the upper side [1]. The uneven growth causes the shoot to bend upwards, away from gravity [1].
Five ideas: name the tropism (negative gravitropism), auxin produced at tip, gravity pulls auxin to lower side, auxin stimulates growth in shoots, unequal growth causes upward bending. Remember: auxin stimulates growth in SHOOTS but inhibits growth in ROOTS — the exam will test this difference.
