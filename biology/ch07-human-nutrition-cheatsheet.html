Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Digestion Breaking large, insoluble molecules into small, soluble molecules so they can be absorbed into the blood. Ingestion (that's just taking food into the mouth)
Enzyme A biological catalyst (protein) that speeds up a specific chemical reaction without being used up. Bile (bile is NOT an enzyme — it emulsifies fat physically)
Peristalsis Waves of muscle contraction that push food along the alimentary canal. —
Absorption Small soluble molecules cross the gut wall into the blood (in the ileum via villi). Assimilation (that's when absorbed molecules become part of body cells)
Egestion Removal of undigested food from the body through the anus as faeces. Excretion (removal of metabolic waste made inside cells, like CO₂ and urea)
Villus Tiny finger-like projection in the ileum wall that massively increases surface area for absorption. Microvilli (even smaller projections ON the villus surface)
Bile Made in liver, stored in gall bladder. Emulsifies fats (breaks into droplets) and neutralises stomach acid. Lipase (lipase is the enzyme that chemically digests fat — bile just breaks it into smaller droplets)
Amylase Enzyme that breaks starch into maltose. Found in saliva and pancreatic juice. Maltase (maltase breaks maltose into glucose — a second step)
Deamination The liver removes the amino group from excess amino acids, forming urea (excreted by kidneys). Transamination (converting one amino acid into another the body needs)
Malnutrition "Bad feeding" — too much food, too little food, or wrong proportions. NOT just starvation. Undernutrition (undernutrition is only one type of malnutrition)

The Enzyme Table — Memorise This Whole Thing

Region Enzyme Substrate → Product Conditions
Mouth Salivary amylase Starch → maltose Alkaline (pH ~7.5)
Stomach Pepsin (protease) Protein → amino acids Acidic (pH 2, HCl)
Duodenum Pancreatic amylase Starch → maltose Alkaline (pH 8)

Exam Trap — Starch Digestion Is TWO Steps
Starch is NOT converted directly to glucose. It goes: Starch → (amylase) → Maltose → (maltase) → Glucose. Two enzymes, two steps. Writing "amylase converts starch to glucose" will lose you a mark.

Adaptation How It Helps Absorption

Say It in One Sentence

Digestion Breaking large insoluble molecules into small soluble ones so they can cross the gut wall into the blood.
Pepsin A protease in the stomach that works in acid (pH 2) to break proteins into amino acids.
Bile NOT an enzyme — it physically emulsifies fat into tiny droplets so lipase has more surface area to work on.
Villus A tiny finger in the ileum wall with a huge surface area, thin walls, capillaries, and a lacteal for maximum absorption.
Liver Stores glucose as glycogen, makes bile, deaminates excess amino acids into urea, and detoxifies alcohol.
Egestion Undigested food (like fibre) passes out as faeces — it was never absorbed, so it's NOT excretion.
Balanced diet All seven components (carbs, protein, fat, vitamins, minerals, water, fibre) in the correct proportions.

Memory Tricks

Enzyme Naming Pattern
Most enzymes end in "-ase" and are named after their substrate: lipase digests lipids, protease digests proteins, amylase digests amylose (starch), maltase digests maltose. If you know the substrate, you know the enzyme name.
Deficiency Diseases — "SRAI"
Scurvy = vitamin C (citrus). Rickets = vitamin D (sunlight/dairy). Anaemia = Iron (red meat). The odd one out: Kwashiorkor = protein deficiency.
The Ham Sandwich Journey
Trace three foods through the gut: Bread (starch → amylase → maltose → maltase → glucose). Ham (protein → pepsin/trypsin → amino acids). Butter (fat → bile emulsifies → lipase → fatty acids + glycerol). If you can do this from memory, you own the chapter.
Bile Is the Sidekick, Not the Hero
Bile doesn't digest anything — it just chops fat into smaller droplets (like cutting a pizza into slices so more people can eat at once). Lipase does the actual chemical digestion.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"Why is digestion necessary?" Large molecules are insoluble and too big to cross the gut wall into the blood
"Name the enzyme that digests fat" Lipase (NOT bile — bile emulsifies but does not chemically digest)
"What is the role of bile?" Emulsifies fat (increases surface area for lipase) and neutralises stomach acid
"Describe the adaptations of a villus" Large surface area (villi + microvilli), thin wall (one cell), blood supply (capillaries), lacteal for fats
"What is the difference between egestion and excretion?" Egestion = removing undigested food. Excretion = removing metabolic waste made inside cells
"Where does most absorption take place?" The ileum (small intestine) — via villi
"What does the liver do with excess amino acids?" Deamination — removes the amino group, converts it to urea (excreted by kidneys)
"Name a deficiency disease caused by lack of iron" Anaemia — too few red blood cells / not enough haemoglobin
