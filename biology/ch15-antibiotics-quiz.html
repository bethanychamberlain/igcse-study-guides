Foundation — Can You Name It?

Worth 1-2 marks each on the exam. Get these right and you're building a solid base.
1
Define the term antibiotic.
2 marks
Answer
An antibiotic is a substance produced by a microorganism [1] that kills or inhibits the growth of another microorganism (especially bacteria) [1].
Both parts needed: made BY a microorganism, kills ANOTHER microorganism. Don't just say "a medicine that kills bacteria" — you'll lose the first mark.

2
State one difference between an antibiotic and an antiseptic.
1 mark
Answer
An antibiotic is taken inside the body to treat an infection, while an antiseptic is applied to the outside of the body to prevent infection [1].
The key difference is inside vs outside. You could also say: antibiotic = treats, antiseptic = prevents.

3
Name the fungus that produces penicillin and explain how penicillin kills bacteria.
2 marks
Answer
Penicillin is produced by the fungus Penicillium [1]. It works by preventing the formation of bacterial cell walls [1].
Spell Penicillium correctly (capital P, double l). For the mechanism, "prevents cell wall formation" is the key phrase.

4
State two reasons why antibiotics cannot be used to treat viral diseases such as the common cold.
2 marks
Answer
Viruses do not have cell walls [1] and viruses have no metabolism of their own [1], so there is nothing for the antibiotic to target.
Two separate reasons for 2 marks. Don't just say "antibiotics only work on bacteria" — you need to say WHY (no cell walls, no metabolism).

Intermediate — Can You Explain It?

Worth 3-4 marks each. You need to explain WHY, not just name things.
5
Penicillin does not harm human cells. Explain why.
3 marks
Answer
Penicillin works by preventing the formation of cell walls [1]. Bacterial cells have cell walls, but human cells do not have cell walls [1]. Therefore penicillin has no target in human cells and does not affect them [1].
The chain: penicillin targets cell walls → human cells don't have cell walls → so penicillin can't harm them. Each link = 1 mark.

6
Penicillin is described as a secondary metabolic product. Explain what this means and describe the conditions needed for its large-scale production.
4 marks
Answer
A secondary metabolic product is one that is made when the growth of the producing organism is slowing down, not during its main growth phase [1]. Conditions needed: food/nutrients for energy [1], oxygen for aerobic respiration [1], correct temperature (24°C) and pH (7.5), and sterile conditions to prevent contamination by other microbes [1].
"Secondary" = produced AFTER main growth. If you mention "sterile" and explain why (no competition, no toxins), that's a strong answer.

7
A scientist tests four different antibiotics (A, B, C, D) on a bacterial culture using paper discs soaked in each antibiotic. Antibiotic B produces the largest clear zone around its disc. What does this tell you?
3 marks
Answer
The clear zone (also called the zone of inhibition) is the area where bacteria have been killed or prevented from growing [1]. The larger the clear zone, the more effective the antibiotic [1]. Therefore antibiotic B is the most effective antibiotic against this particular strain of bacteria [1].
This is the "multidisk" experiment from the textbook. If a disc has NO clear zone, that antibiotic doesn't work at all on that bacterium — possibly a resistant strain.

Advanced — Can You Discuss It?

Worth 5-6 marks each. These need full sentences, multiple points, and clear reasoning.
8
Explain how a population of bacteria can become resistant to an antibiotic. Use the terms mutation, selection pressure, and survival advantage in your answer.
5 marks
Answer
A random mutation occurs in one bacterium, giving it resistance to the antibiotic [1]. When the antibiotic is applied, it acts as a selection pressure — it kills all the non-resistant bacteria [1]. The resistant bacterium has a survival advantage because it is not killed [1]. This bacterium survives and reproduces by cell division, passing the resistance gene to all its offspring [1]. The whole population is now resistant to the antibiotic. Note: the antibiotic does not create the resistance; it selects for bacteria that already have it [1].
5 marks = 5 ideas. Mutation → selection pressure → survival advantage → reproduction → whole population resistant. The last point (antibiotic selects, doesn't create) is a common bonus mark.

9
MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is a serious problem in hospitals. Explain what MRSA is, why it is dangerous, and suggest two actions that could help reduce the spread of antibiotic-resistant bacteria.
5 marks
Answer
MRSA is a strain of Staphylococcus aureus that is resistant to methicillin (a synthetic form of penicillin) [1]. The bacteria produce a protein that prevents the antibiotic from blocking cell wall production [1]. It is dangerous because it is very difficult to treat and hospital patients often have weakened immune systems [1].

Actions to reduce resistance: (1) Only prescribe antibiotics when truly necessary (not for viral infections) [1]. (2) Patients should always complete the full course of antibiotics so that all bacteria are killed [1].
Other valid actions: better hygiene in hospitals, developing new antibiotics, using antibiotics from different classes. Any two reasonable suggestions score.

10
A doctor tells a patient with a cold: "I cannot prescribe antibiotics for this." The patient says: "But I had a chest infection last month and antibiotics cured me. Why won't they work now?" Write the explanation the doctor should give.
6 marks
Answer
The cold is caused by a virus, while the chest infection was caused by bacteria [1]. Antibiotics only work on bacteria, not viruses [1]. This is because antibiotics work by targeting features of bacterial cells, such as cell walls [1]. Viruses do not have cell walls and have no metabolism of their own, so there is nothing for the antibiotic to attack [1]. Taking antibiotics unnecessarily would have no benefit [1] and could contribute to the development of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, making future infections harder to treat [1].
This is the "explain to a patient" style question that combines everything: virus vs bacteria, mechanism of antibiotics, why they don't work on viruses, and the danger of overuse. Practice writing it as a paragraph.
