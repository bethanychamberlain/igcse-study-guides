Key Vocabulary

Word What It Means Don't Confuse With
Pathogen An organism that causes disease. Types: bacteria, viruses, fungi, protoctists. Antigen (an antigen is the marker protein on the pathogen's surface, not the pathogen itself)
Antigen A protein or carbohydrate on a pathogen's surface that the immune system recognises as foreign. Antibody (antibodies are Y-shaped proteins made by lymphocytes that BIND to antigens)
Antibody A Y-shaped protein produced by lymphocytes. Each antibody is specific to one antigen. Antigen (antigen is on the pathogen; antibody is made by the body to fight it)
Phagocyte White blood cell that engulfs and digests pathogens (phagocytosis). Lymphocyte (lymphocytes make antibodies; phagocytes eat pathogens)
Lymphocyte White blood cell that produces antibodies specific to a pathogen's antigen. Phagocyte (phagocytes eat; lymphocytes launch antibodies)
Memory cell A lymphocyte that remains in the body after infection. Enables a faster, stronger response if the same pathogen returns. —
Vaccine A weakened or dead form of a pathogen (or its antigens) injected to stimulate antibody production and memory cell formation. Antibiotic (antibiotics treat bacterial infections AFTER they happen; vaccines prevent them)
Active immunity The body makes its own antibodies (and memory cells). Long-lasting. Happens by infection or vaccination. Passive immunity (receives ready-made antibodies from someone else — no memory cells, short-term)
Passive immunity The body receives ready-made antibodies (e.g. from mother via placenta/breast milk, or by injection). Short-term. Active immunity (body makes its own; passive = borrowed)
Fibrinogen An inactive protein dissolved in plasma. Converted to fibrin (active mesh) during blood clotting. Fibrin (fibrin is the active form that makes the mesh — fibrinogen is the inactive precursor)

Active vs Passive Immunity — Side by Side

Active Immunity Passive Immunity
Source of antibodies Body makes its OWN Receives READY-MADE from elsewhere
Natural example Catching the disease and recovering Mother's antibodies via placenta or breast milk
Artificial example Vaccination Injection of antibodies (e.g. anti-tetanus serum)
Memory cells? Yes — long-term protection No — short-term only
Speed Slow start (days to make antibodies) Instant (antibodies already made)
Exam Trap — Active = Own, Passive = Borrowed
The key test: did the body make its own antibodies? If yes = active (with memory cells, lasts a long time). If no (antibodies came from outside) = passive (no memory cells, wears off quickly).

Say It in One Sentence

Pathogen Any organism that causes disease — it's a parasite that harms its host by releasing toxins or destroying cells.
Antibodies Y-shaped proteins made by lymphocytes; each one is specific to one antigen and can clump, burst, or neutralise pathogens.
Phagocytosis A phagocyte flows around a pathogen, encloses it in a vacuole, and digests it with enzymes.
Blood clotting Damaged platelets release enzymes → fibrinogen converts to fibrin → mesh traps RBCs → scab forms.
Vaccination Inject weakened/dead pathogen → lymphocytes make antibodies + memory cells → second exposure gets a faster, stronger response.
Herd immunity When enough people are vaccinated, the pathogen can't find hosts to infect, so even unvaccinated people are protected.
Antibiotics Kill bacteria by disrupting their cell processes, but do NOT work on viruses because viruses replicate inside host cells.

Memory Tricks

Antigen vs Antibody — "Anti-GEN = Generated by pathogen"
The anti-GEN is what the pathogen generates (its surface marker). The anti-BODY is what your body makes to fight it. Antigen = on the bad guy. Antibody = your weapon.
The Defence Layers (Outside In)
Think of a castle: 1. Walls = skin, mucus, stomach acid (barriers). 2. Guards = phagocytes (eat invaders). 3. Archers = lymphocytes (launch antibodies from a distance). 4. Memory = memory cells (remember the enemy for next time).
Clotting Sequence: "PEF-MS"
Platelets damaged → Enzymes released → Fibrinogen converts to fibrin → Mesh of fibres traps red blood cells → Scab forms. Five steps, five letters.
Why Antibiotics Don't Work on Viruses
Antibiotics attack bacterial structures (like cell walls). Viruses have no cell walls — they hide inside YOUR cells and use YOUR machinery to reproduce. It's like trying to shoot the burglar when he's hiding behind your own family.

Quick-Fire Exam Cues

If the Question Says... The Answer Is...
"What is a pathogen?" An organism that causes disease. It is a parasite that harms its host.
"Describe how phagocytes defend against pathogens" Phagocyte engulfs pathogen into a vacuole → digestive enzymes break it down
"Why are antibodies described as specific?" Each antibody has a unique shape that binds to only one type of antigen
"Explain how vaccination leads to immunity" Vaccine (weakened pathogen) → lymphocytes produce antibodies → memory cells formed → second exposure triggers faster, stronger response
"Distinguish between active and passive immunity" Active: body makes own antibodies, memory cells, long-term. Passive: receives ready-made antibodies, no memory, short-term
"Why can't antibiotics treat influenza?" Influenza is caused by a virus. Antibiotics only work on bacteria.
"What is herd immunity?" If enough people are vaccinated, the pathogen can't spread because there are too few susceptible hosts
"Describe the role of platelets in blood clotting" Platelets release enzymes that convert fibrinogen to fibrin, forming a mesh that traps RBCs to seal the wound
