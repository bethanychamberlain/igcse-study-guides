Section 4.1 Biological Molecules
Living things are made of molecules. These biological molecules are often called organic molecules because they all contain carbon. There are four main groups, and each has a different job.
W
Write This Down — Organic Molecules

Organic molecules contain carbon and hydrogen atoms (often with other elements too). Carbon atoms bond strongly to each other, so organic molecules can be very large with chain and ring structures.

Organisms need organic molecules to:
• Provide energy to drive life processes
• Provide raw materials for the growth and repair of tissues

W
Write This Down — The Four Groups

The four main groups of organic molecules in living things:

1. Carbohydrates — contain C, H and O — main energy source
2. Lipids (fats and oils) — contain C, H and O — energy store and barriers
3. Proteins — contain C, H, O, N (and sometimes S) — growth and repair
4. Nucleic acids — contain C, H, O, N and P — carry genetic information

?
Why This Matters

The exam loves asking: "Which elements does [molecule type] contain?" Carbohydrates and lipids = C, H, O only. Proteins add nitrogen (N). This is also how the biuret test works — it detects the nitrogen in protein.

  • I know what "organic molecule" means (contains carbon + hydrogen)
  • I can name the four groups of biological molecules
  • I know which elements each group contains
W
Write This Down — Carbohydrates

Monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrates (e.g. glucose). Glucose is soluble, so it dissolves in blood and is easily transported. It is the main source of energy for cells.

Polysaccharides are formed when many monosaccharide molecules join together:
• Starch — energy store in plants (made of many glucose units)
• Glycogen — energy store in animals (also made of glucose units)
• Cellulose — structural; makes up plant cell walls

Polysaccharides are insoluble, which makes them good for storage (they won't dissolve away) and for structures like cell walls.

!
Don't Confuse — Starch vs Glycogen

Both starch and glycogen are made of glucose. The difference: starch is the energy store in plants, glycogen is the energy store in animals. Don't mix them up — the exam will test this.

  • I know the difference between monosaccharides and polysaccharides
  • I can name examples of each (glucose; starch, glycogen, cellulose)
W
Write This Down — Lipids (Fats and Oils)

Lipids are formed from one molecule of glycerol combined with three molecules of fatty acid.

• Fats are solid at room temperature
• Oils are liquid at room temperature
• Both are insoluble in water

Functions of lipids:
• Excellent energy stores (even better than carbohydrates per gram)
• Form barriers between watery environments (e.g. cell membranes)

T
Study Tip — Remember "Glycerol + 3 Fatty Acids"

Think: G + 3F = Lipid. If the exam asks you to describe the structure of a lipid, always mention both parts: glycerol AND fatty acids. Just saying "fat" is not enough.

  • I know the building blocks of a lipid (glycerol + 3 fatty acids)
  • I know the difference between a fat and an oil
W
Write This Down — Proteins

Proteins are made of long chains of amino acids (the subunits). There are 20 different amino acids that can be joined in any order, so there is a huge variety of different proteins.

The sequence of amino acids determines the shape of the protein. The shape determines its function:
• Enzymes — have an active site that fits a specific substrate
• Antibodies — have a binding site that fits a specific pathogen
• Keratin — long, thin structural protein in hair and nails

Amino acids are soluble, so they can be transported in blood and take part in reactions in the watery cytoplasm of cells.

?
Why This Matters

This links directly to the enzymes chapter. When the exam asks "Why does an enzyme only work on one substrate?", the answer traces back here: the amino acid sequence gives the enzyme its specific shape, and only one substrate fits the active site.

  • I know that proteins are made of amino acids
  • I understand that amino acid sequence determines protein shape and function
W
Write This Down — Building Up and Breaking Down

Large molecules are built from small subunits by two key reactions:

Condensation — small molecules are joined together, and water is removed (produced).
Example: glucose + glucose → maltose + water

Hydrolysis — large molecules are broken down into smaller ones, and water is added (used up).
Example: starch + water → glucose

!
Don't Confuse — Condensation vs Hydrolysis

They are opposites. Condensation builds up (removes water). Hydrolysis breaks down (adds water). The clue is in the name: "hydro" = water, "lysis" = splitting. So hydrolysis = splitting with water.

T
Study Tip

Remember: Condensation = connecting (building up, water out). Hydrolysis = water (hydro) splitting (lysis) things apart.

  • I can define condensation and hydrolysis
  • I know which one removes water and which one adds water
W
Write This Down — Water as a Solvent

Water is the most important biological solvent — it dissolves other substances so they can be transported and take part in reactions.

Water acts as a solvent in:
• Digestion — enzymes work in watery solutions
• Excretion — waste products dissolve in water to be removed
• Transport — blood plasma is mainly water, carrying dissolved substances

  • I know that water is a biological solvent and can give three roles
Section 4.2 Testing for Biochemicals
Scientists use simple chemical tests to find out which biological molecules are present in a solution. You must know all four food tests — the steps, the reagents, and the colour changes. This is one of the most commonly examined topics.
W
Write This Down — Test for Glucose (Reducing Sugar)

Reagent: Benedict's reagent (pale blue liquid)
Method:
1. Add 2 cm³ of Benedict's reagent to 2 cm³ of the unknown solution
2. Heat the mixture in a boiling water bath for 2–3 minutes
Positive result: Colour changes from blue → green → yellow → orange/brick-red
Negative result: Stays blue (no reducing sugar present)

!
Don't Confuse — You Must Heat Benedict's

Benedict's test requires heating in a water bath. If you forget to mention heating, you lose a mark. None of the other food tests need heating — only Benedict's.

W
Write This Down — Test for Starch

Reagent: Iodine solution (brown/yellow liquid)
Method:
1. Add a few drops of iodine solution to 2 cm³ of the unknown solution
2. Gently shake
Positive result: Colour changes to deep blue-black
Negative result: Stays brown/yellow (no starch present)

W
Write This Down — Test for Protein

Reagent: Biuret reagent (pale blue liquid)
Method:
1. Add a few drops of biuret reagent to 2 cm³ of the unknown solution
2. Gently shake (no heating needed)
Positive result: Colour changes to mauve/purple
Negative result: Stays blue (no protein present)

!
Don't Confuse — Benedict's vs Biuret

Both start as blue liquids, but they test for different things:
• Benedict's = reducing sugar (glucose) → goes orange/brick-red when heated
• Biuret = protein → goes purple with NO heating
Don't mix them up. The exam will try to trick you.

W
Write This Down — Test for Lipids (Emulsion Test)

Reagent: Ethanol + distilled water (this is a physical test, not a chemical one)
Method:
1. Add 2 cm³ of ethanol to the unknown solution and gently shake
2. Pour the mixture into a test tube containing an equal volume of distilled water
Positive result: A milky-white emulsion forms (cloudy white layer)
Negative result: Solution stays clear (no lipid present)

?
Why This Matters — Why Ethanol?

Lipids are insoluble in water, so you can't just add a reagent to a watery solution. The ethanol dissolves the lipid first, then when you add water, the lipid comes out of solution as tiny droplets — that's the milky emulsion. The exam may ask why the emulsion test uses ethanol.

W
Write This Down — Test for Vitamin C

Reagent: DCPIP (a blue dye)
Method:
1. Add drops of the unknown solution to DCPIP one drop at a time
Positive result: DCPIP is decolourised (the blue colour disappears)
Negative result: DCPIP stays blue

The fewer drops needed to decolourise DCPIP, the more concentrated the vitamin C solution.

Write This Down — Controls

Every food test needs two controls:

1. Positive control — test a solution you know contains the substance. This proves the reagent is working properly. It should give a positive result.

2. Negative control — test a sample of distilled water. This proves the test solutions are not contaminated. It should give a negative result.

?
Why This Matters — Fair Testing

When comparing solutions (e.g. glucose levels in different urine samples), you must keep conditions the same:
• Equal volumes of unknown solution
• Equal volumes of reagent
• Same temperature and heating time
The exam often asks about "fair test" conditions for food tests. These are your marks.
