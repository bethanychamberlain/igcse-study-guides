Section 20.1 Food Supply: Humans and Agriculture
As the human population grows, we need more food. Modern agriculture is efficient but comes with serious environmental costs.
W
Write This Down — Monoculture

Monoculture = growing a single crop over a large area.

Advantages: specialised harvesting machines, highly selected strains, precise mineral/water requirements calculated.

Disadvantages: poor wildlife food variety, diseases spread easily between identical plants, loss of genetic variety (vulnerable to environmental change), soil minerals depleted by the same crop year after year.

W
Write This Down — Intensive Farming

Intensive farming of livestock keeps many animals in limited space. Made possible by:

• Temperature control (reduces energy wasted on heat production)

• Selective breeding for high conversion ratio (food → body mass)

• Minimised movement (less energy wasted)

• High-protein food, antibiotics, hygienic conditions

Disadvantages: waste pollutes rivers, animal welfare concerns, disease spreads easily, antibiotics can select resistant bacteria, heating/lighting costs and environmental damage.

?
Why This Matters

Exam questions about intensive farming often ask you to discuss both sides — benefit (more food) vs cost (pollution, welfare, antibiotic resistance). Always give at least one advantage and one disadvantage.

  • I can define monoculture and give advantages + disadvantages
  • I can explain how intensive farming increases food production
  • I know the disadvantages of intensive livestock farming
Section 20.2 Deforestation and Land Use
Humans remove forests for farmland, timber, and resources. The consequences ripple through the entire ecosystem.
W
Write This Down — Consequences of Deforestation

Deforestation = the rapid destruction of woodland. The technique used to clear forest is often called slash and burn — the largest trees are removed for timber, and the rest is chopped down and burned.

Consequences:

• Soil erosion — tree roots no longer hold soil; wind and rain remove it.

• Reduced soil fertility — deciduous trees contain 90% of the forest nutrients; removing them removes nutrients.

• Flooding and landslips — normally in a woodland, 25% of rainfall is absorbed by foliage or evaporates and 50% is absorbed by root systems. After deforestation, water accumulates rapidly in river valleys, often causing landslips from steep hillsides.

• Rising CO₂ — fewer trees means less photosynthesis to absorb CO₂.

• Reduced O₂ — less photosynthesis means less oxygen produced.

• Climate change — drier atmosphere, reduced rainfall, more intense winds.

• Species extinction — many species depend on forest habitats (e.g. orangutans).

W
Write This Down — Hedgerow Removal

Farmers remove hedges to make fields bigger for machinery.

Benefits of hedges: windbreaks, nesting sites for 65+ bird species (predators of crop pests), limit soil erosion, nutrient enrichment from fallen leaves, breeding sites for pollinating insects.

150,000+ km of hedgerow removed in Britain since WW2.

T
Study Tip

For exam questions on deforestation, learn at least four consequences. The question might ask for "three effects" — having four in your head means you can pick the best three. Always link back to scientific processes: "less photosynthesis → more CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect."

  • I can list at least four consequences of deforestation
  • I can explain why hedgerows are important for biodiversity
Section 20.3 Malnutrition and Famine
Damage to ecosystems can lead to malnutrition and famine. This section connects population growth, food supply, and environmental factors.
W
Write This Down — Malnutrition

Malnutrition = a condition resulting from a defective diet where certain important food nutrients (such as proteins, vitamins, or carbohydrates) may be absent.

A malnourished person is more at risk from other diseases because their immune system is reduced in efficiency.

W
Write This Down — Causes of Famine

• Increasing population — more people means greater demand for food, while more domestic animals may reduce land available for growing crops.

• Drought — global warming may upset rainfall patterns, reducing water available for plant growth and dramatically reducing crop yield.

• Flooding — unpredictable flooding can damage crops (e.g. maize) and wash away fertile soil.

• Unequal distribution of food — some areas produce more food due to better environment or advanced agriculture. Food surpluses cannot always be moved to where they are needed.

• Cost of fuel and fertiliser — crop yield may be low in developing countries because poor farmers cannot afford fertiliser or fuel to run machinery.

?
Why This Matters

About 15% of the world's population receive fewer than 10,000 kJ per day. The exam may ask you to connect food supply problems to environmental damage (drought from climate change, soil loss from deforestation, flooding from extreme weather).

  • I can define malnutrition
  • I can list at least three causes of famine
Section 20.4 Pollution: Atmosphere, Water, and Land
Pollution is any effect of human activities that has a harmful effect on the environment. The exam wants you to know causes, effects, AND solutions for each type.
W
Write This Down — Greenhouse Effect

Greenhouse gases (CO₂ and methane) trap infrared radiation (heat) near the Earth's surface.

Causes of increase: combustion of fossil fuels (CO₂), methane from cattle/rice fields/landfill.

Effects (global warming): climatic extremes (stronger winds, heavier rainfall), melting polar ice → rising sea levels, desertification, pests spreading to new areas.

Solutions: reduce fossil fuel burning, use alternative energy, reduce deforestation, replant forests.

W
Write This Down — Acid Rain

Cause: Burning fossil fuels releases sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NO/NO₂). These dissolve in rainwater to form sulfuric acid and nitric acid.

Effects: soils become acidic (minerals leach out, decomposition inhibited), lakes become acidic (fish and invertebrates die), forest trees starve from mineral loss.

Solutions: scrubbers in power stations, catalytic converters in cars, adding crushed limestone to acidified lakes.

W
Write This Down — Pesticide Bioaccumulation

Pesticides (e.g. DDT) can be amplified through food chains. Concentration increases at each trophic level because the pesticide is stored in body tissues, not broken down.

Example: water (0.02 ppm) → plankton (5) → small fish (50) → large fish (250) → bird (1500 ppm). The top predator gets a lethal dose even though the water concentration was tiny.

W
Write This Down — Non-biodegradable Plastics

Many plastics are made from large hydrocarbon molecules and cannot be decomposed by normal microbial methods. They are non-biodegradable.

Problems:

• Block drainage channels, leading to waterlogging and reduced soil fertility.

• Mistakenly consumed by animals on land and in water, blocking their digestive systems and causing death.

• In landfill sites, they do not allow the passage of oxygen, inhibiting natural decomposition of other wastes.

• If burned, they release toxic “smoky” particles that affect breathing and have long-term health effects.

!
Don't Confuse

Greenhouse effect vs acid rain: they are both caused by burning fossil fuels, but by different gases. CO₂ causes the greenhouse effect. SO₂ and nitrogen oxides cause acid rain. Don't mix them up in an exam answer.

  • I can describe causes, effects, and solutions for the greenhouse effect
  • I can describe causes, effects, and solutions for acid rain
  • I can explain how pesticides accumulate through food chains
  • I can explain why non-biodegradable plastics are an environmental problem
Section 20.5 Eutrophication
Eutrophication is what happens when too many nutrients get into water. It's a chain reaction that ends with dead fish. Learn the sequence — the exam tests it step by step.
W
Write This Down — The Eutrophication Sequence

Step 1: Excess nitrates and phosphates enter water from fertiliser runoff (leaching), raw sewage, or liquid manure (slurry) washed from farmyards.

Step 2: Algae and surface plants grow rapidly — an algal bloom.

Step 3: The algae block light to plants below the surface. These rooted plants die.

Step 4: Dead plants + dead algae provide food for aerobic decomposing bacteria, whose population multiplies rapidly.

Step 5: The bacteria consume large amounts of oxygen for respiration (creating a high biological oxygen demand / BOD).

Step 6: Other organisms (fish, invertebrates) cannot get enough oxygen and die.

Step 7: Their bodies provide even more food for bacteria — a positive feedback loop that makes it worse.

!
Don't Confuse

Eutrophication is NOT "fertiliser poisons the fish." The fertiliser feeds algae, which block light, which kills plants, which feeds bacteria, which use up oxygen, which suffocates fish. It's an indirect chain of events. The exam wants all the steps, not just "fertiliser kills fish."

W
Write This Down — BOD

Biological oxygen demand (BOD) = the mass of oxygen consumed by microorganisms in a sample of water. Measured by comparing oxygen concentration before and after a period of microbial respiration. High BOD = polluted water.

T
Study Tip — Learn the Chain

The eutrophication sequence is one of the most commonly tested 5–6 mark questions. Memorise it as a numbered list. Practise writing it out from memory. Each step is worth a mark.

  • I can describe all steps of eutrophication in order
  • I know what BOD means and what a high BOD indicates
  • I can explain why eutrophication involves positive feedback
Section 20.6–20.8 Conservation and Endangered Species
Humans can also have positive effects. Conservation is about balancing human needs with maintaining biodiversity.
W
Write This Down — Why Species Become Endangered

• Habitat loss — land cleared for agriculture or building

• Over-hunting / commercial exploitation — e.g. tigers hunted for fur

• Pollution — rivers and ponds lose oxygen-requiring species

• Introduced species — e.g. stoats on islands eliminate ground-nesting birds

• Climate change — loss of Arctic ice affects polar bears

• Pest control — hunting of predators like cheetahs that kill livestock

W
Write This Down — Conservation Strategies

Sustainable development = using resources at a rate that allows them to be replaced, so future generations can also use them.

Conservation programmes involve:

• Sampling — assess the population

• Management plan — devise strategies to protect/increase the population

• Carrying out the plan

• Re-sampling — check if it worked

Types: Preservation (keep unchanged), Reclamation (restore damaged habitats), Creation (make new habitats, e.g. garden ponds).

Other tools:

• Captive breeding in zoos (e.g. Arabian oryx bred in London, San Diego and Phoenix zoos, then released into Oman and Jordan)

• Seed banks for threatened plants — seeds stored in cool, dry conditions and can be germinated when needed

• Education programmes — local people taught about the tourist value of conserving species

• CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species) — controls transfer of wildlife products between member countries

• IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature)

• WWF (Worldwide Fund for Nature) — attracts public funding for conservation projects

• Protected areas / national parks

?
Why This Matters

Conservation questions often ask you to describe a strategy for a named species. The template is always: (1) why it's endangered, (2) what's being done (protected area, breeding programme, legal protection), (3) why genetic variation matters if populations are small.

  • I can list at least four reasons why species become endangered
  • I can define sustainable development
  • I can describe the stages of a conservation programme
Section 20.7 Managing Fish Stocks
Overfishing is a real-world example of humans disrupting an ecosystem. The exam often uses fish stocks as a case study for sustainability.
W
Write This Down — How Fish Stocks Are Managed

• Education — study fish growth patterns to advise on mesh sizes and fishing methods.

• Net mesh size — use larger meshes that catch mature fish but let young/immature fish escape so they can reproduce.

• Quotas — governments set limits on how many fish can be caught.

• Restocking — fish bred in farms (aquaculture) can be released to replenish wild populations.

• Fish farming — species grown in enclosures with controlled food, temperature, and disease management.

!
Don't Confuse

Fish farming has environmental concerns too: pesticides in the water kill wild organisms, excess food and faeces cause eutrophication below the pens (increased BOD). It's not a perfect solution — know both sides.

  • I can describe at least three methods used to conserve fish stocks
  • I know the advantages and environmental concerns of fish farming
